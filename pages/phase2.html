The Hiring Formula
Mark Roberge's hiring formula is the most rigorous framework available for building predictable sales teams. Its central insight: stop hiring on gut feel and industry experience, and start hiring on a data-driven competency scorecard built from your own top performers.
Why Most Companies Hire Wrong

The default hiring pattern in SaaS sales is dangerously flawed. Hiring managers routinely overweight:

  • Industry experience — believing domain knowledge transfers to sales skill. It rarely does. Most domain knowledge can be taught in 30 days; sales habits cannot.
  • Company brand on resume — assuming someone who sold at Salesforce or Gartner will replicate that performance in a different motion, at a different price point, with a different buyer.
  • Charisma and likability in the interview — selecting for the traits that make people good at interviewing, not at selling.
  • Years of experience — seniority is a proxy for track record, but track record in a mismatched motion is worse than a blank slate.

The result: high variance in rep quality, unpredictable ramp, and attrition driven by rep-role misfit — all of which masquerade as a pipeline problem or a product problem. They are actually hiring problems.

Root Cause Warning

If your team shows high variance in quota attainment (some reps at 150%, most below 60%), the first diagnostic question is not "what are we doing wrong in coaching?" — it is "did we hire correctly?" Coaching cannot fix a competency mismatch.

The 5 Competencies That Predict Rep Success

Roberge's research, derived from building HubSpot's sales team from zero to $100M, identified five universal predictors of sales rep success. These should be the backbone of every hiring scorecard:

Competency What It Means How to Assess It Why It Matters
Coachability The ability to receive feedback, internalize it, and apply it rapidly — without defensiveness Role-play + structured feedback: give a correction mid-exercise, observe response The entire ramp is a coaching process. An uncoachable rep cannot be developed.
Work Ethic & Drive Intrinsic motivation; willingness to outwork the problem when results aren't coming Behavioral: "Tell me about a time you failed and what you did next." Look for self-directed action without being managed. Sales is a grind. Motivated reps survive rough quarters; unmotivated ones churn at 6 months.
Prior Success Pattern A history of setting goals and achieving them — in any domain (sports, academics, prior roles) Evidence-based questions. Require specifics: numbers, ranks, outcomes. "Top 10% of class" or "quota 3 years running." Past pattern of achievement is the single best predictor of future achievement, regardless of context.
Intelligence Ability to rapidly process new information, spot patterns, and adapt in complex buyer conversations Ask them to explain a complex concept simply; walk through a case scenario; observe how quickly they synthesize feedback Complex SaaS deals require strategic thinking, not just script execution. Low intelligence is a ceiling.
Preparation The discipline to research before acting — showing up to interviews, calls, and demos informed What did they research before this interview? Can they speak to your product, your customers, your market? Prepared reps run better discovery, handle objections with evidence, and close faster. Lazy reps wing it.
Roberge's Principle

The competencies that matter most are NOT generic — they must be calibrated to your specific sales motion. Run a correlation analysis: score your current top performers on these dimensions and identify which competencies are most predictive in your context. Then weight your scorecard accordingly.

Building the Competency Scorecard

A competency scorecard converts subjective impressions into structured, comparable data. Every interviewer rates every candidate on the same dimensions using the same rubric. This eliminates recency bias, halo effects, and the interviewer's personal preferences.

  1. Identify your top 5–8 performers Retrospectively score them on the 5 competencies. Find where they cluster. That cluster defines your "A player" profile for this motion.
  2. Weight the competencies If coachability is the bottleneck in your ramp (because your motion changes frequently), weight it at 25%. If intelligence is critical for enterprise deal complexity, weight it higher. Weights should sum to 100.
  3. Define behavioral anchors For each competency, define what a 1, 3, and 5 looks like with specific observable behaviors. "Demonstrates curiosity" is not a behavioral anchor. "Asked 3 follow-up questions after receiving feedback, then applied correction correctly on the next rep" is.
  4. Assign interview questions to competencies Each interviewer owns specific competencies. They ask the same questions every time. This creates cross-candidate comparability.
  5. Score independently before debriefing All interviewers submit scores before the debrief meeting. This prevents anchoring (the first person to speak shapes everyone else's opinion).
  6. Track predictive validity over time Compare hiring scorecard scores to 12-month quota attainment. Refine the model. The scorecard improves as you accumulate data.
The Structured Interview Process

Structured interviews are 2× more predictive of job performance than unstructured interviews (Schmidt & Hunter meta-analysis). Every step must be standardized.

The Reference Check Framework

Most reference checks are performative — a quick call where everyone says nice things. Done correctly, references are one of the most valuable data sources you have. The key is asking the right questions.

  • Work with them directly? Confirm the relationship. How closely did they work together? Direct manager carries far more weight than a colleague.
  • "What did [candidate] do better than most people at that level?" Surfaces genuine strengths, not platitudes.
  • "What would you coach them on if you could?" A good reference will tell you a real weakness. If they can't name one, probe harder or discount the reference.
  • "How did they respond to tough feedback or a down quarter?" The most predictive reference question for coachability and resilience.
  • "Would you hire them again, and into what role?" The closing question. If they wouldn't rehire or hedge significantly, that's a signal.
  • Ask for references not on their list. Request a back-channel reference — someone you can identify yourself who worked with this person. These conversations are unfiltered.
The "Hiring Bar" Concept — Never Lower It

The hiring bar is the minimum acceptable score on your competency scorecard. It should never be lowered because of pipeline pressure, urgency, or competitive offers. This is the single most important discipline in building a world-class sales team.

The Cost of Lowering the Bar

Every below-bar hire triggers a cascade: delayed ramp → underperformance → management distraction → team morale damage → eventual PIP or termination → backfill cycle. The fully-loaded cost of a bad hire is typically 1.5–3× OTE. A role left open for 60 days costs far less than a below-bar hire who leaves at month 8.

The practical rule: If the team isn't unanimously excited about the candidate, pass. "They're good enough" is not a passing grade. Urgency to fill a seat is a solvable problem. A below-bar hire creates problems that cannot be easily solved.

Ramp Design
Ramp is the period between a rep's start date and the date they are expected to carry full quota. It is not a period of forgiveness — it is a structured program with measurable milestones. Poor ramp design is one of the most expensive and invisible sources of revenue leakage in SaaS.
What Ramp Is and Why It Matters

Ramp time is the number of months it takes a new rep to achieve full productivity (100% of quota run rate). Every month a rep is not at full productivity represents foregone revenue. In a team of 10 reps with a 6-month ramp at 50% average productivity during ramp, the opportunity cost is enormous.

Fully-Loaded Ramp Cost
Ramp Cost = (OTE × ramp months / 12) + Opportunity Cost
Opportunity Cost = Pipeline Capacity × (1 − Avg Productivity During Ramp)
Pipeline Capacity = the fully-ramped rep's expected closed revenue per month
Example: OTE $120K, 5-month ramp, $80K/mo capacity, 40% avg productivity during ramp
→ Salary cost = $120K × 5/12 = $50K
→ Opportunity cost = $80K × 5 × (1 − 0.40) = $240K
→ Total ramp cost = ~$290K per hire

This math makes the stakes clear: every week shaved off ramp time, and every point of productivity improvement during ramp, has substantial financial value. A well-designed ramp program is not a training budget item — it is a revenue investment.

Milestone-Based vs. Time-Based Ramp

Most companies operate on time-based ramp: at month 3, the rep is at 50% quota; at month 6, 100%. This is structurally wrong. Time passes regardless of competency development. Milestone-based ramp ties quota exposure to demonstrated capability.

Best Practice

Design ramp certification milestones. A rep does not move to month-2 quota until they have passed month-1 certifications (product pitch, ICP quiz, first X discovery calls completed). This protects the company and gives reps a clear roadmap. It also makes underperformance visible earlier, enabling faster intervention or offboarding.

Management Cadence
The difference between a sales team that performs and one that doesn't is usually the cadence of management — not the quality of reps. Management cadence is the operating rhythm: the structured, repeatable set of meetings, reviews, and coaching touchpoints that keep execution aligned and performance visible.
Management vs. Coaching: The Critical Distinction

Most sales managers conflate management and coaching. They are fundamentally different activities that require different skills and different time allocation.

Dimension Management Coaching
Purpose Ensure process adherence, pipeline accuracy, forecast predictability Develop skill — discovery quality, objection handling, negotiation, executive presence
Focus Deals and numbers People and behaviors
Outcome This quarter's number Next year's performance ceiling
Primary tool CRM review, pipeline inspection, forecast calls Call recording review, live call joins, role play
Failure mode Micromanagement — killing rep autonomy and morale Absence — reps plateau, top performers leave for development opportunities

World-class sales managers spend roughly 50% of their time on each. Most spend 80% on management (pipeline inspection) and 20% or less on coaching. This produces teams that can hit this quarter but cannot grow.

The QBR Structure

A well-run QBR is not a recitation of CRM data. It is a structured strategic review that develops rep ownership and planning capability. The rep presents — the manager listens and challenges.

  1. Previous Quarter Retrospective (15 min) Performance vs. quota. Wins: what drove them? Losses: root cause analysis, not excuses. Deals that slipped: why, and what changed?
  2. Pipeline Review (10 min) Current pipeline health. Coverage ratio. Confidence in each deal. Aging pipeline callout — deals stalled >45 days.
  3. Next Quarter Territory Plan (15 min) Which accounts will be prioritized? What is the outbound strategy? What marketing campaigns are planned? What is the pipeline build plan?
  4. Development Goals (10 min) One or two specific skills the rep will work on. How will progress be measured? What support does the manager commit to providing?

The Inspect What You Expect Principle

Every metric you care about must be reviewed in a cadence meeting. If you care about multi-threading (contacts per opportunity), inspect it weekly. If you care about next-step quality (every deal has a scheduled next step), inspect it in the 1:1. You will get the behaviors you measure and reward. Uninspected metrics decay rapidly in any sales team.

Quota Design
Quota is the single most powerful lever in a sales compensation system. It determines behavior, morale, retention, and ultimately revenue. Poorly designed quotas produce the wrong outcomes even with good people. Correct quota design is both a science and an art.
Top-Down vs. Bottoms-Up Quota Setting
Approach How It Works Strengths Weaknesses
Top-Down Board/CEO sets revenue target → CFO divides by average ACV × expected headcount → individual quota emerges Simple, fast, aligned with investor expectations Ignores territory quality, rep experience, pipeline reality — produces "aspirational" quotas disconnected from what's achievable
Bottoms-Up Analyze historical attainment, pipeline velocity, territory potential → build a model of what each rep/territory can produce → aggregate to team quota Grounded in reality, identifies where the ceiling actually is Can understate ambition if historical data is from an underperforming team
Hybrid (Best Practice) Build bottoms-up model → present to leadership → negotiate gap if bottoms-up falls short of top-down → identify levers (more headcount, better territory, demand gen) to close gap Combines ambition with accountability; surfaces resource gaps early Requires more analytical work; requires honesty in both directions
The Quota-to-OTE Ratio

The quota-to-OTE ratio (also called the "productivity ratio" or "multiple") is one of the most important numbers in sales compensation design. It answers: how many dollars of revenue should one dollar of OTE produce?

Quota-to-OTE Ratio
Quota Multiple = Annual Quota / OTE
Industry benchmarks:
→ SMB AE: 4x–6x OTE (e.g., $120K OTE, $480K–$720K quota)
→ Mid-Market AE: 3x–5x OTE (e.g., $160K OTE, $480K–$800K quota)
→ Enterprise AE: 2.5x–4x OTE (e.g., $200K OTE, $500K–$800K quota)
→ SDR: N/A for closed revenue; measured on pipeline created vs. salary cost

Rule of thumb: If your quota multiple is below 2.5x, the cost of sales is too high for the motion to be profitable. If it's above 7x, reps will perceive quota as unachievable and disengage.
The 60–70% Attainment Distribution Target

The most diagnostically important metric in quota design is the attainment distribution across your team. The goal is for 60–70% of your reps to hit 100% of quota in a given year.

Raise quota for next period. Do this carefully — sudden quota increases destroy trust and drive attrition of top performers who feel the rules changed.
Scenario What It Means Correct Response
<50% attainment rate Quota is too high, territory is too thin, demand gen is insufficient, or ramp is broken. Reps are demoralized. Top performers are being recruited by competitors offering achievable quotas. Audit each factor. Don't reflexively cut quotas — identify the root cause. Usually it's pipeline coverage, not quota level.
60–70% attainment rate Target zone. Quota is stretching reps without demoralizing them. Accelerator economics work. The distribution is healthy. Maintain. Continue refining for equity across territories.
>80% attainment rate Quota is too low. The company is overpaying for results it could get with higher expectations. Leaves revenue on the table and signals to the board that the team is sandbagging.

Territory-Based vs. Role-Based Quota

Territory-based quota assigns each rep a different quota based on the assessed value of their territory. Role-based quota gives every rep in the same role the same number. Each has tradeoffs.

  • Role-based is simpler to administer but produces attainment variance driven by territory quality differences rather than rep performance. It is inherently unfair and creates resentment.
  • Territory-based requires rigorous territory assessment (account potential scoring, historical penetration, competitive presence) but is more equitable and produces a cleaner performance signal.
  • Best practice: Use territory-based quota for enterprise and named account models. Use role-based with territory equity reviews for SMB/round-robin models.
Mid-Year Quota Changes

Never change quota mid-year unless there is a structural change to the role, territory, or product offering that materially alters productivity expectations. Changing quotas mid-year because the team is tracking to exceed plan (the "sandbagging problem") destroys trust, drives attrition of your best performers, and signals that the comp plan is not a real contract. The correct lever for mid-year upside is SPIFs and accelerators, not quota resets.

Compensation Design
Compensation is the most powerful behavioral lever available to sales leadership. Every element of a comp plan — base, variable, accelerators, SPIFs, clawbacks — sends a signal. Reps are economically rational agents who will optimize for the plan you design. Design it incorrectly and they will optimize for the wrong things.
OTE Structure and Base:Variable Ratio

On-Target Earnings (OTE) is the total cash compensation a rep earns when they hit exactly 100% of quota. It comprises a base salary (fixed, paid regardless of performance) and a variable component (commission or bonus, earned through performance).

OTE Formula
OTE = Base Salary + Variable (at 100% attainment)
Commission rate at 100% = Variable / Quota
Example: OTE $160K, 60/40 split → Base $96K, Variable $64K
If quota = $800K → commission rate = $64K / $800K = 8% on every dollar closed
Role Typical Base:Variable Rationale
Enterprise AE (Field) 50:50 High deal complexity, long cycles — greater rep control over outcome justifies high variable leverage
Mid-Market / Inside Sales AE 60:40 Moderate cycle lengths; variable is meaningful but base recognizes process-driven nature
SDR / BDR 70:30 Activity-driven role with limited control over deal outcomes; high base prevents churn, moderate variable drives activity
Sales Manager 70:30 or 75:25 Management is a team sport; variable tied to team attainment, not individual closing
VP of Sales / CRO 50:50 or 60:40 Executive accountability for the number; higher variable leverage appropriate at this level
Accelerators Above 100%

Accelerators are higher commission rates that apply above quota attainment thresholds. They are among the most powerful motivational tools in sales compensation — and among the most misdesigned.

Attainment Tier Commission Rate (Example) Purpose
0–50% of quota 4% (below threshold — reduced rate or draw) Discourages coasting at low attainment; creates urgency to reach threshold
50–100% of quota 8% (base commission rate) Standard rate; earns "at-plan" variable
100–125% of quota 12% (1.5× accelerator) Rewards overperformance meaningfully; makes it worth pushing hard above 100%
125–150% of quota 16% (2× accelerator) Creates "President's Club" economics; top performers earn disproportionate upside
>150% of quota 20%+ (2.5× or uncapped) Legendary performers earn legendary money — retains your 10x reps
Accelerator Design Mistake

Many companies cap commission at 150% of OTE. This is strategically wrong. Your top 10% of performers generate 25–40% of revenue. Capping their upside signals that the company doesn't want to pay for extraordinary results — these reps will find plans at competitors that don't cap. Uncapped commission for top performers is a retention and recruitment strategy, not just a comp policy.

SPIFs: Proper Design and Use

A SPIF (Sales Performance Incentive Fund) is a short-term incentive layered on top of the base comp plan to drive a specific behavior over a defined period.

  • Right use: Product launch adoption ("$500 bonus for first deal on new product"), quarter-end acceleration ("extra 2% on all deals closed in the last 2 weeks"), pipeline building sprints ("$100 per qualified meeting booked this week")
  • Wrong use: Substituting for a broken comp plan; running SPIFs constantly (they lose their signal value); using SPIFs to make reps care about things that aren't in their quota — this signals misalignment between what you measure and what you're paying for
  • Duration: 2–4 weeks maximum. Longer than that and it becomes expected baseline behavior, not a sprint incentive.
  • Visibility: Announce via live team call, post a leaderboard, send daily updates. SPIFs work through social competition and urgency — quiet SPIFs do nothing.
Clawback Provisions

A clawback is a provision that requires a rep to return commission if a deal cancels or churns within a specified period after close. Clawbacks align rep incentives with customer success — not just deal signing.

  • Typical clawback period: 90–180 days after contract signature. Any churn within this period triggers a partial or full commission recapture.
  • Why they matter: Without clawbacks, reps are incentivized to close bad-fit customers, over-promise on capabilities, and discount aggressively to close — since they bear none of the downstream cost.
  • Implementation note: Communicate clawbacks clearly in the offer letter and comp plan document. Surprise clawbacks destroy trust and create legal exposure. Define the trigger conditions precisely.
  • Enterprise deals: Multi-year contracts may use pro-rated clawbacks (if deal churns in month 3 of a 12-month contract, claw back 75% of commission).
Commission Timing: When Does the Rep Get Paid?
Commission paid when customer pays their first invoice
Timing Method How It Works Pros Cons
On Booking Commission paid when contract is signed, regardless of payment Maximizes rep motivation; simplest to administer Company absorbs risk if customer doesn't pay; disconnects rep from collection outcome
On Invoice / Payment Aligns rep incentive with cash collection; reduces bad debt risk Delays commission by 30–60 days; creates cash flow anxiety for reps; complex for multi-year prepay deals

Modeling Comp Plan Scenarios

Every comp plan should be modeled before rollout. Build a scenario model that shows what a rep earns at 50%, 75%, 100%, 125%, and 150% of quota. Then ask three diagnostic questions:

  1. Is at-plan earnings competitive? Benchmark OTE at 100% against market data (Radford, CompTrak, Glassdoor, LinkedIn salary benchmarks). If your OTE is 15% below market, you cannot hire well.
  2. Is the upside meaningful? At 150% attainment, is the rep earning 150%+ of OTE? If accelerators are weak, there is no economic reason to push beyond 100%.
  3. What does the company pay at each scenario? Model total commission cost as a % of revenue at each attainment level. Make sure the plan is sustainable. At 150% attainment and 2× accelerators, total sales cost should still be within the unit economics model.
