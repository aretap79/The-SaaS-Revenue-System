Marketing's Role in the Revenue System
The shift from brand marketing to revenue marketing is the defining organizational transformation in modern B2B SaaS. It changes what marketing measures, what it reports to the board, how it allocates budget, and how it relates to the sales team.
The Pipeline-First Framing

Roberge's central argument is uncompromising: marketing exists to create pipeline. Not brand awareness, not social followers, not press coverage — pipeline. Specifically, qualified pipeline that is traceable to a marketing investment, which is traceable to closed revenue.

This framing has structural implications. It means:

  • Every marketing channel must have a CAC attached to it. If you can't measure the cost per qualified pipeline dollar a channel generates, you should question whether you should be investing in it.
  • Brand and awareness spending is not excluded — but it must eventually be justified by pipeline contribution. "We ran this campaign for awareness" is not an acceptable standalone answer. Awareness spending that doesn't eventually show up in pipeline or win rate improvement needs to be defended on specific evidence.
  • Marketing metrics that don't connect to pipeline are lagging vanity metrics. Social reach, press pickups, and content page views are interesting inputs to understand. They are not the measure of marketing success.
Revenue Marketing Definition

Revenue marketing is a function aligned to measurable business outcomes (pipeline created, pipeline influenced, marketing-sourced revenue %) rather than activity-based metrics (campaigns launched, leads generated, emails sent). Revenue marketers are evaluated alongside the sales team on a shared number.

Why the CMO and CRO Must Co-Own the Revenue Number

In a high-performing revenue organization, the CRO and CMO jointly own the top-of-funnel number. This is not a conceptual alignment — it is a structural accountability. Both sign up to the same pipeline coverage target. Both are measured on MQL volume, MQL quality, and pipeline-to-close conversion.

When this co-ownership is absent, the classic dysfunction emerges: Marketing measures success in MQLs; Sales measures success in closed revenue. Marketing says "we generated 500 MQLs last quarter." Sales says "they were terrible leads." The conflict escalates. Nothing improves.

The structural fix: a shared Service Level Agreement (SLA) between marketing and sales, co-signed by the CMO and CRO, that defines MQL quality, volume targets, follow-up SLAs, and feedback mechanisms. This SLA is reviewed in monthly alignment meetings.

Budget Allocation Framework

There is no universal budget allocation framework — the right mix depends on your segment, ICP, stage of company, and what's been proven to work. The guiding principles are:

  1. Allocate to proven channels first If content/SEO generates 40% of pipeline at 3× better CAC than paid, the majority of budget should flow there before experimenting with new channels.
  2. Reserve 10–20% for channel experimentation New channels are discovered through deliberate testing. Reserve a fixed portion of budget for 90-day experiments. Kill or scale based on results.
  3. Balance inbound and outbound investment A pure inbound model is vulnerable to algorithm changes and brand awareness gaps. A pure outbound model is expensive and doesn't scale as efficiently. Most high-performing B2B SaaS companies run 50–70% inbound / 30–50% outbound at $10M+ ARR.
  4. Model pipeline contribution, not spend Budget allocation decisions should be based on expected pipeline generated per dollar, not on "how much can we spend on this channel." Build a channel ROI model quarterly.

SEO Strategy for SaaS

SaaS SEO is a keyword intent game. The goal is to rank for queries that indicate the buyer is researching your category, evaluating solutions, or seeking to understand their problem.

  • Commercial intent keywords — "[your category] software," "best [category] tools," "[your product] pricing" — these are the highest-value keywords for pipeline. High competition, high conversion.
  • Competitor alternatives — "[Competitor] alternative" and "[Competitor] vs [your product]" pages are among the highest-converting SEO pages in SaaS. They capture buyers who are already mid-evaluation.
  • Pain-point keywords — "how to [solve the problem your product solves]" — awareness stage; lower conversion, higher volume
  • Integration + use case keywords — "[your product] + [popular CRM]," "[your category] for [industry]" — mid-funnel, high specificity, lower volume but higher conversion
  • G2 and Capterra optimization — review platforms now rank on the first page for most software category searches. Your G2 profile is SEO real estate you don't own but must optimize.

Account-Based Marketing (ABM) — Full Playbook
ABM is the coordinated strategy of aligning marketing and sales activity around a defined set of high-value target accounts. When executed correctly, ABM produces higher win rates, faster sales cycles, and larger deals than broad-based inbound programs at the enterprise level.
Building Target Account Lists

The target account list (TAL) is the foundation of every ABM program. It must be built collaboratively between marketing and sales, scored rigorously, and updated quarterly.

  1. Define ICP at the account level Industry, employee count, revenue range, geography, technology stack (CRM, HCM, ERP they use), business model signals (PLG vs. sales-led, B2B vs. B2C), regulatory environment.
  2. Export ICP-matching accounts from data providers ZoomInfo, Apollo, Clearbit, LinkedIn Sales Navigator. Filter by firmographic criteria. Deduplicate against existing customers and open opportunities.
  3. Layer in intent signals Bombora tracks content consumption patterns — companies reading articles about your category are showing intent. G2 Buyer Intent shows which companies are visiting your G2 profile or comparing your competitors. 6sense and Demandbase provide account-level scoring that predicts purchase likelihood. High-ICP + high-intent = Tier 1.
  4. Add trigger events New funding (Crunchbase, PitchBook), executive hire (LinkedIn Sales Navigator alerts), company expansion, regulatory change in their industry, competitor relationship ending (can be sourced from sales intelligence tools).
  5. Tier the list Tier 1: 10–25 highest-potential accounts, fully coordinated 1:1 plays. Tier 2: 50–150 accounts, semi-personalized 1:few plays. Tier 3: 500–2,000 accounts, programmatic 1:many plays.
  6. Get sales buy-in Every Tier 1 account must be approved by the owning AE. Without AE investment, ABM programs are marketing theater. The AE must understand that they own the relationship strategy for Tier 1 accounts; marketing is providing air cover.

Intent Data Tools
  • Bombora — B2B intent data co-op. Tracks content consumption across 5,000+ B2B publisher sites. Identifies companies actively researching topics related to your category. Best for broad category intent.
  • G2 Buyer Intent — Shows which companies are visiting your G2 profile, viewing competitor profiles, or searching your category on G2. High-quality intent signal because it indicates active vendor research.
  • 6sense — Full ABM platform combining intent, predictive scoring, advertising, and orchestration. "Dark funnel" visibility: identifies anonymous buying behavior before a lead ever fills out a form.

  • LinkedIn Sales Navigator — Account signals through job change alerts, company growth signals, and intent indicators within the LinkedIn ecosystem.

Ensuring Sales Actually Uses the Content

The most common failure in sales enablement is the "content graveyard" — a shared drive or Seismic/Highspot/Guru instance full of assets that nobody uses. Marketing creates; sales ignores. The gap is usually one of these:

  • Discovery gap — reps can't find the asset at the moment they need it. Solution: organize content by deal stage and buyer persona, not by asset type.
  • Relevance gap — the asset exists but doesn't match the exact situation the rep is in. Solution: segment assets by industry, company size, and persona combination. More assets that are precisely relevant > fewer assets that are generally applicable.
  • Quality gap — reps have tried the asset and it didn't help. Solution: gather direct feedback from reps on what works and what doesn't. Review call recordings to see how reps use (or avoid) assets in live situations.
  • Training gap — reps don't know the asset exists or how to use it. Solution: launch new assets in team meetings, not just Slack. Run a live role play using the asset. Explain the situation where it's designed to be used.

Marketing Attribution
Attribution is the process of assigning credit to the marketing touchpoints that influenced a deal. Getting attribution right is foundational to making rational budget decisions. Getting it wrong leads to defunding the channels that actually drive revenue and over-investing in channels that look good on paper.
Attribution Models Compared
Model How It Works Best For Blind Spot
First-Touch 100% of credit goes to the first touchpoint that created the lead Measuring which channels drive awareness and bring new names into the funnel Ignores all subsequent touches; a deal that took 20 touches over 9 months looks like it came from one blog post
Last-Touch 100% of credit goes to the final touchpoint before conversion Understanding which content closes — what converts a warmed-up prospect to a demo request Ignores all prior awareness building; makes bottom-of-funnel assets look like the only drivers of pipeline
Linear Equal credit to every touchpoint in the journey Understanding the full breadth of touchpoints without bias toward first or last Treats a homepage visit the same as a demo request — not all touches are equal
Time-Decay More credit to touchpoints closer to conversion; earlier touches receive less credit Short sales cycles where recency is legitimately more important Undervalues early awareness channels that planted the seed months before the decision
U-Shaped (Position-Based) 40% to first touch, 40% to lead creation touch, 20% distributed across middle touches Balanced view that acknowledges the importance of both awareness (first touch) and conversion (lead creation) Arbitrary weighting; doesn't account for touches that actually influenced the deal most
W-Shaped 30% to first touch, 30% to lead creation, 30% to opportunity creation, 10% across others SaaS companies with clear funnel stages and long buying cycles where opportunity creation is a significant milestone More complex to implement; still arbitrary credit splits
Full-Path / Custom Weights assigned based on statistical analysis of which touchpoints actually correlate with conversion Companies with sufficient data volume to run statistical attribution modeling; most accurate for budget decisions Requires data science investment; not feasible below ~$5M ARR or with poor CRM data hygiene
Practical Attribution Recommendation

Use W-shaped attribution for deal-level reporting. Run first-touch attribution for top-of-funnel channel evaluation (which channels bring new names). Run last-touch for conversion optimization (which content converts). Use all three views in your monthly marketing review to get a complete picture. Never make a budget decision based on one model alone.

Why Attribution Fights Between Marketing and Sales Happen

The marketing-sales attribution conflict is structural. Marketing claims credit for pipeline that sales believes they would have created through outbound anyway. Sales claims credit for deals that marketing's content actually influenced. Both are often partially right.

The conflict is prevented (not resolved after the fact) by:

  • Pre-agreeing on definitions — what is "marketing-sourced" (MQL that converted to opportunity), what is "marketing-influenced" (any deal where a marketing touchpoint occurred, regardless of source), and what is "sales-sourced" (rep-generated through outbound with no prior marketing touch)
  • Using influence metrics alongside source metrics — marketing-influenced pipeline is a more generous and often more accurate view of marketing's contribution than pure first-touch sourcing. It should be reported alongside sourcing metrics.
  • Shared dashboards with agreed data sources — if both teams are looking at the same numbers from the same CRM, attribution debates become data discussions instead of territorial arguments
Attribution Infrastructure
  • UTM parameters — every external URL should carry UTM source, medium, campaign, content, and term parameters. This populates the CRM lead source field accurately.
  • CRM campaign tracking — every campaign (event, email send, ad campaign, webinar) should be a CRM Campaign object. Associates leads and contacts with campaigns. The basis for multi-touch attribution reporting.
  • BI tool integration — Salesforce, HubSpot, or your CRM's native reporting rarely has the flexibility for multi-touch attribution modeling. Most mature teams use a BI layer (Looker, Tableau, Metabase) connected to a data warehouse (Snowflake, BigQuery) for true attribution reporting.
  • Cookieless attribution planning — third-party cookies are deprecated in Chrome. Attribution infrastructure must be rebuilt on first-party data, server-side event tracking, and hashed email matching.
The MQL Process
The MQL (Marketing Qualified Lead) is the primary handoff point between marketing and sales. Every weakness in this process — unclear criteria, slow follow-up, poor feedback loops — directly reduces revenue. It is one of the highest-leverage process improvement areas in any revenue organization.
Defining a Marketing Qualified Lead

An MQL must meet two criteria simultaneously:

  • Demographic / Firmographic Fit — the person matches your ICP on at least 70% of criteria: job title (e.g., VP of Sales, Director of Marketing), company size (e.g., 200–2,000 employees), industry (e.g., SaaS, Financial Services), geography (e.g., North America), and technology profile (e.g., uses Salesforce CRM)
  • Behavioral Signal — the person has taken an action indicating they have the problem your product solves: demo request (highest intent), pricing page visit + form fill, content download on a solution-level topic, free trial signup, high lead score based on cumulative behavioral signals

A person who matches ICP but has only viewed a blog post once is not an MQL — they are a lead in nurture. A person who requested a demo but works at a 5-person company below your minimum ICP threshold is not an MQL — they are an unqualified request. Both conditions must be met.

Lead Scoring Models

Lead scoring assigns numerical points to demographic attributes and behavioral actions to produce a composite score. When a lead crosses a threshold, it becomes an MQL.

Category Attribute / Action Example Point Value
Demographic (Positive) Job title match (VP, Director, C-Suite in target function) +15
Company size in ICP range +10
Industry match +10
Company revenue in ICP range +10
Technology fit (uses Salesforce, HubSpot, etc.) +5
Demographic (Negative) Student or academic email −20
Company size well below ICP minimum −15
Behavioral Demo request +50 (auto-MQL regardless of demographic score)
Pricing page visit +25
High-intent content download (ROI calculator, comparison guide) +15
Webinar attendance (live) +10
Email click (3+ in last 30 days) +5
No activity in 90 days −10 (score decay)

MQL threshold: typically 40–60 points. Calibrate by looking at which score ranges historically convert to opportunities vs. which don't. Raise or lower the threshold until your MQL-to-SQL conversion rate is in the 15–25% range.

The MQL SLA: End-to-End

The MQL SLA is the formal agreement that defines what happens between the moment an MQL is created and the moment it is dispositioned by sales. Every step must be defined, timed, and enforced.

  1. MQL Created (T=0) Lead crosses scoring threshold or submits a high-intent form. CRM automatically assigns to rep or SDR per routing rules. Alert sent via email and Slack.
  2. First Contact Attempt (T < 5 min for demo request, T < 60 min for standard MQL) Rep makes first outreach attempt: phone call first (highest response rate), followed by email with context referencing the specific action they took. Not a generic "I see you filled out a form."
  3. Disposition (T < 48 hours) Rep marks the MQL as: (a) Accepted → converted to opportunity (SAL), (b) Rejected with reason code (see below), or (c) In progress (attempts ongoing, escalate if no contact after 5 attempts over 5 business days).
  4. Rejection Reason Codes (Required) Every rejected MQL must carry a reason code: Bad fit — below ICP (company too small, wrong industry), Bad timing (not in market now), Bad data (email bounced, wrong contact), Duplicate (already in pipeline), or Disqualified (talked to prospect, confirmed not a fit). These codes are the feedback loop that enables marketing to improve MQL quality.
  5. Feedback Loop Review (Monthly) Marketing and sales leadership review the disposition data: what % were rejected and why? What MQL sources had the best SQL conversion rates? What should be changed about the MQL definition or scoring model?

Key Marketing Formulas
Cost Per Lead (CPL)
CPL = Total Marketing Spend on Channel / Total Leads Generated from Channel
Track CPL by channel and by lead type (MQL vs. raw lead). A channel with high CPL but high MQL-to-SQL conversion may have better economics than a low-CPL channel with poor lead quality.
Marketing-Sourced CAC
Marketing CAC = Total Marketing Spend / New Customers Acquired via Marketing-Sourced Pipeline
Include all marketing costs: headcount, tools (marketing automation, ABM platforms, event costs), ad spend, agency fees, content production.
Compare against blended CAC (all new customers) to understand marketing's efficiency vs. other acquisition channels.
Pipeline Coverage Contribution from Marketing
Marketing Pipeline Contribution = Marketing-Sourced Pipeline / Total Pipeline Required for Quota Coverage
Target: 30–50% of total pipeline from marketing-sourced MQLs at a healthy SaaS company. The exact target depends on how much of the business is outbound-driven.
Example: Team quota $5M, pipeline coverage target 3×, total pipeline needed $15M. If marketing sources 40%, marketing must generate $6M in pipeline.
MQL-to-SQL Conversion Rate
MQL → SQL = SQLs Created from MQLs / Total MQLs Submitted × 100%
Target: 15–25%. Track by source — inbound demo request MQLs should convert at 40–60%; content download MQLs at 10–15%.
CAC Payback Period
CAC Payback = CAC / (MRR per Customer × Gross Margin %)
Target: under 18 months for venture-backed SaaS. Under 12 months for efficient growth companies. Above 24 months requires large ACV and low churn to be sustainable.

Marketing Across the Revenue System
Marketing does not operate independently of the revenue system — it is woven through every phase. Understanding these connections prevents the silo effect that makes revenue organizations dysfunctional.
Phase 1 — Sales System Design
Marketing must understand the ICP deeply to create demand among the right accounts. The sales motion determines which channels (inbound vs. outbound) and which content types (self-serve vs. sales-assisted) marketing should prioritize.
→
Phase 2 — Team
Marketing's pipeline contribution directly determines how much pressure is on each rep's outbound activity. A strong inbound engine allows reps to spend more time on deep discovery and deal progression. A weak inbound engine forces reps into volume prospecting, reducing deal quality.
→
Phase 3 — GTM
Marketing IS the demand generation engine in Phase 3. The funnel architecture, MQL definition, lead routing, and channel strategy all require marketing to be a co-designer, not just an executer of campaigns.
→
Phase 4 — Sales Execution
Enablement content (battle cards, case studies, ROI tools) is consumed in Phase 4 deal cycles. Attribution data from Phase 4 (which marketing touchpoints were present in won deals) informs Phase 3 investment decisions.
→
Customer Success
Expansion and upsell marketing (email programs, community, events) supports CS in growing accounts. Customer advocacy programs (case studies, reference requests, G2 reviews) are a joint marketing-CS motion.
→
Revenue Operations
RevOps owns the attribution infrastructure, CRM campaign tracking, lead scoring models, and reporting systems that marketing depends on. Marketing strategy is only as good as the data systems RevOps maintains.
→
