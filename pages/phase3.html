Stage 2: GTM Fit →
Market Segmentation
Segmentation is the first GTM decision. Everything else — staffing, motion, pricing, demand gen, channel mix — flows from the segment you choose to attack. Getting segmentation wrong is the root cause of more GTM failures than any other single factor.
SMB, Mid-Market, and Enterprise: Standard Definitions

While definitions vary by industry and company, the SaaS industry has converged on rough boundaries that determine the sales motion required:

Segment Employee Count Annual Revenue ACV Range Typical Sales Cycle Decision Makers
SMB 1–200 <$10M $1K–$15K 7–30 days 1–2 (often the owner)
Mid-Market 200–2,000 $10M–$500M $15K–$150K 30–90 days 3–7 (VP + Manager + Procurement)
Enterprise 2,000+ >$500M $150K–$1M+ 3–18 months 7–20+ (IT, Legal, Security, C-Suite, Procurement)

These numbers are approximate. The more important distinction is deal complexity: how many stakeholders, how much procurement process, and how much technical evaluation is required. A 50-person fintech startup can have an enterprise-complexity procurement process. A 5,000-employee retailer buying a point solution might close in 30 days.

Why Segmentation Drives Motion, Staffing, and Economics

Your segment determines virtually every downstream resource decision:

Dimension SMB Mid-Market Enterprise
Sales Motion High-velocity, transactional — often self-serve or inside sales with light touch Consultative inside or hybrid — discovery, demo, proposal Strategic account selling — executive relationships, custom proposals, procurement navigation
Rep Profile High-energy, resilient, coachable at volume Multi-threaded, process-disciplined, intelligent Strategic, patient, executive presence, politically savvy
Demand Gen Content, SEO, SEM — scalable inbound; outbound for expansion Inbound + outbound; ABM for priority accounts Named account ABM, events, exec relationships, referrals
CAC Target Low — must recover in <12 months; often <$5K CAC Moderate — 12–18 month CAC payback acceptable High — 18–36 month payback is common; justified by LTV
CS Motion Digital/pooled — low-touch, automated onboarding Named CSM per portfolio of accounts Dedicated CSM + executive sponsor program
The Deadly GTM Sin

Building an enterprise-style motion (long discovery, custom proposals, executive selling) to sell to SMB customers is fatal. The economics don't work — you're spending $8K to acquire a $6K ACV customer. The reverse is equally damaging: a product-led or high-velocity inside sales motion for enterprise customers loses deals to competitors willing to do the strategic selling work. Segment first, then design the motion.

Horizontal vs. Vertical GTM

Beyond size-based segmentation, you must choose whether to go horizontal (selling the same product across many industries) or vertical (focusing deeply on one or two specific verticals).

Approach Definition Advantages When to Choose
Horizontal GTM Product solves a common problem across many industries (e.g., HubSpot CRM, Slack, DocuSign) Larger TAM, more diversified risk, faster growth ceiling When the pain is universal and the product is genuinely applicable across industries without customization
Vertical GTM Product is purpose-built for a specific industry (e.g., Veeva for pharma, Procore for construction, Toast for restaurants) Higher win rates, faster sales cycles, better NPS, stronger referral networks, easier pricing (industry-specific ROI) When deep workflow integration and industry-specific compliance, data structures, or buyer vocabulary matter; early-stage companies often start vertical to win before expanding horizontal
Which Segment to Attack First

The sequencing decision — which market to attack first — is one of the most consequential early-stage GTM choices. The wrong answer is "all of them simultaneously."

  1. Identify where you have the strongest product-market fit signal Where are your happiest customers? What segment do your top 10 customers represent? That is your beachhead segment — regardless of where you think the biggest opportunity is.
  2. Assess unit economics by segment Which segment has the best LTV:CAC ratio given your current product and sales capability? Often this is the segment that least resembles your aspiration but most closely matches your current reality.
  3. Evaluate speed-to-revenue In early-stage companies, short cycles (SMB, product-led) generate faster feedback loops and faster cash. Enterprise cycles can be a 12-month experiment with one reference customer. The cost of being wrong is too high.
  4. Dominate before expanding The goal is to become the recognized leader in your first segment before expanding. "Best in the world for mid-market HR software in healthcare" is achievable. "Great for all companies" is not a defensible position at Series A.
TAM / SAM / SOM
The three market-sizing frameworks are a standard part of investor presentations and strategic planning. Understanding how to calculate and use them is foundational to territory planning, board communication, and investment prioritization.
Definitions and Calculation Methods
Metric Definition How to Calculate What Investors Look For
TAM — Total Addressable Market The total revenue opportunity available if your product achieved 100% market share — every company that could theoretically buy it Top-down: use industry analyst reports (Gartner, IDC, Forrester). Bottom-up: (# of target companies worldwide) × ACV TAM should be >$1B for venture-scale outcomes. Investors are skeptical of TAMs below $500M at Series A.
SAM — Serviceable Addressable Market The portion of TAM you can actually reach with your current product, language, geographic presence, and sales motion Filter TAM by: geographies where you sell, languages supported, product-fit segments, regulatory constraints. Example: TAM is all HR software globally; SAM is English-speaking mid-market companies in North America. The SAM is what you're actually competing in. It should be credibly grounded — not just "well, we could expand everywhere."
SOM — Serviceable Obtainable Market The portion of SAM you can realistically capture in your planning horizon (typically 3–5 years) given current resources, competition, and GTM capacity SOM = SAM × realistic market share %. Ground this in: competitive win rates, sales capacity (headcount × quota), and growth trajectory. A company at $10M ARR targeting 3% of a $500M SAM over 3 years = ~$15M ARR target. Investors evaluate whether your financial projections are consistent with your SOM claim. If your 5-year plan requires 40% SAM capture, it will be challenged.
Bottom-Up TAM Formula
TAM = (# of target companies in addressable universe) × (Average Contract Value)
Example: 50,000 mid-market HR departments in North America × $40,000 ACV = $2B TAM
Note: this assumes 100% penetration — a theoretical maximum, not a projection. SAM and SOM are the actionable numbers.
Using TAM/SAM/SOM in Territory Planning

Market sizing frameworks are not just for pitch decks — they are the foundation of territory design. Divide your SAM across territories to ensure each rep has an addressable universe that can support their quota target.

  • If a rep's territory contains 800 accounts and their annual quota is $1M, and average ACV is $40K, they need to close 25 deals from 800 accounts — a 3.1% close rate requirement. Is that achievable given typical conversion rates in your motion?
  • If the math doesn't work, the territory is too small and you need to either expand it, adjust quota, or increase pipeline coverage ratios.
  • Territory whitespace analysis: which accounts in each rep's territory have never been touched? That is the floor of outbound opportunity.
Territory Design
Territory design is the process of dividing the addressable market into defined ownership areas for sales reps. Poor territory design creates inequity, drives attrition of top performers, and produces attainment variance that looks like a talent problem but is actually a structural one.
Territory Model Types
Rep owns all accounts in a specific industry vertical
Model How It Works Best For Weaknesses
Geographic Rep owns all accounts within a defined geography (state, region, country) Field sales with in-person requirements; local market relationships matter; broad horizontal product Geographic account density is uneven — New York vs. rural Midwest has wildly different account density; ignores vertical clustering
Named Account Rep owns a specific list of named accounts, regardless of geography Enterprise sales; accounts require deep multi-year relationships; account potential is concentrated Account assignment is political; some lists have more potential than others; requires rigorous account scoring
Vertical / Industry When vertical expertise matters for credibility; Veeva (pharma), Procore (construction), Toast (restaurants) Vertical account density varies by geography; requires reps with vertical knowledge
Hybrid Combination — geographic regions + named enterprise accounts pulled out; or vertical pods within regions Companies with both SMB/MM velocity and enterprise strategic motion running simultaneously Complex to administer; requires clear rules for account ownership disputes
Balancing Territories for Equity

Territory equity is the degree to which every rep has an equal opportunity to hit quota. Perfect equity is unachievable — but variance should be managed to prevent structural attrition of top performers assigned to thin territories.

  1. Score every account in your CRM Build an account potential score using: firmographic fit (ICP match), employee count, revenue signal, industry, technology stack (e.g., using Clearbit, ZoomInfo, BuiltWith), intent data. Each account has a weighted potential value.
  2. Sum potential by territory Total account potential per territory gives a relative territory value index. If rep A's territory has 150 potential units and rep B's has 80, that's an 88% gap — the quota assignments should reflect this, or the territory should be rebalanced.
  3. Rebalance annually at plan design Territory rebalancing should happen at the start of each fiscal year — not mid-year. Mid-year changes disrupt active deal cycles and destroy trust.
  4. Protect relationship equity When rebalancing, consider rep-account relationships. Pulling a large account out of a rep's territory the week before it closes is a trust-destroying move that will cost you the rep. Build clear rules for how in-flight deals are handled during territory changes.
White Space Analysis

White space is the portion of a rep's territory that has never been contacted, touched, or pursued. In most SaaS companies, 60–80% of the SAM is white space — untouched potential. White space analysis identifies where outbound effort should be concentrated.

  • Filter accounts by: zero opportunity history, zero email/call history in CRM, ICP score above threshold
  • Prioritize white space by: intent signals (G2, Bombora, 6sense), technographic signals (using competitor products), trigger events (new funding, new hire, company expansion)
  • White space is not a pipeline problem — it is an activity direction problem. Reps who work existing pipeline die by single-threading. Reps who work white space build sustainable pipeline health.
Demand Generation
Roberge's core principle: every marketing investment must be traceable to pipeline. Demand generation is not brand awareness — it is the systematic creation of qualified sales opportunities. Everything else is a vanity metric.
Inbound vs. Outbound Demand Generation
Dimension Inbound Outbound
Definition Buyers find you — through content, SEO, word of mouth, community, PLG You find buyers — through cold outreach, prospecting, direct mail, events, SDR calls
Lead Quality Higher intent — they came to you because they have the problem Variable intent — you are interrupting their day; must create urgency and relevance
Economics Lower CPL at scale; high fixed cost (content, SEO investment upfront), low marginal cost per lead Higher CPL but predictable; directly scalable with headcount; delivers ICP precision
Time to Results 6–18 months to build; not a fast solution Immediate — first campaigns can generate leads in week 1
Best Used When Strong product-market fit, clear ICP, growing brand awareness; Series B+ when inbound machine is built Early-stage testing of ICP hypotheses; enterprise segment where buyers don't search; segment expansion into new verticals
Building a Demand Gen Engine from Scratch
  1. Define your MQL (Marketing Qualified Lead) An MQL is not someone who opened an email. An MQL is a person who matches your ICP profile and has taken an action indicating they have the problem your product solves. The MQL definition must be co-owned by marketing and sales. Marketing generates MQLs; sales must agree they are worth pursuing. Misalignment here is the root cause of the "marketing generates garbage leads" conflict.
  2. Identify the 2–3 channels most likely to reach your ICP B2B SaaS options: content/SEO (buyers researching solutions), LinkedIn ads (targeting by job title/company size), events (where your buyers congregate), outbound SDRs (direct prospecting), partner referrals. Start with the channels where your buyers spend time, not the channels you're comfortable with.
  3. Build tracking infrastructure before spending UTM parameters on every URL, CRM campaign tracking for every marketing source, attribution model agreed upon before the first dollar is spent. Without this, you cannot measure which channels produce pipeline, and you'll continue spending on the wrong things.
  4. Run experiments with defined success metrics Each channel test should have: a minimum spend ($), a time horizon (60–90 days), and a success threshold (e.g., CPL < $X, MQL-to-SQL conversion > Y%). Kill channels that don't meet thresholds. Scale channels that exceed them.
  5. Build feedback loops between sales and marketing Sales should report back on every MQL: was it a fit? What was the quality? This feedback refines lead scoring models and MQL definitions. Monthly sales-marketing alignment meetings are the operational mechanism.
Account-Based Marketing (ABM)
ABM flips the traditional marketing funnel. Instead of casting wide and filtering down, ABM starts with a precisely defined list of target accounts and coordinates marketing and sales activity specifically toward those accounts.
The Three ABM Tiers
Tier Account Count Approach Resources Required Example Activity
Tier 1 — 1:1 5–20 accounts Fully customized, bespoke campaigns for each account. Custom content, custom events, executive relationship building. High — dedicated marketing resources per account; AE + SDR + Marketing + exec sponsor per account Custom micro-site for the target company, executive dinner in their city, bespoke ROI analysis using their own data, personalized video from CRO to CHRO
Tier 2 — 1:Few 50–200 accounts Light personalization — segment-specific content adapted for each account cluster. Industry-specific case studies, persona-specific emails. Medium — cluster accounts by vertical/persona, create templates that allow quick personalization Industry-specific webinar + follow-up sequence; LinkedIn ad campaign targeting named contacts at target accounts; SDR sequences with personalized first lines
Tier 3 — 1:Many 500–2,000 accounts Programmatic — use targeting technology to show relevant content to contacts at target accounts. Minimal manual personalization. Low per account — technology-driven (6sense, Demandbase, LinkedIn ABM lists) Retargeting ads to anyone from a target company who visited your site; LinkedIn matched audiences campaigns; behavioral email sequences triggered by intent signals
ABM Account List Construction

The quality of your ABM program is entirely determined by the quality of your target account list. A poorly constructed list wastes resources on accounts that will never buy.

  • Start with ICP criteria — firmographics (industry, size, revenue, geography), technographics (current tools), business model signals
  • Layer in intent data — use Bombora, G2 Buyer Intent, or 6sense to identify companies actively researching your category. An ICP-fit company showing intent is a Tier 1 or 2 candidate immediately.
  • Score by trigger events — new funding round (budget available), executive hire (new decision maker), company expansion, regulatory change affecting their industry
  • Validate with sales — the sales team knows which accounts are actively considering your category. They should co-build the Tier 1 list.
Measuring ABM
Account Engagement Score (contacts touched, content consumed, meetings booked) Multi-Thread Engagement (% of accounts with 3+ contacts engaged) Intent Signal Volume (accounts showing category intent) Pipeline Influenced from Target Accounts Win Rate — Target ABM Accounts vs. Non-ABM Account Penetration Rate (% of Tier 1 list with open opportunities)
Funnel Architecture
A funnel is only as reliable as its stage definitions. The most common source of forecasting inaccuracy in SaaS is not dishonest reps or bad data — it is undefined or inconsistently applied stage criteria. Stage inflation (moving deals forward without meeting entry criteria) destroys forecast predictability.
Every Stage: Entry Criteria and Exit Criteria
Stage Definition Entry Criteria Exit Criteria
MQL A lead that meets ICP criteria and has taken a qualifying action — owned by Marketing ICP demographic fit + qualifying behavioral signal (demo request, content download + form fill + score threshold) Reviewed and accepted by Sales as a Sales-Accepted Lead (SAL) OR rejected with reason code
SAL Sales has accepted the lead and committed to making first contact — transition from Marketing ownership to Sales ownership Sales rep or SDR has reviewed the lead, confirmed ICP fit, and accepted responsibility for outreach within SLA (typically 5–60 min) First contact made; qualifying call completed; either qualified as SQL or disqualified with reason
SQL Sales Qualified Lead — confirmed that the prospect has a genuine need, budget exists, decision-making authority identified, and a timeline is plausible BANT or MEDDIC qualification confirmed: Budget (or funding pathway), Authority (knows the decision maker), Need (confirmed pain/problem), Timeline (realistic within 12 months) Discovery call booked and completed; moves to Stage 1
Stage 1 — Discovery Active discovery of the prospect's problem, current state, desired future state, and decision-making process SQL criteria met; discovery call scheduled Champion identified; core problem validated; budget range confirmed; discovery complete and documented in CRM
Stage 2 — Evaluation Prospect is actively evaluating your product — demo delivered, technical validation, proof of concept Champion has agreed to run a formal evaluation; decision criteria defined; key stakeholders mapped Demo completed; technical questions answered; PoC (if required) complete with success criteria met; economic buyer engaged
Stage 3 — Proposal Formal proposal delivered; pricing discussed; procurement process engaged Economic buyer has been presented to; pricing accepted in principle; legal/security review initiated (if required) Proposal accepted; verbal commitment from economic buyer; legal redlines complete or in progress
Stage 4 — Negotiation Commercial terms being finalized; legal, procurement, or IT review in progress Verbal commitment received; contract sent; negotiation points identified Contract signed; Closed Won OR Closed Lost
Closed Won Contract executed, deal booked to CRM Signed agreement; payment terms confirmed Handed to CS for onboarding; account record updated
Closed Lost Deal will not proceed in this cycle Prospect confirmed no purchase; or opportunity inactive >45 days with no response Loss reason documented (mandatory); future re-engagement date set if applicable
The Stage Inflation Problem

Stage inflation happens when reps move deals forward without meeting exit criteria — typically to protect their pipeline from scrutiny. A deal in "Stage 3 — Proposal" where no economic buyer has been engaged is a Stage 1 deal being miscategorized. Solving this requires CRM field enforcement (required fields at each stage transition) and manager inspection in weekly 1:1s.

Funnel Leakage Audit

A funnel audit identifies where prospects are leaving the funnel at rates higher than expected, indicating a systemic problem at that stage.

Stage Conversion Rate
Stage N Conversion = Opportunities Entering Stage N+1 / Opportunities Entering Stage N
Example benchmarks (vary widely by segment and product):
MQL → SQL: 15–25% (lower is OK if MQL volume is high; higher is better with precise targeting)
SQL → Stage 1: 60–80% (most qualified leads should enter discovery)
Stage 1 → Stage 2: 40–60% (significant drop here = discovery quality problem)
Stage 2 → Stage 3: 50–70% (major drop here = evaluation failure or competitive loss)
Stage 3 → Closed Won: 35–60% (varies massively by segment)

When you see a conversion rate well below benchmark at any stage, investigate the specific failure mode. Ask: what are the three most common reasons deals stall or die at this stage? The answers reveal coaching priorities, product gaps, and messaging failures.

Channel Strategy
A channel is the vehicle through which you reach and close customers. Each channel has different economics, different speed-to-revenue, and different strategic implications. The biggest GTM mistake is investing in channels without understanding their unit economics.
Channel Economics Comparison
Channel How It Works CAC Profile Best For Risks
Direct Sales Your own reps prospect, qualify, and close deals High — full salary + benefits + tools + management overhead Enterprise, complex products, high ACV where the economics justify the cost Not scalable without proportional headcount; high fixed cost
Reseller / Channel Partners Third-party companies sell your product (VARs, MSPs, consultants) Low direct CAC — you pay a revenue share (typically 15–30%). Moderate indirect cost (partner enablement, program management). Geographic reach beyond your sales capacity; verticals where the partner has trusted relationships (e.g., accounting firms selling accounting software) Channel conflict with direct team; partners may not prioritize your product; partner quality control is hard
Technology Alliances Integrations with complementary tools generate co-marketing, co-selling, and referrals Low — primarily an engineering and partnership investment; no per-lead cost Products embedded in a broader stack (CRM → email tools → CS platforms); marketplace exposure Partner prioritization; integration maintenance overhead; partner gets customer data context you don't
Product-Led Growth (PLG) Product is the primary acquisition vehicle — free trial, freemium, or self-serve purchase Very low CAC for the initial acquisition; higher CAC for conversion of free-to-paid if it requires sales assist Products with fast time-to-value, low implementation complexity, individual user use cases that expand (Slack, Notion, Figma, Zoom) Conversion rate management is critical; PLG without a strong activation experience = high churn; difficult to support enterprise deals without a sales overlay
Marketplace Listed on Salesforce AppExchange, AWS Marketplace, G2, etc. Low acquisition cost but marketplace takes 15–30% revenue share; customer acquisition is through the marketplace's traffic Products that complement Salesforce, AWS, or other platform ecosystems; buyers who use trusted marketplaces for procurement Dependent on marketplace algorithm and policies; limited customer relationship ownership; pricing pressure
CAC by Channel
CAC (Channel X) = Total Spend on Channel X / New Customers Acquired via Channel X
Include all costs: ad spend, salaries of people running the channel, tools, events, commissions, partner program costs.
Compare against LTV by channel — a channel with 2× higher CAC but 3× higher LTV is strategically superior.
Track CAC payback period: CAC / (ACV × Gross Margin %). Target: <18 months for SaaS.
When to Invest in Channel vs. Direct
  • Direct first — until you understand your motion, ICP, and deal economics deeply, direct sales gives you the fastest feedback loop. Every channel partner interaction adds a layer of distance between you and the buyer.
  • Add channel when — you have a proven, repeatable direct motion; the geographic or vertical reach of partners adds accounts you can't access directly; ACV economics support the revenue share; and you have the bandwidth to enable and manage partners properly.
  • Never add channel to fix a broken direct motion — if your direct sales conversion rates are poor, channel partners will experience the same issues. Fix the product-market fit and motion first.
Lead Routing
Lead routing is the process of assigning incoming leads to the right sales rep or SDR. It sounds operational and unglamorous — which is why it is consistently under-designed. Poorly routed leads result in delayed follow-up, wrong rep assignments, and permanent lost revenue.
Routing Models
Model How It Works Best For Failure Modes
Round Robin Leads are distributed sequentially across available reps, ensuring equal volume per rep SMB, homogeneous rep teams, inbound volume too high for ownership-based routing Distributes good and bad leads equally; doesn't account for rep availability, skill, or territory fit
Ownership-Based Leads from accounts owned by a specific rep or in their territory are automatically routed to that rep Mid-Market and Enterprise where territory ownership is established; ABM target account models Requires accurate account ownership data in CRM; ownership gaps create leads that fall through the cracks
Scoring-Based Leads are scored on ICP fit and intent, and routed to the most qualified rep (highest performer, enterprise specialist, etc.) based on score Teams with differentiated rep tiers; routing high-value inbound leads to senior reps Scoring model must be maintained; creates inequality in volume that can breed resentment
Skills/Segment-Based Leads are routed based on the segment, vertical, or language match (a French-speaking lead goes to a French-speaking rep) Multi-product or multi-segment companies; international teams; vertical specialists More complex routing logic; requires accurate data on the lead's attributes at the point of submission
Speed-to-Lead: The 5-Minute Rule

A landmark Harvard Business Review study found that responding to a lead within 5 minutes is 9× more likely to result in meaningful contact than responding after 10 minutes. After 1 hour, the likelihood of reaching the lead drops by 60×.

Speed-to-Lead Benchmark

The SLA for inbound MQL follow-up should be:
Business hours: 5 minutes for high-score leads (demo request, pricing page), 60 minutes for standard MQLs
After hours: Acknowledge immediately with automated email + calendar link; call at start of next business day
The single biggest driver of lead conversion improvement is usually not the quality of the pitch — it is simply responding faster.

Enforcing Lead Routing Rules in CRM
  • Use CRM workflow automation (Salesforce, HubSpot) to create automated lead assignment rules — don't rely on manual processes
  • Build alerts for SLA violations: if an MQL is uncontacted after 60 minutes, trigger a manager notification
  • Track lead response time as a managed metric — publish it in the weekly team standup
  • Build an SLA report showing the distribution of response times. Most companies are shocked to discover that 30% of inbound MQLs are not contacted within 24 hours.
  • Create a "fallback" routing rule for leads that don't match any ownership criteria — these should never fall through to "unassigned"
Key Formulas — Phase 3
TAM (Bottom-Up)
TAM = (# of target companies in addressable universe) × ACV
Pipeline Coverage Ratio
Pipeline Coverage = Open Pipeline (all stages) / Remaining Quota
Target: 3× coverage minimum. At 2× you are likely to miss. At 4–5×, you have buffer for deals that slip.
Note: Weight pipeline by stage and probability. A 3× coverage ratio of early-stage deals is not the same as 3× of late-stage deals.
MQL-to-SQL Conversion Rate
MQL → SQL Conversion = SQLs Generated / MQLs Received × 100%
Industry benchmark: 10–20%. Below 10% = MQL definition is too loose or ICP targeting is off. Above 30% = MQL bar may be too high, leaving qualified leads on the table.
Funnel Velocity Formula
Velocity = (# of Opportunities × Win Rate × ACV) / Average Sales Cycle Length (days)
This tells you how many dollars of revenue your funnel produces per day.
Improving any of the four inputs improves velocity.
Example: 100 opps × 25% win rate × $40K ACV / 60 days = $16,667/day in expected revenue
CAC by Channel
CAC = Total Channel Investment / New Customers from That Channel
Include: ad spend, headcount cost (% of time), tools, event costs, partner fees
CAC Payback = CAC / (MRR per Customer × Gross Margin %)

Spray-and-pray demand gen Running 8 marketing channels simultaneously, optimizing for impressions and clicks, no MQL definition, "more leads = better" High CAC, low conversion, sales team overwhelmed with unqualified leads, marketing-sales conflict, no learnable signal from the data Narrow to 2–3 channels. Define MQL. Track pipeline generated per channel, not leads. Kill everything below CAC threshold.
Undefined funnel stages Stage names exist in CRM but there are no written entry/exit criteria. Every rep decides for themselves when a deal "moves forward." Forecast is noise. Review in management training. Audit quarterly.
Ignoring channel economics Continuing to invest in a channel because "it feels like it's working" without tracking CAC, LTV, or payback period by channel Misallocated marketing budget; investing in expensive channels while cheap channels are underinvested; cannot make rational reallocation decisions Build a channel economics dashboard. Track CAC and payback period monthly per channel. Review in monthly marketing/sales alignment.
Attacking all segments simultaneously Building an SMB self-serve motion, a mid-market inside sales motion, and an enterprise field motion all at once with a 15-person team No segment is done well. Resources are spread too thin. Three different ICPs means unclear messaging, unclear comp, unclear hiring. Nothing achieves escape velocity. Pick one segment. Dominate it. Build the proof points and infrastructure. Then expand methodically.
