REVENUE SYSTEM
The Three Critical Handoffs
The most commonly broken processes in a SaaS revenue system. Where handoffs break down, revenue leaks.
Marketing → Sales (MQL) Sales → CS (Close) CS → Sales (Expansion)

Why Handoffs Break

In a SaaS revenue system, Marketing, Sales, and Customer Success are three separate organizations with different leaders, different metrics, different tools, and different vocabularies. Every point where the customer journey crosses from one organization to another is a "seam" — and seams are where revenue leaks.

The Organizational Seam Problem

The handoff problem is structural, not individual. Even with great people on both sides, a handoff fails when two teams have different definitions of success (OKRs), different data systems (CRM vs. MAP vs. CS platform), different vocabulary ("lead" means different things to marketing and sales), and different compensation incentives. The only solution is an explicit, documented, mutually-committed process — not asking people to try harder.

Misaligned OKRs
Marketing is measured on MQL volume. Sales is measured on ACV closed. These incentives create conflict at the handoff — marketing wants quantity, sales wants quality.
Vocabulary Gaps
"MQL," "qualified lead," "demo ready," and "champion" mean different things to different people without a shared definitions document. Ambiguity kills accountability.
System Silos
Marketing runs Marketo. Sales runs Salesforce. CS runs Gainsight. Data doesn't flow between them without explicit integration work — and most companies haven't done it.
No Accountability Loop
When a handoff fails, there's no feedback mechanism. Marketing doesn't know which MQLs converted. Sales doesn't report back quality. CS doesn't tell sales why customers churn.
Comp Misalignment
AEs are comp'd to close, not to hand off well. CSMs are comp'd on retention, not on setting sales up with expansion opportunities. Nobody is comp'd on handoff quality.
Speed Mismatch
Marketing generates leads in bursts (campaigns). Sales follows up linearly (rep capacity). The gap between lead generation and follow-up is where the fastest decay happens.
The #1 Source of Revenue Leakage

SiriusDecisions research consistently shows that 80% of MQLs never receive adequate follow-up. Harvard Business School research shows that responding to a lead within 5 minutes vs. 30 minutes creates a 9x difference in contact rate. The gap between lead generation and first contact is the single highest-ROI improvement most SaaS companies can make — and it costs almost nothing.

The solution to handoff failures is not motivational — it's structural. RevOps owns the design, documentation, and enforcement of all three handoffs. Each handoff requires: a shared definition of what's being passed, a documented process for how it's passed, a two-way SLA with commitments from both teams, a CRM workflow that enforces compliance, and a feedback loop that routes quality data back to the originating team.

The MQL Definition Problem

Marketing and sales almost always disagree on what constitutes a "good lead" — because they have different incentives. Marketing is measured on MQL volume; they want to include more leads. Sales is measured on closed revenue; they want only the hottest leads. Both are rational given their incentives. The solution isn't to debate what counts — it's to define it jointly and contractually through a shared SLA, backed by data on what characteristics actually correlate with higher win rates.

Demographic Scoring (Fit)

  • Company size match to ICP (employees, revenue) — up to 25 pts
  • Industry match to ICP target verticals — up to 20 pts
  • Persona/title match (Champion or EB role) — up to 20 pts
  • Geography in target market — up to 10 pts
  • Tech stack signals (uses complementary tools) — up to 10 pts
  • Growth signals (hiring, funding, expansion) — up to 15 pts

Behavioral Scoring (Intent)

  • Demo request or contact form — 40 pts (auto-MQL)
  • Pricing page visit — 20 pts
  • Case study or ROI calculator viewed — 15 pts
  • Webinar attended — 10 pts
  • Multiple blog visits in a session — 8 pts
  • Trial signup or free plan activation — 30 pts
  • Email sequence click (mid-funnel content) — 5 pts per
  • G2/Capterra review page visit — 15 pts
MQL Threshold Design

Set the MQL threshold at the score that produces a 13–15% SQL conversion rate — the typical industry benchmark. If your threshold produces 20% SQL conversion, you're being too restrictive (leaving good leads for SDRs). If it produces 8% SQL conversion, you're sending too many bad leads and wasting SDR capacity. Calibrate quarterly using actual conversion data.

The MQL Service Level Agreement (SLA)

The SLA is a bilateral commitment: marketing commits to volume and quality, sales commits to follow-up speed and attempt depth. Without this document, the disagreement is permanent. With it, the disagreement becomes measurable — and therefore manageable.

Marketing Commits To

  • Minimum X MQLs per month (target agreed by both teams)
  • Minimum MQL quality threshold (ICP score ≥ 40)
  • Lead data completeness: company, title, email required fields
  • Intent signal present (not just form fill + company fit)
  • Monthly reporting on MQL volume, source, and ICP match rate
  • Quarterly calibration meeting with sales to review SAL rejection reasons

Sales Commits To

  • First contact attempt within 5 minutes of MQL creation (business hours)
  • Minimum 6–8 outreach attempts over 10–14 days before disposition
  • SAL accept or reject within 24 hours with a rejection reason recorded
  • No "cherry picking" — all MQLs in assigned territory worked in order
  • Monthly reporting on SAL accept/reject rates and primary rejection reasons
  • Closed-loop reporting: feed Closed Won and Lost data back to marketing

The SAL: Sales Accepted Lead

The SAL (Sales Accepted Lead) is the most important concept in the MQL handoff — and the most often skipped. When a rep receives an MQL, they must formally accept it (SAL = "I've reviewed this and confirm it meets our definition") or reject it (with a reason code: not ICP, wrong persona, already a customer, bad contact data, etc.). This creates the feedback loop that lets marketing improve.

Without the SAL mechanism, sales can ignore MQLs silently and marketing has no visibility. With SAL, rejection rates and reasons become the primary optimization signal for the marketing team. A 30% rejection rate on a specific campaign tells marketing to rethink targeting. A 5% rejection rate suggests the scoring model is working.

Speed-to-Lead: The Harvard Research

Speed-to-Lead Impact

Harvard Business School research (Oldroyd et al.) found that responding to an inbound lead within 5 minutes vs. 30 minutes creates a 9x difference in contact rate. After one hour, contact rates drop by 7x. The data is unambiguous: the highest-leverage improvement in most MQL programs is not lead quality — it's response speed.

Achieving 5-minute response requires automation, not heroics. The workflow: MQL created in MAP (Marketo/HubSpot) → trigger fires instantly → lead assigned in Salesforce via round-robin or territory rules → Slack notification sent to assigned rep and their manager → automated email + LinkedIn connection sent by rep template → rep has 5 minutes to make first call. Build this in your MAP and CRM before expecting reps to do it manually.

CRM Setup: Routing and Workflow

Lead Routing Logic

  • Named account match → goes to Account Owner immediately
  • ICP company match (by segment) → routed to segment's SDR queue
  • Demo request / high intent → auto-booked via Calendly/Chili Piper
  • Round-robin within segment queue (capacity-weighted)
  • Re-routing rule: unworked MQL after 4 hours → manager alert
  • Recycle rule: uncontacted after 14 days → back to marketing nurture

Required CRM Fields

  • Lead Score (auto-populated from MAP)
  • Lead Source (first touch, last touch)
  • SAL Status (pending, accepted, rejected)
  • SAL Rejection Reason (dropdown)
  • MQL Date and SAL Date (time-to-accept tracking)
  • First Contact Attempt Date/Time
  • Number of Outreach Attempts

Why This Handoff Fails

Sales reps have strong incentives to close deals and weak incentives to hand them off well. The commission is paid at Closed Won; the effort required for a thorough handoff feels like uncompensated work. Meanwhile, CS receives the customer cold — with no context about what pain the customer had, what was promised, who the real stakeholders are, or what success looks like. The customer's first experience with the company after buying is often confusion, delayed onboarding, and broken expectations. This is where churn seeds are planted.

The Promise Problem

The single biggest cause of early churn (within 90 days) is promises made during the sales process that the product cannot keep. A rep trying to close a deal will say "yes" to things that are on the roadmap, partially true, or just untrue. CS inherits these promises with no documentation, and the customer expects them to be delivered. Without a structured handoff document, there is no accountability and no way to flag these promises before they become a crisis.

The Sales-to-CS Handoff Document: Required Fields

This document must be completed by the AE before an opportunity can be marked Closed Won in the CRM. It is not optional, and CRM workflow automation should enforce it. CS should review it before the first onboarding call and use it as the foundation for the mutual success plan.

Champion Name + Contact
Who is the primary internal advocate? Will they still be here in 6 months?
Economic Buyer
Who approved the budget? CS needs to know who to engage for renewal.
Pain Identified
What specific, quantified problem did the customer buy to solve?
Success Criteria
What measurable outcomes did the customer agree to? How will they define ROI at month 6?
Implementation Timeline
What did we commit to for go-live? Customer has this date in mind.
Products Purchased
Exact SKUs, seats, modules — CS needs to know what was sold and licensed.
Customizations Promised
Any "we can do X" statements made in the sales process that may require product/services effort.
Red Flags
Anything the AE noticed during the sale that CS should know: procurement friction, stakeholder conflict, executive skepticism.
Competitive Context
What alternatives did they evaluate? Are they partially using a competitor tool today?
Key Stakeholder Map
All contacts beyond the champion: IT sponsor, finance contact, end-users group.
QBR Expectations
Did the customer request or expect a quarterly review? What format?
Expansion Potential
Are there additional use cases, teams, or geographies that could expand? What's the timeline signal?

The Mutual Success Plan (MSP)

The MSP is a co-created document between the AE and the customer champion during the final stage of the sales process. It contains: the customer's defined success metrics, the implementation timeline with milestones, who owns what on each side, the agreed schedule for check-ins and QBRs, and the criteria for evaluating ROI at 90 days and 12 months. The AE hands this document to CS as the foundation for the onboarding relationship. It removes ambiguity and gives CS a ready-made agenda for the first call.

MSP at Close = Lower Churn

Companies that implement a formal MSP process at close report 20–35% lower early churn rates (within 90 days). The mechanism is straightforward: the customer has made explicit commitments about how they'll define success, which forces them to engage seriously with onboarding rather than treating it as a checkbox.

The Warm Handoff Call

The warm handoff call is a 30-minute call where the AE introduces the assigned CSM to the champion. The AE recaps the key context (pain, success criteria, timeline) and formally passes the relationship. This call does two things: it gives the CSM immediate credibility (they arrive already knowing the customer's context) and it signals to the customer that they're valued enough to receive a structured transition. Cold intros — a calendar invite from CS with no context — damage trust immediately.

Warm Handoff Call Agenda

  • AE: Recap the business pain and why the customer bought (2 min)
  • AE: Introduce CSM and their role (1 min)
  • CSM: Confirm understanding of success criteria (3 min)
  • CSM: Walk through the onboarding plan and timeline (5 min)
  • CSM: Set up the first implementation meeting (2 min)
  • Customer: Open Q&A (5 min)
  • AE: Express confidence in CS, close out relationship (2 min)

Shadow CS Model

  • CS leader joins the final demo and scoping call
  • CS validates that implementation promises are deliverable
  • CS introduces themselves to the champion before close
  • CS co-creates the MSP with the AE in the final sales stage
  • Result: CS arrives at handoff with full context and relationship already established
  • Churn data: Shadow CS companies see 15–25% lower 90-day churn

CRM Implementation Requirements

The handoff document must be enforced by the CRM — not by honor system. Build a required-field validation in Salesforce that prevents an opportunity from moving to Closed Won unless: (1) all required handoff fields are completed, (2) the MSP document is attached or linked, and (3) the assigned CSM is populated. Add a workflow trigger: when Closed Won, automatically create an Onboarding Case in Salesforce/Gainsight assigned to the CSM with the handoff document linked.

Despite this math, most SaaS companies leave expansion revenue on the table because: (1) CSMs aren't comfortable selling, (2) AEs don't want to depend on CS to source deals, (3) there's no clear process for when CS owns vs. when AE owns, and (4) there's no comp structure that incentivizes the handoff. Fixing this requires a clear SLA, a comp model that works for both teams, and a qualification framework that fits expansion deals.

The Expansion SLA: CS vs. AE Ownership

The most common failure mode in expansion is role confusion: CS and AE both expect the other to drive the deal, so nobody does. The SLA must define explicitly when CS owns the conversation and when it escalates to AE. The primary dividing lines are deal size and deal complexity.

CS Owns (Self-Serve Expansion)

  • Deal <$X (typically <$15–25K for Mid-Market)
  • Same product/module — seat or license expansion
  • Existing champion has authority to approve
  • No new procurement or legal review required
  • CSM has commercial conversation authority in comp model
  • Timeline: handle within renewal or at QBR

AE Owns (Assisted Expansion)

  • Deal >$X threshold (deal-size tier)
  • New product module or service requiring a new demo
  • New economic buyer or department involved
  • Procurement or security review required
  • CSM surfaces the opportunity; AE leads the commercial process
  • CSM stays involved as the relationship anchor throughout

The Expansion MEDDICC

Expansion deals deserve the same rigor as new logo deals. A CSM who surfaces an "expansion opportunity" with no economic buyer identified, no defined success criteria for the expansion, and no compelling event is not a pipeline — it's a wish list. Apply abbreviated MEDDICC to all expansion deals before AE takes ownership:

Expansion MEDDICC Checklist

  • Metrics: What measurable outcome does the expansion solve? (Quantified)
  • Economic Buyer: Who needs to approve? Are they engaged?
  • Decision Criteria: What does the customer need to see to expand?
  • Decision Process: What's the approval path and timeline?
  • Champion: Who is pulling this deal internally?
  • Compelling Event: Why now? What's the consequence of waiting?

Expansion Pipeline Qualification Gate

  • Usage trigger or growth event documented
  • Expansion use case identified and pain confirmed
  • Champion has expressed intent to expand
  • Economic buyer identified (even if not yet engaged)
  • Approximate deal size estimated (even if rough)
  • Timeline signal: "by end of Q" or "next fiscal year"

How RevOps Governs All Three Handoffs

RevOps is the architecture team for the revenue system. They design the processes, build the technical infrastructure, set the measurement framework, and run the governance cadence that keeps all three handoffs operating properly.

  1. Design and document the process Each handoff needs a written playbook that defines: who does what, when, with what data, using what tools, and what constitutes a pass or fail. RevOps writes these playbooks with input from all three functions. They are living documents, reviewed quarterly.
  2. Build the technical infrastructure MAP-to-CRM sync, CRM-to-CS platform sync, lead routing rules, stage validation requirements, automated alerts, and closed-loop reporting must all be built and maintained by RevOps. Handoff quality is only as good as the systems that enforce it.
  3. Own the metrics dashboard RevOps publishes a weekly handoff health dashboard that shows all three handoff KPIs in one view — visible to the CRO, VP Marketing, and VP CS simultaneously. Visibility creates accountability.
  4. Run the monthly handoff review Monthly cross-functional meeting: Marketing + Sales + CS + RevOps review handoff metrics, identify failures, and own action items. This is where SAL rejection reasons get analyzed, churn post-mortems get reviewed, and expansion SLA compliance gets discussed.
  5. Quarterly calibration Every quarter, recalibrate SLAs, scoring thresholds, and ownership boundaries based on what the data shows. Handoff processes that made sense at $5M ARR don't work at $50M ARR. RevOps drives these calibration sessions and owns the change management process.
