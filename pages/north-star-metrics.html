REVENUE SYSTEM
North Star Metrics
The six metrics that tell a CRO whether the revenue system is healthy, efficient, and scalable.
Pipeline Coverage Win Rate Ramp Time CAC Payback Net Revenue Retention Quota Attainment
Why Six, Not Sixty

A CRO can track 200 metrics. They should care deeply about six. These six are "north stars" because they are leading or lagging indicators of the entire revenue system's health — and because they interact with each other in predictable ways. If you move one, another moves. Understanding those interactions is what separates a great CRO from a good analyst. Learn these six metrics cold. Know what drives each one, what benchmark your business should hit, and what you'll do when each falls below par.

Metric 01

Pipeline Coverage Ratio

The most fundamental ratio in revenue forecasting — do you have enough pipeline to hit quota?

Formula
Pipeline Coverage = Total Qualified Pipeline ÷ Revenue Quota
"Qualified" = opportunities that have passed stage 1 (discovery completed, ICP confirmed, next step defined). Raw leads and early-stage noise must be excluded.
Weighted Pipeline Coverage = Σ (Deal Value × Stage Win Rate) ÷ Quota — more accurate but requires reliable stage-level win rate data.
Worked Example
Quota for Q3: $5,000,000
Total qualified pipeline (stages 2–5): $16,500,000
Pipeline Coverage = $16,500,000 ÷ $5,000,000 = 3.3x

Weighted pipeline (applying 80%/50%/25%/15% win rates by stage): $3,800,000
Weighted Coverage = $3,800,000 ÷ $5,000,000 = 0.76x

Interpretation: The unweighted 3.3x is at the minimum acceptable level. The weighted 0.76x means the deal mix is skewed toward early-stage pipeline — there's a risk of missing quota unless late-stage pipeline is added in the next 6 weeks.

Benchmarks by Stage and Segment

Segment Minimum Acceptable Healthy Strong Why the Difference
SMB 2.5x 3.0–3.5x 4x+ Shorter cycles, faster conversions, more predictable stage-to-stage rates
Mid-Market 3.0x 3.5–4.0x 4.5x+ Multi-stakeholder deals; more can go wrong in late stage
Enterprise 4.0x 4.5–5.0x 6x+ Long cycles, complex approval chains, high slip rate — need more buffer
Multi-segment mixed 3.0–3.5x 4x weighted average — Weight by quota proportion: if ENT = 60% of quota, skew toward 5x requirement

What to Do at Different Coverage Levels

Coverage Level Situation Immediate Action
<2x Pipeline emergency. Will almost certainly miss quota unless something unexpected happens. All-hands outbound sprint. Pull marketing demand gen budget forward. CRO personally reviews pipeline with every AE. Set realistic board expectation now — do not wait until end of quarter.
2x–3x At risk. Will need above-average conversion rates to hit. Tight quarter. Focus on accelerating late-stage deals. Stop creating new stage-1 pipeline for this quarter — push those to next quarter. Identify 2–3 "swing" deals and put maximum support behind them.
3x–4x Healthy. On track if conversion rates hold at historical averages. Maintain standard pipeline review cadence. Monitor stage conversion weekly. Protect early-stage deals from premature closing pressure that reduces next-quarter coverage.
4x–6x Strong. You have margin for error. Can invest in quality of deals over quantity. Requalify ruthlessly — cull non-ICP deals that are consuming capacity. Focus AEs on top-quartile deals. Build next quarter's pipeline now from a position of strength.
>6x Coverage likely inflated by stale deals. "Junk pipeline" problem. Run a pipeline hygiene audit. Require updated close dates and champion confirmations on all stage 3+ deals. The CRM likely contains zombie deals that haven't moved in 90+ days.

Pipeline Coverage vs. Forecast Coverage

Pipeline coverage measures all qualified opportunities. Forecast coverage measures only the deals in the forecast (commit + best case). Pipeline coverage is used for capacity planning 60–90 days out. Forecast coverage is used for the current quarter. A business can have 4x pipeline coverage but only 1.2x forecast coverage if most of the pipeline is early-stage. This is why stage-weighted analysis matters — the raw number can mislead.

What Drives It Up

  • Increased SDR outbound activity
  • More demand gen spend and better conversion
  • AE prospecting targets added to comp plan
  • ICP expanded appropriately
  • New channels added (partnerships, PLG, events)
  • Referral programs activated

What Drives It Down

  • ICP too narrow or demand gen underfunded
  • SDR underperformance or high attrition
  • AEs spending all time on accounts, no prospecting
  • Bad quarter drains late-stage pipeline faster than refilled
  • Market saturation in primary channels
  • Competitive landscape limits addressable market
Leading: Pipeline Created per Week Leading: SDR Meeting Volume Leading: Stage 2 Entry Rate Lagging: Pipeline Coverage at Quarter Start

How It Connects to the Other 5 Metrics

If win rate drops, you need more pipeline coverage to compensate — a 20% drop in win rate requires ~25% more pipeline volume to maintain revenue output. If ramp time increases, AEs generate less pipeline per quarter, which means you need earlier hiring to protect future pipeline. If NRR drops, you need higher new logo pipeline to maintain growth rate.

Metric 02

Win Rate

The efficiency metric — how much of your pipeline converts to revenue.

Formula (Two Methods)
Method A: Win Rate = Closed Won ÷ (Closed Won + Closed Lost) × 100%
Method B: Win Rate = Closed Won ÷ Total Opportunities Entered × 100%
Method A (competitive win rate): Measures performance only when deals reach a decision point. More accurate for understanding competitive effectiveness. Best for mature, well-qualified pipelines.
Method B (pipeline efficiency): Counts all deals including in-progress and ghosted. Lower number; better for measuring overall pipeline health. Useful when deals frequently go dark without a formal "lost" decision.
Worked Example — Both Methods
Quarter data: 42 Closed Won | 88 Closed Lost | 35 still active | 165 total entered pipeline

Method A: 42 ÷ (42 + 88) = 42 ÷ 130 = 32.3% — competitive win rate
Method B: 42 ÷ 165 = 25.5% — pipeline efficiency rate

Important: Use the same method consistently. Do not switch between methods when the number looks better with one vs. the other.

Win Rate Benchmarks

Segment Below Benchmark Benchmark Range Best-in-Class
SMB (<100 employees) <20% 25–35% 40%+
Mid-Market (100–1K employees) <15% 20–30% 35%+
Enterprise (1K+ employees) <12% 15–25% 30%+
PLG-augmented <30% 40–60% 70%+

Win Rate Segmentation: The Four Cuts

Aggregate win rate is almost never the right number to act on. Win rate must be segmented at least four ways before drawing a conclusion or taking action:

By Rep

  • Rep A: 42% | Rep B: 38% | Rep C: 18% | Rep D: 29%
  • Wide variance = coaching problem. Narrow variance = systemic issue. If Rep C has 18% and Rep A has 42%, Rep C needs intensive discovery coaching — not a process overhaul.

By Competitor

  • vs. Competitor X: 45% | vs. Competitor Y: 18% | no competition: 62%
  • 18% against one specific competitor signals a product gap or positioning failure. Build competitive battlecards. Engage PM on the feature gap.

By Source

  • Inbound: 38% | Outbound: 21% | Partner: 44% | Events: 16%
  • Low event win rate may indicate event attendees aren't ICP. Low outbound may mean targeting is too broad. Partner pipeline's high win rate suggests warm intro quality advantage.

By Deal Size

  • <$25K: 45% | $25K–$100K: 28% | >$100K: 14%
  • Low enterprise win rate may mean the enterprise motion (multi-threading, exec sponsorship, evaluation framework) hasn't been built. Don't push enterprise deals before the process is ready.

The Win Rate / Sales Cycle Trade-Off

There is a fundamental tension between win rate and sales cycle length. A rep who qualifies very aggressively will have a high win rate but a long cycle (they only pursue near-certain deals). A rep who pursues everything early will have a short average cycle but a lower win rate. Neither extreme is optimal. The right answer: qualify hard at Stage 1, pursue with urgency after Stage 2.

The Win Rate / ACV Interaction

A 10% win rate at $150K ACV produces more revenue than a 35% win rate at $25K ACV, assuming equal pipeline volume. Don't optimize win rate in isolation — optimize for Revenue per Deal Entered = Win Rate × Average ACV. This is the true efficiency metric.

Leading: Champion Identified Rate Leading: Economic Buyer Access Rate Leading: Stage 2 → 3 Conversion Rate Lagging: Win Rate (Method A) Lagging: Competitive Win Rate
Metric 03

Ramp Time to Quota

How long it takes a new rep to become fully productive — the hidden cost driver of the entire revenue model.

Formula
Ramp Efficiency = % of Quota Attained at Month 3 / Month 6 / Month 9 / Month 12
Fully-Loaded Ramp Cost = (OTE × Ramp Months) + Recruiting Cost + Training Cost + Manager Oversight Cost
Track the ramp curve by hire cohort (e.g., all Q1 2024 hires) rather than individuals — this smooths noise and shows whether your ramp process is improving over time.
Fully-loaded ramp cost example: $250K OTE rep, 9-month ramp, recruiting at 20% OTE = $50K recruiting + $187K salary/benefits at 50% productivity gap + $15K training = ~$252K total ramp investment.
Healthy Ramp Curve Example — Mid-Market AE
Month 3: 15–20% of quota — still learning product, process, territory
Month 6: 40–60% of quota — first deals closing, pipeline building
Month 9: 70–85% of quota — pattern recognition setting in
Month 12: 90–100% of quota — fully ramped

Actual observed: Month 3: 8%, Month 6: 28%, Month 9: 55%, Month 12: 72%
Conclusion: This cohort is ramp-delayed. The gap at Month 6 (28% vs. 50% target) suggests a pipeline creation problem in months 2–4. Check if early onboarding included territory assignment, tool access, and active deal coaching — or was classroom-only.

Ramp Time Benchmarks by Segment

Segment Time to Full Quota Time to 50% Quota Key Ramp Driver
SMB (<$25K ACV) 3–4 months 6–8 weeks Volume activity, product simplicity, tight ICP
Mid-Market ($25K–$100K ACV) 5–7 months 2.5–3 months Discovery skill development, pipeline building, manager coaching cadence
Enterprise (>$100K ACV) 9–12 months 4–5 months Long cycles mean first closes take longer; ramp appears slow even with good early activity
Complex/Technical 12–18 months 6–8 months Technical product mastery required; long ramp baked into the cost model

The Ramp Time / Hiring Timing Relationship

The ramp time metric drives your hiring calendar more than any other number. If you need 10 fully-productive AEs by Q3, and your ramp is 9 months, you need to complete hiring in Q2 of the prior year. Most CROs hire late relative to their targets because they're optimizing for current-quarter headcount cost rather than next-year revenue capacity. The formula:

Hiring Lead Time
Hire By Date = Revenue Target Date − Ramp Duration − Recruiting Duration
Example: Need 8 productive Enterprise AEs hitting full quota by Q3 2025
Ramp: 9 months. Recruiting: 3 months.
Hire By Date: Q3 2025 − 9 months − 3 months = Q1 2024
If you wait until Q1 2025 to hire, you miss Q3 2025 by exactly one year.

How to Reduce Ramp Time Without Compromising Quality

  1. Pre-boarding access Give offer-accepted reps access to product sandbox, sales tools, and the enablement portal 2 weeks before start date. Start the learning curve before day one.
  2. Structured 30/60/90 onboarding with measurable milestones Day 30: First discovery call solo. Day 60: First demo delivered. Day 90: First opportunity in Stage 3. Define exactly what "ramp week 4" looks like, not just month 3 quota attainment.
  3. Deal shadowing + call coaching from week 2 New reps shadow top performers on live calls in week 2 (not week 8). Manager reviews every call recording in months 1–3 and provides feedback within 24 hours.
  4. Pipeline inheritance at start Where possible, transfer a starter pipeline of 5–10 qualified opportunities from mature reps or CS-sourced expansion leads to new hires. Eliminates the cold-start problem for the first 60 days.
  5. Deliberate territory design Give ramping reps a territory with inbound and PLG signal to work, not purely cold outbound. The first closed deal dramatically accelerates ramp — any mechanism that produces that first close earlier is worth the territory trade-off.

What Shortens Ramp Time

  • Strong structured onboarding program
  • Active call coaching from week 2
  • Clear ICP and simple value proposition
  • Inbound pipeline to work from day 1
  • Strong manager with coaching bandwidth
  • Previous experience in same segment

What Extends Ramp Time

  • Weak onboarding (classroom only, no live deals)
  • Complex product requiring technical mastery
  • Pure cold outbound territory with no inbound
  • Manager stretched too thin to coach
  • Wrong hire profile (skills mismatch)
  • Long average sales cycle (first close takes longer)
Leading: Activity Volume at Month 1 Leading: Pipeline Created at Month 2 Leading: First Demo Date Lagging: % Quota at Month 6 Lagging: Time to First Close
Metric 04

CAC Payback Period

How long it takes to recover the cost of acquiring a customer — the efficiency metric that drives funding requirements.

Formulas
CAC = Total Sales & Marketing Spend (Period) ÷ New Customers Acquired (Period)
CAC Payback = CAC ÷ (ACV × Gross Margin %)
"New Customers" = logos, not ARR. CAC is a per-customer cost.
S&M Spend includes: all S&M salaries + benefits, commissions and bonuses, marketing program spend (ads, events, content), tools and technology, recruiting costs for S&M hires, and a portion of RevOps allocated to demand gen.
Use annualized gross margin, not contribution margin. Use the same time period for spend and customers (typically trailing 12 months or same quarter on a 90-day lag).
Worked Example
Q3 S&M Spend: $2,400,000
New Customers in Q3: 48
CAC = $2,400,000 ÷ 48 = $50,000

Average ACV of new customers: $60,000
Gross Margin: 72%
CAC Payback = $50,000 ÷ ($60,000 × 72%) = $50,000 ÷ $43,200 = 13.9 months

Interpretation: 13.9 months is acceptable but not best-in-class. The company needs approximately 14 months of a customer's revenue (at 72% margin) to cover its cost to acquire them.

Benchmarks and Interpretation

CAC Payback Period Assessment Implication
<12 months Best-in-class Extremely efficient GTM. Can grow rapidly with minimal dilution. Investors will love this metric.
12–18 months Acceptable / Good Healthy business. Standard for most well-run SaaS companies. Growth still requires capital but is fundable.
18–24 months Concerning GTM efficiency problem. Growth is expensive. Must either raise more capital or improve efficiency before scaling.
>24 months Problematic Business model risk. Either CAC is too high, ACV is too low, or gross margins are insufficient. Cannot scale without fundamental change.

CAC Payback and Funding Efficiency

CAC payback is fundamentally a cash flow problem. Every new customer requires upfront S&M investment that isn't recovered for months. The longer your payback period, the more cash you need on hand to fund growth — which means more fundraising, more dilution, and more investor scrutiny. A company with a 12-month payback period needs $12M in S&M spend to add $12M in new ARR. A company with a 24-month payback period needs $24M to add the same $12M in new ARR. The difference is $12M of capital cost.

The LTV:CAC Ratio

LTV:CAC Formula
LTV = ACV × Gross Margin % × (1 ÷ Gross Churn Rate)
LTV:CAC = LTV ÷ CAC
Target: LTV:CAC ≥ 3x for a healthy SaaS business. <3x indicates overspending on acquisition relative to customer value.
Example: ACV $60K, Gross Margin 72%, Gross Churn 12% → LTV = $60K × 72% × (1/0.12) = $360K. CAC $50K. LTV:CAC = $360K ÷ $50K = 7.2x — excellent.

How to Reduce CAC Payback

Reduce CAC

  • Improve inbound efficiency (organic, PLG, content)
  • Build partner channel (lower CAC than direct)
  • Reduce SDR:AE ratio with better inbound quality
  • Focus on higher-intent buyer segments
  • Reduce S&M headcount via tool automation

Increase ACV or Gross Margin

  • Move upmarket (higher ACV customers)
  • Add premium tiers with higher gross margin
  • Reduce COGS (infrastructure optimization)
  • Reduce discounting (improve ACV per deal)
  • Add professional services at higher margin
Leading: S&M Efficiency Ratio (New ARR / S&M Spend) Leading: Inbound % of Pipeline Lagging: CAC (trailing 12 months) Lagging: CAC Payback Period Lagging: LTV:CAC Ratio
Metric 05

Net Revenue Retention (NRR)

The single most important metric for SaaS company valuation and the best proxy for product-market fit among existing customers.

Formula
NRR = (Starting MRR + Expansion − Contraction − Churn) ÷ Starting MRR × 100%
Starting MRR = MRR from existing customers at the start of the period (exclude new logos added this period).
Expansion = MRR added from upsell, cross-sell, or seat additions from those same customers.
Contraction = MRR lost from downgrades or seat reductions (not full churn).
Churn = MRR lost from full customer cancellations.
Gross Revenue Retention (GRR) = same formula without expansion — measures pure retention floor.
Worked Example
January 1 cohort MRR: $500,000
Expansion (upsell) by December 31: +$90,000
Contraction (downgrades): −$18,000
Churn (cancellations): −$35,000

NRR = ($500,000 + $90,000 − $18,000 − $35,000) ÷ $500,000 × 100%
NRR = $537,000 ÷ $500,000 × 100% = 107.4%

GRR = ($500,000 − $18,000 − $35,000) ÷ $500,000 × 100% = 89.4%
Interpretation: The company grows existing customer revenue 7.4% annually. But gross retention at 89.4% signals meaningful churn that expansion is offsetting. Need to address the churn to prevent expansion from masking a structural problem.

NRR Benchmarks

NRR Level Assessment Narrative
>130% World-Class (Snowflake, Datadog tier) Existing customers grow 30%+ per year. Company can shrink new logo acquisition and still grow. Exceptional product-market fit.
120–130% Elite Extremely strong retention + expansion. Top SaaS companies (Veeva, Twilio historically) operate in this range. Investors apply a premium multiple.
110–120% Strong / Top Quartile Healthy expansion motion offsetting churn. Most best-in-class PLG/B2B companies. Fundable and scalable.
100–110% Healthy Baseline Retaining more than losing. Not growing from existing customers meaningfully. Expansion motion needs investment.
<100% Leaky Bucket Losing more from existing customers than gaining from expansion. New logo acquisition is required just to stay flat. Unsustainable at scale.

The Compounding Math of NRR

NRR compounds across years, creating a dramatic divergence between companies at different NRR levels. This table shows the fate of a $10M ARR cohort after 5 years at different NRR scenarios — with zero new logo acquisition:

NRR Scenario Year 1 Year 2 Year 3 Year 5
Starting ARR: $10M Value of original $10M ARR cohort after N years
NRR = 130% $13.0M $16.9M $21.9M $37.1M
NRR = 120% $12.0M $14.4M $17.3M $24.9M
NRR = 110% $11.0M $12.1M $13.3M $16.1M
NRR = 100% $10.0M $10.0M $10.0M $10.0M
NRR = 90% $9.0M $8.1M $7.3M $5.9M

NRR and Valuation

Public SaaS market data consistently shows that NRR is the single highest-correlation metric with revenue multiple. Companies with NRR >120% typically command 15–25x ARR multiples in strong markets. Companies with NRR <100% often trade at 3–6x ARR regardless of growth rate. Investors pay for the quality of the revenue base, not just its size. A $100M ARR company at 130% NRR is worth significantly more than a $100M ARR company at 95% NRR — even if both are growing at 30% — because the 130% NRR company's growth requires far less new logo acquisition to sustain.

What Drives NRR Up

  • Strong product with expanding use cases
  • Proactive CS engagement and QBRs
  • Usage-based pricing with natural expansion ceiling
  • ICP quality at sale (right customers buy)
  • Platform products with cross-sell motion
  • Multi-year contracts reducing churn window

What Drives NRR Down

  • ICP mismatch — wrong customers churn
  • Product not delivering ROI (adoption failure)
  • Competitive displacement by better alternatives
  • Budget pressure in economic downturns
  • CS team under-resourced or reactive only
  • Point solution with limited expansion surface

Metric 06

Quota Attainment Distribution

Not just "what % hit quota" — the full distribution that reveals the health of the entire sales system.

The Right Way to Measure Attainment
Attainment % = (Individual Closed Won ACV ÷ Individual Quota) × 100%
Track the distribution — not just the mean or the "% above 100%." The shape of the distribution tells you far more than any single number.
Group reps into attainment buckets: 0–25%, 25–50%, 50–75%, 75–100%, 100–125%, 125%+. The relative size of each bucket reveals whether you have a sales problem, a quota problem, or a system problem.

The Healthy Distribution

0–25%
25–50%
50–75%
75–100%
100–125%
125%+

Healthy distribution shape: peak at 75–125% attainment with small tails in both directions.

Attainment Bucket Healthy Range What It Means Action If Overrepresented
0–25% (Failing) <5% of reps These reps are in their first quarter or have a fundamental problem Distinguish ramping reps from tenured reps. Investigate root cause for tenured reps. PIP or exit.
25–50% (Struggling) 5–10% of reps Underperformance — may be early ramp, skill gap, or territory disadvantage Coaching intervention. Territory audit. If multiple reps in same territory, consider territory problem.
50–75% (Developing) 15–20% of reps Working but not closing consistently. Usually ramp or skill issue. Active coaching. Pipeline review. Pair with top performer for deal strategies.
75–125% (Core) 50–60% of reps The healthy middle. These reps make or miss the company number. Retain, develop, and protect. These are your culture and performance anchor.
125%+ (High Performers) 20–25% of reps Outperforming. Either exceptional skill, lucky territory, or quota miscalibration. If >25% here consistently, quotas may be too low. Investigate. Develop these reps for senior roles.

What Different Distribution Shapes Tell You

Distribution Red Flags

Bimodal distribution (peaks at both 0% and 150%, empty middle): Suggests a wild variance in territory quality or a highly inconsistent sales process. Your best territories crush quota; others are structurally underfunded. Fix: territory rebalancing and process standardization.

Left-heavy distribution (most reps at 25–75%): Quota is too high, market isn't big enough to support current quota levels, OR onboarding/ramp is broken. Check quota calibration against available TAM per territory.

Right-heavy distribution (most reps at 125%+): Quota is too low. You're paying out excessive commissions for performance that should be the baseline. Recalibrate upward — but do it gradually to avoid attrition.

Attainment Problem vs. Quota Calibration Problem

The most important diagnostic question: is this a sales performance problem or a quota design problem? The test: if your top 20% performers are all at 90–110% attainment, quota is probably right and performance is the issue. If your top 20% performers are all at 50–70% attainment, quota is almost certainly too high regardless of performance. Never PIP a rep for quota miss before ruling out a quota calibration problem.

Quota Calibration Check
Target Attainment Rate: 60–70% of reps at or above 100%
If <40% of reps hit quota: quotas are likely too high, or you have a systemic sales problem.
If >80% of reps hit quota: quotas are likely too low. Investigate comp plan profitability.
The 60–70% attainment target creates sufficient stretch while keeping most of the team motivated.

What Moves Attainment Higher

  • Pipeline coverage ratio improving
  • Win rate improving (process or ICP fix)
  • Shorter ramp time (better onboarding)
  • Better territory design (fair market access)
  • More CS-sourced expansion deals
  • Quota recalibrated to achievable levels

What Suppresses Attainment

  • Pipeline below 3x coverage at quarter start
  • Quota increased without corresponding market
  • High rep attrition disrupting team pipeline
  • Long ramp — ramping reps drag down distribution
  • Competitive landscape intensifying
  • Product-market fit problems reducing win rate
Leading: Pipeline Coverage per Rep Leading: Stage 3 Entry Rate per Rep Lagging: Attainment Distribution Lagging: % Reps at 100%+

The Metrics Interaction Map

The six north star metrics are not independent. Moving one changes at least two others. Understanding these interactions allows a CRO to anticipate second-order effects before they appear in the data.

Win Rate ↓
→
Requires higher Pipeline Coverage to maintain revenue output. A 20% drop in win rate demands ~25% more pipeline volume to hit the same quota number. Also signals potential ICP or messaging problem driving Quota Attainment down.
Ramp Time ↑
→
Reduces effective rep capacity per quarter, which reduces Pipeline Coverage and Attainment Distribution. Also increases CAC Payback because S&M spend includes ramping rep costs before they produce revenue.
NRR ↓
→
Requires more New Logo Pipeline Coverage to maintain growth rate. At 90% NRR, a $20M ARR company needs to replace $2M in churned ARR before growing. This increases pressure on Win Rate and Quota Attainment directly.
CAC Payback ↑
→
Usually driven by Win Rate declining (more pipeline spend per dollar closed) or ACV declining (each deal covers less CAC). Forces a choice: reduce S&M spend (which reduces Pipeline Coverage) or raise pricing to increase ACV.
Quota Attainment ↓
→
If systemic, signals either Pipeline Coverage is insufficient, Win Rate is declining, or quotas are miscalibrated. If isolated to specific reps, signals coaching/onboarding problem increasing effective Ramp Time.
Pipeline Coverage ↑ past 5x
→
May actually suppress Win Rate and Quota Attainment if the excess pipeline is low-quality. Reps spread across too many deals make worse discovery decisions on each. Better to have 3x high-quality pipeline than 6x junk pipeline.

Leading vs. Lagging: 60–90 Day Forward Visibility

Every lagging indicator has a leading indicator that predicts it 60–90 days in advance. Build a leading indicator dashboard and you'll never be surprised by a bad quarter again.

North Star Metric (Lagging) Leading Indicators (60–90 Day Signal) When to Act on Leading Signal
Pipeline Coverage SDR meetings booked, pipeline created per week, AE self-sourced activity, MQL volume If pipeline creation rate falls below (quota ÷ average sales cycle weeks), intervene now — not at quarter start
Win Rate Champion identification rate, economic buyer access rate, Stage 2→3 conversion, discovery call quality score If EB access rate falls below 60% of Stage 3 deals, coach to champion-building and access strategies immediately
Ramp Time First demo date, first opportunity in Stage 2, activity volume at month 1, coaching call frequency If new hire hasn't booked first demo by week 6, the ramp will be delayed — intervention needed now, not at month 6
CAC Payback S&M spend per new opportunity created, demo-to-close conversion, ACV per new logo trend If cost-per-opportunity is rising without a corresponding ACV increase, CAC Payback will deteriorate in 90 days
NRR Health score distribution, product adoption rate, QBR completion rate, expansion pipeline from CS If health score below 70 ("red") accounts exceed 15% of ARR base, next quarter NRR will reflect it
Quota Attainment Pipeline coverage per rep, Stage 3+ pipeline per rep vs. quota, mid-quarter forecast accuracy At week 6 of the quarter, if a rep's Stage 3+ pipeline is below 1.5x their remaining quota, they'll likely miss — intervene now
Build the Leading Indicator Dashboard First

Most CROs build lagging indicator dashboards and wonder why they keep getting surprised. The forward-looking dashboard — pipeline creation velocity, champion identification rates, health score distribution, rep ramp curves — gives you 60–90 days to intervene before a bad quarter becomes a fact. Build this dashboard in your BI tool (Tableau, Looker, or even Salesforce reports) and review it weekly alongside your standard forecast review.
