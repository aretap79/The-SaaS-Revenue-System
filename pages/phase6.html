The Diagnostic Framework

Most CROs react to symptoms. Great CROs diagnose systems. The difference between a good turnaround and a failed one is whether the leader correctly identified the root cause before intervening. Cutting reps when the problem is messaging, or rebuilding process when the problem is ICP, destroys credibility and capital. Diagnosis comes first.

The CRO as Diagnostician

Think of revenue leadership like medicine. A doctor who prescribes without diagnosing is dangerous. A CRO who fires reps, hires reps, changes comp plans, or rebuilds process without first establishing root cause is equally dangerous — and usually more expensive. The diagnostic framework below forces rigor before action.

The Three Questions to Ask First

01
People, Process, or Market?
Is the root cause human performance, broken workflow, or external conditions beyond your control?
02
Isolated or Systemic?
Is this problem limited to one rep or one team — or does the pattern repeat across the organization?
03
Leading or Lagging?
Is this a fixable input problem (leading) or an outcome of past decisions already baked into your results (lagging)?
Question 1: People, Process, or Market?

These are mutually exclusive categories with very different interventions. A people problem means the right process exists but the humans executing it lack skill, motivation, or fit. A process problem means even a great rep, following the current process, would produce poor results. A market problem means the external environment has shifted in a way that renders current strategy ineffective regardless of execution.

The diagnostic test: take your top 20% performing rep and put them on the territory/segment/channel where you see the problem. If they also struggle, it's process or market — not people. If they thrive, it's a people problem. This thought experiment prevents the most common CRO mistake: blaming the person when the system is broken.

Question 2: Isolated or Systemic?

Segment every metric by individual, team, segment, geography, source, and product line before drawing any conclusion. If one rep has a 12% win rate and the team average is 28%, you have a coaching problem, not a process problem. If every rep on the enterprise team has a win rate below 15%, you have a process, ICP, or competitive problem. The segmentation step is non-negotiable — aggregate metrics are almost always misleading.

Question 3: Leading or Lagging?

This question determines urgency and intervention horizon. Lagging indicators — closed revenue, churn, win rate over last two quarters — reflect decisions already made. You cannot fix Q3 revenue with a Q3 intervention; you fix it with Q1 pipeline decisions. Leading indicators — pipeline volume, activity, stage conversion rates, champion identification — are where you have actionable leverage right now. Always identify which type of problem you're facing before setting expectations about when interventions will show results.

The Expectation Trap

A new CRO who walks in, diagnoses the problem correctly, and implements the right fixes still won't see lagging indicators improve for 60–180 days. Board and CEO expectations must be set accordingly. The leading indicators — pipeline volume, discovery quality, champion depth — will move first. Use those to demonstrate progress while lagging indicators catch up.

The Six Revenue Failure Modes

Every revenue problem in a SaaS business falls into one of six failure modes. These are not mutually exclusive — a broken organization often has multiple active at once — but each has distinct root causes, diagnostic questions, and intervention paths.

Failure Mode 01
Low Pipeline Volume
Not enough qualified opportunities to hit quota — the earliest and most reversible revenue failure.
Failure Mode 02
Low Win Rates
Enough pipeline, but deals aren't closing — usually a qualification, messaging, or competitive failure.
Failure Mode 03
Long Sales Cycles
Deals in the pipeline but moving too slowly, killing forecast accuracy and rep capacity.
Failure Mode 04
Missed Forecasts
The number you commit to the board doesn't match what closes — a CRM, discipline, or culture problem.
Failure Mode 05
High Churn / Low NRR
Customers leave faster than you acquire them — often a sales quality problem before it's a CS problem.
Failure Mode 06
Rep Attrition
Your best salespeople are leaving — destroying pipeline, institutional knowledge, and team morale.

1 Low Pipeline Volume

The earliest and most correctable failure mode. Low pipeline is almost always a leading indicator problem, which means interventions will show results within 60–90 days if correctly targeted. The challenge is that pipeline volume is a multi-variable output — it depends on ICP definition, demand gen investment, SDR performance, AE prospecting, messaging resonance, and channel mix. Diagnosing which variable is broken requires segmented analysis, not gut instinct.

Root Causes

  • ICP definition too narrow or too broad
  • Demand gen budget underfunded relative to TAM
  • AEs not prospecting — relying on inbound only
  • SDR team underperforming (activity or quality)
  • Messaging not resonating with target persona
  • Wrong channels for the target buyer
  • Poor lead routing — good leads dying in queue
  • No outbound motion in a segment that needs one
  • Territory design concentrates pipeline too narrowly

Diagnostic Questions

  • What is pipeline coverage ratio right now? (Target: 3–4x)
  • What % of pipeline is sourced by marketing vs. outbound?
  • What is the MQL-to-SQL conversion rate? (Benchmark: 13–15%)
  • What is each AE's average self-sourced pipeline per month?
  • What is SDR connect rate, demo rate, and pipeline per SDR?
  • Which channels have the highest-converting pipeline?
  • What does the ICP look like for the last 20 closed-won deals?
  • Is there a whitespace in the TAM the team isn't working?

Fixes by Layer

Layer Fix Time to Impact
ICP Analyze 20–30 best customers by NRR, LTV, and sales cycle. Rebuild ICP firmographics and technographics from the data. 30–60 days
Marketing Reallocate budget to highest-converting channels. Launch content that addresses specific buyer pain at top of funnel. Build intent data programs (G2, Bombora). 60–90 days
SDR Audit activity metrics (calls, emails, LinkedIn touches). Implement cadence sequences. Add personalization requirements at step 1. 30–60 days
AE Prospecting Set self-sourced pipeline targets per AE. Block prospecting time in calendar. Inspect in 1:1s. Create comp upside for self-sourced deals. 45–60 days

2 Low Win Rates

Win rate problems are the most complex to diagnose because they have the most potential root causes — and because the symptom (deals lost) is usually blamed on the wrong variable (price). In practice, price is rarely the real reason deals are lost. It's almost always a pre-demo qualification failure, a champion failure, a competitive positioning gap, or a value prop weakness. The segmentation analysis is critical here.

Root Causes

  • Wrong ICP — pursuing prospects who shouldn't buy
  • Weak discovery — not identifying real pain
  • No champion — selling to users, not buyers
  • Poor competitive positioning — losing to specific vendors
  • Price/value gap — product not worth perceived cost
  • Weak value proposition relative to alternatives
  • Product gaps the competition covers
  • Demo quality — showing features, not solving pain
  • Sales skills variance across the team
  • No defined close process or economic buyer access

Diagnostic Questions

  • What is win rate segmented by rep, segment, source, and competitor?
  • What are the top 3 stated reasons for loss in CRM?
  • Are losses concentrated with specific competitors?
  • What % of lost deals had an identified champion?
  • What % of lost deals had economic buyer access?
  • Are reps running MEDDICC in late-stage opportunities?
  • What does call recording analysis show in lost deal demos?
  • Are we losing at specific funnel stages disproportionately?

Win Rate Segmentation Analysis

Segment What it Reveals Action if Low
By Rep Skill distribution across the team; isolates coaching targets vs. systemic issues Call coaching, discovery framework retraining, 1:1 pipeline reviews
By Segment (SMB/MM/ENT) Whether your product/process fits the market segment you're targeting Reallocate resources to the segment where you win; rebuild motion for others
By Competitor Specific competitive gaps — product, pricing, perception Build competitive battlecards, address product gaps with PM, adjust pricing
By Source (inbound/outbound) Whether qualification quality differs by channel Tighten qualification criteria at the source with the lower win rate
By Deal Size Whether your process scales to enterprise deals or only works SMB Add enterprise-specific motion: multi-threading, exec sponsors, evaluation frameworks
Win/Loss Interview Program

Build a formal win/loss interview process: call every lost deal decision-maker 2 weeks after loss. Ask what the final decision came down to, who was involved, what alternatives were considered, and what would have changed the outcome. Do the same for wins. 20 interviews reveals patterns that 1,000 CRM "loss reason" checkboxes never will.

Leading: Champion Identified % Leading: Economic Buyer Access % Leading: Discovery Call Quality Score Lagging: Win Rate by Quarter Lagging: Competitive Loss Rate

3 Long Sales Cycles

Long sales cycles are a hidden killer because they don't appear as a problem until the forecast model breaks. Deals in stage 4 for 90 days feel like pipeline — until they ghost. The root causes of long cycles are almost always traceable to early-stage qualification failures: no urgency established, no economic buyer accessed, no champion willing to pull the deal through, no defined process. The fix is almost always at Stage 1, not Stage 4.

Root Causes

  • No access to the economic buyer — stuck with champions only
  • No defined decision process established early
  • Pain not quantified — no urgency to act now
  • Internal procurement delays (legal, IT, finance)
  • Multi-stakeholder complexity with no nav strategy
  • No champion willing and able to drive internally
  • POC scope creep — technical evaluation never ends
  • Budget not confirmed — "we'll find it" purchases
  • Deal stalls because next step is not defined at each stage
  • Competitor-induced delays — buying committee wants "comparison"

Diagnostic Questions

  • What is average days in each pipeline stage?
  • What % of opportunities have a confirmed decision date?
  • What % of opportunities have an identified economic buyer who attended a call?
  • What is the average POC length? Is it defined at kickoff?
  • At what stage do deals begin to stall?
  • What % of late-stage deals have a mutual action plan (MAP)?
  • How many stakeholders are mapped per deal at Stage 3?
  • What is the "slip rate" — deals that push quarter-over-quarter?

How to Compress Sales Cycles Without Discounting

  1. Quantify the cost of inaction at discovery Convert pain into a number: "What does this problem cost you per month?" If the prospect can't quantify, help them. A $50K/month problem creates natural urgency. "Nice to have" pain never does.
  2. Access the economic buyer before Stage 3 Set an explicit gate: no opportunity advances to Stage 3 without the rep having at least one call with the budget holder. Use the champion to facilitate. Without this gate, deals stall in procurement.
  3. Build a Mutual Action Plan at Stage 2 A MAP is a shared document between seller and buyer with every step to close, owner, and date. If a champion won't sign off on a MAP, they aren't a real champion. The MAP creates accountability on the buyer's side.
  4. Define POC exit criteria before POC begins Never start a POC without written success criteria. "We'll know it's working when X" — set that before day one. Prevents endless evaluation and gives you a close trigger.
  5. Time-box every stage Set explicit "expires on" dates for proposals, POCs, and pricing. Urgency is engineered, not discovered. Use a quarter-end, fiscal close, executive sponsor's availability, or a product launch as a legitimate reason to close now.
The Discount Trap

When deals stall, the instinct is to offer a discount to re-create urgency. This works once and teaches the buyer to stall every renewal. A 15% discount to compress a sales cycle by 30 days costs you 15% of deal value permanently, not just this quarter. Engineer urgency through business value and timelines instead.

Leading: MAP Completion Rate Leading: Economic Buyer on Calls % Leading: Pain Quantified % Lagging: Average Sales Cycle Days Lagging: Quarter-Push Rate

4 Missed Forecasts

Forecast accuracy is the most important trust currency a CRO has with the CEO and board. A CRO who misses forecast twice is on a performance plan. Missed forecasts almost never happen because the deals weren't there — they happen because the inspection process was broken, the stage definitions were vague, reps were sandbagging or over-forecasting, and managers weren't challenging with rigor. The fix is systemic, not situational.

Root Causes

  • CRM hygiene failures — stale data, no close dates
  • Rep sandbagging — holding deals to hit next quarter
  • Rep optimism bias — gut feel overrides data
  • Managers not challenging rep commits
  • Stage definitions vague — same stage means different things to different reps
  • No MEDDICC discipline in late stage
  • Over-reliance on "commit" category without verification
  • No rollup inspection process above the manager
  • Late-quarter deal slippage not caught early enough

Audit a Missed Forecast

  • Pull all deals that were in "commit" on day 1 of the quarter — what % closed?
  • For deals that didn't close: at what stage did they enter the quarter? What signals were present that should have flagged risk?
  • What did reps say in week 8 forecast calls vs. what closed in week 13?
  • Was CRM data accurate on close dates at week 6?
  • Were "upside" deals included in the final number?
  • Which managers over-forecasted most consistently? Is this skill or incentive?

Building Forecast Confidence Over Time

Forecast Accuracy Rate
Accuracy = Actual Closed / Called Forecast × 100%
Target: ±5% accuracy. Track this by rep, by manager, and in aggregate each quarter. Managers with >15% variance consistently need coaching or replacement.

The path to forecast accuracy runs through MEDDICC rigor. Before any deal enters the "commit" category, a manager must be able to answer YES to all of: (1) Is the economic buyer personally engaged? (2) Is there a confirmed decision date? (3) Is there a compelling event or consequence of inaction? (4) Has legal/procurement review begun? (5) Has the rep spoken to at least two stakeholders above the champion? If any answer is NO, the deal goes in "best case" — not commit.

Forecast Category Definition Required Evidence Typical Close %
Closed Won Signed and booked Executed contract + PO 100%
Commit Rep betting their job on this Verbal commit from economic buyer, decision date within 30 days, legal in progress 80–90%
Best Case Strong deals with one remaining risk Champion confirmed intent, at least one other stakeholder mapped 40–60%
Pipeline Working opportunities, no close certainty Discovery completed, next step defined 10–25%
Omitted Won't close this quarter — 0%

5 High Churn / Low NRR

Churn is the most dangerous failure mode because it compounds: low NRR means new ARR is filling a leaky bucket, and every new logo at risk creates a permanent drag on growth efficiency. The single most important insight for a CRO is that churn is usually caused by the sales process, not the CS process — the seeds of churn are planted at signature, not at renewal.

Root Causes

  • ICP mismatch — sold to prospects who aren't a great fit
  • Sales promises product can't keep (over-scoped, over-promised)
  • Onboarding failure — customer never gets to first value
  • Low product adoption — champion leaves, no second user base
  • Weak CS team — no proactive engagement model
  • Competitive displacement — better alternative emerges
  • Economic pressure — budget cuts, company downturn
  • No executive sponsor — single-threaded account relationship
  • Success criteria never defined — hard to claim ROI at renewal

The Churn Investigation Framework

  • When did churn risk first appear in health scores?
  • Was this account in ICP? (Run ICP match score on churned accounts)
  • What promises were made in the sales process? (Pull call recordings)
  • Did the customer reach first value milestone? By when?
  • Was there a QBR in the last 6 months? What did the customer say?
  • Who was the champion at sale? Are they still at the company?
  • Was there a red flag in the sales-to-CS handoff document?
  • What was the customer's NPS score at month 3?

How to Prevent Churn at the Sales Stage

  1. Enforce ICP discipline at the pipeline entry gate Every opportunity must pass an ICP score before Stage 2. Reps who sell to non-ICP accounts get lower commissions. Make selling to the wrong customer economically painful for the rep.
  2. Define success criteria jointly before close The mutual success plan (MSP) includes measurable success criteria agreed upon by both parties. CS inherits this document at handoff. No MSP = no Closed Won.
  3. Shadow CS model Bring a CS leader onto the final demo and implementation scoping call. CS commits to what's deliverable, not what sales promises. Prevents scope creep that creates churn.
  4. Chargeback mechanism If an account churns within 12 months, the AE refunds a portion of their commission. This is the most powerful alignment mechanism between sales quality and customer success. Painful, but effective.
The Leaky Bucket Math
Net New ARR Required = Growth Target − (Starting ARR × (NRR − 100%))
Example: $20M ARR company targeting $28M (+40% growth). At 90% NRR, you lose $2M/year to churn, so you need $10M in new ARR. At 110% NRR, existing customers grow $2M, so you only need $6M in new ARR. The NRR difference is massive on new logo requirements.

6 Rep Attrition

Rep attrition is the most expensive and under-measured failure mode. When a rep leaves, the company loses their pipeline (typically 50–60% of in-flight deals die within 30 days), their institutional knowledge, their customer relationships, and months of invested ramp. The loaded cost of one experienced rep departure is 1.5–3x their annual OTE — but most CROs don't calculate it and therefore don't defend against it with appropriate investment.

Root Causes

  • Compensation not competitive vs. market
  • Quota miscalibrated — set to fail, not to motivate
  • Poor management — toxic, micromanaging, or absent
  • No career path — nowhere to grow
  • Toxic culture or unfair treatment
  • Wrong hire — skill mismatch, cultural fit failure
  • Lack of tools, support, or resources to succeed
  • Better territory/segment given to favorites
  • Poached by a better-paying competitor

Exit Interview Framework

  • Why are you leaving? (Probe for real answer, not polished one)
  • What made you look? (There's usually a triggering event)
  • What would have changed your decision?
  • How did you feel about your quota fairness?
  • How would you describe your manager's effectiveness?
  • What tools/support were missing?
  • Would you refer a friend to work here? Why/why not?
  • What does your new role offer that this one didn't?
True Cost of Rep Attrition
Attrition Cost = (Recruiting Cost) + (Lost Pipeline × Pipeline-to-Close Rate) + (Ramp Cost × Ramp Months) + (Lost Productivity Gap)
Typical breakdown for a $200K OTE Enterprise AE:
Recruiting (15–25% OTE): $30–50K
Lost pipeline (6 months of $500K pipeline × 25% win rate): $125K revenue
Ramp cost (6 months × 50% productivity): $100K OTE equivalent
Total loaded cost: $250K–$350K per departure

Intervention Levels

Not every problem requires the same intervention. Using a strategy-level fix on a coaching problem destroys momentum. Using a coaching fix on a strategy problem wastes time. Matching intervention level to root cause is a core CRO competency.

LEVEL 1
Coaching
Use when: The problem is isolated to specific reps, the process is proven, and the gap is skill or behavior. Cost: low. Time to impact: 30–60 days.
LEVEL 2
Process Change
Use when: The problem is systemic across multiple reps following the same process. A good rep would still fail. Cost: medium. Time to impact: 60–90 days.
LEVEL 3
Strategy Change
Use when: The market, competitive landscape, or ICP has fundamentally shifted. Process improvements won't help. Cost: high. Time to impact: 90–180 days.
LEVEL 4
Personnel Change
Use when: Level 1–3 interventions have failed or the individual is a cultural threat to the organization. Last resort. Immediate in some cases.
The Intervention Sequencing Rule

Always start at Level 1. Escalate only when you can prove the lower level won't work. Moving to Level 4 (personnel) before trying Level 1–3 destroys team trust and culture. The exception: when the problem is clearly a role mismatch from the outset, a wrong hire from day one, or illegal conduct. In those cases, speed is appropriate.

When to Move to Personnel Action

Three conditions must all be true: (1) You have given the individual clear, documented, and unambiguous expectations. (2) You have provided adequate support — coaching, resources, process clarity — to give them a fair chance to meet those expectations. (3) A defined timeframe has passed with no improvement. Without all three, a personnel change is premature and will be perceived as arbitrary. With all three, it is both necessary and defensible.

The 90-Day Turnaround Plan

A CRO inheriting a broken sales org faces a sequencing challenge: act too fast and you destroy confidence; act too slow and you miss the window to demonstrate leadership. This framework provides the right cadence.

Weeks 1–2

Diagnose — Listen Before You Build

Do not make any changes in the first two weeks. Hold 1:1s with every rep, every manager, every functional leader (marketing, CS, RevOps, product). Listen for what's working, what's not, and what the org believes the real problems are. Pull the last 6 quarters of data: pipeline coverage, win rates, forecast accuracy, ramp time, churn, NRR, attrition. Map the symptoms to possible root causes. Document all findings without judgment.

Weeks 3–4

Stabilize — Stop the Bleeding

Identify the top three most urgent issues and address them with targeted, fast-win interventions. This might be fixing the forecast process, rescuing a top-3 deal that's at risk, making one critical hire, or stopping a destructive behavior in the field. Build credibility with one visible win. Share your preliminary diagnosis with the CEO and board — both to align expectations and to demonstrate you understand the problem.

Months 2–3

Fix Foundations — Structural Changes

Execute the process, ICP, and structural changes that your diagnostic identified. This typically includes: rebuilding the ICP and qualification criteria, implementing or retraining MEDDICC, fixing the MQL definition and marketing-sales SLA, implementing structured onboarding for new hires, building the first version of a metrics dashboard, and making any talent decisions that can no longer be deferred. These are Level 2–3 interventions with a 60–90 day impact horizon.

Months 4–6

Rebuild Momentum — System-Level Improvements

By now, leading indicators should be improving. Use this window to build the long-term system: structured enablement program, career paths and comp redesign, territory optimization, demand gen investment, CS-sales alignment on handoffs, and a quarterly business review cadence that makes performance visible across the org. Begin presenting to the board with a narrative that shows leading indicators improving even if lagging indicators are still recovering.

The 30-60-90 Board Narrative

At 30 days: "Here is my diagnosis. Here are the root causes I believe are real. Here are the 3 things I'm fixing first." At 60 days: "Here is what we've changed. Here are the leading indicators already moving. Here is what I expect by end of Q." At 90 days: "Here is the evidence that the fixes are working. Here is the plan for the next two quarters." This cadence builds enormous credibility even when lagging indicators haven't caught up yet.
