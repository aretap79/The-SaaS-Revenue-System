1. The Purpose of Prospecting
Prospecting is the top of the funnel — and the most vulnerable link in the GTM chain. A bad prospecting motion is invisible: leads look fine in the CRM, but quality is low and LIR achievement suffers downstream.
The Four Goals of Prospecting: (1) Reach ICP buyers only. (2) Earn their attention. (3) Qualify Green/Yellow/Red fast. (4) Book a discovery call — with the right person, at the right company, about the right problem.
  • Prospecting is the single most upstream quality control point in your revenue motion
  • Every unqualified meeting booked is a tax on every other part of the system — discovery, demo, CS, and forecast accuracy all degrade
  • A bad prospecting motion is invisible: pipeline looks fine, reps are busy, but LIUE (LIR Underlying Economic) suffers because customer quality is wrong from the start
  • The cost of one misfit customer is not just the ARR lost at churn — it's the CSM time, the support load, the product distraction, and the NPS hit
Anti-Goal
Booking meetings with anyone who will take them. This is the most common prospecting failure mode. It destroys SQL quality, inflates pipeline with noise, and misleads the entire revenue forecast.
2. The 4-Step Prospecting Framework
Every prospecting conversation — regardless of channel — should follow these four steps. The framework is built from buyer psychology, not rep preference. Each step earns the right to the next.
1
Step One
Ask Permission

Goal: Create a micro-commitment before pitching. Asking permission shifts the dynamic — the prospect becomes an active participant rather than a passive recipient of a pitch.

Primary Script
"Is it okay if I take 30 seconds to explain why I'm reaching out?"
Alternative
"I know I'm reaching out cold — do you have 2 minutes for a quick context?"
What NOT to Do
"Hi [Name], I wanted to introduce myself and our product..." — This is inward-focused. It assumes the prospect cares about you before you've given them any reason to. Start with them, not you.

Psychology: Asking permission increases engagement vs. launching immediately into a pitch. The simple act of being asked triggers a reciprocity instinct — they said "yes," so they're more likely to listen genuinely.

Expected Response: ~60–70% will say "sure" or "go ahead." This is your opening. If they say no or hang up, that's useful qualification data — they are almost certainly not a buyer right now.

2
Step Two
Value Statement

Goal: Communicate specifically how you've helped companies like theirs — in one sentence. Specificity is everything. Generic value statements are the leading cause of ignored outreach.

Formula: "We help [specific ICP type] who [experience this specific problem] to [achieve this specific outcome]. [Social proof: company name or stat]."

Good Example
"We help mid-market AEC firms who spend 30+ hours per week managing project data to cut that time by 60% — firms like [similar company name] have reduced rework costs by 40%."
Bad Example
"We're a leading SaaS platform that helps companies optimize their workflows." — This says nothing about who you help, what specific problem you solve, or what outcome you deliver. Every competitor could say the same thing.

Persona-Specific: Your value statement must change based on who you're talking to. An economic buyer cares about ROI, headcount savings, and competitive advantage. An end user cares about daily friction reduction. A champion cares about making their team look good and advancing their career.

3
Step Three
Engage

Goal: Convert from monologue to dialogue. Ask ONE question — exactly one. The question must be directly tied to the pain you referenced in the value statement.

Primary Question
"Does managing [specific pain] sound familiar to your team?"
Alternative
"Are you currently using anything to solve this, or is it a manual process?"
What NOT to Do
Ask multiple questions at once. "Does this resonate? Are you dealing with this problem? Who on your team handles this? And what does your process look like?" — This kills momentum and overwhelms the prospect.

If they say "no, we don't have that problem": "That's great — is there a specific area where you're seeing the most manual work or friction right now?" This either re-engages them on a different pain, or qualifies them as Red (no pain = no urgency = no deal).

Silence is your friend. After asking the question, stop talking. The first person to speak after a question is asked loses negotiating position. Let the prospect fill the silence.

4
Step Four
Qualify

Goal: Confirm Green/Yellow/Red before proposing any next steps. You must confirm three things: (1) company meets ICP firmographic criteria, (2) pain is real and felt, (3) there is some form of urgency or reason to act now rather than later.

If Green
"Based on what you've shared, I think it's worth a 30-minute call. Are you open to next Tuesday or Thursday?"
If Yellow
"I want to make sure we'd be a fit for you — can I ask a couple of quick questions first?" Then probe on ICP criteria gaps before proposing a meeting.
If Red
"Based on what you've told me, I'm not sure we'd be the best fit for where you are today — I don't want to waste your time." Then disqualify cleanly.
Key Principle: Disqualifying a Red saves more time than closing them. A disqualified prospect today is a potential referral tomorrow. A closed Red customer is a churn event in 6 months and a drag on your entire CS team.
3. Prospecting Channels
Different channels reach different buyers in different contexts. A well-designed prospecting motion uses all three channels in coordinated sequence — not in isolation. Sequence design is covered in Section 4.
✉️
Cold Email
35–50% open rate (personalized) · 5–10% reply rate
Best Use Case
  • Initial outreach to mid-market ICP prospects
  • Follow-up sequences after LinkedIn connection
  • Breakup and re-engagement campaigns
Key Tactics
  • Subject: [Specific reference] + [question or intrigue] — e.g., "Your Q3 hiring plan → quick thought"
  • Body: 5 sentences max. Line 1: specific personalization. Line 2: value statement. Line 3: single question. Line 4: CTA.
  • Personalization: recent funding, new hire announcement, job posting, tech stack change, company news event
  • Sequence: 6 emails over 14 days (email 1: cold pitch, email 3: different angle, email 5: breakup)
Don'ts
  • Never open with "I hope this email finds you well"
  • Never attach a PDF or deck to a cold email
  • Never use "synergy," "best-in-class," or "revolutionary"
💼
LinkedIn
InMail: 15–25% response rate · Connection note: key to warm sequence
Best Use Case
  • Senior buyers who are active on LinkedIn
  • Warming a prospect before cold email
  • Building a relationship over time via content engagement
Key Tactics
  • Connection request: personalized note (never blank), 300-character limit
  • Note formula: [Why connecting] + [one-sentence value] + [no CTA yet]
  • After connection: wait 24–48 hours, then send first message
  • InMail (non-connection): higher open rate but limited — use for senior buyers only
  • Comment on prospect's posts before reaching out to warm the relationship
Don'ts
  • Never send a blank connection request to a cold prospect
  • Never pitch in the connection request note
  • Never send "LION" or mass connect messages
📞
Phone
8–15% connect rate · Best: Tuesday–Thursday, 8–10am & 3–5pm local
Best Use Case
  • Follow-up after email touch when not getting responses
  • Enterprise buyers who prefer direct communication
  • Time-sensitive sequences and closing sequences
Key Tactics
  • Open with value statement immediately — no "how are you doing?"
  • Voicemail: 20–30 seconds max, one question, email follow-up immediately after
  • Phone + email same day = highest connect rate of any channel combination
  • Reference prior touchpoints: "I sent you an email last Tuesday about..."
Don'ts
  • Never leave a voicemail longer than 30 seconds
  • Never ask "did you get my email?" — it sounds desperate
  • Never call without a voicemail-to-email follow-up plan
4. Sequence Design (Multi-Touch)
A single touch rarely converts. Research shows that it takes 6–10 touches for a qualified prospect to engage. The sequence below is designed for a 14-day window — long enough to demonstrate persistence, short enough to respect the prospect's time.
Day Touch Channel Action
Day 1 Touch 1 Email Initial outreach email with personalized value statement and single-question CTA
Day 2 Touch 2 LinkedIn Connection request with personalized note referencing the context from the email
Day 4 Touch 3 Email Follow-up email referencing LinkedIn connection request — different opening, same core value
Day 5 Touch 4 Phone Call + voicemail referencing email; immediate email follow-up after voicemail
Day 7 Touch 5 LinkedIn Message after connection accepted — pivot to value statement, light CTA
Day 9 Touch 6 Email New angle email — different pain point or social proof from a similar company
Day 12 Touch 7 Phone Second call + voicemail; reference previous touchpoints briefly
Day 14 Touch 8 Email Breakup email: "I'll stop reaching out after this — but wanted to share one last thing that might be relevant."
After Day 14: Archive the prospect and move them to a nurture cadence. Do not abandon them — circumstances change. A prospect who was Red six months ago may be Green after a company event, funding round, or leadership change. Schedule a 90-day re-engagement check.
5. Personalization at Scale
Personalization drives reply rates, but time is finite. The tiered approach below allows you to concentrate deep personalization on your highest-value targets while maintaining a systematic cadence across the broader prospect universe.
Tier 1
Top 50 Accounts
15–20 min / prospect
Full research: company news, LinkedIn activity, tech stack, hiring signals, executive quotes. Fully custom messaging per touch. No templates — every email and call script is written from scratch.
Tier 2
Next 200 Accounts
5–8 min / prospect
Semi-custom: use persona-level templates with 2–3 personalization variables inserted per email. Variables drawn from LinkedIn, funding data, and job postings. First line is always custom.
Tier 3
Broad Outreach
1–2 min / prospect
Template-driven with company name and industry customization only. Acceptable for high-volume demand gen. Lower expected reply rate but high-volume compensates. Validate ICP fit before sequence.

Personalization Variables

{{company_name}} {{recent_funding}} {{tech_stack}} {{job_posting}} {{news_event}} {{mutual_connection}} {{executive_quote}} {{competitor_used}}
  • LinkedIn Sales Navigator — live firmographic data, job change alerts, intent signals
  • Apollo.io — contact discovery, email sequencing, basic enrichment
  • ZoomInfo — enterprise-grade intent data and technographics
  • Clay — waterfall enrichment: tries 10+ data providers in sequence to find valid contact data
6. Prospecting Metrics & Benchmarks
Track these six metrics weekly per rep. Deviations from benchmark indicate specific process failures — the metric tells you where, not just that something is wrong.
Metric Definition Benchmark How to Track
Open Rate Emails opened / emails sent 35–50% Email sequencing tool (Outreach, Salesloft, Apollo)
Reply Rate Replies received / emails sent 5–10% Email sequencing tool
Connect Rate Phone conversations / total dials 8–15% CRM call log / dialer
Meeting Booked Rate Meetings booked / total touches delivered 2–5% CRM opportunity stage
SQLs per Rep per Month Meetings that pass SQL qualification review 4–10 (segment dependent) CRM SQL stage tracking
ICP Hit Rate % of booked meetings that are Green or Yellow ICP >80% CRM ICP qualification field (set at meeting disposition)
Diagnostic Guide: Low open rate → fix subject lines and sender reputation. Low reply rate but high open rate → fix body copy and value statement. Low meeting booked rate but high reply rate → fix the qualification/CTA step. Low ICP hit rate → fix targeting lists and ICP criteria used in list building.
