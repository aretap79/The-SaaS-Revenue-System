Why the Distribution Matters More Than the Average

The most common mistake in quota attainment analysis is reporting only the average: "Our team is at 82% of quota." That number tells you almost nothing about the health of your sales system. A team where 5 reps are at 150%+ and 10 reps are at 30% has an average attainment of ~82% — but the underlying dynamics are completely different from a team where every rep is clustered around 75-90%.

The distribution reveals: which reps need coaching, whether quota is set correctly, whether your ICP is accurate, whether your management is working, and whether your comp plan is driving the right behavior. It is an organizational X-ray.

The Rule: Don't Manage to the Average

CROs who manage only to team average miss the structural problems that the distribution exposes. A team average of 75% could be healthy (most reps at 70-80%, a few outliers) or catastrophic (half the team at 0-30%, kept afloat by 2-3 overachievers). The action plans are completely different. Always review the distribution before reporting the average.

What Different Distribution Shapes Mean

Bimodal Distribution — Two Distinct Groups
Large cluster at 0-30% AND large cluster at 120%+, with very few reps in the middle. Average looks reasonable but the team is polarized.
Diagnosis: Management problem, segmentation mismatch, or territory inequity. Some reps are set up to win; others structurally can't. Investigate: are top reps in better territories? Are all ramping reps counted in the distribution? Is there a comp plan element that's creating binary outcomes?
Left-Heavy (Bottom-Loaded) Distribution
Majority of reps below 75% attainment. Average attainment might be 55-60%. Very few reps at or above quota.
Diagnosis: Systemic problem — quota is too high, product-market fit has shifted, ICP has changed, or training/management has deteriorated. Not a performance management problem with individuals — the system is broken. Check: has anything changed in the market, product pricing, or competitive environment in the last 2 quarters?
Right-Heavy (Top-Loaded) Distribution
Majority of reps at 100%+ attainment. Average well above 100%. Almost everyone makes quota easily.
Diagnosis: Quota is too easy — either the market is very favorable, ICP is very tight (not enough accounts for reps to cover), or quota was set too conservatively. Risk: you are leaving revenue on the table. Consider raising quotas, expanding territories, or investigating whether reps are sandbagging forecast to protect attainment.
Flat / Uniform Distribution
Roughly equal proportions across all attainment bands. No clear clustering. Outcome looks random relative to quota.
Diagnosis: ICP is poorly defined — outcomes vary by luck of territory rather than execution. Alternatively, quota-setting methodology is inconsistent across the team, creating artificial variance. Run ICP consistency analysis: do high attainers share account characteristics?

The Quota Calibration Test

Is Your Quota Set Correctly? — The 5-Question Test
% at 100%+ attainment
Healthy: 40-60% of full-ramp reps at or above quota. Too low (<25%): Quota is too aggressive or reps are under-skilled. Too high (>75%): Quota is too easy — you're leaving revenue on the table and overpaying commission.
% at <50% attainment
Healthy: 10-15% of full-ramp reps consistently below 50%. Danger (>25%): Systemic failure — investigate market, product, quota, or training root causes immediately.
Tenure of top attainers
Healthy: Mix of tenures — some early-career reps in top quartile. Concern: If 90%+ of 125%+ attainers have 3+ years tenure, quota model may be inaccessible to newer reps — signals ramp problem or territory inequity.
Attainment variance Q-over-Q
Healthy: Distribution shape is relatively stable quarter over quarter. Concern: Wide quarter-over-quarter swings in distribution shape suggest lack of process repeatability — results are too dependent on timing or randomness.
Voluntary attrition at attainment bands
Healthy: Voluntary attrition concentrated in <75% attainment band. Warning: If high-attaining reps (>100%) are leaving voluntarily, something is broken in your culture, comp plan, or territory design — your best reps are leaving.

Identifying Sandbagging — The "Hostage" Rep Pattern

The Sandbagging Pattern

A rep who hits exactly 100-105% of quota in 4 consecutive quarters is statistically suspicious. Real deal flow has variance — some quarters are great, some are slow. A rep who is perfectly at quota every quarter is likely sandbagging: holding deals for the following quarter when they are already above quota, pulling in deals early when they are below, or submitting conservative forecasts to avoid pressure.

Why it matters: Sandbagging creates false pipeline information, causes forecasting errors, and means the rep is optimizing for their own comp (commission smoothing) rather than the company's revenue target. A rep at exactly 100% every quarter may be leaving 20-30% more revenue in the market by deliberately not pushing deals to close in their best quarters.

How to identify it: Run a "Q-over-Q variance" analysis on every rep's attainment. Reps with <10% variance across 4+ quarters warrant a deeper look. Review their deal timing — do deals consistently close in the first week of the quarter (pulled from previous quarter) or last week (held from current quarter)?

How to fix it: Accelerator above 100% that makes closing more, sooner, financially superior to smoothing. Remove comp plan design elements that penalize over-performance (quota resets that are too aggressive, club qualification that creates "enough" thresholds). See Compensation Design page.

Relationship Between Distribution and Comp Plan Design

The shape of your attainment distribution is partially a product of your comp plan design. Specific comp plan elements create specific distribution distortions:

  • Quota resets that are too steep (e.g., +20% each quarter regardless of market): Creates increasing left-heavy distribution over time as reps burn out chasing escalating numbers they can never sustainably hit.
  • Club / President's Club thresholds that create "enough" goals: Can create artificial ceiling at club qualification threshold (e.g., everyone at exactly 110% if club requires 110%). Investigate if your distribution has a suspicious cluster at exactly your club threshold.
  • Low or no accelerator above 100%: Reduces incentive to push for more after hitting quota. Creates behavioral "stop selling" effect. Compare monthly close rates for reps who hit quota early in the month vs. reps still below — the difference reveals accelerator effectiveness.
  • Draw/recovery plans for new reps: New reps who are advanced salary against future earnings often behave differently once they've repaid the draw — a discontinuity in attainment behavior at the draw payback date is a comp design signal.
