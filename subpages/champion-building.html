The Champion: Definition and Taxonomy

In enterprise sales, a champion is the single most important person in the buyer organization. Understanding the difference between a champion, a coach, an influencer, and a sponsor is the foundational distinction that separates enterprise sellers from mid-market sellers.

Definition: Champion

A champion is an individual inside the buyer organization who has organizational power, actively wants you to win, will fight internally for budget and resources on your behalf, has access to or influence over the economic buyer, and will share confidential inside information with you that helps you win. A champion is not someone who likes your product. A champion is someone who is invested in your success because your success is their success.

Role Definition What They Do For You What They Won't Do Deal Outcome Without Them
Champion Power + want + action combination Fight for budget, coach on politics, introduce EB, share competitive intel Nothing — they go all-in N/A — you need one to win
Coach Friendly contact who shares information Tell you who the players are, share process steps, be pleasant on calls Fight internally, spend political capital, introduce EB without approval Deal stalls or loses; coach is insufficient
Influencer Technical or functional evaluator with opinion but no power Evaluate product, share requirements, influence technical criterion Make budget decisions, override EB, approve spending Win technical eval, lose commercial deal
Economic Buyer (EB) Person with budget authority and final sign-off Approve spending, sign contract Often disengaged from eval; need champion to carry message Without champion access to EB, deal dies in committee
Sponsor Executive who supports the project in theory Lend credibility to the initiative, attend executive meeting Actively fight for you; often passive without personal stake Better than nothing; insufficient on its own
The #1 Champion Mistake

Treating a coach as a champion. Coaches are friendly, responsive, enthusiastic about your product. They schedule calls promptly and seem engaged. They will not spend political capital for you. They will not introduce you to the economic buyer without explicit permission from above. When the deal goes to committee, coaches go quiet. Champions go to war.

The 5 Tests of a True Champion

Never assume you have a champion. Test for it. A contact who fails any of these five tests is a coach, not a champion — and your deal strategy must account for that gap.

  1. Organizational Power The champion has genuine organizational authority — either positional (VP or above) or influence-based (a director who everyone listens to because they've been right before). Ask yourself: if this person stands up in an internal meeting and says "we're going with Vendor X," does that move the needle? If not, they don't have power.
  2. Personal Stake in Your Win The champion's career, credibility, or professional outcome improves when you win. This is the selfish motivation test. It might be: they proposed this initiative, so they need it to succeed. Or: the problem you solve is their biggest headache, so your product makes them a hero internally. Or: they're on a promotion track and this win is part of their Q3 proof points.
  3. Will Fight for Resources and Budget A true champion will go into budget meetings and argue for resources for your initiative. They will compete for budget with other priorities. They know the opportunity cost of not solving the problem and they will make that case internally. Test by asking: "If budget gets cut 20%, can you make the case to protect this project?"
  4. Access to the Economic Buyer The champion can get you a meeting with the economic buyer. Not "I'll try to set something up" but "yes, I can make that happen — let me talk to them first to prep them." Champions have either direct access (reporting relationship) or influence access (trusted advisor). If they can't or won't make the introduction, they are not a champion.
  5. Shares Inside Information Freely The champion tells you things they probably shouldn't: "Competitor A quoted us $X." "The decision committee is split." "My CEO has concerns about [specific issue]." "The real reason we need this is [confidential business driver]." This willingness to share inside information is the clearest signal of true championship — they are treating you as a strategic partner, not a vendor.

Identifying a Potential Champion Early in Discovery

Champion identification begins in the very first discovery call. You are evaluating every contact you meet against the five tests — not in a robotic checklist way, but through deliberate observation and targeted questions.

Champion Identification Signals in Discovery
  • They scheduled the meeting themselves (not an assistant or a coordinator). Signals ownership.
  • They asked questions before you did — they're already thinking about how this works for their specific situation.
  • They reference internal politics or budget dynamics unprompted — "we've been trying to get budget for this for 18 months" signals both pain and willingness to share inside information.
  • They mention a personal stake — "I proposed this initiative," "I've been tasked with solving this," "my team's performance metrics depend on this working."
  • They invite you to speak to others — "you should meet our CFO" or "let me introduce you to the team who would use this" signals organizational confidence and access.
  • They push back constructively — a contact who asks hard questions and challenges your claims is more likely to become a champion than one who nods along. Skeptical engagement signals serious consideration.
Champion Identification Discovery Questions
  • "How did this initiative come onto your radar — was this your idea or was it handed to you?" (Tests personal stake)
  • "What does success look like for you personally if this project goes well?" (Tests personal motivation)
  • "Who else in the organization would need to see this before a decision could be made?" (Tests organizational awareness and access)
  • "When you present a new initiative internally, how do you typically build support for it?" (Tests organizational influence)
  • "Are there other priorities competing for budget right now? How would you think about advocating for this?" (Tests willingness to fight for resources)

Champion Development Stages

Champions are not found — they are built. The development of a champion is a deliberate, staged process that moves a contact from passive interest to active advocacy.

Stage Contact Behavior Your Actions Signal to Advance Stage
1. Interested Contact Takes calls, attends demos, asks questions, shares basic information Deliver genuine value in every interaction; share relevant insights; make them look good internally Voluntarily shares internal information you didn't ask for
2. Engaged Advocate Actively promotes your solution internally; introduces you to others; shares competitive context Equip with internal selling tools; involve in content co-creation; reciprocate insider knowledge; quantify the ROI in their terms Takes a visible action that required spending internal political capital
3. Active Champion Fights for budget; introduces EB; coaches on deal strategy; shares competitive intel proactively; advocates in meetings where you're not present Deep partnership; co-develop business case; build mutual success plan; protect their reputation; ensure they get internal credit for the win Confirmed EB introduction + budget advocacy

How to Equip Your Champion for Internal Selling

Your champion sells for you when you're not in the room. Most deals are won or lost in internal conversations you will never witness. Your job is to make your champion the best possible internal advocate by giving them every tool they need to argue your case effectively.

The Champion's Internal Selling Toolkit
  1. The Business Case / ROI Model A one-page (or brief deck) that quantifies the financial return on investment in terms the CFO and CEO care about. Not your generic ROI calculator — built specifically for their numbers, their baseline, their industry metrics. The champion can hand this to any executive and it holds up independently.
  2. The Competitive Comparison (Their Format) A side-by-side comparison tailored to their stated decision criteria. Not a generic feature matrix — a comparison that maps directly to what their evaluation team cares about. Your champion should be able to answer every competitive question without going back to you.
  3. The Internal Presentation Deck A PowerPoint/slides deck your champion can present to their leadership without you on the call. Branded in their language, not your vendor language. Tells the story of the problem, the solution, the ROI, the risk of inaction, and the implementation plan. Never force your champion to build this themselves — provide it.
  4. The Reference Contacts Peer customers in their industry and role who have had success with your product and are willing to take a call. The champion can use these to handle the "but has anyone else in our space done this?" objection — the most common executive-level blocker.
  5. The Risk Mitigation Narrative A prepared answer to every concern the CFO, legal, or skeptical executive might raise: data security, implementation risk, vendor stability, contract flexibility, support model. Your champion should not be caught off guard by any objection you can predict.

The Multi-Champion Strategy in Enterprise Deals

A single champion creates a single point of failure. Enterprise deals at $150K+ ACV should have at least two champions in different functional areas of the buyer organization.

The Multi-Champion Principle

Enterprise deals involve multiple stakeholders with different agendas. A champion in IT does not protect you when the CFO raises pricing concerns. A champion in Finance does not protect you when the IT security team raises a compliance blocker. The goal is to have a champion with credibility in every functional area that participates in the final decision.

Multi-Champion Coverage Map
Functional Area Their Primary Concern Champion Profile to Seek Risk if Uncovered
Business / Line-of-Business Owner Solving the operational problem; meeting their KPIs VP or Director who proposed the initiative Loses the "why buy" narrative
Finance / CFO Office ROI, TCO, budget impact, vendor risk Finance business partner or CFO direct report Budget approved at business level, killed by finance
IT / Technical Security, integration, scalability, support model IT director or architect who was involved in evaluation Technical objection kills deal at contract stage
Legal / Procurement Contract terms, data privacy, liability GC or procurement manager who wants the deal done Contract negotiations stall or fail at 99-yard line

When Your Champion Goes Dark

At some point in every complex deal, your champion will stop responding to emails, cancel calls, and go silent. This is one of the most dangerous deal patterns in enterprise sales. It is also recoverable if you act quickly.

Why Champions Go Dark
  • Internal priority shift — their organization has a new crisis and this project is deprioritized
  • Budget freeze or reallocation — they have bad news to deliver and are avoiding the conversation
  • You lost the internal meeting — the deal is effectively dead but they haven't told you yet
  • Competitive shift — a competitor has made a strong move and your champion is reconsidering
  • Personal circumstance — the champion is dealing with a promotion, role change, or personal issue
Re-engagement Sequence (Champion Gone Dark)
  1. The Honest Email (Day 1 of silence threshold) "I want to be direct with you — I'm noticing we haven't connected in [X] days and I'm not sure where things stand. I'd rather know the truth, even if the news is that this project has stalled or changed. Can you give me 10 minutes this week for an honest update?" This email has a dramatically higher response rate than cheerful follow-ups because it lowers the social cost of delivering bad news.
  2. Escalation to EB (Day 5 if no response) If you have EB contact, send a brief executive note: "We've been making great progress with [champion]. I wanted to check in directly to ensure the initiative is still on track given [business context]. Happy to reconnect for 15 minutes." This surfaces the deal status through a different channel and signals to the champion that you are not going away.
  3. Find a Second Champion (Simultaneously) While re-engaging the dark champion, begin cultivating a second contact. Multi-threading is not betrayal of your champion — it is deal risk management that any sophisticated buyer expects from a serious vendor.
  4. Provide a New Reason to Re-engage Share a relevant piece of news: a new customer win in their industry, a product update that addresses their specific concern, a published ROI study from a peer company. Give them a substantive reason to open your email that is not just "checking in."

When Your Champion Leaves the Company

Champion departure is a high-frequency, high-severity deal risk in enterprise sales. The best reps build account maps that make champion departure a managed event, not an emergency.

Pre-Departure Prevention: Account Mapping

Long before your champion shows any sign of leaving, build a complete account map that identifies every key stakeholder in the buying committee, their relationship to your champion, and their position on your deal. The question to answer: "If my champion left tomorrow, who is the next most likely champion?" If you can't answer that, you have a single point of failure.

When Departure Is Confirmed
  1. Immediate: Ask for the Handoff Introduction Before your champion leaves, ask them to introduce you to their successor and co-author a transition briefing document that explains the project status, the business case, and their recommendation. A champion who genuinely cares about your success will do this.
  2. Week 1: Reintroduce Yourself to the EB Send a direct note to the economic buyer acknowledging the leadership transition and reconfirming the business case. Don't assume the new stakeholder has been briefed.
  3. Week 2: Begin New Champion Development Identify the person who inherited your champion's initiative. Start the champion development process from scratch — do not assume they adopt their predecessor's enthusiasm.
  4. Follow Your Departed Champion A rep who championed you at Company A and then moves to Company B is one of the most valuable pipeline sources available. They know your product, they trust you, and they bring your solution to their new employer. Stay in touch and bring them into your champion community.

The Champion Testing Framework

The only way to confirm a champion is to ask them to do something that requires spending internal political capital. If they do it, they're a champion. If they won't or can't, they're a coach.

The Champion Test Principle

A champion test is a request you make of a contact that requires them to invest their internal credibility on your behalf. It must be specific, real, and slightly uncomfortable to carry out — not a favor, but an advocacy act. If they do it willingly and capably, you have confirmation. If they make excuses or delay indefinitely, you have a coach.

Champion Test What It Tests Champion Response Coach Response
"Can you set up a 30-minute call between me and your CFO to walk through the ROI model?" Access to EB; willingness to sponsor introduction "Yes — let me prep the CFO first and get back to you by Thursday." "I'm not sure that's the right step yet. Let me think about it."
"Can you share the competitive scoring matrix from your evaluation committee?" Willingness to share inside information Shares the document or paraphrases the criteria in detail "I'd have to run that by legal" / Never follows up
"We need to move the deal to Q3 from Q2 — can you advocate internally for keeping it on the Q2 timeline?" Willingness to fight for the project's prioritization "I can make that case. Here's what I'll say to my VP..." "Timeline is really up to leadership, not much I can do."
"Can you arrange a site visit / reference call with one of your peers who used us before?" Champion uses social capital to validate your product Proactively arranges the reference, preps the referee on what to emphasize "I'll pass along the contact but I can't really set it up."
