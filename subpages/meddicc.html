History & Evolution: MEDDIC → MEDDICC → MEDDPICC

MEDDIC was born at PTC in the early 1990s when Dick Dunkel and Jack Napoli needed a systematic approach to control forecast accuracy in complex enterprise deals. PTC grew from $300M to $1B+ in large part by enforcing MEDDIC as a discipline across its sales organization.

The original acronym — Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Identify Pain, Champion — proved so effective that it spread through the enterprise SaaS world. Companies including Salesforce, Workday, and hundreds of venture-backed SaaS companies adopted it as the lingua franca of enterprise deal management.

MEDDICC added a second C — Competition — recognizing that in modern SaaS deals, how you position against alternatives (including the do-nothing option) is a fundamental qualification dimension. MEDDPICC adds P — Paper Process — explicitly calling out legal, procurement, and contract complexity as a qualification element in regulated industries and large enterprise deals.

Why Qualification Frameworks Exist

The average enterprise SaaS AE has 3–5x more pipeline than they can realistically close in a given quarter. Without a qualification framework, reps work everything equally, spreading effort across deals that will never close. MEDDICC forces a brutal prioritization of time, resource, and management attention on deals that are genuinely qualified.

Organizations that enforce MEDDICC discipline in deal reviews consistently report higher win rates and more predictable forecasts. The framework works because it makes invisible information visible — and invisible information is the primary cause of forecast misses.

Unqualified pipeline is the most dangerous object in a CRO's forecast. It creates false confidence, misallocates sales engineering and exec resources, and produces the two most damaging words in revenue leadership: "late-stage slip."

The Forecasting Lie

When reps forecast deals without fully qualified MEDDICC, they are forecasting their hope, not the deal's probability. Managers who accept incomplete MEDDICC in commit-stage deals are accepting lies in their forecast. The entire value of the framework is that it forces honesty about what is known and unknown.

The Seven Elements — Deep Dive

Each element is both a qualification gate and a coaching dimension. Unknown elements are not neutral — they are risks. The job of the rep and manager in a deal review is to name what is unknown and build a plan to learn it.

M
Metrics — Quantified Business Value
If you can't put a number on it, you don't have metrics. You have a story.

Metrics is not about your product's features. It is about what the customer's business will measurably look like before and after your solution. Without metrics, the deal has no business case, the Economic Buyer has no reason to approve budget, and you have no defense against "we'll revisit in Q3."

The Three Categories of Metrics

CategoryExamplesWho CaresHow to Quantify
Revenue Growth New pipeline generated, faster sales cycles, higher win rates, more cross-sell revenue CRO, CEO, Board Current baseline × improvement % × deal volume × ACV
Cost Reduction Headcount avoided, manual process hours eliminated, infrastructure savings, reduced churn cost CFO, COO, VP Ops FTE hours saved × fully loaded cost; license/infra cost eliminated
Risk Mitigation Compliance penalty avoided, security breach cost reduced, SLA breach exposure lowered CRO, CFO, Legal, CISO Probability × cost of event × coverage reduction factor

ROI Modeling — How to Build It With the Customer

The worst ROI models are built by the vendor in a vacuum and presented to the customer. The best ROI models are built collaboratively with the champion, using the customer's own data. A customer who co-builds the model owns the number — and will defend it to the Economic Buyer.

3-Year ROI Formula
ROI = ((3-Year Benefits − Total Cost of Ownership) / Total Cost of Ownership) × 100
Benefits = revenue impact + cost savings + risk-adjusted value. TCO = implementation + licenses + training + migration + ongoing admin.
Payback Period
Payback (months) = Total Investment / Monthly Benefit Run-Rate
Target: under 12 months for SMB/Mid-Market; under 18 months for Enterprise. Longer payback = harder to get EB approval without strong strategic justification.

How to Validate Metrics With the Economic Buyer

A champion can tell you the operational metrics. The Economic Buyer's validation is what transforms a number into a budget justification. Walk the EB through the model directly: "We worked with your team to calculate $2.4M in productivity gains over 3 years. Does that align with how you'd think about the value here?" If the EB pushes back on the model, that is critical discovery — not failure. You are learning what they actually care about.

The "We'll Figure Out ROI Later" Trap

This is the most common and most dangerous metrics failure mode. If the customer says they'll figure out ROI later, it means: (a) the champion doesn't know how to build a business case, (b) there isn't a compelling enough problem to justify the spend yet, or (c) they're collecting information and have no real budget authority. Push back: "We've found that deals move fastest when we align on value metrics early. Can we spend 30 minutes building a rough model together? If the numbers don't make sense, you'll know immediately."

E
Economic Buyer — The Person Who Signs
The Economic Buyer is the single individual who can say yes when everyone else says no — and no when everyone else says yes.

There is exactly one Economic Buyer in any deal. They control the budget. They have ultimate approval authority. They are not always the most senior person in the room, but they are the person whose signature releases the purchase order. Confusing the EB with a champion, influencer, or committee is a leading cause of late-stage deal failures.

EB vs. Champion vs. Influencer vs. User

RoleDefinitionTheir PowerYour Goal With Them
Economic Buyer Budget owner, final approval authority Can approve or kill the deal unilaterally Get direct access; validate metrics; remove deal blockers
Champion Internal advocate who wants the project to succeed Can influence; cannot approve budget Develop, equip with ROI story, test with the 5 champion tests
Influencer Technical evaluator, department head, compliance officer Can block (veto) but cannot approve Identify and address objections; do not treat as EB
User End user of the product Can create grass-roots support or resistance Build user advocates; do not conflate with EB

The Budget Holder Test

To test whether you've identified the real EB, ask your champion: "If you wanted to approve this purchase today, would you need anyone else's sign-off?" If yes — that other person is the EB. Keep going up the org chart until you find the person who answers no.

How to Gain EB Access When Blocked

Champions often resist introducing you to the EB because they fear losing control of the process. The frame that works: "Help me help you. The most common reason deals stall is that the EB hears about the project for the first time when they're asked to sign. A 30-minute intro now will make your internal process 10x smoother. I'll make you look good."

EB Access by Deal Stage

Discovery: Identify who the EB is, confirm via budget holder test. Qualification: Validate metrics with EB, confirm strategic priority. Proposal: Present business case to EB directly; never present only to champion. Negotiation: EB involvement is non-negotiable — procurement alone cannot commit without EB.

D
Decision Criteria — How They Will Choose
Formal criteria are written. Informal criteria are the ones that actually matter.

Decision Criteria are the explicit and implicit standards by which the customer will evaluate vendors. Understanding them is table stakes. Shaping them before they're locked is the real skill.

Formal vs. Informal Criteria

TypeWhat It Looks LikeYour Strategy
Formal Written RFP, scoring rubric, vendor questionnaire, procurement requirements Respond completely. Identify where you're strong and where you're weak. Get help from champion to understand weighting.
Informal Unstated preferences, cultural fit, prior vendor experience, champion's personal preferences, internal politics Surface via relationship depth. Ask champion: "What's really important to [EB name] that might not be in the RFP?"

The Criteria Influence Window

The window to influence decision criteria closes as the evaluation formalizes. Early in discovery (pre-RFP), you can shape what criteria matter and how they're weighted. Once an RFP is issued, the criteria are largely locked. This is why leading reps prioritize discovery conversations before the formal evaluation kicks off — they help customers build evaluation frameworks that align with where they're strongest.

Criteria Shaping Technique

During early discovery, introduce your differentiators as questions: "Have you thought about how you'd evaluate data portability? Some of our customers found that was a sleeper issue at implementation." You're not pitching — you're helping them think clearly. If they add it to the criteria, you've shaped the evaluation in your favor.

D
Decision Process — How the Decision Gets Made
Winning on merit is not enough. You must navigate the process.

Decision Process maps every step the customer must take from "vendor selected" to "contract signed." This includes not just business approval but legal review, security review, IT review, procurement, and executive sign-off. Missing any step will cause a surprise slip at end of quarter.

The Full Procurement Map

  1. Business ApprovalChampion's team agrees on vendor selection. Who specifically approves? Is it a committee or an individual?
  2. EB Sign-offEconomic Buyer approves the business case and authorizes budget. Have you presented directly to the EB?
  3. Security ReviewInfosec team reviews vendor questionnaire, SOC 2, penetration test results, data handling practices. How long does this take? Who owns it?
  4. IT/Infrastructure ReviewIT assesses integration requirements, SSO, data residency, and technical architecture. Involves your SE. Timeline: 2–4 weeks at enterprise.
  5. Legal ReviewLegal redlines your MSA/DPA. Average time: 3–6 weeks at mid-market; 6–12 weeks at enterprise. Ask: "Does your legal team have capacity now, or is there a queue?"
  6. Procurement / FinancePO issuance, budget code, vendor registration, payment terms negotiation. This step alone can add 2–4 weeks at large enterprises.
  7. Executive Sign-offAny deal above a threshold (often $50K–$100K) requires C-suite or VP-level approval. Map this in advance.

The Mutual Action Plan (MAP)

A MAP is a shared document between the vendor and the customer that maps every remaining step to a named owner and a specific date. It transforms the decision process from something the vendor observes to something both parties own together. See the full MAP section below for construction details.

Red Flag Signals in Decision Process

1. "We haven't done security review yet" at Stage 4 — adds 4–8 weeks minimum. 2. "Our legal team is really backed up right now" — typical 6–10 week slip. 3. "We'll need to register you as a new vendor" — procurement delay of 2–4 weeks. 4. Committee approval with no named decision-maker — process can loop indefinitely. 5. Champion unsure of EB's approval threshold — deal may require more levels of sign-off than expected.

I
Identify Pain — The Problem Worth Solving
No pain, no deal. The strength of a deal is proportional to the urgency of the pain it solves.

Pain is the business problem that is costing the customer something real — time, money, competitive position, or risk exposure. Pain is not the customer saying "we'd like to improve our reporting." Pain is "we lost three enterprise deals last quarter because we couldn't produce the compliance reports they required."

The SPIN Connection

Neil Rackham's SPIN Selling (Situation → Problem → Implication → Need-Payoff) is the discovery methodology that surfaces MEDDICC's "I" element. The implication questions are the most powerful: they force the customer to articulate what the problem is costing them in terms the organization will act on.

SPIN TypePurposeExample Question
SituationEstablish context and baseline"Walk me through how you're currently managing X today?"
ProblemSurface dissatisfaction with current state"Where do you feel the most friction in that process?"
ImplicationExpand the cost of the problem"What does it cost you when that breaks down? How does it affect the team downstream?"
Need-PayoffGet customer to articulate value of solution"If you could eliminate that bottleneck entirely, what would that mean for the business?"

Three Types of Pain

Pain TypeDefinitionWho Feels ItHow to Surface It
Organizational Business-level problem: lost revenue, excess cost, competitive disadvantage CxO, VP, Board Financial data, industry benchmarks, competitive intelligence
Individual Personal pain the champion feels: too much manual work, looks bad to leadership, job security Champion, Manager Relationship conversations; ask "what does this mean for you personally?"
Political Internal tensions between departments; someone is being blamed; turf wars Champions, managers, cross-functional stakeholders Ask about what happened in the past; who owns this problem; who is affected

The Pain Urgency Spectrum

Not all pain creates buying urgency. Pain that is old, tolerated, or unclear in cost rarely drives a procurement process. The pain that closes deals is recent, visible to leadership, getting worse, and has a dollar figure attached to it.

Pain Quantification Formula
Annual Cost of Pain = (# of affected FTEs × hours lost/week × 52 × fully loaded hourly rate) + (revenue impact from problem × probability)
Example: 20 ops analysts × 10 hours/week × 52 weeks × $75/hr = $780,000/year in productivity cost alone — before revenue impact.
When There Is No Pain

If a prospect cannot articulate a specific pain — a problem with real cost, urgency, and organizational visibility — there is no deal. "Nice to have" projects get killed in budget cuts. "We need to fix this or it will cost us X by Q3" projects get funded. Reps who continue working painless deals are wasting time that should go to qualified pipeline.

C
Champion — Your Internal Seller
A champion is not someone who likes you. A champion is someone who sells for you when you're not in the room.

The champion is the most misidentified element in MEDDICC. Reps routinely mistake their main contact, their most responsive contact, or the person who invited them in for the champion. These are not the same person. A true champion has specific attributes — and the absence of any one of them means you don't have a champion, you have a contact.

The 5 Tests of a True Champion

  1. Has Organizational PowerThe champion is respected internally. When they advocate for a project, people listen. They have a track record of getting things done. A junior analyst who loves your product is not a champion — they're a user advocate.
  2. Wants You to WinThe champion has a personal stake in your success. This is usually because your solution solves their pain (Test 3 of SPIN — individual pain). They want the project to happen, and they want you specifically to be the vendor.
  3. Will Fight for YouWhen the deal hits internal resistance — budget cuts, competing priorities, competitive pressure — the champion pushes back. Test this with light pressure: "There's a competing priority for that budget — what will you do if your CFO asks you to defer to Q2?" A true champion answers without hesitation.
  4. Has Access to the Economic BuyerThe champion must be able to get a meeting with the EB and present the business case. If your champion can't get EB time, you are locked out of the deal's critical approval path.
  5. Will Share Inside InformationThe champion tells you what's really happening: who the internal competition is, what the real budget is, what the EB actually cares about, where the risks are. If your contact only tells you what they'd tell any vendor, they're not a champion — they're a gatekeeper.

Champion vs. Coach

A coach is a friendly contact who gives you information but cannot or will not advocate. A champion advocates. Many deals have coaches and no champion — a genuinely dangerous position. The presence of a coach can mask the absence of a champion. Always test for Test 3 (will fight) and Test 5 (will share inside info) to distinguish the two.

How to Develop a Champion

Champions are not found — they are developed. The development process follows a progression: first build genuine trust and understand their personal pain (not just organizational pain), then co-build the business case so they own the numbers, then equip them with the internal presentation materials they need to advocate, then test their advocacy progressively with low-stakes asks before high-stakes asks.

When the Champion Goes Quiet

A champion who stops responding is a critical red flag. Possible causes: (1) Internal competitor killed the project. (2) Budget was cut. (3) The champion lost political power or left the company. (4) A competing vendor has a better champion. Do not wait — escalate to a secondary contact, ask a peer in a different function, or reach out directly to the EB with a value-forward message. Silence kills deals. The champion going quiet is almost never neutral.

Multiple Champions in Enterprise Deals

Enterprise deals typically involve stakeholders across 6–10 functions. Each key function should ideally have an internal advocate for your solution. Map champions across: the buying team (champion), IT (technical champion), Finance/CFO office (ROI champion), Security (compliance champion), and End Users (user champion). The more advocates you have across functions, the more resilient the deal is to internal disruption.

C
Competition — The Full Competitive Landscape
The most dangerous competitor is the one you're ignoring: the status quo.

Competition in MEDDICC covers three categories, and failing to qualify all three creates forecast blind spots.

TypeWhoHow They WinHow You Counter
Direct Named competitors in the same category Price, features, existing relationships, marketing dominance Win/loss data, battlecards, champion coaching, differentiated POC
Indirect Adjacent solutions or build-your-own alternatives "We can cobble this together with tools we already have" TCO analysis showing hidden costs; risk-adjusted build vs. buy calculation
Do-Nothing The status quo; no decision Inertia, risk aversion, budget pressure, changing priorities Cost of delay calculation; urgency creation via business event deadline

Competitive Intelligence Sources

  • Win/loss analysis: interview every recently lost deal (external win/loss firm or structured internal calls)
  • G2, TrustRadius, and Gartner Peer Insights: competitor reviews reveal real customer objections
  • LinkedIn job postings: what competitors are hiring for reveals product strategy
  • Your champion: ask directly "Who else are you evaluating? What's their pitch?"
  • SEC filings and earnings calls for public competitors: strategy shifts, customer wins, pricing changes
  • Former employees of competitors (ethically, via LinkedIn conversations and rep hires)

The "Why Us vs. Them" Framework

Every AE should be able to answer, in 90 seconds, why a specific customer should choose your product over Competitor X on the dimensions that customer has said they care about most. Generic "why us" decks don't work. Personalized positioning — "Given that you told us data security is your #1 criterion, here is how we compare on that dimension specifically" — does.

The Competitive Trap to Avoid

Never speak negatively about a competitor without evidence. Every unsubstantiated negative claim risks your credibility. Instead, use contrast questions: "Have you asked Competitor X how they handle [your key differentiator]?" Let the customer discover the gap themselves. When they find it, it's their insight — and it's much more powerful than your assertion.

Score Usage Rule

No deal should be in the commit forecast where the key elements — Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Process, Identify Pain, and Champion — are unknown or unvalidated. Significant gaps in any of these elements warrant a Best Case designation, not a Commit. Any deal in commit with no validated Champion or where the Economic Buyer has not yet been identified or engaged should be immediately moved to Best Case regardless of stage. Stage is a lagging indicator. MEDDICC qualification depth is the leading indicator of close probability.

The Mutual Action Plan (MAP)

The MAP is the operational expression of a qualified Decision Process. It is a shared, co-built document that maps every remaining milestone — with named owners on both sides — to specific dates. The word "mutual" is critical: a plan that only the vendor uses is a project tracker. A plan that both parties co-own is a commitment.

How to Build a MAP

  1. Anchor on the Target Close Date (TCD)Start from the desired go-live date or contract start date and work backwards. Ask: "When do you need this live? What's driving that date?" A business event (board meeting, compliance deadline, budget reset) gives urgency. "Someday" is not a TCD.
  2. Map All Required Steps to TCDInclude every step from current stage to contract signature: proof of concept, security review, legal, procurement, EB approval. Include your steps (solution design, proposals, SOW) and their steps (internal reviews, approvals).
  3. Assign Named Owners and DatesEach step has one named owner on their side and one on yours. Shared ownership means no one owns it. A date without an owner is not a commitment.
  4. Share and ConfirmSend the MAP to your champion and ask them to confirm the steps and dates are correct. "I want to make sure I haven't missed anything. Can you review this and tell me what we should add or adjust?" This act of sharing transfers co-ownership.
  5. Reference It in Every InteractionEvery customer call, email, and deal review references the MAP. If a step slips, you have a structured, non-adversarial way to discuss it: "According to our plan, the security review was supposed to start this week. What's the status?" You are managing to a shared commitment, not pressuring the prospect.
MAP Cycle Compression Effect
MAPs shorten close timelines by eliminating ambiguity around next steps
(widely reported by practitioners) Primary mechanism: MAP eliminates "waiting for the customer to remember the next step" time and makes slippage visible and discussable before it causes quarter-end surprises.

MEDDICC in Deal Reviews

The deal review is where MEDDICC earns its keep as a management tool. The structure forces the rep to surface what they know, what they don't know, and what they plan to do about the gaps — rather than telling a story that sounds good but hides critical unknowns.

Manager Coaching Philosophy in MEDDICC Reviews

The manager's job in a MEDDICC review is not to challenge the rep's optimism — it is to make the unknown unknowns visible. The most powerful coaching questions are not "are you sure?" but "what would have to be true for this to slip?" This reframe forces the rep to identify risks they've been unconsciously suppressing.

Leading & Lagging Indicators

Leading Indicators (Predictive)

Average MEDDICC score by stage — tracks qualification health of the pipeline as a whole. Should increase as deals advance.

% of Stage 3+ deals with validated champion — champion absence at Stage 3 is the leading indicator of late-stage slip.

% of deals with direct EB engagement — EB access rate predicts close rate more reliably than deal stage.

MAP completion rate — % of active deals with a co-signed MAP; predicts forecast accuracy.

Competitive coverage rate — % of deals where competitive position is known and documented.

Lagging Indicators (Outcome)

Win rate — ultimate MEDDICC output; track by segment, rep, and MEDDICC score tier.

Forecast accuracy — % variance between commit forecast and actual bookings; should be <10% with disciplined MEDDICC use.

Average sales cycle — should decrease as MAP adoption increases.

Average MEDDICC score of won vs. lost deals — benchmark to calibrate scoring standards and identify which elements most predict outcomes in your specific motion.

Slip rate — % of commit-stage deals that slip beyond TCD; direct measure of decision process qualification quality.
