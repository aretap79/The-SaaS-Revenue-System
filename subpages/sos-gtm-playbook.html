GTM Playbook Framework
The GTM Playbook is the codified, repeatable set of plays that allows any rep — not just your best rep — to consistently close ICP customers who succeed. It is built outside-in, from buyer behavior, not inside-out from rep behavior.

What is a GTM Playbook?
A documented, repeatable system that any trained rep can execute to generate qualified pipeline and close customers in your ICP. It is not a collection of decks, scripts, and email templates — it is an integrated system built on a single foundation: how your buyers actually make decisions.
Inside-Out (The Failure Mode)

Built around the founder's or top rep's personal style. "Here's how Sarah closes deals — everyone do what Sarah does." The playbook is a transcript of one person's instincts, not a system. It fails when Sarah isn't on the call — which is every call at scale.

Outside-In (The World-Class Approach)

Built from buyer behavior. Start with "What does a buyer who succeeds actually experience?" and work backwards to design every touchpoint. The product is the answer to a question the buyer is already asking. The playbook teaches reps to find that question first.

"World class selling is about buying first."

📊 Gong.io research: Top-performing reps (>120% quota) talk 46% / listen 54% in discovery. Bottom performers talk 72% / listen 28%. Outside-in playbook design is what trains the discipline to listen.

1
ICP & Buyer Persona Definition
The firmographic and behavioral profile that predicts customer success
ICP Template →

ICP (Ideal Customer Profile) is the firmographic profile of the company that succeeds: industry, company size, tech stack, business model, growth stage. It answers "which companies are the right fit at the company level?"

Buyer Persona is the individual buyer within that company: their role, title, pain points, goals, and the metrics they personally own. One ICP company typically has 2–4 buyer personas involved in the purchase process.

Green/Yellow/Red Qualification Framework
Green — Pursue Aggressively
Meets all ICP criteria. Invest full sales resources. Move through all playbook stages without hesitation.
Yellow — Qualify Through Discovery
Meets most criteria, 1–2 gaps. Run discovery to confirm the gaps don't disqualify. Invest cautiously until confirmed.
Red — Politely Disqualify
Missing core criteria. These customers won't achieve LIR. Closing them hurts LIUE and demoralizes CS. Walk away gracefully.
Time to Qualify

A trained rep should assign Green/Yellow/Red within the first 15–20 minutes of a discovery call. If qualification requires more time than this, the ICP framework is too complex or the rep hasn't been sufficiently trained on it. Simplify or re-train.

The ICP and persona definitions are validated by LIR data, not by hypothesis. After 6 months of GTM Fit phase execution, run the analysis: do Green-qualified customers achieve LIR at higher rates than Yellow? Do Yellow customers achieve LIR at higher rates than Red? If the differentiation doesn't appear in the LIR data, revise the framework.

2
Buyer Journey Map
The foundation everything else is built on — mapped from the buyer's perspective, not the seller's

The buyer journey is the sequence of experiences, questions, and decisions a buyer moves through — from first recognizing they have a problem to signing a contract and achieving success with the solution. The critical principle: map it from the buyer's perspective, not the seller's process.

Stage 1
Problem Awareness
Buyer recognizes they have a problem. Still unclear whether it's solvable. May not be actively researching solutions yet.
Stage 2
Solution Exploration
Buyer explores categories of solutions. Not yet comparing specific vendors. Asks peers, reads content, attends events.
Stage 3
Vendor Evaluation
Buyer narrows to 2–4 vendors. Requests demos, references, and pricing. Decision criteria are forming.
Stage 4
Decision & Negotiation
Buyer selects preferred vendor and negotiates terms. Internal approval processes engaged. Timeline urgency increases.
Stage 5
Implementation Planning
Buyer plans rollout. Success metrics defined. Stakeholders aligned. Timeline for LIR achievement established.
Swim Lanes: Why One Map Is Never Enough

The buyer journey for a 50-person company is completely different from a 500-person company. Their timelines differ, their approval processes differ, their success criteria differ, and their onboarding capacity differs. One playbook for both will underperform on both.

3
Prospecting Guide
A 4-step permission and qualification sequence for awareness-stage buyers
Template →

The prospecting guide defines how reps initiate contact with buyers who may not know your product exists. It is not a pitch sequence — it is designed to gain permission, deliver a relevant value statement, and determine if there's a genuine fit before investing further sales resources.

StepObjectiveWhat to Define
1. Ask Permission Establish rapport and respect the prospect's time before making any value claim "Is it okay if I take 30 seconds to tell you why I'm reaching out?" — a simple permission request that immediately differentiates you from a cold pitch
2. Value Statement Describe the problem you solve using the buyer's language — not product features Specific, persona-tailored value proposition. Reference a specific company context (funding event, recent hiring, tech stack trigger) to demonstrate relevance
3. Engage Create a two-way conversation by asking one targeted, open-ended question A single question that surfaces whether this prospect has the problem you solve. Not a qualification interrogation — one question that invites a real response
4. Qualify Determine if they meet Green/Yellow/Red ICP criteria before proposing next steps Apply the ICP framework quickly. If Green or Yellow, propose a specific next step with a clear date. If Red, disqualify gracefully and move on
Multi-Channel Sequence Design
  • Personalization: Reference specific company context — funding event, recent job posting, tech stack, recent news. Generic outreach is the fastest path to the delete folder
  • Multi-channel: LinkedIn connection request + email sequence + phone. A minimum of 3 distinct channels before drawing a conclusion
  • Sequence length: 6–8 touch sequence over 14 days before marking a prospect as unresponsive. 1 attempt yields ~40% contact rate; 6+ attempts yields ~90%
  • Timing: Tuesdays and Thursdays, 8–10am and 3–5pm local time to prospect, consistently outperform other windows in response rate data
  • Story progression: Each touch adds a new angle — insight, case study, trigger event, different medium. Sending the same email six times is not persistence, it is spam
Mistake: CTA Misaligned with Buyer Stage

Awareness-stage buyers have not yet decided they want to see a demo. Asking for a "30-minute product demo" as the CTA for a first outreach asks them to skip two journey stages. Awareness buyers respond to insight, peer stories, and education — not demo requests. Design your CTA to match where the buyer is, not where you want them to be.

4
Discovery Guide
Outside-in questions organized by buyer journey stage — not by product feature
Template →

Discovery is the most important component of the playbook. Its purpose is to locate the buyer on their journey and identify their specific pain before presenting anything. Reps who skip discovery and jump to demo are the ones with below-quota attainment and frustrated buyers.

📊 Gong.io: Reps who talk >60% in discovery calls close at less than half the rate of reps who talk <50%. Discovery is where deals are won or lost — not in the presentation.
The MEDDICC Discovery Framework
M
Metrics
What is the quantified impact of the problem? What is the cost of inaction? Force the buyer to articulate the economic magnitude of what they're solving.
E
Economic Buyer
Who owns the budget? Who has authority to sign? Selling to non-economic buyers is the most common reason deals die in late stages.
D
Decision Criteria
What are they evaluating? What does "good" look like to them? Map the explicit and implicit criteria so the presentation hits every one of them.
D
Decision Process
Who else is involved? What are the steps to a decision? What is the timeline? Map the entire buying committee and the sequence of approvals required.
I
Identify Pain
What is the specific pain point? How long have they had it? What have they tried? Pain that hasn't been solved by a prior solution has a reason — find it.
C
Champion
Is there an internal advocate who will fight for this solution when you're not in the room? Without a champion, you don't have a deal — you have an interest.
C
Competition
Who else are they evaluating? What do they like and dislike about alternatives? Understanding the competitive context shapes the entire presentation strategy.
✓
Discovery Close
"Based on what you've shared, here's what I'm hearing..." — always summarize the buyer's pain back to them before ending discovery. This confirms understanding and demonstrates you listened.
Discovery Best Practices
  • Use open-ended questions — "What," "How," and "Tell me more about" open thinking. "Do you" and "Are you" close it
  • Take notes visibly — signals you're listening and creates a paper trail for the presentation
  • Never pitch during discovery — stay in question mode. If you pitch during discovery, you've ended the discovery
  • Follow the MEDDICC sequence as a mental framework, not a script — move between elements based on the conversation
  • End with a recap: "Based on what you've shared, here's what I'm hearing..." — then summarize their pain before suggesting a next step
5
Presentation / Demo Guide (3–5 Swim Lanes)
A structured flow built on discovery context — not a one-size-fits-all product demo
Template →

The presentation is where discovery context is converted into a tailored business case. The worst presentations replay the same demo regardless of what was learned in discovery. The best ones feel like they were built specifically for that buyer — because they were, from one of 3–5 pre-built swim lanes selected based on what discovery revealed.

StepObjectiveWhat to Say / Do
1. Agenda-Setting Set expectations and secure agreement on the structure of the call "Today I'm going to show you how we can help you [goal discovered in discovery] so we can decide together if this is a fit and discuss next steps. Does that work?"
2. Discovery Recap Demonstrate you listened; confirm context before showing anything "Last time you told us [pain point X, context Y, timeline Z]. Is that still accurate? Has anything changed?" — this is not a formality, it's the most important 2 minutes of the call
3. Executive Summary Set up the three things you're going to show and why they matter to this buyer "Today I'm going to show you three ways we can [benefit]. First [feature 1 tied to primary pain], then [feature 2], then [feature 3]."
4. Feature 1 (Primary Pain) Show only what's relevant to their discovered primary pain; confirm agreement Current process → Show feature and how it solves their specific pain → "Does this address the problem you described? Does this make sense?" Get explicit agreement before moving on.
5. Features 2 & 3 (Supporting Value) Additional value proof tied to secondary pain points from discovery Same structure as Feature 1. Never show features that weren't surfaced in discovery — every feature you show that isn't tied to a discovered pain weakens the business case.
6. Recap & Close Confirm buy-in, ask for the business, define a specific next step with a date "What we showed you was X, Y, Z. Does this address the problems you described? What are the next steps?" — then stop talking. Wait for the response.

Film Review After Every Presentation

After every recorded presentation, review against three questions: (1) Did the rep recap discovery before showing anything? (2) Did they show only features tied to discovered pain points? (3) Did they close clearly with a specific next step? If any answer is no, that rep has a specific coaching need — addressed in the Film Reviews section below.

6
CS Onboarding Guide (LIR-Focused)
The bridge between "customer signed" and "customer achieves LIR"
Template →

The CS guide is not a generic onboarding checklist. It is the system for converting a signed contract into a customer who achieves LIR — the event that triggers both long-term retention and the second half of the sales rep's commission. Every element of the CS onboarding process is designed to reach that milestone.

The Most Common CS Mistake

Treating every new customer identically. The sales process gathered buyer context, documented pain points, and tailored a solution across 3–5 swim lanes. Then the CS handoff discards all of that with a generic onboarding template. The customer feels the disconnect at the exact moment they are most sensitive to whether they made the right decision. The CS swim lanes prevent this.

LIR Achievement Program Structure
PhaseTimingCS ActionLIR Connection
Kickoff Day 0–3 Kickoff meeting; set LIR as explicit success metric; intro to CSM; review implementation plan Customer formally acknowledges LIR as the definition of success. This is not optional — it must be said explicitly in the kickoff call.
Onboarding Phase Day 4 – T/2 Guided setup, training, first value delivery. Follow the CS swim lane matching the sales presentation used Progress toward LIR tracked weekly. Customer is moving through the milestones defined in the LIR framework.
LIR Achievement Check Day T/2 Formal mid-point check: is the customer on pace? If behind, trigger escalation intervention immediately Midpoint intervention is the highest-leverage moment. A customer who is behind at T/2 has a statistically low probability of achieving LIR at T without intervention.
LIR Completion Day T Confirm LIR achieved. Document it. Release second half of sales rep commission. LIR achieved = PMF confirmed for this customer. Renewal probability increases dramatically from this point.
Expansion Conversation Day T+30 Now that value is proven, explore: where else in the organization can we expand? What's the next use case? Expansion is only productive after LIR is achieved. Expansion conversations before LIR dilute focus and risk the core relationship.
3–5 CS Swim Lanes

Each CS swim lane mirrors the corresponding sales presentation swim lane. The onboarding path, milestone timeline, and intervention triggers are all adapted to the specific use case, company size, or competitive scenario that defined the sales swim lane used.

Film Reviews: The Continuous Improvement Engine
A playbook on paper is a document. A playbook lived through film reviews is a culture. Film reviews are the mechanism by which the playbook stays current, reps develop skill, and the team builds a shared vocabulary for great selling.

Record every call. Review calls together. Update the playbook based on what you learn. The cadence and structure of film reviews is not optional — without it, the playbook decays as market conditions change and rep turnover introduces new habits.

1
Record all calls using Gong, Chorus, or equivalent. Every rep, every stage — from prospecting through close. Not selectively.
2
Daily review (GTM Fit phase) / Weekly review (Growth & Moat phase). The cadence is the governance. Without it, the playbook decays between updates.
3
Structured review questions: What worked? What didn't? What objections came up? What questions generated the best buyer responses? What would you do differently?
4
Update the playbook in real-time. Every film review session produces at least one candidate update. Draft it. Test it with two reps. If it produces better results, publish it.
5
Share winning recordings across the full team. Identify the 1–2 calls each month that best exemplify the playbook in action. Label them "Playbook of the Week." Make them required listening for new hires.
6
Identify rep-level coaching needs. Film reviews surface patterns. A rep who consistently fails to recap discovery before presenting has a specific, addressable coaching need — not a general performance problem.
Film Review Cadence by Stage
GTM Fit Phase
→
Daily, 30-minute team session reviewing one call together. The playbook is being built in real time — every call is a data point.
Growth & Moat Phase

Playbook Governance
A great playbook is never finished. The market changes. Buyer behavior evolves. New competitive pressures emerge. Governance ensures the playbook reflects reality rather than history.
1
Single Source of Truth
One document, version-controlled. Not ten Slack messages, three Google Docs, and a deck from last quarter. Every rep knows exactly where the playbook lives — and that version is always current.
2
Update Process
New insights from Film Reviews → Draft update → Test with 2 reps → Validate results over 2 weeks → Publish. Do not update the playbook during a call. The update process protects playbook integrity.
3
Quarterly Audit
Full playbook audit against current win/loss data. Are the ICP criteria still producing Green customers who achieve LIR? Are the swim lanes still matching the most common buyer scenarios? Are the discovery questions surfacing what they were designed to surface?
4
Ownership Structure
RevOps maintains the document and version history. Sales Leader approves all changes. CS inputs on onboarding sections. Product provides monthly update on new features and competitive shifts. Marketing contributes on messaging evolution.
5
New Hire Certification
Every new rep must pass a playbook certification before their first live discovery call. The certification includes a discovery call roleplay scored against the MEDDICC framework, a presentation roleplay against the swim lane that matches their assigned territory, and a written ICP qualification exercise.
