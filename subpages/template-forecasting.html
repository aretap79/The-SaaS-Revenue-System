Demand-Driven Forecasting Model

Replace subjective rep-by-rep estimates with a pipeline-based model that calculates expected revenue by segment, source, and time-in-pipeline — then tracks forecast accuracy to improve over time.

Section 1 — What is Demand-Driven Forecasting?

The fundamental problem with traditional sales forecasting is that it relies on human judgment — which is optimistic by nature. Demand-driven forecasting replaces judgment with pipeline math.

Traditional ("Gut") Forecasting
Rep-by-rep estimates
  • Each rep submits their "commit" for the month
  • Manager adds their own judgment on top
  • Systematically optimistic — reps and managers both have incentives to show confidence
  • Provides no insight into which part of the funnel is responsible for variance
  • Cannot be improved systematically — no model to calibrate
Demand-Driven Forecasting
Pipeline math by segment and source
  • Calculate expected revenue from pipeline using historical close rates by age
  • Break down by segment (SMB/MM/Ent) and source (inbound/outbound/AE)
  • Objective — not subject to individual rep optimism
  • Reveals exactly which segments or sources are under/over-forecasting
  • Improves over time as close rate data accumulates

Section 2 — The Model Structure

Build the model segment by segment (SMB, Mid-Market, Enterprise) and source by source (MQL from inbound, SDR-sourced, AE-sourced). Each combination has its own close rate profile.

Inputs per Segment × Source
  • Volume of open opportunities in pipeline
  • Age of each opportunity (days in pipeline)
  • Close rate by age band (<1 month, 1-2 months, 2-3 months, etc.)
  • ARR per customer for this segment
  • Source (MQL inbound / SDR-sourced / AE-sourced)
Outputs
  • Expected customers closing this month by segment
  • Expected ARR by segment and source
  • Total forecast revenue for the month
  • Pipeline coverage needed to hit target
  • Variance analysis vs. prior forecast

Why Close Rates Vary by Source

AE-sourced pipeline closes at higher rates because the AE has direct context on the buyer and self-selects to create opportunities where they have a genuine relationship. SDR appointments are pre-qualified but early. MQL inbound has the widest variance — some highly qualified, some exploratory.

Section 3 — Running the Forecast

At the beginning of each month, run the forecast using current pipeline data. The four-step process takes 30–60 minutes in a spreadsheet or CRM-connected model.

1
Pull open pipeline by segment and source
Export all open opportunities. Tag each by segment (SMB / Mid-Market / Enterprise) and source (MQL / SDR appointment / AE-sourced). Calculate the age of each opportunity in days from creation date.
2
Apply close rates by age band
Multiply the count of opportunities in each age band × the historical close rate for that band, segment, and source. This gives expected customers closing this month from each pipeline bucket.
3
Multiply by ARR per customer
Expected customers × average ARR per customer for that segment = expected revenue from each bucket. Sum across all buckets to produce the total forecast.
4
Compare to quota and calculate pipeline coverage needed
If total forecast is below quota, calculate how much additional pipeline you need to create to close the gap — at the average close rate for that segment and source. This is your demand gen gap for the month.

Section 4 — Improving Forecast Accuracy Over Time

The model only becomes valuable when you close the feedback loop. Track forecast vs. actual every month and update close rates based on observed performance.

1
Record
At start of month, record the model forecast for each segment × source bucket
2
Track Actual
At end of month, record actual closed customers and revenue by bucket
3
Variance
Calculate (Actual − Forecast) ÷ Forecast. Target: ±10% total forecast accuracy
4
Diagnose
Identify which buckets consistently over or under-forecast and why
5
Update
Update close rate assumptions in the model. Most significant updates in first 6 months
Target Accuracy

A well-calibrated demand-driven model should achieve ±10% total forecast accuracy within 3–4 months of consistent use. Segment-level accuracy will vary — manage this variation at the model level, not by reverting to gut forecasting.

Common Forecast Accuracy Issues

Issue Symptom Root Cause Fix
SDR-sourced pipeline overestimates SDR bucket consistently 40%+ above forecast Reps inflate pipeline stage to show activity metrics. Opportunities are not as qualified as the stage implies. Add a stage gate that requires a demo completed or a specific qualification criterion. Re-calibrate the close rate down for this bucket.
Recent-quarter opportunities over-count New pipeline created in current month rarely closes that same month Sales cycle is longer than assumed. Opportunities in the <30-day bucket are truly exploratory — close rate for this band should be near zero. Reduce close rates for <30-day bucket to near zero. Add a minimum qualification threshold before an opportunity enters the forecast model.
Enterprise pipeline is unreliable High variance in enterprise forecast — sometimes 3× the forecast, sometimes 0 Enterprise deal count is small (single digits) so each deal is a large % of the total. Close rates are meaningless with small sample sizes. For enterprise deals, use deal-specific probability estimates from the AE rather than statistical close rates. Treat enterprise as a list, not a funnel.
AE-sourced pipeline is most accurate AE-sourced actual within ±5% of forecast consistently AEs have direct relationship context and self-select to create opportunities where they have genuine close probability. This is expected and healthy. No fix needed. Use this as a benchmark for calibrating other sources. Consider increasing the AE-sourced pipeline % of total.
