1. The Purpose of the Presentation/Demo
The demo is not a product tour. It is the proof that you can solve the specific problems the buyer told you they have. Discovery earned you the right to present. The presentation earns you the right to next steps — and eventually, the contract.
  • Not a product tour — a proof that you can solve their specific problem, framed in their language
  • The discovery call earned you the right to present; the presentation earns you the right to next steps
  • Only show features directly tied to pains discovered in the discovery call — everything else is noise
  • The buyer should feel, after a great demo: "They clearly understand my problem better than I do — and they have a solution"
Fatal Error: Feature Dumping
"Let me show you everything we can do" → The buyer gets confused, sees 15 features they don't need, focuses on what's missing from their specific use case, and leaves the demo with less confidence than they arrived with. Every feature you show that doesn't address a discovered pain is a liability, not an asset.
The Core Principle: A demo following poor discovery is a random walk through your product. A demo following excellent discovery is a highly targeted answer to a question the buyer already knows they need answered. Always run discovery before every demo — no exceptions.
2. The 6-Step Presentation Framework
This structure works for every demo — 45 minutes to 90 minutes, SMB to enterprise. The steps are not optional. Skipping any one of them degrades conversion. The order matters: you are building a logical arc that the buyer follows to a natural "yes."
1
Step One
Agenda Setting

Goal: Set expectations and create a micro-commitment before you begin. An agreed agenda eliminates surprises and signals that you are organized and respectful of their time.

Opening Script
"Today I want to: (1) confirm I understood your situation correctly from our last conversation, (2) show you three specific areas where I think we can help, and (3) get your completely honest reaction — including what doesn't fit. Does that work?"

Why it works: "Get your honest reaction" signals you're not there to pitch — you're there to collaborate. This drops buyer defenses. The micro-commitment ("does that work?") also makes the buyer an active participant from the first moment.

2
Step Two
Discovery Recap

Goal: Spend 3–5 minutes summarizing what you heard in discovery — in the buyer's words, not product language. This is the single highest-ROI step in the entire presentation.

Discovery Recap Script
"Last time, you told me that [pain 1 in their exact words], and that [pain 2] is costing you [quantified impact they gave you]. And you mentioned that the biggest priority for you heading into Q[X] is [primary need]. Does that still reflect where things stand?"

Why it works: Confirms you listened, demonstrates you took notes, allows them to correct misunderstandings before you pitch, and builds the trust that makes every subsequent product claim more credible.

If they correct you: Write it down, thank them, and adapt your demo sequence in real-time. Do not proceed with the original plan if the priority has changed — nothing destroys a demo faster than showing a solution to a problem the buyer no longer has.

3
Step Three
Executive Summary

Goal: Before opening the product, give a 60-second roadmap of exactly what you'll show and why. This respects the buyer's time and sets up a mental framework they can follow.

Executive Summary Script
"Based on what you shared, I'm going to show you three specific things today: [Area 1] to address [Pain 1 in their words], [Area 2] to address [Pain 2], and [Area 3] to address [Pain 3]. I'm going to skip everything else — we can always come back to other features if you want. But today I want to stay completely focused on what's most relevant for your team."

Why it works: This signals respect for their time and keeps the entire demo relevant. It also primes the buyer to evaluate the three specific areas you've chosen — which you've pre-aligned to their stated priorities.

4
Step Four
Feature 1 — Primary Pain

Goal: Show the feature that most directly solves their primary, highest-urgency pain. Use a story format — not a walkthrough format.

Story Format for Every Feature
Step 1 — Their reality: "You told me that [pain]. Most [ICP type] we work with experience this because [root cause]."
Step 2 — The product: "Here's exactly what you would do in [product name]: [show it live, not as a slideshow]."
Step 3 — The outcome: "The result: [specific, quantified outcome]. [Customer name] reduced [metric] by [X%] in [timeframe]."

After every feature, ask: "Does this address the challenge you described to me? Any questions before we move to the next area?" Never advance to Feature 2 if they still have questions about Feature 1 — unresolved questions become unspoken objections.

5
Step Five
Features 2 & 3 — Supporting Value

Goal: Show 2 additional pain points from discovery using the same story format. Quality over quantity — maximum 3 main features per 45–60 minute demo. Every additional feature shown beyond 3 dilutes the impact of the first three.

  • Use the same story format: their reality → product action → quantified outcome
  • Save a "wow moment" for Feature 2 or 3 — something unexpected but directly relevant to their stated goals
  • The wow moment should make them think: "I didn't know it could do that — and I need that"
  • After each feature: check in before moving forward — never leave an open question behind you
The 3-Feature Rule: For every additional feature you show beyond 3, you add cognitive load and reduce the buyer's confidence that they understand what they're buying. A buyer who is confused cannot say yes.
6
Step Six
Recap & Close

Goal: Summarize, confirm fit, and advance to a specific next step. A demo without a definitive close is a performance — not a sales motion.

Closing Summary
"Let me summarize what we showed you today: [Feature 1] addresses [Pain 1 in their language], [Feature 2] addresses [Pain 2], and [Feature 3] addresses [Pain 3]. Based on everything we've covered — does this solve the problems you described to me?"
Discovery Close
"Based on what we've seen — what does your internal process look like from here to move forward?" (This surfaces the decision process and economic buyer without asking directly.)

Do not leave without: A specific next step with a confirmed date, time, and named attendees. "I'll follow up" is not a next step. "Can we schedule 30 minutes next Thursday at 2pm to include [Economic Buyer name] and discuss next steps?" is a next step.

3. Swim Lane Design
A swim lane is a pre-built version of the presentation customized for a specific ICP segment, use case, or buyer situation. Swim lanes eliminate the improvisation that leads to feature-dumping and ensure every rep tells the same best-in-class story.
Swim Lane 1
Mid-Market, Primary Use Case
Features: A, B, C
  • Buyer journey: evaluation stage, 1–2 stakeholders
  • Primary pain: operational efficiency, manual process
  • Demo emphasis: ease of implementation, time-to-value
  • Social proof: similar mid-market customer names and results
  • Close path: champion → economic buyer approval in 2–3 weeks
Swim Lane 2
Enterprise, Compliance-Focused
Features: D, E, F
  • Buyer journey: procurement + security review, 4–6 stakeholders
  • Primary pain: risk, compliance, auditability
  • Demo emphasis: security architecture, permission controls, audit logs
  • Social proof: enterprise customer logos and compliance credentials
  • Close path: security review → legal → executive approval in 6–12 weeks
Swim Lane 3
Competitive Displacement
Modified features + differentiation framing
  • Buyer journey: switching from [Competitor], switching cost concern
  • Primary pain: gaps in current solution, migration risk
  • Demo emphasis: migration path, feature comparison, switching cost ROI
  • Social proof: customers who switched from [Competitor] and their results
  • Objection handling: 2–3 specific slides addressing common competitive objections

How to Build Swim Lanes

  • Analyze your top 20 closed-won deals — which features were cited as most important in the post-sale survey or call recording? Look for patterns, not individual preferences.
  • Group by segment and use case — do clusters emerge around company size, vertical, or primary pain? Each cluster is a candidate swim lane.
  • Build a base demo script for each cluster — opening narrative, 3 core features with story format, specific social proof for that segment, close language.
  • Test with 2–3 reps over 4–6 demos each — collect Film Review scores and conversion data.
  • Iterate based on Film Review data — which features drove the most positive buyer reactions? Which objections appeared repeatedly in that swim lane?
4. Handling Demo Objections
Objections during a demo are opportunities, not threats. They signal the buyer is engaged and evaluating. The goal is not to eliminate objections but to handle them in a way that deepens trust and moves the conversation forward.
Objection Poor Response Strong Response
"We'd need to see [feature you don't have]" Defensive: "We have something similar..." — vague and unconvincing. Signals you know it's a gap. "Tell me more — what would that feature need to do specifically? I want to understand whether what we have addresses the need underneath that request."
"This looks complex to implement" Dismissive: "Actually, it's really easy" — invalidates their concern and destroys trust. "That's a fair concern to raise. Customers similar to yours typically go live in 6 weeks. Want me to walk you through exactly what that onboarding looks like?"
"We're looking at [Competitor]" Competitive: "We're much better than them" — triggers defensiveness. Never badmouth. "What do you like about [Competitor]? I want to make sure you have full information to make the comparison — I can speak to specifically where we're different."
"The price seems high" Anxious: "I can probably do a discount" — erodes your value and trains the buyer to extract concessions. "Can you tell me more about what you're comparing it to? I want to understand what the right value frame is — the ROI conversation looks very different depending on the comparison point."
"We don't have budget right now" Eager: "We have flexible payment terms" — signals desperation and skips the real diagnosis. "Is budget something that opens up at a specific time — like a new quarter or fiscal year reset? Or is this more of a priority question? I want to understand whether timing or priority is the real barrier."
"We need to involve [other person/team]" Passive: "Sure, have them reach out to me" — you lose control of the process entirely. "Absolutely — that makes sense. Can we set up a 30-minute call that includes [other person]? I'd like to make sure they have the same context we've built together. What's the best way to get time on their calendar?"
Universal Objection Principle: Acknowledge before answering. Never jump straight to the response — always validate the concern first. "That's a completely fair question / concern" before every response. Buyers who feel heard are dramatically more receptive to the answer that follows.
5. Film Review Protocol
Film reviews are the highest-ROI coaching tool in a sales organization. A 30-minute weekly session with call recordings improves demo quality faster than any training program. The goal is not to critique — it is to identify and replicate what works.
  • Record every demo — Gong, Chorus, Clari Copilot, or Zoom built-in recording. Non-negotiable.
  • Weekly 30-minute session: manager + team reviews one full recording together
  • Rotate recordings: the same rep should present their own call at least once per month
  • Share top recordings as "Gold Standard" examples — positive reinforcement drives behavior change faster than critique
  • Never review a recording to punish performance — always to understand what works and replicate it at scale

Film Review Scoring Rubric (Score 1–5 on Each Dimension)

  • Discovery Recap Present Did the rep spend at least 3 minutes summarizing what was heard in discovery — in the buyer's words? Did the buyer confirm or correct before the demo began?
  • Agenda Was Set Was an agenda explicitly stated at the start? Did the rep get agreement ("does that work?") before proceeding? Was the agenda actually followed?
  • Only Relevant Features Shown Were all features shown directly tied to pains from the discovery call? Were any features shown that weren't tied to a stated buyer need? Count the features shown — was it more than 3?
  • Objections Handled Well Did the rep acknowledge before answering? Was the response specific and evidence-based? Did the rep recover control of the conversation after each objection?
  • Clear Close With Next Steps Did the rep use a discovery close to surface the decision process? Was a specific next step confirmed — date, time, named attendees? Was the next step confirmed before the call ended?
Gold Standard Benchmark: A demo that scores 4–5 on all five dimensions should be shared immediately with the entire team as a model. Index your coaching on replicating top performance — not managing up from the bottom.
6. Presentation Metrics
These five metrics tell you whether your demo motion is working. Track them at the rep level and the team level. Divergences between reps on the same metrics reveal coaching opportunities and process gaps.
Metric Definition Target How to Track
Demo-to-Opportunity Rate % of demos that advance to formal opportunity stage in CRM >60% CRM stage transition report
Demo-to-Close Rate % of all demos that result in a closed-won deal (all-in) 20–35% CRM closed-won / total demos in cohort
Average Demos to Close Average number of demos per closed-won deal <3 CRM demo count field per deal
Discovery Recap Usage % of demo calls that include a documented discovery recap in the first 5 minutes 100% Film review scoring, Gong call analysis
Objection Handle Rate % of recorded objections that are successfully navigated (no stall or topic drop) >70% Film review scoring, weekly session
