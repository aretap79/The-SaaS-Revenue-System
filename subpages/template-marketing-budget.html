Budget Planning Philosophy

Most marketing budgets are built top-down — "allocate X% of revenue to marketing." This approach is backward. A budget built from pipeline targets tells you exactly how much you need to spend, and which channels to spend it on, to hit the revenue number.

The Right Framing

"Marketing budget exists to generate SQLs at a LIUE-positive cost." Everything else — brand awareness, events for the sake of events, content with no distribution plan — is waste until the core demand gen engine is proven. Build the bottom-up model first. Add brand and awareness spend only after the SQL-generation engine is healthy.

The Bottom-Up Process

  1. Set the revenue targetThis comes from the board/CEO annual plan. Break it into new ARR (from new logos) and expansion ARR (from existing customers). The marketing budget covers new ARR — expansion is driven by CS.
  2. Calculate customers neededNew ARR ÷ Average ACV = new customers needed. Use your actual ACV from the last 12 months, not an aspirational number. If ACV is shifting, model both scenarios.
  3. Calculate SQLs neededNew customers needed ÷ SQL-to-customer rate = total SQLs needed. SQL-to-customer rate is your historical close rate — do not assume it will improve unless you have a specific plan to improve it.
  4. Allocate SQLs to marketing vs. salesDetermine what % of SQLs marketing will source vs. sales (outbound, partnership). A typical Stage 2 split is 50-60% marketing, 40-50% sales. Stage 3: marketing sources more as channels scale.
  5. Calculate budget by channelFor each channel, multiply marketing-sourced SQLs by the cost per SQL for that channel. Sum to get total budget required. This is your budget request — not a % of revenue, a specific amount tied to a specific pipeline target.

The Bottom-Up Budget Model

Walk through this model with your actual numbers before the budgeting cycle. The output is a specific budget request with a clear pipeline commitment — not a spend allocation without accountability.

Budget Request Framing

Present this model to your board as: "We need $128,000 to generate 112 marketing-sourced SQLs. At our current SQL-to-customer rate of 30%, that produces 34 new customers, generating $1.2M of the $2M ARR target. Sales outbound covers the remaining 74 SQLs."

This framing is accountable and fundable. The alternative ("we need $128K for marketing initiatives") is not.

Budget Categories

Every marketing dollar falls into one of these six categories. The allocation percentages are directional benchmarks for Stage 2–3 B2B SaaS companies — adjust based on your channel strategy and stage.

Category Examples Typical % of Total Notes
Paid Advertising Google SEM, LinkedIn Ads, paid social, retargeting, display 30–40% Highest ROI when validated. Largest single category for most Stage 2–3 companies. Scale up when LIUE >3, cut when LIUE <2.
Events & Conferences Event sponsorships, booth fees, field events, hosted webinars, executive dinners 15–25% High cost per SQL but very high LIR quality. Best for companies with a defined 2–3 "must-attend" events for their ICP. Track meeting-booked rate, not badge scans.
Consulting & Agency SEO agency, content writing, PR agency, design, video production 20–30% Helps scale output without growing headcount. Best for content velocity (SEO writers) and specialized expertise (PR, paid media management).
Software & Tools Marketing automation (HubSpot), SEO tools (Semrush/Ahrefs), analytics (Amplitude), ad management 10–15% Tools should enable measurement first — attribution, cohort tracking, and cost per SQL calculation. Avoid tool proliferation before proving the need.
Content Production Video production, photography, graphic design, infographics, interactive content 5–10% Invest in high-quality production for cornerstone content (case studies, State-of-Industry reports). Lower production standards are acceptable for evergreen blog content.
PR & Earned Media PR agency retainer, press release distribution, analyst relations, awards submissions 5–10% Stage 3 investment. Builds long-term brand authority. Difficult to attribute directly to pipeline — measure through aided brand awareness and backlink acquisition instead.

ROI-Based Reallocation

Every quarter, review LIUE by channel and reallocate budget accordingly. Channels that are not generating LIUE-positive returns do not get more budget — regardless of how many impressions or brand metrics they produce.

LIUE > 5
Excellent
Increase budget 25–50%. These channels are generating outsized returns — scale now while efficiency holds.
LIUE 3–5
Healthy
Maintain budget. Optimize creatives and landing pages. Look for marginal efficiency improvements.
LIUE 1–3
Concerning
Reduce budget and investigate. Either cost per SQL has risen or LIR rate has dropped. Fix before next cycle.
LIUE < 1
Destroying Value
Pause immediately. Do not reallocate — kill the budget. Redesign the channel strategy before resuming.

Quarterly Budget Review Process

  1. Pull LIUE by channel for the quarter Calculate LTV/CAC using cohort-level data. Use actual LIR achievement rates from the quarter — not lifetime averages which may mask recent deterioration. Segment by channel source (paid search, LinkedIn, events, content, outbound).
  2. Identify channels with LIUE > 5 These channels are delivering returns far above the minimum viable threshold. Increase their budget allocation by 25–50% in the next quarter. Monitor closely for efficiency degradation as volume increases — every channel has a scale ceiling.
  3. Investigate LIUE 1–3 channels Diagnose whether the problem is in the numerator (LTV — poor LIR achievement, low ACV from this channel) or denominator (CAC — rising cost per SQL). Each requires a different remediation: LIR problems are ICP or CS problems; CAC problems are channel efficiency problems.
  4. Reallocate from low-LIUE to high-LIUE The total budget envelope may stay the same, but the mix shifts toward what's working. Document the reallocation decision and the LIUE data that drove it — this creates institutional memory and improves future budgeting cycles.
  5. Update the annual plan If LIUE shifts significantly, update the bottom-up budget model for the remaining quarters. A channel that dropped from LIUE 4 to LIUE 1.5 may require its SQL contribution to be replaced by another channel — update the plan before the quarter begins, not after it ends.
LIUE Formula Reminder

LIUE = LTV ÷ CAC
LTV = ACV × (1 ÷ Churn Rate) × Gross Margin %
CAC = Total demand gen spend on channel ÷ New customers from that channel

A LIUE of 3 means: for every $1 of CAC invested through this channel, the company recovers $3 of lifetime gross profit. The minimum viable threshold is 3. Below 3, the channel is not sustainable at scale.
