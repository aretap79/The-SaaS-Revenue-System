Why LIR Exists
The fundamental problem with traditional retention metrics: they tell you about the past when you need to know about the future. LIR solves this.

NPS, CSAT, revenue retention, and churn rate all share the same flaw: they are lagging indicators. By the time they show a problem, the customers have already decided to leave. The churn event precedes the churn measurement by weeks or months. You can't intervene on a customer who has already made their decision.

LIR operates on a different principle: it identifies a specific, early customer behavior that predicts renewal 12 months in advance. If a customer achieves LIR, they are highly likely to renew. If they don't, they are likely to churn — and you know this within the first 30-90 days of their contract.

Origin: Mark Roberge at HubSpot

Mark Roberge discovered this principle while scaling HubSpot from $0 to $100M ARR. His team found that customers who completed a specific set of actions within their first 60 days — publishing their first blog post and sending their first email campaign — retained at 85%+ through month 12. Customers who didn't achieve these actions retained at approximately 55%.

This 30-percentage-point gap was the signal they needed. It allowed HubSpot to:

  • Build CS programs explicitly designed to drive customers toward these actions within 60 days
  • Define their ICP around customers who were structurally capable of hitting these actions
  • Tie sales compensation to LIR achievement — paying reps for the right sale, not just any sale
  • Show investors a leading retention signal before 12-month cohorts matured
The Central Insight

Customer success is not about what you do for customers after they churn risk appears — it's about driving them to a specific product milestone in their first 30-90 days that locks in the retention outcome. LIR identifies that milestone and makes it the operational center of your CS motion.

The LIR Formula
LIR is defined by three variables that your team must discover, set, and iterate based on data.
LIR Definition
LIR is "True" if
P% of customers achieve E event(s) within T days
where P, E, and T are discovered through cohort analysis and customer interviews

Each variable has a specific function and must be calibrated carefully:

VariableNameDefinitionStarting Target
P Percentage The % of customers who must achieve the event for LIR to be declared "True" for a given cohort 60–80%; start at 70%
E Event A specific, objective, instrumentable action inside your product that is statistically correlated with long-term retention Customer-specific; discovered via cohort analysis
T Time The number of days after contract start by which the customer must achieve E for it to count toward LIR As short as possible; 30–90 days typical
What Makes a Good LIR Event (E)
E is the hardest variable to get right. Most teams pick an event that seems correlated to value but isn't — or pick a vanity metric that looks strong but predicts nothing. The criteria are strict.
The E Event Must Be:
  • Objective — measurable with a number, not a judgment. "Customer completed first project" is objective. "Customer feels successful" is not.
  • Instrumentable — trackable automatically via your product or via a defined manual process in your CRM. If tracking requires someone to remember to log it, it won't be tracked reliably.
  • Predictive — historically, customers who hit E at T renew at significantly higher rates (≥20 percentage point gap vs. non-achievers is a meaningful signal).
  • Causal (ideally) — hitting E actually causes customer success, not just correlates with it. Causality is hard to prove but worth pursuing — it means that driving customers to E will improve their outcomes, not just select for customers already likely to succeed.
✗ Invalid — Do Not Use
  • Logged in 3 times (vanity — no product value signal)
  • Customer feels satisfied (not measurable)
  • Renewed contract (that's the outcome, not the predictor)
  • Completed upsell (too late — reflects already-retained customers)
  • Invited 1 user (too easy — no correlation to deep product adoption)
  • Attended a training webinar (passive consumption ≠ product use)
✓ Valid — Strong LIR Events
  • Completed first core workflow end-to-end
  • Processed X transactions through the platform
  • Integrated product with primary business system
  • Achieved measurable business outcome (e.g., X calls analyzed, X leads converted)
  • Invited Y team members who themselves became active
  • Exported first report or dashboard (evidence of ongoing use)

Industry & ProductLIR Event (E)TRationale

Project Management (Asana) Invited 3+ team members + created 5+ tasks 14 days Solo users churn. Team users stay. 3 invitees + 5 tasks signals organizational adoption, not individual experimentation.

CRM (Salesforce) Created 10+ contacts + 5+ opportunities with activity 30 days 10 contacts + 5 active opportunities signals the rep is managing their real pipeline in the CRM, not just exploring. Customers at this threshold rarely churned in early cohorts.
How to Discover Your LIR
LIR is not invented — it is discovered through a structured analysis of your existing customer data. This is a 6-8 week process the first time. Plan for it before you need the result.
1
Cohort Your Customers
Group customers by acquisition month or quarter. You need at least 6 cohorts with 12-month retention data to draw meaningful conclusions. Fewer than 6 cohorts means the signal may be noise.
Minimum viable sample: 50+ customers with at least 12 months of history. Less than this and statistical significance is impossible.
2
Identify Your Best Cohorts
Which cohorts have the highest 12-month retention rates? Sort by retention percentage. The top quartile cohorts are your signal — what made these customers different?
Also look at best individual customers within each cohort, not just cohort averages.
3
Interview Customers in Best Cohorts
Run 5-10 structured customer interviews with retained customers from your best cohorts. Focus question: "What did you do in your first 30/60/90 days? What was the moment you knew this product was working for you? What specific outcome did you achieve first?"
Interview churned customers from the same period as a control group. The contrast between retained and churned reveals the pattern.
4
Identify the Pattern
What specific action do retained customers consistently take that churned customers don't? Look for an action that is: mentioned frequently in retained customer interviews, absent or delayed in churned customer stories, and measurable in your product data.
Often the pattern is a combination of two actions, not just one. A single login event rarely predicts; a combination of integration + output creation typically does.
5
Make It Objective
Convert the qualitative pattern into a measurable, binary event. "Customer used product meaningfully" → "Customer processed at least X transactions within T days." The event must be something you can pull from a database, not estimate from feel.
6
Set P and T — Start at P=70%, T=60 Days
P of 70% means: if 70% of a cohort achieves E within T days, declare LIR "True" for that cohort. T of 60 days is a reasonable default for most B2B SaaS products. Both will be iterated based on back-testing.
If fewer than 60% of your best cohorts hit the event: either P is too high, T is too short, or E is wrong. Start with E — the event definition is the most common failure point.
7
Back-Test Against Historical Data
Apply your LIR hypothesis to the customers you already have historical data for. For each customer: did they achieve E within T? Did they renew at month 12? Plot the correlation. A strong LIR event shows a ≥20 percentage point retention gap between achievers and non-achievers.
Strong signal: achievers renew at 85%+, non-achievers at ≤55%. Weak signal: 84% vs. 77% — that's noise, not a LIR.
8
Validate Prospectively
Track the next 3 cohorts of new customers using your LIR hypothesis. Do customers who achieve E within T renew at significantly higher rates 12 months later? Prospective validation is stronger than back-testing because it avoids look-ahead bias.
9
Institutionalize
Build LIR tracking into your CRM and CS tool. Every new customer's LIR achievement status should be visible to their CSM, their AE (for comp purposes), and in the executive dashboard. LIR becomes the operational center of your customer success motion.
Setting P and T
Getting P and T right requires data, not intuition. The following guidance applies to most B2B SaaS products but must be calibrated against your specific cohort data.
Variable P — Percentage Threshold
How to Set P
P ValueInterpretation
<60%Too many exceptions — LIR is unreliable as a PMF signal
60–70%Starting range for SMB / transactional models
70–80%Standard range for mid-market SaaS
80%+High-touch enterprise — strong CS required to achieve

Start at P = 70% and adjust based on back-test results. If only 40% of your best cohorts hit E within T, the problem is likely E (the wrong event), not P (too high a bar).

Variable T — Time Window
How to Set T
Product TypeTypical T
PLG / Self-Serve7–14 days
SMB SaaS30 days
Mid-Market SaaS45–60 days
Enterprise / Implementation-Heavy60–90 days

Shorter T is always better — the faster you know a customer is at-risk, the sooner you can intervene. T too long: you find out a customer missed LIR 90 days after the window when there's far less you can do.

LIR vs. Other Retention Metrics
LIR occupies a unique position in the retention metric landscape: it combines early timing with high actionability — a combination no other standard metric achieves.
MetricTypeTimingActionabilityKey Limitation
LIR Leading Days 1 to T Very High — intervene before churn decision is made Requires discovery process; specific to your product
NPS Lagging Quarterly / ad hoc Medium — survey-based, infrequent, response bias Survey timing doesn't correlate with churn risk moments
CSAT Lagging Post-interaction Low — reactive, doesn't predict renewal Measures satisfaction with a transaction, not product value
Churn Rate Lagging Monthly / Quarterly Low — after the fact, no intervention possible The customer left before you measured the signal
Health Score Mixed Ongoing High — but requires significant investment to build Complex to build; weighting is often subjective
Time to Value Mixed Variable Medium — proxy for LIR; less specific "Value" is hard to define objectively across customers
The LIR Advantage

LIR's power comes from the combination of early timing (you know within 30–90 days) and high specificity (you know exactly what the customer did or didn't do). NPS tells you a customer is unhappy at month 9. LIR tells you at day 45 that a customer hasn't yet done the thing that would make them happy at month 9 — and you have 15 days left to help them do it.

Using LIR in CS Playbooks
LIR transforms customer success from reactive relationship management into a proactive, measurable intervention playbook. Every CS activity is evaluated against one question: does this help the customer achieve E within T?
The LIR-Driven CS Journey
Contract Close / Kickoff Call
Introduce LIR to the Customer Explicitly
The CSM tells the customer directly: "Our goal for the next [T] days is to help you achieve [E]. That's the milestone we know predicts long-term success with [product]. Let's build your onboarding plan around getting there." Setting the LIR goal explicitly creates shared accountability and makes the success metric transparent.
Deliverable: Onboarding plan with LIR milestone clearly defined
Week 1–2
Initial Check-In: Has the Customer Started Toward LIR?
Review product usage data. Has the customer logged in? Begun setup? Started toward E? A customer who has not engaged in week 1 is a strong early warning signal. CSM touches base via email or call with specific, concrete next step.
Trigger: If no usage in 7 days → CSM proactive outreach
Week 3–4 (T/2 Mark)
Mid-Point Alert: Is the Customer On Track?
At the halfway point of the T window, assess progress toward E. If the customer has completed <50% of the activity pattern that leads to E, trigger an intervention. This may include: extra training session, executive sponsor call, implementation help, or direct troubleshooting of blockers.
Trigger: If <50% progress at T/2 → escalation protocol initiated
Day T: LIR Assessment
Did the Customer Achieve E?
Binary assessment: did the customer achieve E by day T? If yes: celebrate, document the win, transition to expansion motion. If no: immediately initiate the remediation protocol — do not wait to see if they catch up organically. Non-achievers rarely self-correct without structured intervention.
If achieved: expand motion. If missed: immediate QBR + 30-day remediation plan.
Day T + 1–30: Remediation Window
Root Cause Analysis + 30-Day Rescue Plan
For customers who missed LIR: conduct an immediate QBR to identify the specific blocker (technical, organizational, onboarding gap, wrong-fit customer). Develop a concrete 30-day remediation plan with specific milestones. Document root cause for ICP and playbook improvement.
Output: Written remediation plan, revised LIR target date, executive sponsor engaged
Ongoing: LIR Dashboard
Every CSM Sees Every Account's LIR Status in Real Time
The LIR dashboard shows each account color-coded by LIR status. No account should be invisible to the CS team. Weekly team meeting reviews the red and yellow accounts collectively.
On track to achieve E within T
Behind pace — intervention recommended
Missed LIR or critically behind — escalate

LIR and Sales Compensation
Tying sales compensation to LIR achievement is the single most powerful structural change you can make to sales culture. It shifts rep incentives from closing any deal to closing the right deal — and the quality of your customer cohorts reflects this over time.
LIR-Linked Comp Structure
50%
Paid at Contract Close
Standard immediate commission — rewards the sale being made
50%
Paid When Customer Achieves LIR
Paid within T + 30 day grace period. If customer misses LIR, commission held until LIR is achieved or grace period expires — then adjusted or forfeited per comp plan terms.
Why This Works
  • Reps self-select better-fit customers. When half the commission depends on customer success, a rep who is tempted to close a poor-fit deal thinks twice. The deal looks less attractive when the 50% at-risk commission is factored in.
  • Reps participate in onboarding. A rep who has 50% of their commission at risk wants the customer to achieve LIR. They'll make intros, escalate blockers, and support the CS team in ways they wouldn't if all commission was paid at close.
  • LIR achievement rate rises over time. As reps learn that certain customer types miss LIR (and they lose commission), they stop targeting those customers. The ICP self-reinforces. Cohort LIR rates improve without top-down mandates.
  • Bad reps self-select out. Reps who consistently sell to wrong-fit customers earn less than reps who sell to right-fit customers — even at the same quota attainment level. The comp structure correctly rewards quality, not just volume.
Implementation Watch-Out

The grace period must be defined in the comp plan. If a customer misses LIR at day T but achieves it at day T+25, the rep should be paid. The purpose is to discourage wrong-fit selling — not to punish reps whose right-fit customers took slightly longer than expected. Typical grace period: T + 30 days. After that, the at-risk commission is forfeited for that deal.

Iterating Your LIR
LIR is not a permanent fixture — it must evolve with your product, your market, and your customer base. But it must also be stable enough to track meaningful cohort trends. The iteration discipline is: change when the data demands it, not when the achievement rate is uncomfortable.
✓ When to Change LIR
  • Product changes significantly (core workflow redesigned)
  • Back-test correlation drops (old E no longer predicts renewal at 20+ pt gap)
  • Business model changes (new pricing, new segment, new ACV tier)
  • New customer type is systematically different from historical ICP
  • New competitive analysis reveals a more predictive early behavior
✗ When NOT to Change LIR
  • Achievement rate is low (the LIR may be right — CS process may be wrong)
  • Reps are frustrated that the LIR-linked commission is hard to earn
  • A few customers complain the milestone is too hard
  • Less than 12 months since the last LIR definition change
  • You haven't back-tested the proposed new E against historical data
The Transition Process — Run Old and New in Parallel

When a LIR change is warranted, run both definitions simultaneously for 2 quarters before fully switching. This ensures:

  • Cohort comparability is maintained — you can compare old-definition cohorts to new-definition cohorts without a gap
  • The new E is validated prospectively before it becomes the comp-linked metric
  • The CS team can adapt their playbook to the new milestone before it's the official target
  • Sales reps receive clear communication about the transition timeline with sufficient advance notice
