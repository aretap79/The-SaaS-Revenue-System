Why This Is the #2 Revenue Leak

The MQL handoff is the #1 revenue leak in B2B SaaS. The sales-to-CS handoff is #2. When a customer closes, they have a set of explicit and implicit expectations set during the sales process: the implementation timeline the AE described, the use cases the champion believes will be solved, the success metrics the economic buyer committed to when signing, and the red flags the AE never surfaced because they were focused on closing.

If the CSM who takes ownership of this account doesn't know any of that context — because the AE wrote "great deal" in the CRM notes and moved on — the customer's first 90 days become a trust reconstruction project. That costs time, energy, and NRR. Research by Gainsight consistently finds that churn risk in the first year correlates directly with the quality of the sales-to-CS handoff, not the quality of the product.

The Silent Handoff Problem

Most companies believe their handoff process works because the CSM does eventually receive access to the closed opportunity in Salesforce. But access to CRM data is not a handoff. A handoff is a bilateral knowledge transfer where the CSM understands the customer's context well enough to have an intelligent first conversation without the AE present. Test this: ask any CSM to describe the top 3 success criteria for a recently onboarded account without reading from the CRM. If they can't, you don't have a handoff process.

The 12-Field Handoff Document

This document is the minimum information required for a complete handoff. It is created during the sales process — not after close — and is treated as a mandatory Closed Won field in the CRM. No Closed Won status without a complete handoff document.

Sales-to-CS Handoff Document
Required: Complete before or at deal close. Reviewed jointly in warm handoff call.
1. Champion
Person driving internal adoption. CS needs to know who to go to when decisions are stuck.
Name, title, direct email, direct phone. Why they championed this deal. What they personally stand to gain from a successful implementation. Their management relationship — do they have access to the executive buyer?
e.g., "Sarah Chen, Director of Revenue Ops. Championed because she owns the data quality problem that our product solves. Reports to VP Sales. Strong internal credibility — has CEO's ear on operational projects."
2. Economic Buyer (EB)
The person who signed the contract. CS must know who owns the renewal decision.
Name, title, relationship to champion. Their priorities for the investment — what do they expect to see at the QBR? What does success look like to them in their own language?
e.g., "Michael Torres, CRO. Cares about pipeline visibility and forecast accuracy — not tool features. He expects the first QBR to show improved forecast accuracy %. Make it quantitative."
3. Pain Identified
The specific problem that drove the purchase. Onboarding must solve this first.
The primary business pain in the customer's own words (use their language, not yours). The secondary pains that surfaced during discovery. Prioritize: which pain is most urgent vs. which is aspirational?
e.g., "Primary: CRM data is 40% incomplete because reps don't update. Sales ops spending 15 hrs/week manually cleaning data. Secondary: No visibility into multi-thread status of enterprise deals."
4. Success Criteria
The measurable outcomes the customer expects. These become the QBR scorecard.
Quantified success metrics, agreed with the economic buyer during the sales process. Should be SMART: specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound. If success criteria were not defined in the sales process — this is a red flag that must be surfaced and resolved before close.
e.g., "1. CRM data completeness to 90% within 60 days. 2. Rep time on CRM updates reduced from 4hrs/week to <1hr/week. 3. Forecast accuracy within 15% by end of Q2."
5. Implementation Timeline
What was promised. Misaligned timelines are the #1 cause of early churn.
What implementation timeline was described in the sales process. What milestone dates were committed to. Who owns implementation on the customer's side (an IT lead, a project manager, or the champion). Any technical constraints discovered during the sale.
6. Products Sold
Precise SKU-level visibility. CS needs to know what was purchased, not just the contract value.
Exact products/modules purchased, seat count, tier, contract start/end dates, renewal auto-renew or manual, payment terms. Any add-ons discussed but not purchased — these are expansion opportunities.
7. Customizations / Commitments
Non-standard promises made during the sale that CS must honor and Product must know about.
Any non-standard feature requests, integration commitments, custom reporting, SLA upgrades, or professional services included in or alongside the deal. Any roadmap items that were referenced as reasons to buy. These are landmines if undocumented.
e.g., "AE committed to a custom Salesforce field mapping integration by March 1st. This needs a Product/Eng ticket immediately. Customer signed partly based on this."
8. Red Flags
The concerns that were present at close that CS needs to monitor proactively.
Executive sponsor leaving the company. Budget was a stretch. Champion had internal resistance from IT or Finance. The product is replacing a legacy system with deep-rooted processes. Another department using a competing tool. These are things the AE knows but never writes down.
9. Stakeholder Map
CS must know all relevant people — not just the champion — to successfully drive adoption.
Key stakeholders involved in the buying process and likely involved in adoption. For each: name, title, their attitude toward the purchase (champion/supportive/neutral/skeptic), and their primary concern. The skeptics from the sales process are the churn risks if not converted to advocates.
10. Next Steps / First Actions
The customer expects continuity — no restart, no confusion about what happens next.
Exactly what was promised to happen post-signature. Kickoff meeting scheduled? Technical discovery call needed? Who sends the kickoff invite — AE, CS, or customer? What document is the customer expecting to receive? Clear ownership prevents the "we signed but haven't heard from anyone" email.
11. Renewal Date
CS must start the renewal motion 90+ days before this date.
Exact contract end date. Auto-renew terms and notification window. Any opt-out provisions. The CS calendar event for the renewal should be created on day 1 of onboarding — not 60 days before renewal.
12. Expansion Potential
Turns CS into a revenue team from day 1 of ownership.
Products discussed but not purchased. Other departments or business units that could benefit. Growth signals (hiring plans, funding rounds, geographic expansion, new initiatives mentioned in the sales process). These become the first expansion plays within 6 months of onboarding.

The Mutual Success Plan (MSP)

Mutual Success Plan

The Mutual Success Plan is a co-created document, built jointly by the AE and the customer champion during the final weeks of the sales process, that defines what success looks like, who is responsible for each milestone, and how it will be measured. At close, it is handed to the CSM as their primary onboarding guide — it is both a handoff document and the first working agenda for the customer relationship.

Business Goals (from the Customer)
The 3 quantified business outcomes the customer is investing to achieve. Written in their language. Used as the scorecard for QBRs.
Implementation Milestones
Key technical milestones with dates and owners. Kickoff date, integration completion, user training, go-live. Red/yellow/green status tracked.
Customer Responsibilities
What the customer must do for success: provide technical access, assign an internal project lead, complete user setup, attend training. Explicit, not assumed.
Vendor Responsibilities
What you commit to: onboarding timeline, training sessions, escalation response times, integration support. Your signed commitments — not marketing language.
Success Metrics and Review Dates
The 30/60/90-day metrics to be reviewed. The cadence for check-ins. Who attends the QBR and what the scorecard will show.
Escalation Path
Who to call if something goes wrong. Named escalation contacts on both sides. Not a support ticket queue — a relationship path.

The Warm Handoff Call Structure

Warm Handoff Call Agenda (30 Minutes) — AE + CSM + Champion
0–5 min
Introductions. AE introduces CSM. CSM introduces themselves and their role. Establishes that the CSM is the primary owner from this point forward — not the AE (important for setting expectations, otherwise the champion keeps calling the AE).
5–15 min
AE Context Transfer. AE walks through the top 3 items from the handoff document: the primary pain, the success criteria, and any red flags. This is not a document dump — it is a story about why the customer bought and what they need. Champion confirms, corrects, or adds context.
15–25 min
CSM Transition. CSM takes ownership: walks through the Mutual Success Plan, confirms implementation timeline, confirms who the internal project lead is, schedules the kickoff meeting. Champions feel continuity — the CSM already knows the context.
25–30 min
Next steps and AE exit. CSM owns all next steps. AE confirms they are available for strategic support if needed but that the CSM is the go-to contact. AE does not remain the primary relationship owner — if they do, CSM has no authority and the handoff has failed.

The Shadow CS Model

The Shadow CS model requires the assigned CSM to join the final 1-2 AE calls before close — specifically the negotiation/commercial stage and the stakeholder alignment call. This achieves three things simultaneously:

  • The CSM hears the success criteria and red flags firsthand — from the customer's mouth, not filtered through AE notes
  • The champion meets the CSM before the deal closes — the handoff becomes a continuation of a relationship, not the start of one
  • The CSM can raise concerns about implementation feasibility or resource requirements before the contract is signed — preventing post-close surprises

Shadow CS requires AE willingness (some AEs are territorial about "their" calls) and CSM availability. At scale, this means matching CSMs to strategic deals based on capacity, not randomly. For deals above $50K ACV, Shadow CS should be standard practice.

Failure Modes

1. AE writes "great customer" in notes and considers the handoff done
Diagnostic
High rate of CSM/customer "expectation reset" meetings in months 1-2. CSMs spending time on discovery they should not need to do. Customer saying "I thought X was included in our contract."
Fix
Make handoff document a mandatory CRM field for Closed Won. Build a RevOps process that flags any deal with incomplete handoff data within 24 hours of close. Include handoff quality in AE performance reviews — not just quota attainment.
2. AE remains the primary relationship owner post-close
Diagnostic
Champion keeps calling the AE for product questions and support issues. CSM has no authority because customer bypasses them. AE spends 20% of time on post-sale support for closed accounts.
Fix
Warm handoff call must include explicit statement: "From today, [CSM name] is your primary contact for everything." AE sets email auto-forward for customer emails to CSM. After 60 days, AE involvement in daily customer management should be zero.
3. Success criteria were never defined during the sale
Diagnostic
CSM receives a handoff document where success criteria is "customer wants to use the product better." QBR has no scorecard. At renewal, customer says "I'm not sure we got value."
Fix
Add success criteria definition as a mandatory step in the late-stage sales process — it should be completed before the commercial proposal is sent, not during onboarding. Train AEs that undefined success criteria is a churn risk that costs them renewal commission.
4. Red flags are hidden because AE feared they would delay the deal
Diagnostic
High year-1 churn in certain deal segments or from certain AEs. Post-churn interviews reveal known issues at close (exec sponsor leaving, budget fragility, IT resistance) that were never documented.
Fix
Create a "risk assessment" field in the handoff document. Reward AEs for honest red flag documentation — it helps CS manage the account proactively. Punish AEs who hide red flags only through churn cost analysis shown in comp plan design.
5. CSM is overloaded and onboarding is deprioritized
Diagnostic
Days-to-kickoff trending above 21 days. Customer complaints about slow start. Health scores at day 30 below 50 for new accounts. CSMs managing 80+ accounts in a segment designed for 30.
Fix
Onboarding capacity must be modeled separately from steady-state CS capacity. Build a "launch queue" view in RevOps dashboard showing all accounts pending kickoff. Hire onboarding specialists (lower cost than CSMs) for the first 90 days of the customer lifecycle.
Leading: Handoff document completeness %, days to kickoff, warm handoff call completion Lagging: Year-1 NRR by cohort, 90-day churn rate, customer health score at 30/60/90 days
