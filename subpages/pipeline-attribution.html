What Attribution Is and Why It Matters

Attribution is the process of assigning credit for pipeline creation and revenue to the marketing and sales activities that influenced a deal. It answers the question: "If we close this $150K deal, which activities caused the buyer to enter our funnel, engage with our brand, and ultimately say yes?"

Attribution matters for three reasons. First, budget allocation: if you don't know which channels produce the most pipeline per dollar, you are allocating your marketing budget on gut feel. Second, channel optimization: attribution data tells you which channels are over-invested (high spend, low pipeline) and which are under-invested (low spend, high pipeline). Third, marketing/sales alignment: attribution creates a shared language for discussing how pipeline is created — preventing the marketing/sales blame game when pipeline is short.

The Attribution Paradox

No attribution model is perfectly accurate. Every model is a simplification. The goal is not perfect attribution — it is a consistent, agreed-upon model that makes your channel decisions less wrong over time. Perfect is the enemy of useful.

Choosing the Right Model for Your Stage

Seed / Pre-Series A
Use last-touch or first-touch only. You don't have the data volume or technical infrastructure for multi-touch. Focus on tracking what channel generated your first 50 customers — that pattern is your ICP signal.
Series A–B (<$10M ARR)
Linear multi-touch is the right step up. Install UTM parameter discipline across all channels. Build CRM campaign tracking. Choose 3-5 key channels to measure. Establish "sourced" vs. "influenced" as separate categories.
Series B–C ($10M–$50M ARR)
Implement W-shaped or full-path attribution via Bizible (Marketo Measure) or Attribution.io. Integrate CRM + marketing automation + BI tool. Build channel-level pipeline report by segment (SMB/MM/Enterprise separately — attribution patterns differ dramatically by segment).
Series C+ (>$50M ARR)
Move toward data-driven attribution if deal volume permits (500+ closed-won/lost per year). Invest in cookieless attribution methodology for post-2024 tracking. Separate pipeline sourced, pipeline influenced, and pipeline accelerated as three distinct attribution buckets.

Technical Infrastructure Required

Four Non-Negotiable Technical Requirements for Accurate Attribution
UTM Parameter Discipline
Every paid link, email link, partner link, and event link must carry consistent UTM parameters: utm_source, utm_medium, utm_campaign, utm_content, utm_term. Without this, Google Analytics shows "direct" traffic when the source is actually a newsletter, a LinkedIn ad, or a partner referral.
CRM Campaign Tracking
Every marketing campaign must be created as a Campaign object in Salesforce/HubSpot with campaign members (contacts/leads) tracked on a Campaign Member record with a response status. This is the chain that links a marketing activity to a contact to an opportunity to revenue.
BI Integration
Raw CRM + marketing automation data must flow into a BI tool (Tableau, Looker, Metabase) to produce cross-channel attribution reports. Salesforce's native reporting cannot handle multi-touch attribution across channels. Data warehouse (Snowflake, BigQuery) is the integration layer at scale.
Cookieless Attribution Planning
Third-party cookies are dead for most browsers. Server-side tracking (via CDP: Segment, mParticle) and 1st-party data strategies are the replacement. Build a 1st-party data collection strategy (email capture, progressive profiling, authenticated web sessions) before your attribution data disappears.

Pipeline Sourced vs. Pipeline Influenced

Pipeline Sourced (Marketing-Sourced)
Marketing was the first touch that created the contact record and drove the initial conversion that led to the opportunity. The deal would not exist without that initial marketing touchpoint. This is the metric marketing "owns" in most organizations. Typically 30-50% of pipeline in well-run B2B SaaS companies.
Pipeline Influenced (Marketing-Influenced)
Marketing touched the contact or account at some point in the buyer's journey — even if the deal was originally sourced by an SDR or AE via outbound. An event the prospect attended, a piece of content they downloaded, a webinar they watched — all count as marketing influence. Typically 50-80% of pipeline shows marketing influence when tracking is tight.
The Attribution Fight — and How to Prevent It
The most common marketing/sales conflict is the attribution fight: marketing claims pipeline credit for deals that sales "actually won," while sales claims they found the deal through outbound and marketing just sent one email. This is organizational friction that kills collaboration. Prevention: agree on definitions before the quarter starts. Define "sourced" (first touch is marketing) vs. "influenced" (any marketing touch) vs. "outbound-sourced" (first touch is SDR/AE) in writing, get CRO and CMO to sign off, and enforce the definitions in the CRM data model. Disagreements become data questions, not political fights.
