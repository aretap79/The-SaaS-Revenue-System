Sales Compensation Design — Aligning Behavior with Revenue Strategy
The compensation plan is the most powerful behavioral lever a sales leader has. Design it wrong and you will get exactly the wrong behaviors.
Home › Phase 2 › Compensation Design

The Compensation Principle

Before designing any element of a comp plan, answer two questions: What behaviors do we want to incentivize? What outcomes are we trying to optimize? Every other design decision flows from these answers.

The Behavior-Outcome-Comp Alignment Chain

Good comp design is simple: identify the behaviors that lead to the outcomes you want, then pay for those behaviors. If you want reps to close multi-year deals, pay a premium on multi-year TCV. If you want reps to prioritize new logos over expansion, create separate quotas with separate commission rates. If you want reps to sell higher-margin products, tier commission rates by product margin. The plan will produce exactly what it rewards — design it intentionally.

If You Want…Design the Plan to…Warning If You Don't
Multi-year deal growthPay commission on TCV (not just Year 1 ACV) with a multi-year kickerReps will default to annual deals; less cash commitment friction but lower LTV
New logo acquisitionSeparate new logo quota with higher commission rate than expansionReps will farm existing accounts because it's easier; new logo pipeline atrophies
High-margin product mixTier commission rate by product contribution marginReps will sell whatever is easiest to close; low-margin SKUs dominate mix
Accurate forecastingInclude a forecast accuracy component (% of quarterly commit achieved) in accelerator unlockReps will sandbag or overcommit without consequence; manager loses forecast visibility
CS handoff qualityClawback % of commission on deals churned within 90 days post-signatureReps will close bad-fit customers to hit quota; CS burden and churn increase

OTE (On-Target Earnings) — Definition and Benchmarking

OTE is the total compensation a rep earns when they achieve exactly 100% of their quota. It is the market-clearing price for talent in your segment, role level, and geography. Set it wrong and you will lose candidates to competitors or overpay relative to productivity.

OTE Benchmarking Sources

  • Radford / Aon Compensation Survey: Gold standard for enterprise-grade comp benchmarking; subscription-based; most reliable for Series C+ companies
  • Carta Total Comp: Particularly strong for early-stage startup benchmarks; uses anonymized equity + cash data from Carta customers
  • Levels.fyi: Primarily tech/engineering but has expanding sales data; useful for cross-validation
  • LinkedIn Salary: Free, large sample, but lacks role nuance; use as directional check only
  • RepVue: Sales-specific compensation database with verified reported earnings by company, role, and team; increasingly authoritative for AE/SDR benchmarking
  • Bridge Group SaaS Metrics Report: Annual survey of inside sales comp by ACV, segment, and company size; free and highly reliable for SaaS-specific context

Base:Variable Ratios by Role

The base:variable ratio reflects how much behavioral control you have over the activity (high control = more base) and the risk tolerance required of the rep (high risk tolerance = higher variable). The ratio also signals role expectations — a 50:50 ratio signals "this is a high-accountability revenue role" while 80:20 signals "this is primarily a relationship management role."

Field AE (Enterprise) 50 : 50
Base (50%)Variable (50%)

Highest risk/reward ratio; enterprise AEs with long cycles and large ACVs should be motivated by large upside potential. The 50:50 ratio attracts self-motivated hunters.

Inside AE (Mid-Market) 55 : 45
Base (55%)Variable (45%)

Slightly more base than field AE; inside motion is more process-driven and managers have more visibility into activity. Some companies go 60:40 for junior inside AEs.

SDR / BDR 65 : 35
Base (65%)Variable (35%)

SDRs have limited control over downstream deal outcomes; they should be paid primarily for activity and qualified pipeline. Some companies use 70:30. Avoid going below 60% base — it creates instability and early attrition.

CSM (Expansion-Focused) 70 : 30
Base (70%)Variable (30%)

CSMs who own expansion and upsell targets should have a variable component. The 30% variable motivates expansion behavior without making retention-risk decisions feel personally costly. Pure retention CSMs can be 80:20.

Quota-to-OTE Ratio — The Productivity Standard

The quota-to-OTE ratio (sometimes called the "coverage ratio" or "leverage ratio") is the amount of quota a rep is expected to carry per dollar of OTE. It is the fundamental unit economics of your sales compensation model.

Quota-to-OTE Ratio
Quota-to-OTE Ratio = Annual Quota Target / OTE
A commonly cited heuristic for enterprise B2B SaaS: 4x–6x OTE. This varies by ACV, motion type, and stage — benchmark against companies at your segment and scale. Example: AE with $200K OTE might carry $800K–$1.2M quota. Below 4x may indicate the company is overpaying for output; above 6x warrants scrutiny that quota is realistic given territory and support.
RatioSignalImplication
< 3xOver-investment in sales capacity relative to productivityReview whether OTE is above-market, quota is too low, or territory is undersized
3x–4xLow productivity; acceptable for early-stage companies building foundationViable if ramping large territories, but not scalable long-term
4x–6xStandard SaaS range; healthy productivity expectationSustainable; allows both rep upside and company return
6x–8xHigh productivity; requires strong market, product, or supportWatch attainment distribution — if <60% hit 100%, quota may be too high
> 8xQuota is likely aspirational / unattainableExpect low attainment, high attrition, and demoralization

The Commission Rate — Design and Calculation

Commission Rate Derivation
Commission Rate = (OTE × Variable %) / Annual Quota
Example: $200K OTE, 50% variable, $1M quota → ($200K × 50%) / $1M = 10% commission rate. At this rate, the rep earns $100K variable when they hit $1M in quota exactly.

Flat Rate vs. Tiered Rate

StructureHow It WorksBest ForWatch Out For
Flat Rate Same rate on every dollar from $0 to infinity. Simple to administer and communicate. SMB/velocity motion; SDR variable comp; simple inside sales teams Does not differentially reward over-achievement; top performers can feel capped relative to their contribution
Tiered Rate (Accelerated) Base rate up to 100% quota; higher rate above 100%. Often 1.5–2× rate above quota. Enterprise AE; any role where top performer retention is critical; companies wanting to create earnings dispersion More complex to administer; reps will optimize deal timing around thresholds; retroactive vs. prospective design matters enormously

Retroactive vs. Prospective Accelerators

Retroactive accelerators apply the higher rate to all deals in the period once the threshold is crossed. Example: if a rep sells $1.05M against a $1M quota, the 1.5× rate applies retroactively to the entire $1.05M, not just the last $50K. This creates dramatic behavioral effects near the threshold — reps will push hard to cross it. Prospective accelerators apply only to dollars above the threshold, producing a smoother incentive curve with less "hockey stick" behavior.

The Deal-Timing Gaming Risk

Any threshold-based accelerator creates an incentive to time deals. If the accelerator kicks in at 100% quota and the year-end is December 31, reps near threshold in mid-December will pull deals forward aggressively — sometimes by pressuring customers or offering excessive discounts — to cross the accelerator. Design around this with quarterly accelerators that reset, or caps on discount authority that require manager approval.

Accelerators — Designing for Top Performer Retention

Accelerators are the primary tool for creating earnings upside that retains your best performers. A flat commission plan with no accelerators means a rep at 150% of quota earns 50% more than a rep at 100% of quota. An accelerated plan can make that same 150% rep earn 80–120% more. That difference is the retention mechanism for your top 10–20% of performers.

Attainment Range Rate Multiplier Example: 10% Base Rate Earnings on $1M Quota
0% – 50% 0.5× 5% rate $0–$25K on first $500K (draw back; some plans don't do this)
50% – 100% 1.0× 10% rate $50K earned at 100% = full variable component
100% – 125% 1.5× 15% rate +$37.5K on the $250K above quota
125% – 150% 2.0× 20% rate +$50K on next $250K = total earnings well above OTE
150%+ 2.5× 25% rate Uncapped; president's club territory
Accelerated Commission Earnings Formula
Total Commission = (Quota × Base Rate) + ((ACV − Quota) × (Base Rate × Accelerator))
Example: $1M quota, 10% base rate, 1.5× accelerator, $1.2M ACV closed: Commission = ($1M × 10%) + ($200K × 15%) = $100K + $30K = $130K variable. OTE was $100K variable — rep earns 30% above plan variable at 120% quota attainment.

The Attainment Distribution Test

If fewer than 60–70% of reps can achieve 100% of their quota, the comp plan is structurally broken. Quotas that are unattainable by most reps create demoralization, attrition, and sandbagging — not better performance.

The Healthy Attainment Distribution

A healthy attainment distribution typically shows the majority of reps at or above quota, a small percentage significantly exceeding it, and a small percentage underperforming. The specific percentages below are illustrative starting points:

Attainment BandHealthy % of RepsInterpretation
< 50%5–10%Chronic underperformers; PIP territory; should rarely exceed 10%
50%–75%10–15%Developing reps; ramping; territory challenges
75%–100%20–30%Solid contributors; not blowing out quota
100%–125%25–35%Core performers; plan working as designed
125%–150%15–20%High performers; accelerator territory
150%+5–10%President's Club; top performers; comp cost is justified by revenue
What Different Distributions Signal

Bimodal distribution (many under 50%, many over 150%): Territory inequity — some reps have great territories and some have broken ones. Fix: territory rebalancing, not quota changes.

Almost everyone under 75%: Quotas are too high (over-setting), ramp time insufficient, or product/market fit issues are blocking pipeline. Do not fire reps before diagnosing root cause.

Almost everyone at 100–110%: Quotas are too low (sandbagging in quota setting). Fix: raise quota 10–20%; implement upside accelerators so high performance is rewarded more aggressively.

High variance regardless of average: Territory inequity is likely the primary driver. Build territory equity analysis before changing comp structure.

Commission Timing — When Reps Get Paid

Commission timing has major implications for rep behavior, cash flow, and the quality of deals closed. The timing policy should align with the point at which value is actually confirmed — which is not always at signature.

On Booking (Contract Signature) — Most Common
Maximizes motivation to close; aligns with when the company records ARR
Creates risk if customer does not activate or pays late; can incentivize closing bad-fit customers
Mitigate with: 60–90 day clawback provision for churned/cancelled deals. Standard in SaaS.
On Invoice / On Billing
Cash is verified before commission paid; reduces bad-deal incentive
If billing is delayed, rep motivation suffers; common friction in enterprise deals with net-60/90 payment terms
Best for: companies with high invoice-to-cash delay risk or frequent deal restructuring post-signature.
On Cash Payment
Company only pays commission when cash is received; zero credit risk
Enterprise deals with net-90 terms mean a Q1 close pays commission in Q2; severely dampens motivation; uncommon in SaaS
Rarely used in SaaS; creates cash flow alignment at significant motivational cost.
On Activation / On Go-Live
Incentivizes rep to ensure smooth handoff and customer launch; aligns with actual value delivery
Activation is partially outside rep's control; can create conflict between AE and CS on handoff accountability
Consider for companies where implementation lag or activation failure is a major churn driver.

Clawback Provisions

A clawback recovers paid commission when a deal cancels, churns, or is restructured within a specified period. Clawbacks create accountability for deal quality — not just deal quantity. Standard design: 100% clawback on cancellations within 90 days, 50% clawback on cancellations within 180 days, no clawback after 180 days. Clawback should never exceed what the rep earned on the specific deal (i.e., cannot create net negative commission).

SPIFs — Sales Performance Incentive Funds

A SPIF is a short-term, targeted incentive layered on top of the base comp plan to drive specific behaviors during a defined window. Used well, SPIFs create urgency, focus, and excitement. Overused, they destroy the baseline comp plan's credibility — reps learn to wait for the SPIF.

When SPIFs Are Appropriate

Product Launch
New Product Acceleration
Drive early adoption of a new SKU by paying 2× commission on first X deals. Builds pipeline data and customer references for the new product in the first 90 days.
End-of-Period
Q-End Pipeline Pull
Bonus for deals closed in the last 2 weeks of the quarter. Drives urgency on deals that are "almost ready." Use sparingly — customers learn to wait for Q-end discounts.
Market Entry
New Vertical / Geo Kicker
Pay 1.5× on first N deals in a new target vertical or geography. Creates focus on the strategic objective without permanently restructuring the base plan.
Competitive
Competitive Displacement Bonus
Specific bonus for displacing a named competitor. Creates focus and motivation on competitive takeouts that may have lower win rates but high strategic value.
Why SPIF Overuse Destroys Effectiveness

If a SPIF runs every quarter, reps will mentally adjust their base behavior downward and wait for the SPIF before putting deals in. The SPIF becomes expected income, not incremental motivation. The rule: SPIFs should be rare (no more than 2–3 per year), specific (one behavior, one time window), and surprising (announced with short notice to prevent behavioral adjustment before launch).

Multi-Year Deal Treatment

Multi-year deals are strategically valuable: they lock in revenue, improve GRR, and often come with higher TCV at lower marginal sales cost. But if your comp plan doesn't reward them appropriately, reps will default to annual deals because they are easier to close and consume less negotiating capital.

TCV vs. ACV Commission Treatment

ApproachHow It WorksProCon
Pay on ACV (Year 1 only) Rep earns commission only on Year 1 contract value regardless of deal length Simple; predictable commission cost Creates no incentive for multi-year; 3-year deal = same pay as 1-year deal
Pay on TCV with a Cap Rep earns on total contract value but capped at 2–3× ACV to limit commission in blowout deals Incentivizes multi-year; limits financial exposure Cap creates cliff effect at threshold; reps optimize deal structure around cap
TCV with Multi-Year Kicker Standard ACV commission on Year 1 + 25–50% kicker paid upfront on Years 2–3 committed value Strong multi-year incentive; keeps cash timing close to deal close Requires clawback mechanism if customer cancels in Year 2–3; more complex administration

The Discounting Incentive Problem

Multi-year deals are often won with year-over-year price discounts (e.g., "we'll lock in flat pricing for 3 years"). This creates a margin problem: the deal looks big on TCV but the company gives up revenue growth. Solve this with comp design: pay commission on committed TCV net of any discounts vs. list price. A rep who gives away 20% of list price gets 20% less commission. This aligns rep incentives with margin, not just volume.

Channel, Expansion & Partner Comp

Expansion Compensation for AEs

In many SaaS companies, AEs retain ownership of their accounts for 12–24 months post-close and are eligible to earn on expansion (upsell, cross-sell). The design question: what rate and what quota mechanism?

OptionDesignBest For
Separate expansion quotaAE has a new logo quota and an expansion quota; separate commission rates (expansion often lower, 50–75% of new logo rate)Enterprise accounts where expansion is a named AE responsibility
Blended quotaAll revenue (new logo + expansion) counts against one quota; same rateSMB/Mid-Market where expansion is opportunistic
No expansion comp for AECS owns expansion; AE has no financial incentive but is expected to facilitateHigh-velocity models where post-sale engagement is fully owned by CS

The Double Compensation Problem in Co-Selling

When an AE and a partner (reseller, integrator, technology partner) are both involved in closing a deal, both may have expectation of commission or fee. Define co-sell compensation upfront: AE receives full commission on net new ACV (no reduction for partner involvement); partner receives a separate referral fee (typically 5–15% of first-year ACV) paid from a separate G&A or sales program budget. Never split the AE's commission to pay the partner — it creates resentment and misaligned incentives.

The Comp Plan Document

Every rep must receive a written, signed compensation plan document at the beginning of each plan period. Verbal commitments are not compensation plans — they are liability. The comp plan document is a contract.

1
Effective Date and Plan Period

Start and end date of the plan. Typically January 1 – December 31 for annual plans, or by quarter for quarterly plans. State what happens mid-year if the plan changes.

2
Quota and OTE

Annual quota amount (and quarterly breakdown if applicable). OTE amount split by base and variable. Both in dollars — no ambiguity.

3
Commission Structure

Exact commission rate(s) at each attainment tier. Whether it's flat or tiered. Whether accelerators are retroactive or prospective. What qualifies as commissionable revenue (new logo only? expansion? renewals?).

4
Commission Timing and Clawbacks

When commission is paid (booking, billing, payment). Clawback terms: percentage and duration. Process for disputed clawbacks.

5
Accelerators and SPIFs

Accelerator thresholds and multipliers. Any known SPIFs for the plan period (if pre-announced). Statement that additional SPIFs may be issued during the plan year.

6
Dispute Resolution

Process for disputing commission calculations. Who to contact. Timeline for resolution. Statement that all commission calculations are based on CRM/billing system of record.

7
Plan Modification and Termination Clauses

Company's right to modify the plan with notice (typically 30 days). What happens to in-progress deals if the rep's employment terminates — earned vs. unvested commission.

When to Involve Legal

Always involve legal on: clawback provisions (jurisdiction-specific wage law implications), plan modification clauses, termination and commission treatment, and any commission dispute resolution mechanism. In California particularly, commission clawbacks and forfeiture provisions are heavily regulated — what is standard in other states may be unenforceable in CA. Legal review of comp plan terms is a modest annual expense; commission disputes that escalate to litigation — particularly in states with protective commission laws — can be extremely costly. Always have an employment attorney review clawback and termination provisions.

Annual Comp Planning Process

Timeline

  1. Q3: Data Collection and AnalysisPull attainment distribution for the current plan year. Identify what behaviors were and were not incentivized. Survey team on plan satisfaction and clarity. Benchmark against market data (Radford, Bridge Group) for the coming year.
  2. September–October: Strategy AlignmentAlign with CRO, CFO, and VPs on strategic priorities for next year. What behaviors needs to be rewarded differently? New products? New market entry? Margin improvement? These drive plan design changes.
  3. October–November: Plan Design and ModelingDesign new plan structure. Model it against historical performance data: if 30% of current reps had been on the new plan, what would they have earned? Ensure the new plan is cost-neutral or within budget envelope. Model for attainment distribution scenarios (bear, base, bull).
  4. November: Legal and Finance ReviewLegal reviews for compliance. Finance reviews cost model and budgetary impact. HR reviews for internal equity issues. Secure all approvals before communicating to the team.
  5. December: CommunicationPresent changes to the team with at least 30 days before effective date. Explain the "why" — what behaviors the new plan is designed to reward. Give individuals examples of what they would earn at various attainment levels under the new plan.

Key Formulas Reference

OTE Derivation
OTE = Base Salary + (Quota × Commission Rate)
Commission Rate = (OTE × Variable %) / Annual Quota. If OTE = $200K, variable = 50%, quota = $1M: rate = ($100K / $1M) = 10%.
Standard Commission
Commission = ACV Closed × Commission Rate
At 10% rate on $1.2M ACV closed: Commission = $120K variable. Base + Variable = full comp for the period.
Accelerated Commission
Commission = (Quota × Rate) + ((ACV − Quota) × (Rate × Accelerator Multiplier))
At $1M quota, 10% rate, 1.5× accelerator, $1.3M ACV: = ($1M × 10%) + ($300K × 15%) = $100K + $45K = $145K variable. Rep earns 45% above plan variable.
Quota-to-OTE Ratio
Quota:OTE = Annual Quota / OTE | Target Range: 4×–6×
$1M quota / $200K OTE = 5× — within healthy range. $600K quota / $200K OTE = 3× — likely over-paying or under-quoting. $1.5M quota / $200K OTE = 7.5× — likely unrealistic; expect low attainment.
Sales Compensation Cost of Sales
Comp CoS% = Total Sales Comp / Total ARR Closed × 100
Benchmark: 8–12% for mid-market SaaS; 12–18% for enterprise; 5–8% for high-velocity SMB. Above benchmark → productivity or quota-setting problem. Below benchmark → possible under-investment in talent.

Leading & Lagging Indicators

Leading Indicators

Pipeline coverage by attainment band — reps working toward accelerator thresholds will show elevated pipeline coverage in the 100–130% range; reps below 50% often have thin pipeline and need intervention.

Average deal size trend — if comp rewards larger deals, average ACV should trend up over time; flat or declining ACV suggests pricing pressure or comp misalignment.

Multi-year deal rate — if comp structure rewards multi-year deals, this rate should increase each quarter; flat rate signals the plan is not working as intended.

Discount rate by rep — if comp penalizes discounting (margin-linked commission), discount rate should be lower for reps with strong comp management discipline.

Lagging Indicators

Quota attainment distribution — run monthly; the shape of the distribution tells you more about plan health than any single number. Target: 60–70% above 100%, mean at 105–115%.

Voluntary attrition rate — top performers leaving is the primary comp plan failure signal; exit interview data on comp satisfaction is the diagnostic.

Average OTE achieved vs. plan OTE — if average achieved OTE is significantly below plan OTE (e.g., 75% of OTE on average), quotas are too high or territory is too thin; if above 130% of plan OTE, quotas are too low.

New logo rate vs. expansion rate — if the plan has separate quotas, track actual achievement on each; imbalance signals behavioral gaming toward the easier quota.
