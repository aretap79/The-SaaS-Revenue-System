Managing vs. Coaching: A Critical Distinction

Most sales managers spend 80% of their time managing (reporting, forecasting, deal status updates) and 20% coaching (developing skills). The best managers invert that ratio — and their teams outperform by 20–30% consistently.

The Distinction (after Iannarino)

Managing is the administrative function: pipeline review, forecast rollup, deal status, metrics reporting, CRM hygiene enforcement. It produces information. Coaching is the developmental function: skill identification, behavioral feedback, role-play practice, call debrief, career development. It produces performance.

Activity Managing or Coaching? Time Allocation (Best Practice) Primary Outcome
Pipeline review / forecast call Managing 20% of 1:1 time Accuracy of forecast, deal visibility
Gong call review + debrief Coaching 30% of 1:1 time Skill development in specific competency
Role-play objection handling Coaching Part of coaching 60% Behavioral change before next live deal
Deal strategy workshop Coaching (with managing benefits) Part of coaching 60% Specific deal advancement + skill transfer
Career development conversation Coaching 20% of 1:1 time Retention, motivation, growth
CRM hygiene review Managing Minimal — delegate or automate Data quality

The Weekly 1:1 — Structure and Agenda

The weekly 1:1 is the atomic unit of sales management. Everything that happens in deal reviews, forecasts, and QBRs flows from the quality of the weekly 1:1. It should be non-negotiable, never cancelled, and always purposeful.

Standard 45-Minute 1:1 Structure
Block 1: Pipeline (9 min)
20%
Quick pipeline scan — new deals in, stale deals out, forecast contribution update
Block 2: Skill Dev (27 min)
60%
One Gong call reviewed + debrief, one skill coached, role-play if needed
Block 3: Personal (9 min)
20%
Career development, motivation, blockers, wellbeing check
The Most Common 1:1 Failure

The 1:1 devolves entirely into a deal status update meeting. Manager asks "where are you on the Acme deal?" for 45 minutes. Rep reports status. No skill is developed. No coaching occurs. The rep learns nothing. This is a management failure, not a time constraint problem.

1:1 Preparation (Both Manager and Rep)
  • Rep comes prepared: CRM updated before 1:1. One Gong call identified for review. One specific question or skill area for coaching. Personal topic ready if needed.
  • Manager comes prepared: CRM reviewed before 1:1 (not during). Gong call pre-listened. One coaching focus identified based on prior session. Career development notes reviewed.
  • Use a shared 1:1 doc: Running Google Doc or Notion page with weekly agenda. Ensures continuity — coaching focus from last week carries forward.

The Pipeline Review Meeting (Team Weekly)

The team pipeline review is distinct from individual 1:1s. It serves a different purpose: surfacing risk in the team's aggregate pipeline, sharing best practices across reps, and creating peer accountability.

Agenda: 60-Minute Weekly Team Pipeline Review
  1. Pipeline Coverage Check (10 min) Manager reviews team aggregate coverage ratio vs. target. Calls out reps below 3× coverage. Not a blame session — a data conversation.
  2. Deal Spotlights — 2–3 deals (30 min) Rep presents their most important deal in MEDDICC format (5 min each). Team asks questions. Manager coaches in front of peers. Drives learning across team, not just individual.
  3. Stuck Deal Clinic (10 min) One rep brings a deal that's stalled or confusing. Team brainstorms re-engagement strategy. Creates ownership and creative problem solving.
  4. Win/Loss Flash (5 min) Any win or loss from the prior week reviewed in one sentence each. What worked? What did we learn?
  5. Manager Share (5 min) Manager shares one piece of competitive intel, one buyer trend, one playbook update. Keeps team connected to market reality.
Pipeline Review Anti-Pattern

Do not go rep-by-rep through every deal. This produces 90-minute status update marathons where every rep except the one presenting is bored. Instead, use the deal spotlight + stuck deal clinic format — it's selective, high-engagement, and actually develops skills.

The Structured Deal Review (MEDDICC-Based)

A deal review is a deep-dive coaching session on a specific high-priority deal. It differs from pipeline review in depth and purpose: the goal is deal strategy, not status reporting.

MEDDICC Deal Review Framework
MEDDICC Letter Questions Manager Asks Red Flags
M — Metrics What is the quantified business impact for the buyer? What's the ROI case in their terms? Rep says "they just want better efficiency" — no hard numbers
E — Economic Buyer Who has final budget authority? Have you spoken to them? What do they care about? Rep has never met EB; "my champion handles that"
D — Decision Criteria What are their formal and informal criteria? How do we rank against each? Rep doesn't know criteria; discovered criteria late
D — Decision Process What steps happen between now and signed contract? Who approves at each step? Rep says "they just need to review internally" — no specifics
I — Identify Pain What is the specific, quantified business pain? Is it acknowledged by the EB? Pain is rep-invented, not buyer-owned
C — Champion Who is your champion? Have you tested them? What have they done internally for you? Champion hasn't done anything requiring internal capital
C — Competition Who are we competing against? What's our relative position on each decision criterion? Rep doesn't know who else is in the evaluation
Deal Review Discipline

The deal review is not a "how can I help you close this?" session. It is a Socratic coaching session where the manager asks questions and the rep works to answer them. If the rep can't answer a MEDDICC question, that is the coaching output — the rep now knows exactly what they need to go find out.

The Forecast Call — Weekly or Biweekly Rollup

Forecast accuracy is a lagging indicator of pipeline quality and deal qualification discipline. The forecast call is both a management tool and a coaching lever — reps who learn to forecast accurately are, by definition, reps who qualify deals rigorously.

The Forecast Call Hierarchy

Forecasts roll up from the bottom: each rep commits to their AE-level forecast, which rolls to the manager, which rolls to the CRO. Each level applies judgment.

Level Frequency Duration Input Output
Rep → Manager Weekly (in 1:1) 5–10 min CRM stage + MEDDICC score + rep gut feel Rep commit, upside, pipeline
Manager → VP/CRO Weekly or biweekly 30 min Rep commits + manager adjustment Team commit, risk-adjusted call
VP/CRO → CEO/Board Monthly / Board cadence 60 min All team commits + market signals Company revenue forecast with ranges
Forecast Categories — Definitions That Must Be Standardized
Category Rep Definition Close Probability Manager Treatment
Commit I will stake my credibility this closes this quarter ≥90% Include in forecast; flag if misses
Best Case Could close if everything goes right 50–89% Include partial credit; monitor closely
Pipeline Active deal, not expected to close this quarter <50% Not in forecast; tracked for next quarter
Omitted CRM opportunity but rep explicitly excludes <20% Watch list; ask why in next 1:1
Forecast Accuracy Tracking
Accuracy = |Forecast − Actuals| / Actuals × 100%
Target: ±10% accuracy at the team level. Individual rep accuracy target: ±15%. Track by rep, by quarter, by cohort.

The Quarterly Business Review (QBR) with Each Rep

The manager-to-rep QBR is distinct from the all-hands QBR. It is a private, deep performance review and forward planning session. It is the most important career development conversation in the management cadence.

QBR Structure: 90-Minute Individual Rep QBR
  1. Trailing 90-Day Performance Review (25 min) Review quota attainment, pipeline metrics, activity metrics, and win/loss data. No surprises — these should all have been discussed in 1:1s. The QBR codifies patterns, not individual events.
  2. Skill Assessment (20 min) Manager and rep jointly assess the rep's skills against the competency rubric: discovery, demo, objection handling, negotiation, MEDDICC application, pipeline creation, forecasting accuracy. Where are the gaps? Ranked by revenue impact.
  3. Forward-Looking 90-Day Plan (25 min) Rep presents their Q+1 plan: territory focus, key accounts targeted, pipeline creation plan, quota attainment roadmap. Manager challenges assumptions. They agree on 2–3 specific priorities.
  4. Career Development (20 min) Where does the rep want to be in 12 months? 24 months? What specific milestones must they hit to get there? Manager offers candid assessment of trajectory. Action items with dates.
QBR Failure Mode

The QBR becomes a backward-looking post-mortem focused entirely on what went wrong last quarter. The rep leaves feeling judged, not developed. The forward-looking plan and career development sections are the most important — they create ownership and motivation for the next 90 days.

Field Coaching and Call Shadowing

No amount of 1:1 conversation can replace direct observation of a rep selling. Field coaching (or call shadowing via Gong/Chorus) is the most impactful coaching activity available to a manager.

Gong/Chorus-Based Call Review Protocol
  1. Pre-selection Manager identifies one call per rep per week to review. Prioritize deal-critical calls (discovery calls on large opportunities, late-stage calls before commit). Use Gong Deals to surface high-risk deals automatically.
  2. Pre-listen (Manager only) Manager listens to call before 1:1. Uses Gong comment feature to timestamp key moments. Identifies one primary coaching focus (maximum two) to avoid overwhelming rep.
  3. Collaborative Review in 1:1 Manager and rep review the specific timestamped moments together. Manager uses Socratic method: "What were you trying to accomplish here? What happened instead? What would you do differently?" Rep self-discovers most corrections.
  4. Role-Play the Alternative For the primary coaching focus, immediately practice the corrected behavior in a role-play. Do not let the lesson be abstract. Make it muscular.
  5. Commit to Next Application Rep commits specifically: "In my next discovery call with [Company], I will apply this by..." Manager follows up in next 1:1.
Gong Scorecards

Build a Gong scorecard aligned to your discovery or demo methodology. Score calls against: question ratio (talk-to-listen), number of discovery questions asked before pitching, next steps defined with specific date, mention of MEDDICC qualification elements. Track trend over time per rep.

Inspect What You Expect

The single most powerful principle in sales management. If you expect something from a rep — pipeline coverage, CRM hygiene, discovery question quality — you must inspect it visibly and consistently. What you inspect signals what you value.

The Inspection Framework
Expectation How to Inspect Frequency Signal to Rep
Pipeline coverage ≥3× Salesforce/HubSpot coverage report in every 1:1 Weekly Coverage matters more than any single deal
CRM data hygiene Spot-check 5 deals per rep per week; flag missing fields Weekly Data quality is a professional standard
Discovery call quality Gong call review with scoring rubric Weekly per rep How you sell is as important as what you sell
Mutual action plan on every deal ≥$25K Request MAP link in deal record during review Every deal review Process discipline separates winners from hopeful thinkers
Multi-threading (≥3 contacts on every enterprise deal) Contact count in Salesforce opportunity Weekly pipeline review Single-threaded deals die when champions leave

Span of Control Benchmarks and Manager Economics

Manager span of control directly determines coaching quality. Too many reps per manager means coaching time per rep collapses below the threshold of effectiveness. Too few means management overhead is destroying unit economics.

Field AE / Enterprise AE
6–8 reps
Complex deals require intensive per-rep coaching time. Beyond 8, coaching collapses.
Inside Sales / Mid-Market AE
8–12 reps
Shorter cycles allow lighter per-deal coaching. Gong scales efficiently here.
SDR / BDR Team
12–15 reps
Highly structured activity; coaching is repetitive and scalable. Metrics drive accountability.
Manager OTE as % of Team OTE
12–18%
Manager cost must be justified by team performance delta (typically 20–30% uplift).
Manager ROI Calculation
Manager ROI = (Coached Team Revenue − Benchmark Team Revenue) / Manager Fully-Loaded Cost
Best-in-class managers generate 2–4× their cost in incremental team revenue vs. an unmanaged peer group

The Performance Improvement Plan (PIP)

A PIP is not a punitive tool — it is the formal documentation of a developmental or exit decision. It should only be deployed after informal coaching has failed, and it should be structured to produce a clear binary outcome: improvement or dignified exit.

When to Initiate a PIP
  • Rep has missed quota for 2+ consecutive quarters with no clear external explanation
  • Rep has failed to pass a certification milestone twice after remediation coaching
  • Rep's pipeline quality consistently falls below team minimum thresholds
  • Behavioral issues: CRM non-compliance, poor forecast hygiene, poor collaboration
PIP Structure — 30/60/90 Day Plan
  1. Specific Measurable Objectives Not "improve pipeline quality." Specific: "Pipeline coverage must exceed 3× quota. All opportunities must have MEDDICC minimum populated. Gong discovery call score must reach ≥75 by Day 30."
  2. Intensive Coaching Support Manager commits to bi-weekly 1:1s (double normal cadence). Gong review every week. One live call co-pilot per week. Rep must not feel abandoned — PIP is a development tool.
  3. Clear Review Gates Day 30 checkpoint: met all objectives? If yes, continue. If no, manager escalates to HR, considers role reassignment. Day 60: same assessment. Day 90: final outcome decision.
  4. Documentation All PIP meetings documented in writing. Manager keeps record of every coaching intervention, every checkpoint, every outcome. Legal protection and fairness both require this.
PIP Failure Mode

The "drive-by PIP" — manager puts someone on a PIP without prior coaching history, without specific measurable objectives, and without genuine support. This is neither ethically defensible nor legally safe. If you can't point to 3+ months of documented 1:1 coaching before the PIP, you are not managing, you are ambushing.
