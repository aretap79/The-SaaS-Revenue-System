The Role of the SDR in the Revenue System

The Sales Development Representative (SDR) exists at the top of the revenue funnel. Their singular job is to generate qualified pipeline — not to close deals. Understanding this boundary is everything. SDRs who try to sell, and AEs who resist taking qualified handoffs, are both symptoms of a dysfunctional revenue system.

Definition: SDR vs. BDR

The terms SDR (Sales Development Representative) and BDR (Business Development Representative) are used interchangeably at most companies, but carry functional distinctions in precision-designed orgs. SDRs typically qualify inbound leads generated by marketing. BDRs typically prospect outbound into cold accounts. In practice, most reps do both; the distinction matters most for metrics design and quota calibration.

Inbound SDR vs. Outbound SDR: Fundamentally Different Roles

Inbound SDRs receive leads from marketing — form fills, content downloads, demo requests, free trial sign-ups, webinar attendees. Their job is rapid response (the "speed-to-lead" problem: studies show that responding to an inbound lead within 5 minutes increases connect rate by 9x vs. 30 minutes), qualification against ICP criteria, and routing to the correct AE or deal motion.

Outbound SDRs begin with a blank slate. They prospect into cold accounts using research, signals, and sequencing. They generate awareness in accounts that have never heard of your product, create curiosity, diagnose pain, and book discovery meetings with AEs. The conversion rates are radically different: a strong inbound SDR converts 25–40% of MQLs to SQLs; a strong outbound SDR books 3–8 meetings per week across 80–120 daily activities.

When to Build the SDR Function

Do not build an SDR team before you have a repeatable sales playbook, a documented ICP, and at least 2–3 AEs closing deals consistently. SDRs amplify a process that already works — they cannot create one. Founders who hire SDRs before achieving product-market fit waste 6–12 months of burn. The founders must sell first, document what works, then hand the playbook to SDRs.

SDR Team Structure: Ratios, Span of Control, and Manager Design

Getting the structural design right determines whether your SDR team scales or collapses. The critical variables are the SDR-to-AE ratio, the manager span of control, and whether SDRs are aligned by territory, segment, or function.

SDR-to-AE Ratio (Standard)
1 SDR : 2–3 AEs (Mid-Market) | 1 SDR : 1–2 AEs (Enterprise)
The ratio depends on deal complexity, sales cycle length, and AE capacity to work pipeline. Enterprise AEs need more qualified pipeline per deal; mid-market AEs can handle more accounts simultaneously.
Manager Span of Control

A first-line SDR manager should carry a span of 6–10 direct reports. Below 6, the economics don't support a dedicated manager (consider a player-coach model). Above 10, coaching quality degrades — the manager spends all time on admin and loses the ability to listen to calls, review emails, and run meaningful 1:1s. The ideal team is 8 SDRs per manager for a pure coaching role.

Org Size SDR Team AE Team Manager Structure Recommended Model
Seed / Series A 0–2 SDRs 1–3 AEs AE or founder manages SDR Founders sell; 1 SDR max
Series B 3–8 SDRs 6–15 AEs 1 SDR Manager (player-coach) Hire SDR Manager first, then build team
Series C+ 10–30+ SDRs 20–60+ AEs Director + multiple team leads Segment by geo, market, or function
Alignment Models: Territory vs. Segment vs. Pooled

Territory-aligned: Each SDR is assigned to specific AEs and covers their territory. Creates clear ownership and AE accountability for SDR output. Risk: SDR productivity is dependent on AE responsiveness and acceptance rates.

Segment-aligned: SDRs specialize by market segment (SMB, Mid-Market, Enterprise). Creates messaging expertise and faster ramp. Best for companies with materially different buyer profiles across segments.

Pooled (centralized): SQLs go into a queue and AEs pull from it. Maximizes SDR output but creates less accountability and can lead to AE cherry-picking. Rarely recommended for complex sales motions.

The SDR Job: What They Actually Do Every Day

The daily job of an SDR is a structured set of activities designed to move cold prospects through to a qualified meeting. Understanding the daily workflow — from target identification to meeting booking — is essential for hiring, training, and managing to the right outcomes.

  1. Account Research and Target Selection (30–45 min/day) Identify the day's target accounts using ICP filters, intent signals, trigger events (funding rounds, leadership changes, product launches, job postings in relevant departments). Prioritize accounts where pain is most likely and timing is favorable. Use tools like LinkedIn Sales Navigator, ZoomInfo, Bombora, or G2 intent data.
  2. Contact Identification and Data Enrichment (20–30 min/day) Find the right buyer persona within each target account. Typical SDR buyers: VP of Sales, CRO, VP of Revenue Operations, CFO, or functional buyer aligned to product use case. Verify email, phone, and LinkedIn. Use tools like Apollo.io, Lusha, Cognism, Hunter.io. Ensure data accuracy before sequencing — bad data is the #1 killer of SDR productivity.
  3. Outreach Execution (3–4 hours/day) Execute multi-channel sequences: cold calls, personalized emails, LinkedIn messages. Outbound SDRs average 50–80 dials per day (with a 6–12% connect rate) and send 25–40 personalized emails. Inbound SDRs respond to MQLs within 5 minutes, qualify against BANT/MEDDICC criteria, and route to appropriate AE.
  4. Meeting Booking and Confirmation (30 min/day) Once a prospect agrees to a meeting, send calendar invite with prep materials, confirm the meeting 24 hours in advance (reduces no-show rate from 30% to 12%), brief the AE with account context, notes from the conversation, pain discovered, and stakeholders identified.
  5. CRM Hygiene and Documentation (20–30 min/day) Log all activity in CRM: calls made, emails sent, connections, responses, objections, meeting outcomes. This data is the management layer — without it, managers cannot coach, and revenue leaders cannot forecast pipeline generation accurately.
  6. Training and Skill Development (30–60 min/day, early ramp) During ramp, SDRs spend significant time on product training, industry research, call recording review, roleplay with manager, and messaging refinement. Ongoing: listen to 2 recorded calls per week even at full productivity, attend sales all-hands, participate in win/loss reviews.

SDR Quota Setting Formula
Monthly SQL Quota = (AE Pipeline Coverage Target × # AEs Covered) ÷ Average Deal Size
Example: AE needs $400K in pipeline/month, average deal size $80K → SDR needs 5 SQLs/month per AE covered. If SDR covers 3 AEs → 15 SQLs/month quota. Validate against historical conversion rates.

The SDR Ramp Timeline: 60–90 Days to Full Productivity

Most companies under-invest in SDR ramp and over-expect on early output. A well-designed ramp reduces early attrition, builds muscle memory, and produces SDRs who hit quota sustainably — not reps who burn out in month 3 because they were never properly trained.

Month Focus Activities Quota % Success Criteria
Month 1 Training & Orientation Product deep-dive, ICP study, competitive landscape, messaging certification, shadowing 10+ AE calls, 20+ recorded call reviews, roleplay certification 0% Passes product quiz, ICP quiz, delivers 3 mock cold calls without coaching cues
Month 2 Live prospecting with support Begin full outreach sequences, 3–4 meetings/week with manager listening, daily debrief, sequence A/B testing, first 3–5 SQLs passed 50% Books 5–8 meetings, passes 3–5 SQLs, AE acceptance rate >60%
Month 3 Full productivity Full activity load, weekly 1:1 only, self-manages sequence, identifies own target accounts, calibrates messaging independently 100% Hits 15–20 SQLs/month, pipeline created at target, AE acceptance >70%
Month 4–6 Optimization & ownership Refines own messaging, begins specializing in vertical or persona, contributes playbook improvements, participates in peer coaching 100%+ Consistent quota attainment, >80% SQL acceptance, identifies as operator not just executor
Ramp Best Practice

The most effective ramp programs pair SDRs with a senior AE "buddy" for weeks 2–4, with the new SDR listening to AE discovery calls daily. Nothing accelerates product and pain comprehension faster than hearing real buyers articulate why they bought. Classroom training alone produces SDRs who know the product but can't sell. Call shadowing produces SDRs who understand the buyer.

Hiring the SDR: Scorecard Criteria and Interview Design

The most common SDR hiring mistake is over-indexing on prior SDR experience. The skills that make a great SDR are largely trainable given the right raw ingredients. The raw ingredients are not: coachability, work ethic, and verbal communication acuity are near-impossible to install post-hire.

The 5 Core SDR Hiring Criteria (Roberge Scorecard Adapted)

1. Coachability — Will they apply feedback immediately, without ego? Ask for a time they received hard feedback and what they changed. Watch for defensiveness, rationalization, or blame-shifting.

2. Work Ethic and Persistence — Do they have a track record of sustained effort in difficult environments? Athletes, commission-only sales, high-output academic environments, or demanding service roles all signal this trait.

3. Curiosity — Do they ask good questions? Do they research the company before the interview? A great SDR is endlessly curious about their prospect's business — because curiosity is how they find pain.

4. Verbal Communication Acuity — Can they articulate ideas clearly, concisely, and confidently in real-time? The cold call is the test. Do an in-interview roleplay — ask them to cold call you. Nothing reveals verbal communication faster.

5. Process Adherence — Can they follow a structured system reliably? SDRs who "freestyle" the playbook produce inconsistent results. Ask about a time they had to follow a process they didn't personally design.

SDR Compensation Design: OTE, Base/Variable, and Accelerators

SDR compensation must balance enough base salary to attract talent with enough variable incentive to drive the right behaviors. The most common design error is paying too high a base relative to variable — SDRs who are comfortable on base alone have no reason to push through the rejection that outbound requires.

SDR OTE Structure
OTE = Base (60–70%) + Variable (30–40%)
Example: $70K OTE = $42–49K base + $21–28K variable. Variable paid monthly on SQL quota attainment. Accelerators: 125% payout at 110%+ quota, 150% payout at 125%+ quota.
Market / Segment Typical OTE Range Base Variable Primary Quota Metric
SF / NYC (Tier 1) $75K–$95K $50K–$65K $25K–$35K SQLs + Pipeline Created
Austin / Denver / Boston (Tier 2) $60K–$80K $40K–$55K $20K–$28K SQLs Generated
Remote / Tier 3 Markets $50K–$70K $35K–$48K $15K–$25K SQLs Generated
Enterprise BDR $80K–$110K $55K–$75K $25K–$40K Pipeline Created ($)
Compensation Design Principle

Pay primarily on SQLs generated, not meetings booked. Meetings booked incentivizes quantity over quality — SDRs will book meetings with unqualified prospects just to hit the number. SQLs accepted by AEs align SDR incentives with actual pipeline quality. Add a secondary bonus (10–15% of variable) on pipeline created (in dollars) to keep SDRs focused on deal size, not just volume.

The SDR Playbook: Codifying the System

A well-documented SDR playbook is the difference between a team that scales and a team that depends on individual brilliance. Every system, process, and decision that a great SDR makes naturally should be written down, tested, and taught.

What the Playbook Must Include
  • ICP Definition: Firmographic and behavioral criteria for target accounts; persona definition for contacts; negative ICP criteria (who to avoid)
  • Prospecting Lists: How to build a list using approved tools; data enrichment requirements; list review and approval workflow
  • Outreach Sequences: Day-by-day cadence across email, phone, LinkedIn; exact templates with personalization instructions; sequence length (typically 10–14 touches over 21–28 days)
  • Messaging Frameworks: Subject line formulas; email body structure; cold call openers; LinkedIn connection messages; objection handling scripts
  • SQL Qualification Criteria: Exact definition of what constitutes a qualified lead; BANT/MEDDICC questions to ask; handoff documentation requirements
  • Handoff Process: CRM fields required; briefing note format for AE; meeting confirmation process; no-show protocol
Playbook Failure Mode

A playbook that lives in a shared Google Drive folder and is never reviewed is not a playbook — it is documentation theater. The real playbook is what managers reinforce in weekly 1:1s, what is role-played in team meetings, and what is updated quarterly based on A/B testing results. A playbook that isn't iterated on is a playbook that slowly dies.

SDR-to-AE Handoff: The Most Dangerous Moment in the Funnel

The SQL handoff is where pipeline is either preserved or destroyed. A poor handoff — missing context, no briefing, late AE response, or an SDR who books unqualified meetings to hit quota — wastes the SDR's prospecting work and creates negative experiences for prospects who were qualified to buy.

SQL Definition (Minimum Standard)
SQL = ICP-Fit Account + Identified Pain + Authority to Evaluate + Meeting Confirmed
Optional but preferred: budget range discussed, timeline for decision established, 2+ stakeholders identified. MEDDICC overlay: at minimum Metrics (pain quantified), Economic Buyer identified, and Decision Process understood at a high level.
Handoff Element SDR Responsibility AE Responsibility SLA
CRM Qualification Notes Complete before meeting confirmation Review before meeting SDR: same day as meeting booking
SQL Briefing Document Account overview, pain discovered, stakeholders, conversation notes Add discovery plan before meeting SDR: 24h before meeting; AE: 1h before
AE Acceptance / Rejection N/A Accept or reject with reason within 24h AE: within 24 hours of SQL submission
Meeting Confirmation Confirm with prospect 24h in advance, include AE on thread Confirm receipt; send prep questions if appropriate SDR: 24h before; AE: optional add-on
No-Show Protocol Notify AE immediately; attempt to reschedule same day Reach out independently with alternate times SDR: within 15 min of no-show
AE Acceptance Criteria

AEs should have the right to reject an SQL that does not meet minimum criteria — but rejections must be documented with specific reasons (wrong ICP, no identified pain, wrong title/persona, prospect not ready). Rejection rates above 25% signal a misaligned SQL definition and require manager intervention and SDR recalibration. Rejection rates below 10% may signal AEs are being too permissive and accepting poor-quality leads that kill forecast accuracy.

SDR Management Cadence: Daily, Weekly, Monthly

SDR management is fundamentally about activity accountability, skill development, and motivation. The cadence creates the structure for all three. Without a consistent cadence, the SDR function drifts — activity metrics drop, messaging stagnates, and attrition accelerates.

Inbound SDR vs. Outbound SDR: Running Both Motions

Companies that run both inbound and outbound SDR motions must design separate playbooks, separate metrics, and separate management approaches. Blending the two into a single role without clear separation produces reps who under-perform on both.

The Speed-to-Lead Imperative (Inbound)

Research consistently shows that the odds of qualifying an inbound lead drop by 10× if you wait more than 5 minutes to respond vs. calling within 1 minute. Inbound SDR teams must be designed around response speed. This means: real-time MQL alerts, SLA enforcement for first contact, and staffing models that cover all hours when inbound volume is high (often including weekend coverage for PLG motions where prospects sign up on their own time).

The Persistence Imperative (Outbound)

Studies on outbound SDR effectiveness show that 80% of meetings are booked after the 5th or 6th touch, yet most SDRs give up after 2–3 attempts. The outbound SDR playbook must be designed for 10–14 touches across 3–4 weeks. Persistence paired with personalization — not spam — is what converts cold outreach into qualified meetings.

When NOT to Build an SDR Team

The SDR function is one of the most frequently premature hires in early-stage SaaS. Understanding when NOT to build this team is as important as understanding how to build it.

Pre-Conditions Required Before Hiring SDRs

1. Product-Market Fit: You have 10+ customers who would be "very disappointed" without your product, and they are not close personal contacts of the founders.

2. A Documented Sales Playbook: Founders or early AEs have closed at least 20–30 deals using a repeatable process. The ICP is specific. The value proposition is proven. The objections are documented. You can describe, in writing, how a deal moves from cold contact to signed contract.

3. AE Capacity: You have AEs who are ready, willing, and capable of running discovery with SDR-sourced leads. An SDR feeding leads into an AE team that can't close is pipeline destruction.

4. Infrastructure: CRM is operational, lead routing is configured, sequence tooling is in place, and someone has authority to manage and coach the SDRs. An unmanaged SDR is an unmanaged cost center.

Common SDR Mistakes and Fixes

Mistake Why It Happens Fix

The SDR-to-AE Promotion Model

The highest-performing revenue organizations use the SDR role as a training ground for AEs. SDRs who earn promotion have already learned product, ICP, messaging, and objection handling. They come into their AE role with context no external hire can match. Design an explicit promotion path with measurable gates — and make it known in the hiring process. It attracts the most ambitious candidates and reduces attrition.
