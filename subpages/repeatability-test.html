Roberge's Core Thesis

The Scaling Trap — From The Sales Acceleration Formula
Roberge has described a test for repeatability: if three different salespeople can execute the same motion successfully across three different territories over three consecutive months, the motion is repeatable enough to scale.
— Mark Roberge, former SVP Sales at HubSpot, author of The Sales Acceleration Formula

Roberge's core insight is that most SaaS companies scale their sales teams before they have proven that the motion is repeatable. They hire aggressively after a great quarter — but great quarters from 3 exceptional reps are not evidence of a scalable process. Repeatability means the motion works for an average rep following the playbook, not just for the founders or the 3 star performers who happen to be on the team right now.

The Cost of Premature Scaling

Premature scaling produces predictable failure patterns: e.g., significantly elevated first-year churn, materially higher S&M spend with poor payback, and extended CAC recovery timelines. The math is unforgiving. Test before you scale.

The 3 Repeatability Tests

1
The Playbook Test
Can a new rep follow the playbook and produce results?
Hypothesis Being Tested
The sales process is documented, taught, and followed — and produces consistent outcomes when a new rep adheres to it. Results are not dependent on the founders' personal relationships or on 1-2 star performers with unique skills.
How to Run This Test from CRM Data
  • Take your last 4 cohorts of new hires (grouped by start date). For each cohort: what was their 6-month attainment rate?
  • Compare the 6-month attainment of reps hired before you had a documented playbook vs. after. If post-playbook cohorts ramp faster, the playbook is working.
  • Look at deal stage distribution by rep: do newer reps have deals progressing through stages at the same velocity as veterans? If newer reps' deals stall at Stage 2 while veterans' move to Stage 3, the process transfer is not working.
  • Survey ramping reps: "Rate your confidence in running discovery independently (1-10)." "Rate your confidence in handling the top 3 objections (1-10)." Average confidence below 7 signals playbook gaps.
  • Have a rep who joined 90 days ago run a live discovery call with a real prospect, with the manager observing. Does the rep follow the playbook? Does it produce the expected outcome?
Pass Condition
Average 6-month attainment >60% for post-playbook cohorts. Win rate variance between best and worst ramping rep <2x. Reps can name the top 5 ICP signals without prompting.
Fail Condition
6-month attainment below 40%. New reps consistently describe their biggest challenge as "not knowing who to call" or "not knowing how to handle objections." Founders still closing most deals personally.
2
The Consistency Test
Is win rate consistent across reps — less than 2x variance between best and worst?
Hypothesis Being Tested
The team's win rate distribution is tight — results are not entirely explained by individual talent variance. The system (ICP, playbook, pipeline quality) produces consistent outcomes regardless of which rep is working the deal.
How to Run This Test from CRM Data
  • Calculate win rate for every fully-ramped rep (exclude reps in first 4 months). Compare highest win rate vs. lowest win rate among full-ramp reps only.
  • Roberge's standard: less than 2x variance. If your best rep wins 45% of deals and your worst wins 10%, that is 4.5x variance — a system problem, not a rep problem.
  • Segment win rates by deal source (inbound vs. outbound). Is win rate variance lower within a specific deal source? That tells you the source quality is the variable, not the rep.
  • Look at stage conversion rates by rep: does one rep convert Stage 2 to Stage 3 at 70% while others convert at 20%? That gap indicates a specific skill deficit, not general performance difference.
  • Correlate win rate variance with ICP score of deals: are low-win-rate reps working worse-fit accounts? If so, you have a pipeline assignment problem — not a rep performance problem.
Pass Condition
Best vs. worst full-ramp rep win rate variance <2x. No single rep accounts for >30% of team revenue. Team win rate is within 5pp of the historical average across all quarters.
Fail Condition
Win rate variance >3x. 1-2 reps account for 50%+ of team revenue — the "hero rep" dependency. Removing any single rep would materially impact forecast. Win rate is extremely quarter-specific (great Q1, terrible Q2) — driven by individual timing, not process.
3
The ICP Quality Test
Is the ICP consistently producing the right customers — NRR of recently acquired cohorts meeting the standard?
Hypothesis Being Tested
The customers you're selling to are genuinely good fits — they get value from the product, renew, and expand. The ICP is not just a lead generation filter; it is predictive of customer success. If your newest customer cohorts are churning at higher rates than older cohorts, your ICP has drifted or your selling standards have relaxed under pipeline pressure.
How to Run This Test from CRM Data + CS Systems
  • Take your last 4 quarterly customer acquisition cohorts. Calculate the NRR at the 12-month mark for each cohort. Is NRR improving, stable, or declining quarter over quarter?
  • Compare NRR of customers acquired from ICP-fit accounts (ICP score >80) vs. non-ICP-fit accounts. If non-ICP customers churn 2x faster, your ICP is directionally correct — your problem is enforcement.
  • Look at 90-day health scores for new cohorts. If health scores are declining for recently onboarded customers vs. older cohorts, you are either selling to worse-fit accounts or your CS capacity is insufficient for the onboarding volume.
  • Interview your 10 most recently churned customers (within 12 months). What do they have in common? Are they outside your historical ICP? Were specific reps responsible for selling them? Are there use case patterns in churn that indicate ICP drift?
  • Calculate the "ICP mismatch rate" in your pipeline: what % of Stage 2+ opportunities have an ICP score below your minimum threshold? If 30%+ of pipeline is sub-ICP-threshold, your lead generation is too broad.
Pass Condition
NRR of most recent 2 acquisition cohorts within 10pp of historical NRR baseline. Year-1 churn rate below 15%. ICP-fit customers renew at 2x+ the rate of non-ICP-fit customers (confirms ICP accuracy).
Fail Condition
NRR of most recent cohort 15pp+ below prior-year baseline — you are selling to worse-fit customers. Year-1 churn above 25%. Churn interviews reveal recurring ICP mismatch themes. CS team spending disproportionate time on new accounts relative to older accounts.

Signs You Are NOT Ready to Scale

High Variance in Rep Performance
Win rate range of 10-50% across fully-ramped reps. No identifiable pattern in who succeeds and who doesn't. Results depend on who the rep happens to know, not on repeatable process.
Declining NRR in Newer Cohorts
Customers acquired in the last 2-4 quarters are churning faster than historical averages. The sales team is selling to accounts that can't achieve success — a ticking time bomb that only appears at scale.
Win Rate Below Segment Benchmark
Mid-Market win rate consistently below 15% (benchmark 20-30%) over 4 quarters. This is a signal that either the ICP is wrong, the product-market fit in that segment is insufficient, or the competitive positioning is broken.
Ramp Time Extending
New reps are taking longer to ramp each successive cohort. Cohort from 12 months ago ramped in 5 months; the most recent cohort is at 9 months with the same quota. The playbook is degrading or the ICP definition has drifted.
Founders / A-Players Carrying the Team
The top 2-3 performers account for 60%+ of team revenue. Remove them from the data and the remaining team looks like it's barely surviving. This means the motion is not yet systematized — it is a talent sport, not a process sport.
No Documented, Used Playbook
"We have a playbook" means "It exists in our shared drive but nobody uses it." The test: can a new hire get through their first customer call without asking a founder for help? If not, the playbook is not sufficient for autonomous rep operation.

The Three Stages of GTM Evolution

🔬
Finding Product-Market Fit
Do customers need this? Will they pay? Does it work? This is the product stage — the primary signal is customer retention in early cohorts, not revenue scale.
Stage: Pre-$1M ARR
🗺
Finding GTM Fit
Which customers buy? Through which channel? With what message? Can a rep learn this and repeat it? This is where the Repeatability Tests are run — the transition point to scaling.
Stage: $1M–$5M ARR
🚀
Scaling GTM
Add headcount, expand channels, invest in marketing at scale, enter new segments. Only safe to do this after GTM fit is proven and all 3 repeatability tests pass consistently.
Stage: $5M+ ARR

The most common strategic mistake in SaaS is jumping from Stage 1 (PMF found) directly to Stage 3 (scaling GTM) without spending enough time in Stage 2 (finding GTM fit). The result: expensive scaling of a motion that doesn't work, followed by a painful retrenchment — often a 20-40% headcount reduction and a board-driven CRO change. The Repeatability Tests are the gate between Stage 2 and Stage 3.

The Scaling Decision Rule

All 3 Repeatability Tests must pass for 2 consecutive quarters before you commit to a significant headcount scaling plan. One passing quarter can be luck. Two consecutive quarters is a trend. Three consecutive quarters before you triple headcount is the professional standard.

Leading and Lagging Indicators of Repeatability

Leading: Win rate variance (Q-over-Q trend), ramp efficiency by cohort, ICP mismatch rate in pipeline, playbook usage rate, rep confidence survey scores Lagging: Year-1 NRR by cohort, win rate stability, ramp time by cohort, rep performance variance, voluntary attrition of ramping reps
