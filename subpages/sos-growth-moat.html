What Is Stage 3?
Stage 3 begins the moment both prior stages are validated. The question changes entirely — from "does this work?" to "how fast can we grow without breaking it?"

In Stage 1, you discovered whether customers genuinely succeed with your product. In Stage 2, you proved your sales and marketing motion generates positive unit economics. Stage 3 accepts both as given and asks: what is the maximum sustainable growth rate?

The Three Shifts at Stage 3
DimensionStage 1–2 FocusStage 3 Focus
The Goal Proving unit economics Maximizing revenue growth rate
The Risk Scaling too early Not scaling fast enough
The Metrics LIR achievement rate, LIUE approaching 3 NRR, market share, moat construction — with LIUE > 3 and LIR ≥ threshold as guardrails
Key Principle — Stage 3

"Scale as fast as possible without losing GTM Fit (LIUE > 3) or PMF (LIR ≥ threshold)."
LIUE and LIR no longer need to be improved — they need to be preserved. The moment either degrades, you slow down and fix before continuing to add headcount.

Why Stage 3 Is Hard

The most common Series B failure mode is confusing revenue growth with sustainable growth. Companies hire large batches of salespeople, open new markets simultaneously, and launch new demand gen channels — all at once. The result: diluted management attention, degraded playbook fidelity, corrupted LIUE measurement, and a cohort of underperforming reps who are expensive to remove.

Stage 3 discipline means growing as fast as your gauges allow — not as fast as your board demands.

The Speedometer — Knowing When to Accelerate
Two continuously monitored gauges define how fast you can grow. Both must stay green. The moment either turns red, you stop adding headcount and diagnose the cause.
Gauge 1
LIR Cohort Chart
Tracks the percentage of new customers hitting LIR by acquisition cohort over time. Each monthly cohort should be achieving LIR at the same rate as the cohorts that proved PMF. If the rate drops below threshold, STOP scaling and investigate — it means the playbook is not transferring correctly to new reps or new customers.
Green: LIR rate ≥ threshold Yellow: within 10 pts Red: below threshold
Gauge 2
LIUE Dashboard
Tracks the LTV/CAC ratio by cohort and by segment. All four LIUE input metrics (SQLs per rep, Cost per SQL, SQL-to-Customer%, ACV) must stay on target. If LIUE drops below 3, STOP scaling — adding headcount when unit economics are degrading destroys value at scale.
Green: LIUE > 3 Yellow: 2.5–3 Red: LIUE < 2.5
Either Gauge Goes Red → Pause
Stop adding salespeople immediately. The signal is real — something in the playbook, the target market, or the onboarding has broken. Diagnose the root cause. Do not resume at pace until both gauges return to green for two consecutive months.
Both Gauges Stay Green → Accelerate
Both green means the playbook is scaling cleanly. Double the hiring pace. Add new demand gen channels. Begin investing in EXPERIMENT segments. The green light is your evidence-based authorization to move faster than you feel comfortable moving.
Scientific Answer to Board Pressure

When investors push you to hire 20 reps immediately: "We will hire 2 salespeople per month and monitor our leading indicators. If both speedometers stay green for 60 days, we will double to 4 per month. This is the fastest safe rate." Show the board the gauges. Let the data make the decision.

Segment Analysis: Scale, Experiment, or Ignore
The most dangerous analytical mistake in growth-stage companies is using consolidated metrics that average across all segments. A company with LIUE of 4 overall may have a segment with LIUE of 0.8 hidden inside a segment with LIUE of 7. That 0.8 segment is destroying value. The 7 segment is being underinvested. Consolidated metrics make both invisible.

Every product × market × channel combination must be evaluated independently using the Growth & Moat decision matrix. The three possible decisions:

Decision 1
SCALE

All criteria must be true:

  • LIUE > 3 (positive unit economics validated)
  • LIR achievement rate ≥ threshold (PMF maintained)
  • Sufficient TAM to justify continued investment
  • Proven, codified playbook in place

Action: Add headcount at 2/month cadence. Scale demand gen. Build moat.

Decision 2
EXPERIMENT

One or more conditions present:

  • LIUE between 1–3 (marginal economics — optimize before scaling)
  • LIR below threshold in some cohorts (PMF uncertain — investigate)
  • Emerging segment with promising early signals but insufficient data
  • GTM playbook not yet codified for this segment

Action: Small cross-functional team (2 AE + 1 CSM). Run the Stage 1 → Stage 2 sequence independently.

Decision 3
IGNORE

Any one condition present:

  • LIUE < 1 (actively destroying value)
  • LIR persistently below threshold (customers don't succeed)
  • Market too small or too competitive to build a moat
  • No credible path to LIUE > 3 within 12 months

Action: Reallocate resources. Do not invest management attention here.

Evaluating four segments independently reveals that consolidated metrics would have masked the International segment's value-destructive economics:

Scaling Pace: The 2-Per-Month Rule
The recommended pace for Growth & Moat: hire 2 salespeople per month for 6 months. Then review. Then decide whether to accelerate, maintain, or pause. This isn't conservative — it's scientific.
Why Not Hire in Batches?

Hiring 10 reps at once means: if the hiring scorecard is wrong, all 10 are wrong. If onboarding has a gap, all 10 experience it. You won't know for 6 months. By month 6, you've committed to all 10. With paced hiring, you catch problems in month 2 before months 3–6.

Why Paced Hiring Works: Four Mechanisms
1
Ramp StaggeringYou get early signals from Month 1 reps before you've committed to Month 6 reps. If Month 1 reps show a pattern of selling to wrong-fit customers, you catch it before it compounds.
2
Cohort LearningEach monthly cohort becomes a natural experiment. You iterate the hiring scorecard and onboarding based on what you learn from each cohort before the next begins.
3
Manager Span ControlEach manager can handle a controlled ramp. A manager with 2 new reps per month can maintain coaching quality. A manager with 10 new reps at once cannot. Quality of coaching → quality of early customer relationships.
4
LIR/LIUE ProtectionIf a cohort of reps sells to wrong-fit customers, the LIR drop appears in month 2–3, not month 6. You stop and fix before it cascades into a cohort of customers who churn at month 12.
The 6-Month Iteration Cycle
Month 1
Hire First 2 Reps
Apply the hiring scorecard rigorously. Begin structured onboarding. Set 30/60/90 milestones. Do not compromise on scorecard criteria under timeline pressure — a below-threshold hire costs 18+ months and damages LIUE.
Months 2–5
Hire 2 Per Month, Monitor Gauges
Track ramp speed, quota attainment rate, and quality of customers closed (LIR achievement by rep cohort). If Month 1 reps show LIR rates below threshold from their customer base, stop and investigate before Month 6 hiring.
Month 6
Scorecard Review
Compare top performers vs. bottom performers against the original scorecard. Which attributes actually predicted success? Which were false positives? Update the scorecard to reflect observed predictors, not assumed ones.
Month 7+
Accelerate or Maintain Based on Gauges
If both speedometers stayed green through the first 6 months: accelerate to 4 per month. If either broke: fix, then restart at 2. Update onboarding based on Month 1–6 learnings. Begin next iteration cycle.

Compensation in Stage 3: Add Promotion Path
Stage 2's 50/50 compensation structure is maintained. Stage 3 adds a data-driven, objective promotion path — because in high-growth companies, retaining your best reps is as strategically important as hiring new ones.
Maintain the 50/50 Commission Structure
  • 50% at contract close — rewards the sale
  • 50% when customer achieves LIR (within T + 30 day grace) — rewards the right sale
  • This structure ensures reps self-select better-fit customers. A rep who closes 10 poor-fit customers earns 50% of expected commission. A rep who closes 10 great-fit customers earns 100%.
Why Promotion Path Matters in Stage 3

In a 10-person company, career progression is visible. In a 100-person company, it's invisible unless you make it explicit. Ambitious top performers need a clear, objective path forward. Without it, they listen to recruiters. With it, they stay to build.

The No-Subjectivity Rule

Every promotion criterion must be measurable and objective. No "demonstrated leadership" without definition. No "ready in the manager's judgment." Subjective promotions create fairness perceptions and destroy culture. Objective promotions create predictability and trust.

Data-Driven Promotion Path — AE Track
AE1 → AE2
Achieve ≥110% quota attainment for 2 consecutive quarters AND LIR achievement rate > 75% of their closed customers. Both criteria required — a rep who sells to wrong-fit customers is not eligible regardless of quota attainment.
AE2 → Senior AE
≥110% quota attainment for 3 consecutive quarters AND LIR achievement rate maintained AND demonstrated active mentorship of 1+ new AE through ramp (verified by manager and mentee).
Senior AE → Team Lead
≥110% quota attainment for 4 consecutive quarters AND LIR achievement rate > threshold AND peer evaluation score > 8/10 from fellow ICs AND demonstrated coaching ability (at least 1 mentee who independently achieved quota attainment).
Team Lead → Sales Manager
Minimum 2 quarters as Team Lead with team quota attainment ≥100% AND team LIR rate ≥ threshold AND 360° eval from direct reports > 8/10. Transition to manager quota (team-based) replaces individual quota.
The 5 Sustainable Moats
Growth attracts competition. The "Moat" half of Growth & Moat is the deliberate construction of structural competitive advantages that make your position increasingly difficult to replicate — even by a better-funded or better-engineered competitor.
The Disruption Test

"If 10 brilliant ex-Google engineers replicated your product and sold it for half the price — why would you still win?" If you don't have a clear answer, you have a first-mover advantage, not a moat. First-mover advantages erode. Moats compound.

1
Network Effects
The product becomes more valuable as more customers use it. Each new customer increases value for all existing customers, creating a compounding advantage that replication cannot match — because replication can copy the product but not the network.
Examples: Marketplaces (more buyers attract more sellers), communication tools (more users = more utility), data networks (more data = smarter outputs).
How to Build Design product interactions that create value for existing users when new users join. Add data aggregation layers.
Leading Indicator DAU/MAU ratio, inter-user connections, data contributed per customer/month
Defensibility Extremely High
2
Brand & Category Ownership
Becoming the default mental association when someone thinks of your problem category. The goal is not brand awareness — it's category definition. When buyers search for a solution, they think of your name first.
Examples: Salesforce = CRM, Zoom = video calls, HubSpot = inbound marketing, Slack = team messaging. Each company did not just win the category — they named it.
How to Build Consistent thought leadership, category-level content, customer success stories at scale, analyst relations
Leading Indicator Branded search volume, share of voice, analyst placement, inbound % of pipeline
Defensibility High
3
Viral Distribution
Product-led growth where each customer naturally acquires additional customers through normal product use. Measured by the viral coefficient (K-factor): number of new users each existing user brings in. K > 1 creates exponential growth without proportional CAC increase.
Examples: Slack (invite teammates to a channel), Dropbox (share a folder → recipient needs an account), Calendly (share a scheduling link → sender exposes recipient to the product).
How to Build Design sharing moments into core workflows. Make the product's value visible to non-users. Reduce friction to join.
Leading Indicator K-factor (viral coefficient), % of new customers from existing-customer referral, organic signup rate
Defensibility High
4
Switching Costs
Deep product integration into customer workflows such that the cost of migrating to a competitor is high — in time, risk, and organizational disruption. Switching costs don't prevent churn from failing to deliver value, but they create meaningful friction for customers who are satisfied but curious about alternatives.
Examples: ERP systems (years of financial data and configured workflows), data platforms (petabytes of stored data), deeply integrated APIs (rewriting integrations is a multi-month engineering project).
How to Build Deliberate onboarding design that deepens integration. Data import and configuration tools. API ecosystem development. Multi-team adoption.
Leading Indicator Integrations per customer, data volume in platform, % of customer teams actively using product, multi-year contract rate
Defensibility High
5
Learning Algorithms
AI/ML models that improve in accuracy and performance as more customer data flows through them. Creates a compounding data moat: more customers → more data → better model → more customers. A new entrant cannot replicate this advantage without equivalent data volume, which requires equivalent market share — which they don't have.
Examples: Recommendation engines (Netflix, Spotify), predictive forecasting tools, fraud detection models, speech-to-text (more usage = more training = better accuracy).
How to Build Instrument data collection from day 1. Train models on customer behavior. Surface model outputs as product features to create a data flywheel.
Leading Indicator Model accuracy over time, training data volume, feature adoption rate of AI-powered features
Defensibility Extremely High
Demand Gen in Stage 3: Multiple Aligned Channels
Stage 2 proved one scalable demand gen channel. Stage 3 adds additional channels — but only using the same selection criteria that worked in Stage 2. Each channel must align to ICP, be measurable, be testable rapidly, and produce customers who achieve LIR.
The Multi-Channel Framework

Stage 3 demand gen targets a portfolio of aligned channels. The sequencing matters — scale proven channels first, then experiment with adjacent channels:

Content Marketing
SEO-driven inbound. Long-term compounding asset. Measures: organic traffic, MQL quality, SQL conversion from organic. Requires 6–12 month investment before scale.
Paid Acquisition
Fastest to test. Immediate feedback on cost per SQL. Measures: Cost per SQL by channel, SQL-to-Customer%, LIR achievement rate from paid cohorts.
Outbound Sales
Rep-driven prospecting. Highest control over ICP targeting. Measures: response rate, meeting rate, SQL quality score, pipeline from outbound.
Partner / Channel
Leverage partner networks to reach segments outside direct sales reach. Measures: partner-sourced ARR, partner-close LIR rate (often lower — watch carefully).
Events & Community
Category building. High cost, high brand impact. Measures: pipeline sourced from events, brand search lift, community member-to-SQL conversion rate.
Product-Led Growth
Freemium or free-trial self-service. Viral coefficient driver. Measures: free-to-paid conversion, time-to-conversion, LIUE of PLG segment vs. direct sales segment.
The 4 Channel Selection Criteria (Same as Stage 2)

Every new channel must pass all four criteria before investment scales:

  • ICP Alignment — does this channel reach buyers who match your ICP? If not, the customers it produces will have lower LIR achievement rates.
  • Measurability — can you track cost per SQL, SQL-to-Customer%, and LIR rate by channel? If attribution is impossible, LIUE by segment becomes unmeasurable.
  • Rapid Test Potential — can you run a 30-60 day test with statistically meaningful results? Channels that require 12+ months to evaluate consume budget before feedback arrives.
  • Customer Success Alignment — does the customer acquisition process set expectations that match what successful customers actually experience? Channels that overpromise produce customers who miss LIR.
Attribution Danger in Stage 3

Adding multiple channels simultaneously corrupts LIUE measurement unless attribution is clean. If you can't tell which channel sourced each SQL, you can't calculate cost per SQL by channel. If you can't calculate cost per SQL by channel, you can't know which channels are producing positive LIUE and which are destroying it. Track SQL quality by channel — not just quantity.

Key Metrics in Stage 3
Stage 3 introduces revenue growth and market position metrics alongside the Stage 1–2 guardrails. All prior metrics remain active — they are floors, not targets. The targets in Stage 3 are NRR, ARR growth rate, and market share.
Metric Definition Target Role
NRR Net Revenue Retention — ARR retained + expanded from existing customers >120% Primary growth flywheel signal
ARR Growth Rate Year-over-year ARR increase Maximize (within LIUE > 3) Primary Stage 3 objective
LIUE LTV/CAC ratio (full formula) >3 (guardrail) Gauge 2 — must stay green
LIR Achievement Rate % of monthly cohort hitting LIR event within T days ≥ threshold (guardrail) Gauge 1 — must stay green
Market Share Share of serviceable addressable market captured Growing vs. competitors Moat construction signal
Viral Coefficient (K-Factor) New customers acquired per existing customer organically >0.5 for material impact; >1.0 for virality Distribution moat indicator
Payback Period Months to recover fully loaded CAC from gross margin <18 months Capital efficiency signal
Magic Number Net new ARR ÷ S&M spend from prior quarter >1.0 GTM efficiency at scale
Metric Hierarchy in Stage 3

LIUE > 3 and LIR ≥ threshold are floors — non-negotiable conditions for continued scaling. ARR growth rate is the primary objective. NRR > 120% is the highest-leverage input to ARR growth, because expansion revenue compounds without additional CAC. If you can only fix one thing, fix the expansion motion first.

In the 6-Phase Revenue System

Stage 3 (Growth & Moat) is the operational context for Phase 5. Roberge's repeatability test IS the Stage 2→3 gate condition. NRR and revenue growth rate are the Stage 3 north star metrics.

Phase 5 — Primary Home
Scale, Repeatability & NRR
Stage 3 execution: 2-per-month hiring pace, segment analysis (Scale/Experiment/Ignore), NRR > 120% target, moat construction through CS and product strategy.
Phase 2 — Hiring Mode
Process Executor at Scale
Stage 3 hiring: Process Executor profile, data-driven promotion path (AE1→AE2→Senior AE→Team Lead), cohort scorecard iteration every 6 months.
Phase 3 — Multi-Channel
Multiple Demand Gen Channels
Stage 3 demand gen: diversify to multiple aligned channels. Each must still pass the 4 criteria. Track LIUE by channel — reallocate quarterly based on ROI.
Phase 6 — If Growth Stalls
Growth Stall Diagnostic
Growth slowing with LIUE > 3? Run segment analysis. Are you over-investing in EXPERIMENT or IGNORE segments? Is a moat being eroded by a new competitor?
