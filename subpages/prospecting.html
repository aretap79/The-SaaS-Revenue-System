Why AEs Must Also Prospect

The most dangerous dependency in a sales organization is an AE who cannot generate their own pipeline. Anthony Iannarino calls this the fundamental fragility of modern SaaS sales: reps who have been so insulated from prospecting that they treat the SDR team as an entitlement rather than a supplement.

Iannarino's Law

Never outsource pipeline creation entirely. An AE who cannot prospect is not an Account Executive — they are an Account Manager waiting for opportunities to appear. The ability to open new relationships is the core skill of selling. Without it, performance is entirely dependent on external inputs the AE cannot control.

The Fragility Problem

When AEs rely exclusively on SDR-generated pipeline, several structural weaknesses emerge. First, pipeline volume becomes hostage to SDR headcount, SDR ramp time, and SDR attrition — none of which the AE controls. Second, deal quality suffers because SDR-sourced leads are often top-of-funnel qualifications, not deeply understood business problems. Third, AE performance becomes unforecastable: quota attainment fluctuates with marketing spend and SDR effectiveness rather than with AE skill.

The AE who prospects creates a self-reinforcing loop. They understand the market firsthand. They know what triggers buyers. They write messages that land because they've tested and iterated. They build relationships before a deal is live. When the SDR pipeline thins — and it will — self-prospecting AEs barely notice.

AE-Sourced Pipeline: The Quality Advantage

Research consistently shows that AE-sourced deals have higher win rates and faster close cycles. The reason is structural: when an AE opens a deal themselves, they enter with full context, a genuine point of view on the buyer's problem, and the credibility of having initiated a thoughtful conversation rather than executing a scripted discovery. The buyer perceives the AE differently — not as a vendor rep following up on a download, but as an informed peer who identified a real issue.

AE Pipeline Self-Sufficiency Target
AE-Sourced Pipeline ≥ 30–40% of Total Pipeline Required
World-class AE teams source 40%+ of their own pipeline. Sub-20% is a fragility warning sign requiring immediate coaching intervention.

AE vs. SDR Prospecting: Different Styles, Accounts, and Messages

AE prospecting is not SDR prospecting done by a more senior person. The two motions are fundamentally different in their target, their message architecture, their channel mix, and their expected conversion rates. Conflating them produces mediocre results from both.

AEs Go Deeper, Higher, and More Strategically

The defining characteristic of AE prospecting is the quality of insight embedded in every outreach. An SDR can send 80 emails by crafting strong personas. An AE sends 8 emails, each containing a specific observation about that company's business — a recent earnings call reference, a publicly stated strategic priority, a relevant customer win from a competitor — that immediately signals that the AE is not cold-pitching but has done real work.

AEs also target the economic buyer directly, which SDRs rarely do effectively. Economic buyers receive enough prospecting traffic that only genuinely insightful, peer-level outreach earns a response. This is the AE's natural domain.

The AE Prospecting Rhythm: Hours, Activities, and the Prospecting Block

Prospecting is not an ad hoc activity — it is a scheduled professional practice. AEs who prospect consistently outperform those who prospect episodically. The difference is not effort or skill; it is habit architecture.

Best Practice

World-class AEs commit 5–8 hours per week to prospecting activities, organized into protected calendar blocks. These blocks are treated with the same inviolability as customer meetings — they are not cancelled for internal reviews or administrative work.

The Weekly Prospecting Block

Effective AE prospecting schedules segment activities by cognitive demand. Research — reading earnings reports, reviewing LinkedIn activity, analyzing trigger events — requires high focus and should be done in a 60–90 minute block on Tuesday or Wednesday morning when cognitive energy is highest. Outreach execution — writing and sending emails, making calls, sending LinkedIn messages — can be batched in a separate block later in the day or week.

Account Prioritization: Tier 1 / Tier 2 / Tier 3

Not all accounts in an AE's territory deserve equal prospecting investment. The fundamental error in AE prospecting is spending equal time across accounts of wildly different potential. A disciplined tiering system directs effort where it generates the highest expected return.

The Three-Tier Model

Tier 1 accounts are the 10–20 highest-potential accounts in the territory. These are the accounts where a win would be a career-defining deal — where ACV potential is highest, where strategic fit is strongest, where executive relationships would have the most long-term value. Tier 1 accounts receive deep, individualized prospecting: research-backed outreach, executive introductions, multi-threaded engagement, and a dedicated account plan.

Tier 2 accounts are the 30–50 accounts with solid potential but less certainty — either because company size is lower, fit is partial, or there is limited information to build a strong hypothesis. These accounts receive sequenced outreach with moderate personalization — persona-level customization rather than account-specific research.

Tier 3 accounts are the long tail — hundreds of accounts that match the ICP broadly but represent small ACV or speculative fit. These accounts receive automated sequences, low-touch LinkedIn outreach, and content syndication. The AE may never personally write a message to a Tier 3 account.

Tier Account Count Prospecting Depth AE Time % Primary Goal
Tier 1 10–20 accounts Deep account research, bespoke messaging, multi-thread 60% Executive meeting, account plan
Tier 2 30–50 accounts Persona-level personalization, light research 30% Discovery call, qualification
Tier 3 100+ accounts Automated sequence, generic ICP messaging 10% Inbound signal, response
Account Scoring Criteria

Tiering should be based on a consistent scoring framework, not intuition. The most reliable criteria combine potential ACV (based on company size and use case fit), strategic fit (ICP alignment across vertical, tech stack, growth stage), buying signals (recent funding, headcount growth, job postings in relevant departments), and relationship capital (existing contacts, mutual connections, prior engagement).

Account Score Calculation
Account Score = (ACV Potential × 0.35) + (ICP Fit × 0.30) + (Intent Signal × 0.25) + (Relationship Capital × 0.10)
Score each dimension 1–10. Accounts scoring 7.5+ are Tier 1. 5–7.4 are Tier 2. Below 5 are Tier 3 or deprioritized.

Identifying the Right Contacts: Intent Data, LinkedIn, and Trigger Events

Knowing the right account is only half the equation. Knowing who to contact, when to contact them, and why they should respond is the other half. Elite AEs build a contact map before sending a single message.

Building the Contact Map

Within each Tier 1 account, AEs should identify 4–6 relevant stakeholders: the economic buyer (typically VP or C-suite), the power user / champion candidate (Director or Senior Manager who would use and advocate for the product), the technical evaluator (IT, Security, or Architecture depending on the product), and any political allies (executives whose teams would benefit indirectly). This multi-thread map is the foundation for coordinated, non-redundant outreach.

Using Intent Data

Intent data platforms (Bombora, G2, 6sense, Demandbase) surface accounts that are actively researching topics relevant to your product category. An account with a spike in intent around "sales enablement" or "revenue intelligence" signals that there is an active buying conversation happening internally. This is the AE's cue to accelerate outreach — the buyer is already in-market, and the goal is to shape the conversation before they short-list competitors.

LinkedIn as an Intelligence Layer

LinkedIn Sales Navigator is the most powerful single tool for AE prospecting. Beyond basic search, AEs should monitor account alerts for job postings (especially VP-level or department build-outs, which signal expansion and budget), recent executive activity and content engagement, and organizational changes. A new VP of Sales at a target account is not just a contact to reach — it is a trigger event: new executives are pre-disposed to make changes in their first 90 days.

The AE Outreach Framework: Strategic Prospecting vs. Spray-and-Pray

The failure mode of AE prospecting is borrowing the SDR playbook — volume-based, generic, sequenced outreach that signals effort but not intelligence. Strategic AE outreach is the opposite: fewer touches, higher signal, designed to make the recipient feel that the AE has specifically studied their situation.

The Spray-and-Pray Trap

AEs who send generic sequences to executive contacts are actively damaging their brand. A VP of Sales who receives a templated "I noticed you might have a problem with X" email from an AE registers it as noise — and the AE's name becomes associated with low-signal vendor outreach. At the executive level, one bad message costs you access for 6–12 months.

The Strategic Prospecting Formula

Every AE outreach message — whether email, LinkedIn, or voicemail — should contain three elements: a specific observation about the company or the contact's situation (not a generic compliment), a connecting insight that links that observation to a problem the AE's product solves, and a low-friction ask (not "a demo" — a brief conversation to explore whether there's a fit).

AE Outreach Formula
[Specific Observation] → [Connecting Insight] → [Low-Friction Ask]
Example: "You recently closed a Series B and your job postings show you're building a 40-person sales team. Companies at that stage typically hit a revenue predictability wall around rep #20. I've worked with 4 companies in your exact position — would 15 minutes be worth it to compare notes?"

Multi-Channel Outreach: The AE's Full Toolkit

Research consistently shows that multi-channel prospects convert at 2–3× the rate of single-channel outreach. For AEs targeting senior executives, the channel mix is not about volume — it is about finding the path of least resistance to an authentic first conversation.

Email: The Foundation

Email is the workhorse of AE prospecting — asynchronous, permanent, forwardable. A well-crafted AE prospecting email can be forwarded internally by the recipient to a colleague, multiplying its reach without any additional AE effort. The AE email should be under 150 words, contain exactly one insight, make exactly one ask, and require no attachments.

LinkedIn: The Credibility Layer

LinkedIn serves two prospecting functions. First, as a research platform — reviewing a contact's recent posts, articles, and engagement patterns gives the AE raw material for personalization. Second, as an outreach channel — a LinkedIn connection request with a personalized note signals peer-level engagement rather than vendor outreach. The AE's LinkedIn profile should be buyer-facing (accomplishment-oriented, not resume-oriented) before any prospecting campaign begins.

Cold Call: The Fastest Path to Conversation

Phone is underutilized in AE prospecting because it requires confronting rejection in real time. Yet a cold call that connects converts at 5–10× the rate of email. AEs should block dedicated calling time, use a brief and insight-led opener, and treat every live conversation — even a brief rejection — as an opportunity to learn about the account.

Executive Introduction: The Highest-Convert Channel

A warm introduction from a mutual connection, a board member, or a customer reference converts at 10–30× cold outreach. AEs should actively map their existing network against Tier 1 target accounts and request introductions when a genuine connection exists. This is not name-dropping — it is using relationship capital to earn a conversation that might otherwise take months.

Crafting the AE Prospecting Email

The AE prospecting email is a precision instrument, not a broadcast medium. Every word must earn its place. Executive buyers are pattern-matching for signals of genuine relevance within the first two sentences — if those signals are absent, the email is deleted.

The Architecture of an Effective AE Email
  1. Subject Line: Specific + Intriguing Avoid generic subject lines ("Quick question" or "Reaching out"). Use something specific to the company or contact: "Series B + 40-person sales team build-out" or "Your Q3 earnings call — one observation." Specificity signals relevance before the email is opened.
  2. Opening: The Observation (not the compliment) Do not begin with "I saw your LinkedIn profile" or "Congratulations on your funding." Begin with a specific observation about the company's business situation: "You're building a product-led growth motion on top of a historically enterprise sales team — that transition has a 60% failure rate without the right infrastructure." This immediately signals that the AE has done real work.
  3. Body: The Connecting Insight In one to two sentences, connect the observation to a specific implication or problem. Do not pitch the product. The goal is to make the buyer think "how did they know that?" — not "what are they selling?"
  4. Close: The Low-Friction Ask Never ask for a demo in a first cold email. Ask for a 15-minute conversation to "compare notes," "share what we've seen," or "test a hypothesis." The ask should feel like it benefits the buyer regardless of whether they buy.
  5. Signature: Social Proof Without Bragging Include one customer name or metric inline — "We've helped [Competitor Name] and [Similar Company] navigate this transition" — that makes the AE's experience credible without an attachment or a case study link.

The Cold Call for AEs: Consultative, Insight-Led, Different from SDR Calls

The AE cold call is not a qualification script — it is a consultative opening. The objective is not to confirm BANT criteria in 90 seconds. It is to say something insightful enough in the first 15 seconds that the executive chooses to give you 5 more minutes.

The AE Cold Call Framework
  1. Permission Pattern (3 seconds) "Hi [Name], this is [AE] from [Company] — I know this is a cold call, so I'll be brief. Can I have 30 seconds?" This disarms the pattern-match reflex. Most executives will say yes.
  2. The Insight Hook (15 seconds) Deliver the single most relevant observation about their business — something specific enough that they immediately recognize it as real knowledge, not a script. Reference a recent press release, earnings comment, or industry trend that is directly relevant to their role.
  3. The Connecting Question (10 seconds) Ask one high-quality diagnostic question that invites them to share their perspective. Not "are you interested?" but "is that pattern showing up in your org?" This converts a pitch into a dialogue.
  4. The Earned Ask (5 seconds) If they engage, ask for a structured next step — a 20-minute call the following week. If they decline, ask if you can follow up by email with the specific insight you referenced. Either outcome gives you something to work with.

Using Trigger Events: The AE's Timing Advantage

Timing is the hidden variable in prospecting effectiveness. The same message sent at different times produces dramatically different results. Trigger events — changes in a company's situation that create urgency or new buying potential — are the AE's primary timing signal.

Trigger Event Why It Matters Prospecting Angle Time to Act
Funding Round New budget, growth mandate, board pressure to scale "New funding means new headcount targets — what's your plan to scale revenue infrastructure?" Within 72 hours
New Executive Hire New leaders make changes in first 90 days; new budget cycle "Congratulations on your new VP of Sales — what they inherit vs. what they'll want to build is always an interesting conversation" Within 1 week
Product Launch New GTM motion, new team, new revenue target Show how similar product launches succeeded with your solution At launch announcement
Competitor Win/Loss If they lost to competitor, they're dissatisfied; if competitor lost to you, they may be at risk Position based on what your customers said when they switched Within 1 week

The AE Prospecting Scorecard

Track prospecting activity weekly: accounts researched, emails sent, calls made, LinkedIn messages sent, responses received, meetings booked. Review this data monthly to identify where in the prospecting funnel you are losing — if activity is high but responses are low, it is a messaging problem; if responses are reasonable but meetings don't convert, it is a qualification problem; if meetings convert but opportunities stall, it is a discovery or champion problem.

Prospecting Accountability: The Manager's Role

Prospecting without accountability reverts to convenience — AEs work the warmest inbound leads and neglect the harder self-sourced outreach. The manager's job is to make prospecting a visible, structured, and rewarded behavior.

"Show Me Your Prospecting"

In every weekly pipeline review, the first question should be: "What new outreach did you run this week, and what did you learn?" This is not a gotcha — it is a signal that prospecting is a professional expectation, not an optional activity. Over time, AEs internalize this accountability and the question becomes unnecessary because the behavior is habitual.

The Weekly Pipeline Review Protocol

A structurally sound pipeline review for AE prospecting covers three areas: (1) Existing pipeline velocity — are deals advancing on their MAP timeline? (2) New pipeline created this week — what meetings were booked, what opportunities were opened? (3) Prospecting activity — what accounts were researched, what outreach was sent, what responses came in? The third category is the leading indicator; the first two are lagging. Managers who only review existing pipeline are flying blind on the AE's future performance.

Manager Best Practice

Review actual prospecting emails and LinkedIn messages in your 1:1 sessions. Ask the AE to share the last three emails they sent to Tier 1 accounts. Read them together. This is the fastest way to diagnose messaging quality and provide targeted coaching — more effective than any amount of pipeline data review.

Rewarding AE-Sourced Pipeline

The compensation architecture should reinforce prospecting behavior. Some organizations pay a higher commission rate on AE-sourced deals than SDR-sourced deals — typically 10–15% uplift. Others use SPIF programs for AE-sourced meetings or opportunities created in a given month. The specific mechanism matters less than the cultural signal: the organization values self-sufficiency and rewards it.
