Section 1 — Template Overview

Choose the tracking option that matches your current stage. All three options produce the same output: a chart showing what % of customers are achieving your LIR threshold over time.

The LIR Formula
P% of customers achieve E event(s) within T days
P = Percentage
e.g., 70% of customers
E = Event
e.g., complete 5 transactions
T = Time window
e.g., within 60 days
1
Track Raw Event Counts
Best for: Pre-PMF, Early Stage

Track the raw number of your key event per customer per month. Simple to implement — just count how many times each customer completes the event each month.

Template Inputs
Customer Name Close Date ACV ICP? (Y/N) Month 1 Events Month 2 Events ... Month 12
Template Output
Chart: % customers achieving LIR threshold over time
2
Track % of Expected Events Achieved
Best for: Pre-PMF, Intermediate Stage

Instead of raw counts, track what fraction of the total expected events each customer has completed. Useful when customers have different expected usage volumes based on their size or plan tier.

Additional Field
Expected Total Events e.g., 20 transactions expected
Monthly Calculation

Monthly score = Actual events ÷ Expected total events
LIR threshold example: customer achieves 25% of expected events

3
Track Milestone Progression (Graduated Thresholds)
Best for: Pre-PMF, Advanced Stage

Instead of a single pass/fail threshold, use graduated milestones to see the pace of adoption. This reveals customers who are progressing toward the LIR milestone, not just those who have or haven't hit it.

Graduated LIR Thresholds
10% 15% 20% 25% 40% 50% 60% 70% 80% 90% 100%

Shows not just pass/fail but the pace of adoption toward the full LIR milestone — critical for early warning and CS intervention planning.

Section 2 — Step-by-Step: How to Fill Out the Template

Follow these setup steps before entering any data. Getting the definitions right upfront determines whether the template produces actionable signal.

Pre-PMF Setup
  1. Define your LIR Write it in the formula: P% of customers achieve E event(s) within T days. Be specific — the event must be measurable and unambiguous.
  2. Enter your P threshold Example: 70% of customers. This is the line between PMF confirmed and PMF at risk.
  3. Choose your E event The specific, measurable action that correlates with long-term retention. Examples: "completes 5 API calls," "creates 3 projects," "invites 2 team members."
  4. Choose your T window Common windows: 30, 60, or 90 days. For complex enterprise software, 90–120 days may be appropriate. Match to your typical onboarding arc.
  5. Enter monthly event counts per customer The template auto-calculates whether each customer is tracking toward LIR based on the event definition and threshold you set in steps 1–4.
Post-PMF Setup (Cohort Tracking)
  1. Group customers by acquisition month (or quarter) For larger companies (>50 new customers/month), consider quarterly cohorts to reduce noise.
  2. For each cohort, track % achieving LIR by month Each cell = (customers in cohort who achieved LIR by month N) ÷ (total customers in cohort).
  3. Watch for cohort trends Improving cohorts: positive trend — each new cohort hits LIR faster than the last. Flat cohorts: stable PMF. Declining cohorts: alarm — investigate immediately.

Section 3 — How to Interpret the Cohort Chart

The cohort chart is your primary PMF health monitor. Read it by zone, then ask the right diagnostic questions when a zone turns yellow or red.

Green Zone
Above Threshold

PMF is holding. The product is delivering value at the rate required to retain customers. Continue monitoring monthly.

Yellow Zone
Approaching

Watch closely. May indicate product changes, onboarding issues, or ICP drift. Do not wait for red — investigate now.

Red Zone
Below Threshold

PMF at risk. Stop scaling new customer acquisition. Investigate immediately and root-cause before resuming growth spending.

Diagnostic Questions When Cohorts Drop

  • Did ICP change? Are you selling to customers outside your defined ICP? Non-ICP customers consistently underperform on the LIR metric.
  • Did the onboarding process change? Was a step removed, automated poorly, or handed to a less experienced CSM? Onboarding changes often show up in the LIR chart 30–60 days later.
  • Did the product change? Was a feature that drove the LIR event deprecated, moved in the UI, or changed in behavior? Engineering and CS must align on this.
  • Did the CS team change? New CSMs, departures of experienced CSMs, or a CS org reorg can affect the LIR rate for 1–2 cohorts before stabilizing.

Section 4 — Verification Analysis (12–18 Month Cohorts)

Before trusting your LIR definition, verify it using customers acquired 12–18 months ago. You now have enough retention data to check whether the LIR actually predicts retention.

How to Run the Verification

Take all customers acquired 12–18 months ago. Split them into two groups: Achieved LIR and Did not achieve LIR. Calculate the actual annual retention rate for each group. Compare.

Strong Validation

Achieved LIR group: retains at 85–95%
Did not achieve LIR group: retains at 30–50%

A 35–65 percentage point gap confirms the LIR event is a genuine predictor of retention. Lock in this definition.

Weak Validation — Redefine

Gap < 20 percentage points between the two groups

The LIR event is not sufficiently predictive. The event you chose does not separate retained from churned customers. Redefine the event and re-run the analysis.

Common Mistake

Choosing an LIR event that is correlated with success but not causal. Example: "sent first email" is correlated with retention but does not cause it. Choose events that represent deep product adoption, not superficial onboarding completion.
