Phase 3 — Market & Territory
Territory Design — The Foundation of Equitable, Scalable GTM
Territory design is one of the most impactful and most overlooked drivers of quota attainment variance. Unfair territories destroy morale. Overlapping territories destroy deals.
Home› Phase 3› Territory Design

Types of Territory Design

There is no single correct territory design model. The right choice depends on your sales motion, segment, product, and how your buyers cluster. Most mature GTM organizations use a hybrid approach.

Territory Model Definition Best For Pros Cons
Geographic Assign accounts by physical location (state, region, country, metro) Field sales; accounts where physical presence matters; early-stage simplicity Simple to explain; clear ownership; no overlap conflicts Uneven account density; rep in rural territory may have 1/10th the opportunity of a NYC rep
Named Account Assign a specific list of named companies to each rep, regardless of geography Enterprise segment; strategic accounts; high-ACV hunting Maximum focus; high-value accounts get dedicated rep attention Complex territory carve; harder to add new hires mid-year; no whitespace coverage
Vertical / Industry Assign accounts by industry vertical (e.g., all Healthcare, all Financial Services) Vertical GTM strategy; product has compliance/regulatory specificity Rep becomes a vertical expert; buyer credibility; faster sales cycles Vertical health dependency; product gaps if vertical needs differ widely
Hybrid Combination of above (e.g., named accounts by vertical within a geographic pod) Scale-stage SaaS; multi-segment go-to-market Optimized for your specific market structure Organizational complexity; requires strong governance to prevent overlap

The Principles of Good Territory Design

Before running the mechanics of territory carving, anchor on these four non-negotiable principles. Any territory design that violates them will produce attrition, conflict, and forecast instability.

Principle 1: Equitable Potential

Every rep carrying the same quota title should have roughly equivalent total addressable opportunity in their territory. This does not mean identical accounts — it means similar potential revenue if the rep executes at the median. The most common territory design failure is assigning quota-parity to reps with dramatically different territory potential, then wondering why attainment variance is so high.

Equity vs. Equality

Equitable territory does not mean equal number of accounts. A territory with 20 Fortune 500 accounts is not equitable with a territory containing 200 mid-market accounts just because the count is different. Measure potential revenue opportunity, not account count.

Principle 2: Logical Ownership

Account ownership must follow a clear, defensible logic that reps and managers can explain without argument. "All companies in the Southeast with 500–2000 employees" is logical. "Some companies in the Southeast unless there's an existing relationship" is not — it creates ambiguity and conflict.

Principle 3: Minimal Overlap

Overlapping territories destroy deals and relationships. When two reps both believe they own an account, the customer receives duplicate outreach, gets confused, and loses confidence in the vendor. Revenue Operations must establish clear first-touch and primary ownership rules before any territory conflict arises, not after.

Principle 4: Manageable Scope

A rep can only deeply engage with a finite number of accounts. Enterprise AEs should own 15–30 named accounts maximum. Mid-market AEs should carry 50–100 accounts at most (with active focus on the top 25). A rep with 500 accounts in their CRM and no priority framework is not a rep with a territory — they have a lead list and no strategy.

How to Calculate Territory Potential

Territory potential is the estimated total revenue you could capture from a territory if you achieved your target penetration rate at your expected win rate. It is the foundation of equitable territory design and rational quota setting.

Territory Potential
Territory Potential = # ICP Accounts × Estimated ACV × Win Rate
Estimated ACV = weighted average of ACV for that account size tier and vertical. Win Rate = historical win rate for that segment.
Worked Example: Mid-Market Territory
  • Territory has 80 ICP-qualified accounts (250–1,000 employees, target vertical, no existing customer relationship)
  • Average ACV for this profile: $55,000
  • Historical win rate in this segment: 22%
  • Territory Potential = 80 × $55,000 × 22% = $968,000
  • Quota set at $1.2M — implies needing 2.9× ACV win rate, or a pipeline of $5.5M to support

If territory potential math doesn't support the quota, you have one of three choices: lower the quota, expand the territory, or accept that you're setting the rep up to fail.

Territory Coverage Ratio (Safety Check)
Coverage Ratio = Territory Potential / Quota
Minimum safe ratio: 3:1. Below 2:1 means the territory cannot support the quota even at perfect execution. Above 5:1 means quota is too conservative.

The Territory Carve Process — Step by Step

Territory carving is an annual (or semi-annual) RevOps-led exercise that must happen before quota setting and before annual planning is locked. The sequence matters — territories inform quotas, not the reverse.

  1. Build the Account Universe Pull all ICP accounts from your CRM, Salesforce/HubSpot, enriched with firmographic data from ZoomInfo, Apollo, or Clearbit. Tag each account with: employee count, industry, geography, current relationship status (prospect, customer, former customer), and estimated ACV potential. This is your universe.
  2. Score and Rank Accounts by Potential Build an account scoring model that weights: (a) firmographic ICP fit, (b) intent signals (G2, Bombora, Demandbase), (c) technology signals (tech stack fit from BuiltWith/Clearbit), and (d) existing relationship warmth. Rank every account in the universe by expected potential and likelihood to convert.
  3. Cluster Accounts by Territory Principle Group accounts by your primary territory dimension (geographic, named, vertical). For geographic: by state/metro/region. For named account: by target account list priority tier. For vertical: by industry cluster. Ensure each cluster has roughly equitable potential before proceeding.
  4. Assign Existing Customers and Active Pipeline First Before assigning new territories, lock in existing customer and active pipeline ownership. Existing customers must stay with the rep who owns the relationship (usually the CSM/AE hybrid or expansion AE). Active pipeline follows the original rep. These accounts are excluded from the rebalancing pool.
  5. Balance Territories Across Reps Assign clustered accounts to reps, targeting ±15% potential variance across equivalent rep roles. Use a territory balancing tool (Salesforce Territory Management, Varicent, Xactly, or a RevOps-built spreadsheet). Negotiate with sales leadership where to make trade-offs.
  6. Establish White Space Rules Not every account will be assigned to a named rep. Define a clear process for whitespace accounts: who can work them (SDR, inbound-only, or first-to-engage AE), when they get assigned to a rep, and how to resolve conflicts when two reps both touch a whitespace account simultaneously.
  7. Document and Publish Every rep must receive a written territory definition with account lists, rules of engagement, and the governance process for disputes. Territories published verbally are territories that will be disputed.

Territory Rebalancing — When and How

Territories must be rebalanced when the underlying assumption that made them equitable changes. The two most common triggers are headcount changes and market shifts.

Trigger What to Do Timing
New AE hired mid-year Carve a new territory from whitespace and from under-penetrated accounts in high-coverage existing territories. Do not take active pipeline from an existing rep. Complete within 30 days of hire; new rep starts with clean territory
Rep departs mid-year Reassign their accounts to manager temporarily; split across team for deals in late-stage. Do not leave accounts unowned for more than 2 weeks. Immediate; manager owns account until replacement hired
Annual planning Full territory rebalance based on prior year win data, new headcount plan, and updated ICP scoring. Most disruptive — do this in Q4 for next fiscal year. Q4 process; published by December for January start
Market shift (vertical boom/bust) Rebalance verticals based on new TAM data; adjust quota proportionally; never hold a rep to a quota built on a market that no longer exists. Quarterly review; mid-year adjustment if shift is significant (>20% potential change)
Company enters new geography Build new territory from scratch using account universe process. Hire locally if field presence required. 90-day planning lead time before first rep in territory

The Politics of Territory Changes

Territory changes are one of the highest-friction management events in sales. Reps who lose accounts feel robbed. Reps who gain accounts feel watched. You must manage the human element as carefully as the analytical one.

  • Explain the methodology, not just the outcome: A rep who loses 10 accounts but understands that the territory potential was 40% above their peers will accept it. A rep who loses 10 accounts without explanation will start looking for a new job.
  • Grandfather mid-stage deals: Never rip an active deal from a rep during a territory rebalance. If an account moves territory, the deal stays with the original rep through close. The new rep takes over for expansion and renewal.
  • Communicate before finalizing: Allow reps to flag accounts with active relationship context before territories are locked. A rep who has been nurturing an account for 3 months has legitimate grounds to keep it even if the rebalance logic would move it.
  • Separate comp from territory: If a rep took accounts in good faith and worked them, any revenue from those accounts before the territory change is theirs. Do not retroactively reallocate commission for work already done.
  • Use RevOps as the neutral arbiter: Territory disputes between reps should be resolved by RevOps using the documented governance rules, not by the two reps negotiating, and not by the manager making a judgment call that creates favoritism perception.

Named Account Model: Enterprise Design Details

The named account model is the dominant territory design for enterprise segments ($150K+ ACV). It concentrates effort, enables deep account strategy, and prevents the diffuse hunting that destroys enterprise win rates.

Accounts Per Enterprise AE
15–30
Pure enterprise AE with no inbound; focused hunting and developing. 20 is the sweet spot.
Accounts Per Mid-Market AE
50–100
Top 25 get active attention. Rest are sequenced/SDR-nurtured for pipeline generation.
Active Pursuits Per Enterprise AE
5–8
Realistic number of enterprise deals an AE can actively manage simultaneously.
Global Account Designation Threshold
$500K+ ARR
Accounts above this potential should be designated Global Accounts with centralized ownership.

Territory Governance Rules: Credit and Co-Selling

One of the most contentious territory management issues is credit allocation when multiple reps touch a deal — especially in complex enterprise deals involving an inside rep, a field rep, and an overlay specialist.

Scenario Recommended Credit Split Governance Rule
SDR sources deal → AE closes it AE: 100% quota credit. SDR: pipeline sourcing credit for bonus/comp. SDR never gets quota credit; AE always owns the deal
AE and overlay specialist co-sell AE: 100% quota credit. Overlay: separate overlay quota Overlay specialists have their own quota; no split reduces AE incentive
Two AEs both claim account ownership First documented touchpoint in CRM with qualified opportunity RevOps rules based on timestamped CRM activity; no retroactive credit
Named account rep + partner-sourced deal AE: 100% quota credit. Partner: partner program commission paid separately. Partner never reduces AE quota credit
AE transfers mid-deal due to territory change Original AE: credit through close. New AE: credit from next deal/renewal. Deal follows the rep who built it; territory follows the map

RevOps' Role in Territory Management

Territory management is a core RevOps function, not a sales leadership decision made informally. Without RevOps ownership, territory design becomes political instead of analytical.

  • Build and maintain the account universe: RevOps owns the master account database, kept current via data enrichment integrations (ZoomInfo, Clearbit, Apollo).
  • Run the territory carve model: RevOps builds and maintains the territory potential scoring model, balancing algorithm, and carve logic.
  • Govern territory changes: All in-year territory changes (new hires, departures, account disputes) are processed through a documented RevOps workflow, not ad-hoc email chains.
  • Track territory performance: RevOps produces quarterly territory coverage and penetration reports: what % of territory potential has been engaged, what % converted, which accounts are untouched.
  • Audit territory equity: Quarterly audit of attainment variance across territories. If attainment variance exceeds 30% among reps on equivalent quotas, territory imbalance is a likely cause.
