What is PLG?

Product-Led Growth is a go-to-market strategy where the product itself drives user acquisition, retention, and expansion with minimal or no traditional sales motion. PLG compounds because each user brings in more users.

PLG Definition

PLG = the product drives user acquisition, retention, and expansion with minimal or no traditional sales motion. The key distinction from SLG (Sales-Led Growth): the product creates value before money changes hands, and that value experience drives the purchase decision bottom-up.

Classic PLG Companies

Slack viral team invitation loop Dropbox referral + viral sharing Figma real-time collaboration virality HubSpot freemium → upsell model Calendly scheduling link creates viral distribution Zoom external meeting creates free user acquisition Notion public pages + template sharing

PLG Requirements

  • Value in the first session. Users must experience meaningful value before hitting a paywall or needing help. If onboarding requires a 2-hour implementation, PLG will not work.
  • Natural sharing or viral mechanism. The product must have a built-in reason for users to invite others — collaboration features, public outputs, invitation flows, or network-effect value.
  • Clear upgrade trigger. The moment when a free user encounters friction that is resolved by paying must be obvious, low-friction, and directly tied to a value they've already experienced.
  • Bottom-up adoption path. PLG works best when individual users adopt before the organization buys. If the economic buyer must be involved before anyone can use the product, PLG is not the right motion.
PLG is Not for All Companies

PLG requires a product that individual users can adopt independently and immediately see value from. Complex enterprise software that requires IT integration, multi-stakeholder configuration, or custom onboarding is not a PLG product. Forcing PLG on a fundamentally SLG product destroys both motions.

PLG Relationship to LIR

PLG companies must define an activation metric — the moment when a free user first experiences core product value. This activation metric is the PLG equivalent of the LIR event in sales-led companies. The AHA moment is the activation event, and the percentage of free users achieving it within a defined time window is the PLG equivalent of LIR achievement rate.

The AARRR Funnel Framework

AARRR (Awareness, Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Referral, Revenue) maps every stage of the PLG funnel to a North Star Metric, growth tactics, conversion gate, and benchmark. Work from Activation backward — this stage determines whether your PLG motion is viable.

A
Awareness
Potential users discover your product exists
Benchmark: 20–40% of traffic → sign-up

Potential users discover your product through organic search, word-of-mouth, viral sharing, content, or an integrations marketplace. For PLG products, awareness is often driven by seeing the product output in the wild — a Calendly link, a Figma file, a Notion page, a Loom video.

North Star Metric
Monthly organic + viral visits from ICP-relevant sources
Growth Tactics
SEO, viral output sharing, word-of-mouth, content marketing, integrations marketplace listings
Conversion Gate
Sign-up page — conversion rate to free account creation. Strong PLG products: 20–40%.
A
Acquisition
Visitors sign up for free trial or freemium account
Benchmark: 30–60% sign-ups → initial activation

Visitors convert to registered free users. The goal is to minimize friction at this gate — every field on the sign-up form, every required permission, and every step before the product is accessible reduces acquisition rate.

North Star Metric
Sign-up conversion rate: visitors → registered free users
Growth Tactics
Frictionless sign-up (email only or Google SSO), immediate product value demo, contextual in-app onboarding tour
Conversion Gate
Activation trigger: the first key action that brings the user toward the AHA moment
  • Remove every unnecessary sign-up field — each additional field reduces conversion by 5–10%
  • Google/Microsoft SSO sign-up consistently outperforms email/password forms for B2B products
  • Show the product value immediately after sign-up — do not send users to an empty dashboard
  • If your product requires data import or integration to show value, consider a data sandbox or template to create immediate first-session value
A
Activation ⭐ Most Critical Stage
User experiences core product value for the first time — the AHA moment
Benchmark: 40–60% activation rate
⚡ The AHA Moment — PLG's LIR Equivalent

Activation is the single most important stage in the PLG funnel. The AHA moment is the specific, instrumentable product event that correlates with long-term retention. Users who reach the AHA moment retain at 3–10× the rate of those who do not. Your entire PLG optimization effort should start here.

Activation is defined as the first time a user experiences the core value of the product — not just logging in, not completing a tutorial, but genuinely using the product in the way it was designed to create value.

North Star Metric
Activation rate: % of sign-ups reaching AHA moment within T days (typically 7–30)
Growth Tactics
Guided onboarding checklists, empty state best practices, progress indicators, quick-win templates, tooltip walkthroughs
Conversion Gate
Retention trigger: will the user return tomorrow? D1 retention is the first proof that activation created lasting value.

How to Find Your AHA Moment

  1. Analyze your best-retained customersIdentify which specific events your highest-LTV customers completed in their first 7–14 days. Look for events that are strongly correlated with 12-month retention.
  2. Run a cohort analysisFor each candidate AHA event, split users into "completed" and "did not complete" and compare 90-day retention. A valid AHA event shows a 30+ percentage point retention gap.
  3. Define it preciselyWrite the AHA moment in the LIR format: "P% of sign-ups complete E event within T days." This is your PLG activation metric.
  4. Optimize the pathMap every step between sign-up and the AHA moment. Remove every unnecessary step. Add guidance and empty-state content to reduce friction at each step.
R
Retention
Users return and use the product repeatedly after activation
Benchmark: D30 retention >30%

Retention measures whether activated users continue using the product over time. D1/D7/D30 retention tracks the percentage of users active on day 1, 7, and 30 after their activation event. A retention curve that flattens (even at a low level) is a strong signal of genuine value delivery.

North Star Metric
D1 / D7 / D30 retention — % of activated users still active at each milestone
Growth Tactics
Habit-forming features, email re-engagement sequences, in-app notifications, usage-based reminders, collaborative features that bring users back
Conversion Gate
Monetization trigger: the moment when usage or team size crosses the upgrade threshold
Retention Curve Interpretation

A retention curve that drops to 0% at Day 30 means you have a discovery product, not a retention product — there is no PLG moat. A curve that flattens above 20% at Day 30 means a core user base is genuinely retained and the product has a chance to compound through viral and referral mechanics.

R
Referral — The Viral Loop
Existing users invite others, creating organic growth
Benchmark: K-factor >0.5

The referral loop is what makes PLG compound over time. Each existing user brings in additional users, reducing CAC toward zero for the viral portion of acquisition. The viral coefficient (K-factor) measures this: K = (invites sent per user) × (invite accept rate).

North Star Metric
Viral coefficient (K-factor) = invites sent per active user × invite accept rate
Growth Tactics
Team invite flows ("invite your colleague"), public shareable outputs, integrations into shared workflows, referral incentive programs
Conversion Gate
Invitee activation: does the invited user also reach the AHA moment? Referrals that don't activate add no retention value.
Viral Coefficient Formula
K = Invites Sent Per Active User × Accept Rate
K < 0.5 = supplementary viral component. K = 0.5–1.0 = meaningful viral growth. K > 1.0 = exponential (viral product). Most successful PLG companies operate at K = 0.3–0.7 at scale.
R
Revenue / Monetization
Free users convert to paid plans
Benchmark: 2–5% free-to-paid in 90 days

Monetization converts retained free users to paying customers. The key principle: monetization gates should be encountered by users after they have experienced significant value — not before. Premature paywalls kill activation rates and viral loops.

North Star Metric
Free-to-paid conversion rate: % of free users converting to paid within 90 days
Growth Tactics
Usage-based limits (seat limits, export limits), feature gates on power features, upgrade prompts triggered at AHA moment, team/admin features behind paywall
Expansion Gate
Post-conversion expansion: additional seats, higher plans, add-ons. PLG monetization compounds through expansion, not just conversion.
  • The best time to show an upgrade prompt is immediately after a user has experienced the AHA moment — they have maximum perceived value and minimum price resistance
  • Usage limits work better than feature gates for bottom-up PLG — users understand "you've reached your limit" more clearly than a hidden premium feature
  • Admin/team features behind the paywall work for collaborative products — the individual user who champions the upgrade needs a reason to make the business case to their manager

PLG vs. SLG (Sales-Led Growth)

PLG and SLG are not mutually exclusive — many mature companies run both motions simultaneously. However, mixing them prematurely in Stage 2 creates conflicting incentives and unclear unit economics. Pick one primary motion.

Dimension PLG SLG (Sales-Led Growth)
Primary Sales Motion Product drives sign-up, activation, and conversion with minimal human involvement Sales reps drive qualification, demo, evaluation, and close
ICP Entry Point Individual end user discovers and adopts — organization follows Economic buyer and champion engaged from first touch
Sales Cycle Days to weeks from sign-up to first payment Weeks to months from SQL to closed-won
Contract ACV $0–10K (individual, small team, or departmental) $10K+ (team-wide, departmental, or enterprise)
LIR Equivalent Activation metric — the AHA moment is the PLG LIR event Formal LIR: P% of customers achieve E event within T days
Moat Type Viral distribution, data network effects, switching costs from embedded workflows Relationship moat, brand authority, institutional knowledge
Best Company Stage PMF validation and network effect building (product must prove value before asking for money) GTM Fit through Growth & Moat (proven product value, focus on scaling revenue capture)
CAC Structure Near-zero for viral acquisition; higher for paid channels that feed the PLG funnel High CAC (rep salaries, tools, marketing) — justified by high ACV and LIUE >3
