Why a Checklist?
The single most common startup mistake is scaling GTM too early. Investors, operators, and boards all feel the pressure to grow headcount and "show velocity" — but each premature hire multiplies the cost of an unproven playbook.
  • Each prematurely hired rep costs $150–300K loaded plus 6 months of ramp with no return
  • Bad cohort data corrupts your LIR and LIUE metrics, making it harder to diagnose what's broken
  • Reps can't make quota on a broken playbook — you end up with a demoralized team that blames itself for a systems problem
  • VCs punish premature scaling more harshly than slow scaling — they see the churn in the cohort data and draw conclusions about ICP quality and product-market fit
How to Use This Checklist

Work through both gates sequentially. Gate 1 governs the transition from PMF to GTM Fit — do not hire salespeople at scale until all Gate 1 items are checked. Gate 2 governs the transition from GTM Fit to Growth & Moat — do not accelerate headcount growth beyond 2/month until all Gate 2 items are checked. Track your progress using the interactive checklist below.

Product-Market Fit Validation
✓
LIR is defined with specific P, E, and T values
Not "we think customers are happy" — you have a measurable formula: P% of customers achieve E event(s) within T days. Written down, agreed upon, and tracked in your CRM or analytics tool.
✓
LIR achievement rate ≥ 60–80% for at least 2 consecutive cohorts
Measured quarterly. One good cohort is not PMF — it could be selection bias. Two consecutive cohorts above your threshold is the minimum signal required to declare PMF.
✓
12-month retention rate for LIR customers is ≥ 85%
Your LIR is only a valid leading indicator if customers who achieve it actually retain. Validate the correlation with 12-month retention data from your earliest cohorts before declaring the LIR threshold correct.
✓
Clear correlation proven: LIR customers retain 20+ percentage points better than non-LIR customers
If customers who achieve LIR are not retaining significantly better than those who don't, the LIR definition is wrong. Run the analysis: LIR cohort 12-month retention vs. non-LIR cohort. The gap should exceed 20 percentage points.
✓
Interviewed ≥ 10 retained customers AND ≥ 5 churned customers to validate ICP
Qualitative validation of the quantitative signal. Churned customer interviews are as valuable as retained — they tell you who NOT to sell to and what promise was broken. Both sets are required.
✓
LIR is instrumentable — tracked automatically or via defined process
Not by asking the customer. If you can't track LIR achievement without a manual survey, you can't run the scale-phase LIR program that protects your LIUE. LIR must be instrumentable before you scale.
ICP Definition
✓
ICP is defined with specific firmographic criteria
Industry, company size, tech stack, business model, growth stage — written down with enough specificity that any rep can apply it. "Mid-market SaaS companies" is not specific enough. "Series A–C SaaS companies, 50–500 employees, using Salesforce or HubSpot, B2B, US or Canada" is a starting point.
✓
Green/Yellow/Red framework applied and documented
Green: meets all ICP criteria — pursue aggressively. Yellow: meets most criteria, 1–2 misses — qualify through discovery. Red: missing core criteria — do not pursue, politely disqualify. Every rep applies this framework consistently.
✓
ICP customers achieve LIR at significantly higher rates than non-ICP customers
Run the segmented analysis. If ICP and non-ICP customers achieve LIR at similar rates, the ICP definition is wrong. The ICP definition is valid only if it predicts customer success — and LIR achievement is how you measure that.
✓
Sales team can qualify ICP vs. non-ICP within the first discovery call
The qualification framework must be executable in a live conversation. If a rep can't assign Green/Yellow/Red within the first 15–20 minutes of discovery, the criteria are too complex or not well-trained.
GTM Playbook Foundation
✓
At least 10 ICP customers have been closed and are actively using the product
Not "prospects" or "trials." Ten paying ICP customers who are actively using the product and from whom you can derive LIR data. This is the minimum corpus for playbook pattern recognition.
✓
Stage 1 "Win at All Costs" playbook has generated enough wins to identify patterns
The founder-led sales phase produced enough data to answer: what messaging worked? Which buyer personas engaged? What objections were common? What sales cycle patterns emerged? These patterns are the raw material for the documented playbook.
✓
Buyer journey stages documented with typical sales cycle length
You know which buyer journey stages are present in your specific market and what the typical sales cycle looks like from first contact to signed contract. This doesn't need to be perfect — it needs to be documented and testable.
✓
Founder/CEO is no longer required on every deal to close
If the founder must be on every close call, the playbook isn't documented — it's in the founder's head. A scalable playbook must be executable by a trained rep. If it isn't, you're not ready to hire salespeople.
Team & Hiring Readiness
✓
Sales hiring scorecard is drafted
Even if not yet validated, the scorecard documents what attributes you believe predict rep success in your specific context. It will be iterated based on data — but it must exist before you start the process so interviews are consistent.
✓
Budget approved for 2 salespeople per month for 6 months (minimum)
Hiring one salesperson is not a test — you need a cohort to generate statistically meaningful data. Budget for 2 per month for 6 months (12 reps minimum). If the budget isn't approved, you're not ready to commit to the GTM Fit phase.
✓
First Sales Leader is hired or actively being recruited
Someone who can codify the playbook — not just execute it. The "Process Builder" profile. See the Hiring the First Sales Leader page for the detailed framework. This hire must happen before or concurrent with the first cohort of sales reps.
✓
Customer Success function exists to maintain LIR post-sale
Even if just one CSM. Without a CS function, LIR achievement rates will fall as sales volume increases — because no one is guiding new customers through the onboarding path that produces LIR. CS is not optional when scaling GTM.

Unit Economics Validation
✓
LIUE > 3 for at least 2 consecutive quarters
Calculated with real data, not projections. The formula: (ACV × GM% ÷ Annual Churn%) ÷ (Cost per SQL ÷ SQL-to-Customer% + Salesperson Monthly Cost ÷ (SQLs per Salesperson/Month × SQL-to-Customer%)). Two consecutive quarters removes the noise of a single good period.
✓
LIUE > 3 calculated separately for at least one segment
Not just blended. Consolidated LIUE above 3 can mask a failing segment being averaged with a strong one. Calculate LIUE separately by ICP segment, channel, or company size. You must know which segment is driving the economics before scaling.
✓
Payback period < 18 months at current ACV and CAC
CAC payback = CAC ÷ (ACV × GM%). If payback exceeds 18 months, the business requires too much capital to scale efficiently. This is a structural issue — either ACV is too low, GM% is too low, or CAC is too high.
✓
Gross margin ≥ 70%
Gross margin below 70% distorts LTV calculations and signals excessive COGS in a SaaS business. If your GM% is below 70%, your LIUE calculation is being flattered by an LTV that won't hold as you scale the delivery cost base.
✓
No single large deal > 30% of ARR
Concentration risk distorts LIUE. One large outlier customer can inflate ACV, suppress churn rate, and make LIUE look healthy when the underlying economics of the mid-market business are broken. If one deal is > 30% of ARR, segment it out of your LIUE calculation.
GTM Playbook Validation
✓
Full 5-component GTM playbook is documented in writing
ICP & Buyer Persona, Buyer Journey Map, Prospecting Guide, Discovery Guide, Presentation Guide (3–5 swim lanes), and CS Onboarding Guide. Written. In a single, version-controlled document. Not in anyone's head.
✓
Playbook has been executed consistently by at least 3 different reps
Not just the founder or top rep. If only one person can execute the playbook successfully, you don't have a scalable playbook — you have a talented individual. Three different reps executing with comparable results is the minimum validation signal.
✓
Film reviews happening regularly with playbook updates
Weekly (daily during active GTM Fit). Every call reviewed generates one potential playbook update. If you haven't run a film review in two weeks, your playbook is stale. The cadence is the governance mechanism — without it, the playbook decays.
✓
Sales cycle length predictable within ±30% for primary segment
Predictability is a leading indicator of playbook maturity. If sales cycles are wildly inconsistent, the playbook isn't reliably guiding buyer behavior through the stages. Target ±30% variance around the median cycle length before scaling headcount.
✓
Average quota attainment for reps >6 months tenure is ≥ 80% of target
Exclude reps still in ramp. Reps past 6 months (fully ramped) achieving below 80% of quota is a playbook or demand gen problem — not a rep problem. Fix the system before hiring more people into it.
Demand Generation
✓
At least 1 demand gen channel generating SQLs measurably (with known cost per SQL)
One channel that you understand well enough to predict: if we invest $X, we get Y SQLs. "We get some leads from LinkedIn" is not measurable demand gen. Cost per SQL from this channel must be known and tracked month-over-month.
✓
Cost per SQL is stable or decreasing quarter-over-quarter
A rising cost per SQL signals channel saturation or declining relevance — and your LIUE will degrade as you scale. Stable or decreasing cost per SQL signals a scalable channel. Do not pour headcount into a CAC structure that's moving in the wrong direction.
✓
SQL-to-customer rate ≥ 20%
Below 20% indicates the demand gen channel is generating low-quality SQLs — leads that look qualified at first pass but don't convert. This inflates your cost per customer and compresses LIUE. Quality of demand gen matters as much as volume.
✓
Demand gen channel brings in ICP customers: LIR achievement rate for SQLs ≥ threshold
The final demand gen validation: customers from this channel are achieving LIR at the same rate as your ICP average. If the channel brings in leads that convert to customers but don't achieve LIR, you're buying churn, not growth.
Team & Organization
✓
Sales leader is "Process Builder" profile (can codify and scale, not just sell)
The Growth & Moat phase requires someone who can convert the GTM Fit playbook into a scalable hiring and training system. If your current sales leader's strength is personal selling rather than system building, consider whether they can evolve or need to be complemented.
✓
Hiring scorecard has been validated: reps with high scores are outperforming
After 6 months of hiring 2 reps/month, compare the scorecard to actual performance data. Are high-scoring hires outperforming? If not, the scorecard needs to be revised before you scale the hiring volume. A broken scorecard multiplied by 4× hiring speed is catastrophic.
✓
CS team has formal LIR program (customers tracked, interventions triggered)
Every new customer is enrolled in a defined LIR achievement program. Progress is tracked. If a customer falls behind on the LIR trajectory, an intervention is automatically triggered. This is not optional at scale — LIR achievement rate is a direct input to LIUE.
✓
RevOps function exists to maintain data quality in CRM
At scale, data quality degrades without a dedicated owner. RevOps maintains CRM hygiene, ensures stage definitions are enforced, tracks LIUE leading indicators, and flags anomalies before they distort the speedometer. Without RevOps, you lose visibility into the metrics that govern scale pace.
✓
Forecasting process in place with >80% forecast accuracy over 3 consecutive quarters
Forecast accuracy is itself a leading indicator of GTM process maturity. If your forecasts are consistently off by more than 20%, you don't have visibility into your pipeline — which means you can't predict when to accelerate or decelerate hiring. Three consecutive quarters of >80% accuracy is the bar.

The Ratchet Problem

Scaling GTM is not easily reversed. Each person you hire creates: recruiting time invested, offer letters signed, onboarding resources consumed, management bandwidth consumed, and — if it doesn't work — performance management cycles and severance. The cost of unwinding premature scaling is 18–24 months of disruption. Moving slowly through the gates is not conservative — it is the high-velocity choice.

What Happens If You Fail a Gate
Failing a gate is not a failure — it's a diagnosis. Each gate failure points to a specific intervention, not a general "work harder" response.
Gate 1 Failure
Can't transition from PMF to GTM Fit
  • Don't hire salespeople — invest in CS instead to improve LIR achievement rates
  • Re-examine LIR: is it the right event? Is T (the time window) correct for your onboarding cycle?
  • Check ICP: are you selling to customers who have the organizational capacity to succeed with your product?
  • Run structured customer interviews with churned accounts — at least 5, led by a founder
  • If LIR achievement is below 40%, consider whether the product itself requires iteration before GTM investment
Gate 2 Failure
Can't transition from GTM Fit to Growth & Moat
  • Identify which LIUE lever is broken: LTV side (churn too high, ACV too low, GM% too low) or CAC side (cost per SQL too high, SQL-to-customer% too low, rep productivity too low)?
  • If CAC is the problem: examine demand gen channel quality and rep productivity per the per-rep funnel analysis
  • If LTV is the problem: check churn root causes, review pricing model, and examine contract length
  • Do not add headcount until LIUE recovers to > 3 — adding reps with broken unit economics multiplies the problem
  • Run film reviews daily until playbook quality improves — treat it as a GTM Fit rebuild, not a Growth & Moat execution problem
The Founder's Job When the Gate Fails

When you fail a gate, the founder's job is to go back into the field. Not to manage the problem from the office — to personally re-run discovery calls, re-examine customer interviews, and rebuild intuition about why the LIR or LIUE metrics aren't hitting threshold. The data tells you what is broken. The field tells you why.
