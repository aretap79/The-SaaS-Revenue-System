The Anti-Pattern: Hiring on Gut Feel

Most sales hiring is broken at the evaluation layer — not the sourcing layer. Fixing how you evaluate candidates is the highest-leverage intervention in the hiring process.

Most sales interviews evaluate the wrong things. Interviewers walk out of an interview saying "I really liked them" or "they had great presence" — and those judgments are heavily influenced by factors with no predictive value: confidence, charisma, whether the interviewer saw themselves in the candidate, or whether the candidate could articulate a good answer to "where do you see yourself in five years?"

Mark Roberge's research at HubSpot across hundreds of sales hires produced a counterintuitive finding: the attributes that most commonly lead interviewers to make a hire — confidence, charm, prior sales experience, and "presence" — have weak to no correlation with quota attainment. The attributes that actually predict success are different, and most of them are not naturally surfaced in a conventional interview.

The fix is systematic: define 10 specific attributes, weight them by predictive importance, and score every candidate consistently against those attributes. This creates two benefits: (1) your hire quality improves because you're evaluating the right things, and (2) you accumulate data that lets you refine the model over time by comparing scorecard results to rep performance.

Watch for: Likability bias in sales scorecards

The most common reason good candidates get rejected and bad candidates get hired is the likability trap — confusing a pleasant interview experience with predicted job performance. Likability predicts nothing. Coachability, curiosity, and work ethic predict almost everything. Build your process to surface those, and explicitly subtract likability from your evaluation criteria.

★ Coachability — The #1 Predictor of Sales Success

Coachability is the single highest-weighted attribute and the most counterintuitive one to evaluate. The interview question that surfaces it most reliably is deceptively simple — but the response tells you almost everything.

"Tell me about a time when you received critical feedback. What was the feedback? What did you do with it?"

Red flag response: Explains why the feedback was wrong, or pivots immediately to what they changed and how it was the right call. The response is about them, not about what they learned.

Green flag response: Describes the specific feedback clearly, acknowledges it directly without defensiveness, describes the specific action they took, and states the measurable outcome that resulted. The response is concrete, specific, and shows ownership without defensiveness.

Detailed Scoring Rubrics by Attribute

1. Coachability
★ Top Predictor Weight: 9 Max: 90 pts
Definition Actively seeks feedback rather than waiting for it. When they receive feedback, they implement it immediately and demonstrate the change in their next interaction — not the next quarter. They do not defend past behavior or explain why the feedback was wrong. They have a growth mindset at the operational level: every call, every conversation is a data point for improvement.
Interview Questions
  • "Tell me about a time when you received critical feedback. What was it? What exactly did you do with it?"
  • "Who has given you the most useful coaching in your career, and what made their feedback useful to you?"
  • "Describe a specific situation where you changed a behavior based on feedback. How long did it take you to make the change?"
Score 10: Elite
Proactively solicits feedback with specific questions. Implements changes within days, not weeks. Can describe multiple coaching relationships and exactly what they learned from each. Their self-assessment of weaknesses matches what their managers would say.
Score 7–9: Strong
Accepts feedback without defensiveness and implements it. Has good self-awareness. Can describe specific changes they made. May not proactively seek feedback as consistently, but responds well when given it.
Score 4–6: Adequate
Generally accepts feedback but occasionally explains context or defends the original behavior. Implements changes, but it takes multiple sessions. Has awareness of their weaknesses but is still working on responding non-defensively.
Score 1–3: Risk
Responds to feedback by explaining why the feedback was wrong, or why the situation was unique. Doesn't change behavior after coaching. Will stagnate under any manager after a quarter of non-improvement. Do not hire.
2. Curiosity
★ Top Predictor Weight: 9 Max: 90 pts
Definition Genuinely curious about the buyer's business, not just their pain point. Researches before every call. Asks questions that demonstrate preparation and interest. In a discovery call, curiosity is what generates the insights that lead to a differentiated pitch. Curious reps retain information and become domain experts faster.
Interview Questions
  • "What did you learn about our company before coming in today? What was most interesting to you?"
  • "Tell me about the most interesting thing you learned about a prospect's business in the last 90 days."
  • "Walk me through how you prepare for a first discovery call with a new prospect."
Score 10: Elite
Has researched your company in depth — knows your recent news, products, competitive positioning, and customer base. Asks intelligent questions about the business that demonstrate genuine interest. References specific things they learned during preparation.
Score 7–9: Strong
Has done solid preparation. Asks good questions during the interview. Shows genuine interest in the problem space. May not have gone as deep as elite candidates, but demonstrates the behavior pattern consistently.
Score 4–6: Adequate
Basic preparation. Asks generic questions. Knows the company exists and what you sell. Lacks depth in preparation. Discovery approach is formulaic rather than genuinely interested.
Score 1–3: Risk
Has not researched the company. Asks no questions or asks questions easily answered on the website. In a discovery scenario, would talk more than they listen. Unlikely to develop into a consultative seller.
3. Work Ethic
Weight: 8 Max: 80 pts
Definition Volume and consistency of activity over time. Self-directed — doesn't need external motivation to hit activity targets. Shows up consistently regardless of recent results. Work ethic in sales is not about hours at the desk; it's about sustained activity volume and the discipline to execute the playbook even when results are slow.
Interview Questions
  • "Tell me about a stretch of time when your results were not where you wanted them. How did your activity change during that period?"
  • "Describe your daily routine in your last role. What time did you start? What did the first two hours look like?"
  • "What motivates you when you're in a cold streak with no deals closing?"
Score 10: Elite
Has a documented history of sustained high activity. Can describe their daily routine with specificity. During cold stretches, activity volume held or increased — they doubled down rather than pulled back. Has a clear internal motivation framework.
Score 7–9: Strong
Consistent performer. Activity held steady during difficult periods. Describes self as self-motivated and provides evidence. No external whip required. May have had periods of dip but recovered independently.
Score 4–6: Adequate
Generally hardworking but activity dipped during difficult periods. Required some external push to re-engage. Can describe the behavior but cannot cite sustained patterns across time.
Score 1–3: Risk
Activity is results-dependent — when things are going well, they work hard. When in a slump, activity drops further. Requires constant external motivation. Will underperform in a ramp period where results are naturally slow.
4. Intelligence
Weight: 8 Max: 80 pts
Definition Pattern recognition and synthesis speed — not academic credentials. Synthesizes new information quickly, adapts message to context, handles complexity without getting flustered. In sales, intelligence shows up as the ability to listen to a prospect, quickly map their situation to the relevant product value, and articulate that connection clearly.
Interview Questions
  • "Tell me something complex you've had to learn quickly in a previous role. How did you approach it?"
  • "Give me your honest take on our competitive positioning versus [competitor]. What do you see as our main vulnerability?"
  • "If you had to explain what we do to a CFO vs. a VP of Engineering, how would those conversations differ?"
Score 10: Elite
Makes insightful observations during the interview. Handles multi-layered questions without simplifying. Quickly grasps the nuances of complex situations. Can give a credible competitive assessment having researched the company for a few hours.
Score 7–9: Strong
Clear thinker. Processes questions quickly. Handles complexity with moderate nuance. Adapts their communication style based on context. Occasionally oversimplifies but recovers well when pushed.
Score 4–6: Adequate
Solid on simple questions; struggles with complex or layered ones. Not fast enough to handle complex discovery in real-time without a script. May be fine in a very structured, playbook-driven role.
Score 1–3: Risk
Slow to process novel information. Sticks closely to prepared answers. Cannot adapt to off-script scenarios. Will struggle in technical or consultative selling contexts.
5. Prior Success
Weight: 7 Max: 70 pts
Definition Demonstrated achievement in any domain — not necessarily sales. Academic, athletic, creative, or career achievement all count. Look for the pattern of effort-driven results: set a goal, put in the work, achieved the outcome, and can articulate clearly what made the difference.
Interview Questions
  • "Tell me about the achievement you're most proud of in your career so far. What made it hard, and what role did you specifically play?"
  • "What does your track record look like across the roles you've held? Where were you in the top 25% of your peer group?"
  • "Describe a time you set a goal for yourself that required sustained effort over 6+ months. What was the goal and what was the outcome?"
Score 10: Elite
Multiple domains of demonstrated achievement. In sales roles, was top quartile consistently. Has a personal narrative of setting ambitious goals and achieving them. Can describe the mechanisms of their success — not just the outcome.
Score 7–9: Strong
Clear track record of achievement in at least one domain. Demonstrates pride in their work. Can articulate what made them successful. In sales roles, performed above average.
Score 4–6: Adequate
Some achievement but inconsistent. Performs well in some contexts and not others. Struggles to identify a defining success. Average performer in previous roles.
Score 1–3: Risk
No clear track record of achievement in any domain. Describes themselves as hardworking without evidence. In previous roles, was below average or has no comparable performance data.
6. Passion
Weight: 5 Max: 50 pts
Definition Genuine enthusiasm for the specific problem being solved — not generic sales enthusiasm. Knows the product domain. Has sought out information about the space without being asked. Would be less satisfied selling anything else. Passion correlates with longevity and discretionary effort.
Interview Questions
  • "What drew you specifically to this space? What do you find most interesting about the problem we're solving?"
  • "How do you stay current in this industry? What do you read or follow?"
Score 8–10
Can speak at length about the space. Has formed opinions about the industry. Wouldn't be equally enthusiastic about another role in a different domain.
Score 5–7
Genuine interest in the space. Knows the basics. May not be deeply studied but shows real enthusiasm for the problem.
Score 3–4
General sales enthusiasm without domain-specific interest. Would likely be equally excited about any sales role with a good quota.
Score 1–2
No domain interest evident. This is a stepping stone opportunity for them. Will leave when a better offer arrives.
7. Preparation
Weight: 3 Max: 30 pts
Definition Researched your company before the interview. Has specific, smart questions ready. This attribute evaluates whether the candidate brings the same preparation to a sales call as they bring to an interview — which is a direct behavioral signal.
Interview Questions
  • "What did you learn about us before today that surprised you?"
  • "What questions have you prepared about the role or the business?"
Score 8–10
Deep research: knows your customers, recent announcements, competitive position. Questions are specific and demonstrate preparation. Treats the interview like a discovery call.
Score 5–7
Solid preparation. Knows the company well. Has reasonable questions. Clearly spent meaningful time before the interview.
Score 3–4
Basic preparation. Knows the homepage content. Generic questions. Did the minimum required.
Score 1–2
Little to no preparation. Cannot describe what the company does in detail. No questions ready. This behavior predicts how they'll approach discovery calls.
8 & 9. Adaptability & Competitiveness
Weight: 3 each Max: 30 pts each
Adaptability — Definition Handles change without freezing. When the playbook changes, the territory shifts, or the product pivots, they find new approaches quickly rather than waiting for instructions. Comfort with ambiguity is a survival trait in early-stage sales.
Adaptability Questions
  • "Tell me about the most significant change to your sales process in the last 2 years. How did you respond to it?"
  • "Describe a time when the playbook you were following stopped working. What did you do?"
Competitiveness — Definition Drives to win; dislikes losing; has internal scoreboards. Channels competitive energy into work — not politics, not undermining peers, not toxic behavior. Great competitors set goals, track them obsessively, and feel genuine discomfort when behind.
Competitiveness Questions
  • "Where do you rank yourself on your current team? How do you know?"
  • "Tell me about the last time you lost a deal you thought you had. How did you feel? What did you do next?"
10. Brevity
Weight: 3 Max: 30 pts
Definition Communicates clearly and concisely. High signal-to-noise ratio. In a discovery call, the ability to ask a precise question and then listen — without filling silence with unnecessary words — is what separates top reps from average ones. This attribute is assessed by listening to how the candidate speaks, not what they say.
How to Assess in the Interview
  • Watch for rambling answers that take 2+ minutes to make a point that could be made in 30 seconds.
  • Notice how they handle open-ended questions — do they structure their response or meander?
  • Ask: "Explain what we do to me in one sentence." Listen for precision.
Score 8–10
Every answer is precise, structured, and exactly the right length. No filler phrases. Gets to the point and stops. Uses examples economically.
Score 5–7
Generally concise. Occasionally over-explains but recovers. Signal-to-noise is good. Would be effective on a discovery call.
Score 3–4
Tends to ramble. Takes too long to get to the point. In a discovery call, this translates to talking through the silence that buyers need to think.
Score 1–2
Structural communication issues. Answers are consistently much longer than necessary. Will struggle in any sales motion that requires precise, controlled conversations.

How to Run the Interview

The interview structure matters as much as the questions. A structured process produces consistent, comparable data. An unstructured conversation produces vibes.

1
Pre-Interview Setup
Two interviewers per candidate. Both review the scorecard before the interview. Divide attributes between interviewers — each is responsible for surfacing evidence for 4–5 attributes. Do not share observations until both have scored independently.
2
60-Minute Interview Structure
10 minutes: background and prior success. 20 minutes: curiosity, coachability, and intelligence probes. 15 minutes: role-play scenario. 10 minutes: work ethic and passion probes. 5 minutes: candidate questions (score preparation and brevity here).
3
Post-Interview Debrief
Each interviewer submits scores independently before discussing. Debrief rule: justify every score with a specific behavioral example from the interview — no "I liked/didn't like them." Any attribute scored below 5 by either interviewer requires explicit written discussion before a hire decision is made.
Panel Consensus Rule

If any interviewer scores any attribute below 5, that attribute must be explicitly addressed in the debrief. The hire decision requires either (a) evidence that the low score was an evaluation error, or (b) a written plan for how the gap will be addressed in the first 90 days. You cannot average away a 2 with someone else's 9.

The Role-Play Component

Every AE candidate should complete a standardized role-play. This provides behavioral evidence for curiosity, brevity, and coachability that interview questions alone cannot surface.

Standard Role-Play Scenario
"You've just finished a 30-minute discovery call with Jordan Lee, VP of Operations at a 200-person manufacturing company. During the call, Jordan mentioned that their team spends 2–3 hours per week on manual reporting across spreadsheets, that their current system hasn't been updated in 4 years, and that they're evaluating solutions because a board member raised it in Q3 planning. You have 3 minutes to do a discovery recap and present your most relevant product feature."
Evaluate: Discovery Recap
Did they recap the buyer's specific pain (manual reporting, 2–3 hrs/week, outdated system) before pitching? Or did they skip straight to the feature? Recapping demonstrates listening and earns the right to present.
Evaluate: Pain-to-Feature Link
Did they tie the feature directly to the stated pain? Great: "Because you're losing 2+ hours/week to manual reports, [feature] eliminates that specific step..." Average: Generic product description without connecting to what Jordan said.
Evaluate: Coachability Under Pressure
After the role-play, redirect them: "That was good — try that again, but lead with the pain before naming the feature." If they implement the feedback immediately and precisely, that's a 10 on coachability. If they explain why their approach was right, score 3 or below.

Conditional Hire Protocol

If a candidate scores below the threshold but is strong on the top 3 weighted attributes (Coachability, Curiosity, Work Ethic — combined weight 26), they may be a conditional hire. Requirement: a written plan signed by the hiring manager that specifies which gaps will be addressed, how, and by what milestone in the first 90 days.
