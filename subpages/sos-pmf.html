The Definition of PMF

Most teams declare PMF prematurely. The framework below replaces intuition with a precise, falsifiable definition — one that every team member and investor can point to with shared understanding.

PMF Is NOT
  • "We have happy customers" — happiness is subjective and does not predict renewal
  • "We closed our first deals" — first deals are experiments, not patterns
  • "NPS is high" — NPS is supplementary signal, not a definition of fit
  • "We're growing month over month" — new bookings can mask high churn indefinitely
  • "Investors are excited" — investor enthusiasm is not a customer success proxy
PMF IS
  • A specific, measurable Leading Indicator of Retention (LIR) that correlates with long-term customer retention
  • A LIR that is proven to be True across multiple consecutive customer cohorts
  • A pattern you can replicate — not a one-time event or a single flagship customer
The PMF Formula
PMF Definition Formula
[PMF] = "Yes" if:
([LIR] correlates with long-term customer retention) AND ([LIR] is "True" — i.e., P% of customers achieve E event(s) within T days)
Both conditions must be simultaneously true. The first validates that the LIR is predictive. The second confirms the LIR threshold is being met consistently across cohorts.
Definition

PMF is achieved when your LIR threshold is met consistently across a cohort of customers. One cohort is not enough — you need 2–3 consecutive monthly cohorts all clearing the threshold before you can declare PMF achieved.

The Leading Indicator of Retention (LIR)

LIR is the most critical metric in Stage 1. It replaces the lagging, slow-moving retention number with an early, measurable signal — giving you months of lead time to course-correct before churn materializes.

Full LIR Formula
LIR = P% of customers achieve E event(s) within T days
P = 60–80% threshold E = Objective, instrumentable event T = As short as possible
P
Percentage Threshold
The minimum % of customers who must hit the event. Setting P too high makes it impossible; too low makes it non-predictive.

60–70% — SMB / transactional
70–80% — Mid-Market
75–85% — Enterprise
E
The Event
A specific customer action that predicts long-term success. Must be all of:

• Objective — not "they seem happy"
• Instrumentable — trackable in product/CRM
• Value-aligned — reflects actual product use
• Correlated — validated against retention data
T
Time Window
The time window in which E must occur. As short as possible — the tighter the window, the faster you know.

7 days — PLG / self-serve
30 days — SMB SaaS
60–90 days — Enterprise SaaS
Examples of Good LIR Events
  • → "60% of customers complete 3 discovery calls within 30 days of signing" — specific count, specific action, short window
  • → "70% of customers invite 3 team members within 14 days" — measurable in CRM, tied to activation signal
  • → "65% of customers run their first report within 7 days" — objective, product-trackable, tied to core value delivery
  • → "75% of customers process 100+ transactions within 60 days" — quantifiable, tied to product utility and economic value
  • → "70% of customers integrate their primary data source within 30 days" — reflects real implementation, predicts long-term stickiness

How to Define Your LIR

LIR definition is not a workshop exercise — it is an empirical process. You derive it from customer data, validate it statistically, and iterate as your product evolves.

  1. Interview your best-retained customers Ask what they did in their first 30/60/90 days that made the product essential. Look for patterns in behavior, not just sentiment.
  2. Identify the "aha moment" The specific action or milestone where the customer first realized the true value of your product. This is the candidate for E.
  3. Make it objective and instrumentable The event must be measurable in your product data, CRM, or CS systems. If you can't instrument it, you can't track it. Redefine E until it is measurable.
  4. Set P threshold at 60–80% Start with 70% as a default. Set it high enough to be statistically meaningful, low enough to be achievable by a healthy customer base. Adjust based on cohort analysis.
  5. Set T as short as possible The earlier you can confirm success, the more valuable the LIR becomes as a management tool. Test shorter time windows — if 70% can hit E in 14 days, don't use 30 days.
  6. Validate correlation against 12-month renewal data Using customers from 12–18 months ago: compare the 12-month retention rate of customers who hit LIR vs. those who did not. A valid LIR requires a gap of 20+ percentage points.
  7. Iterate as the product evolves The LIR that is valid at $1M ARR may not be valid at $10M ARR. Revalidate correlation data annually or whenever major product changes are made. Do not change LIR arbitrarily — changes break cohort comparability.
Warning

Do not change your LIR definition more than once per year unless a major product change makes the existing E event invalid. Frequent LIR changes prevent cohort-to-cohort comparison and make your PMF data meaningless.

Target Market in Stage 1: Early Adopters

Every element of your Stage 1 GTM strategy must be optimized for learning — not efficiency, not scale. That starts with targeting the right customers: early adopters who will succeed despite an imperfect product.

Who to Target (and Who NOT to Target)
  • DO target: Innovators and early adopters who have an acute, urgent pain point and will tolerate product imperfection in exchange for a potential breakthrough solution
  • DO NOT target: Early majority or late majority — they need a polished, reference-backed, proven solution before they will buy
  • ICP in Stage 1 is about psychographics as much as firmographics — you need the right buyer mindset, not just the right company profile
Early Adopter vs. Early Majority: Contrast
Attribute Early Adopter (Stage 1 Target) Early Majority (Stage 2+ Target)
Tolerance for bugs High — willing to work around issues Low — expects polished, stable product
Need for references Low — visionary, self-convinced High — wants 3+ case studies before buying
Decision speed Fast — moves on conviction Slow — requires committee consensus
ROI requirements Flexible — willing to bet on potential Rigid — needs documented ROI proof
Implementation tolerance High — will invest time in setup Low — expects turnkey implementation
Feedback quality Rich — deep engagement, honest critique Shallow — less exploratory, lower signal

GTM Playbook in Stage 1: Win at All Costs

In Stage 1, the playbook is not codified — it is exploratory. You are not executing a defined process; you are discovering what works. Selling in Stage 1 is a product discovery activity.

What the Stage 1 GTM Motion Looks Like
  • Founder / PM + AE hybrid leads GTM: the person selling should be deeply embedded in the product — they are learning and selling simultaneously
  • Deep discovery: every sales conversation is a research interview — document every objection, every use case, every competitive mention
  • Customized demos: presentations are tailored to the specific buyer context discovered in the prior conversation — not templated
  • Heavy post-sale involvement: founders and senior team are personally involved in onboarding to accelerate LIR achievement and extract product insights
  • Obsessive success tracking: every new customer is tracked individually against LIR milestones from day one
What NOT to Do in Stage 1
  • Do NOT hire a large sales team — you don't have a repeatable playbook to give them
  • Do NOT spend on demand generation — you don't know your ICP well enough to target it efficiently
  • Do NOT build rigid sales processes — process optimization requires data you don't have yet
  • Do NOT optimize for call volume or pipeline quantity — optimize for learning and LIR achievement
The Right Stage 1 Sales Hire Profile
PM + AE Hybrid

The Stage 1 sales hire is someone who can LEARN the playbook, not execute a pre-defined one. They have enough product intuition to participate in discovery, enough sales skill to close, and enough intellectual curiosity to ask the right questions. Prior domain expertise matters more than a polished sales pedigree.

Pricing in Stage 1: Solve for Customer Commitment

Pricing in Stage 1 is not about maximizing revenue — it is about creating the conditions for customer success. Underpriced customers do not implement. The right price creates motivation to engage.

  • Price to create skin in the game: customers who pay almost nothing do not prioritize implementation — charge enough that success matters to them
  • Annual contracts preferred: multi-month commitments reduce churn noise and create mutual accountability for onboarding success
  • Avoid freemium and PLG until PMF is proven: free users don't implement, don't give you quality feedback, and distort your LIR data
  • Simple pricing structures: per-seat or flat-rate pricing reduces cognitive load and eliminates pricing friction that slows decisions
  • Pricing is not permanent: you will raise prices in Stage 2 as you quantify the ROI you deliver — Stage 1 pricing is provisional
The Free Customer Trap

Free or heavily discounted customers are the most expensive customers in Stage 1. They consume CS resources, pollute your LIR cohort data with low-motivation implementers, and provide misleading signals about market demand. If they won't pay, they won't succeed.

Compensation in Stage 1: Based on Customer Retention

The most common Stage 1 compensation mistake is paying salespeople 100% at contract close. This incentivizes closing bad-fit customers who will never succeed — exactly the wrong behavior when you are trying to prove PMF.

50%
At Contract Signing
Rewards closing the deal
50%
At LIR Achievement
~90 days post-close, when customer achieves E within T
Why This Comp Structure Works
  • Prevents the "closing hero" problem: reps who close bad-fit customers lose 50% of their commission when the customer fails to achieve LIR
  • Aligns rep behavior with PMF discovery: reps become obsessive about selling to the right customers — they only get full commission when the customer succeeds
  • Makes ICP self-enforcing: reps who consistently close non-ICP deals will have below-target commissions — creating natural ICP discipline without micromanagement
  • Generates better discovery data: reps who care about LIR achievement ask better qualification questions and conduct deeper discovery
The PMF Cohort Dashboard

The cohort dashboard is how PMF progress is tracked, managed, and communicated. It should be the first slide in every board deck — before the P&L, before ARR, before any other metric.

How to Build the Dashboard
  1. Create monthly acquisition cohorts Group all customers by the calendar month they were signed. Each row = one cohort.
  2. Track LIR achievement by time elapsed For each cohort, record the % achieving LIR by month 1, 2, 3 … 12 after signing. Each column = months since acquisition.
  3. Color-code each cell Green = above threshold (≥P%), Yellow = within 10 points of threshold, Red = below threshold. Gray = insufficient time has elapsed.
  4. Track the PMF declaration threshold Target: 60–80% LIR achievement sustained across 3+ consecutive cohorts. Once sustained for 2–3 quarters, declare PMF achieved and move to Stage 2.

Jan 38% 62% 79% 83% 92%

Mar 44% 65% 74% — —

LIR threshold = 70%. Jan, Feb, Mar cohorts cleared threshold at Mo. 3 (T). Apr cohort still developing — flagged for review at Mo. 2 (58%). High LIR cohorts show 91–92% 12-month renewal — validates LIR as predictive.

PMF Declaration Rule

Cohorts with LIR achievement ≥ threshold should have 90%+ 12-month renewal. If your high-LIR cohorts are not renewing at 90%+, your LIR event (E) is not predictive enough. Redefine E before declaring PMF.

Common PMF Mistakes

These are the six failure modes that prevent teams from reaching genuine PMF — or cause them to falsely believe they have it when they do not.

  1. Declaring PMF on gut feel. "Customers seem excited" is not PMF. Without LIR data and a validated correlation to 12-month renewal, you do not have PMF — you have optimism. Build the cohort table first.
  2. Selling to non-ICP customers. Non-ICP customers inflate booking numbers but produce low LIR achievement rates that destroy cohort data quality. Every non-ICP deal makes it harder to identify the signal within the noise.
  3. Hiring salespeople before LIR is defined. Salespeople without a defined LIR will sell to anyone. They optimize for signed contracts, not customer success. The result: high bookings, low LIR achievement, high churn.
  4. Optimizing for top-line ARR. Growing ARR by closing deals faster than customers churn creates a treadmill. Once growth slows, the underlying churn problem becomes visible — and it is much more expensive to fix at scale.
  5. Changing LIR too frequently. Every LIR change resets your cohort clock. If you change the definition quarterly, you will never have enough historical data to validate it. Commit to a definition and measure it for at least 2 full cohort cycles before revisiting.
  6. Setting T too long. If T is 6 months, you won't know whether customers are on track until half a year has passed. By then it's too late to intervene. Compress T aggressively — a 30-day LIR is 6x more actionable than a 180-day LIR.
Key Metrics to Track in Stage 1

These five metrics define the Stage 1 measurement framework. LIR Achievement Rate and Time to LIR are the primary operating metrics — everything else is secondary validation.

Metric Definition Target Why It Matters
LIR Achievement Rate % of each monthly cohort achieving E within T days 60–80% Primary PMF indicator — the core metric of Stage 1
Time to LIR Average days from contract signing to E event completion Minimize — below T Speed predicts retention; faster time-to-LIR = higher renewal probability
12-Month Retention Rate % of cohort (signed 12 months ago) that renewed at month 12 >85% Lagging validation of LIR — confirms the LIR formula is working
LIR Correlation Gap 12-month retention rate of LIR-achieved customers minus retention rate of LIR-not-achieved customers >20 percentage points Validates that E is genuinely predictive — a gap below 20pp means the event doesn't predict retention
NPS Net Promoter Score collected at 60–90 days post-onboarding >40 Supplementary signal — useful for qualitative triangulation, not a PMF definition

PMF Achievement Checklist
  • ✓ LIR is defined with P, E, and T — and the E event is instrumented in your product or CRM
  • ✓ LIR correlation is validated: customers who achieved LIR renew at >20pp higher rate than those who did not
  • ✓ LIR threshold (P%) is met across 2–3 consecutive monthly cohorts
  • ✓ 12-month retention rate for LIR-achieved cohorts is >85%
  • ✓ ICP is documented: you can describe the firmographic and psychographic profile that predicts LIR achievement
  • ✓ Sales comp is structured 50/50 at signing / LIR achievement — not 100% at close
In the 6-Phase Revenue System

Stage 1 (PMF) is the scientific validation layer that must precede Phase 3 (GTM). It operates primarily within Phase 1 (ICP, Messaging, Playbook) and shapes every hiring decision in Phase 2.

Phase 1 — Primary Home
ICP, Messaging & Playbook
Stage 1 work happens here. Define LIR event, identify Early Adopter ICP, build exploratory “Win at All Costs” playbook, set comp to LIR-based structure.
Phase 2 — Hiring Mode
PM+AE Hybrid Hire
In Stage 1, hire only 1–2 reps who can discover the playbook. Do NOT scale headcount until LIR ≥ threshold for 2 cohorts. Comp: 50% at close, 50% at LIR.
Phase 6 — If PMF Fails
LIR Diagnostic
LIR rate below 60%? Check: Is ICP targeting Early Adopters? Is E event objective and instrumentable? Is T short enough? Is CS driving LIR achievement?
