Phase 3 — Demand Generation
Funnel Architecture — Defining the Revenue Pipeline from Lead to Close
Without agreed funnel definitions, every pipeline number is meaningless, every forecast is fiction, and marketing and sales will always be at war.
Home› Phase 3› Funnel Architecture

Why Funnel Definitions Matter

The single biggest source of sales and marketing conflict in SaaS companies is undefined or inconsistently applied funnel stage definitions. Marketing claims they delivered 500 MQLs. Sales says 450 were garbage. Both are correct — because no one defined what an MQL actually is. The result is a broken trust relationship, a meaningless pipeline number, and a forecast that management can't rely on.

The Cost of Undefined Stages

When funnel stages are not precisely defined with entry criteria AND exit criteria — and when those definitions are not consistently applied in the CRM — every downstream process breaks. Forecasting becomes guesswork because Stage 3 means different things to different reps. Pipeline coverage analysis is meaningless because you can't compare pipeline velocity when stages have different entry thresholds. Marketing and sales finger-pointing about lead quality is unresolvable because there are no objective criteria to resolve the dispute.

The solution is a shared Revenue Funnel Document — a single, written, CRM-enforced definition of every stage, its entry criteria, exit criteria, associated SLAs, and probability weight. This document must be co-created by marketing, sales, and RevOps leadership and must be enforced technically in the CRM (required fields at stage entry, automated alerts for SLA violations).

The Most Common Funnel Definition Mistake

Defining stages by rep activity ("Stage 2 = demo delivered") rather than by buyer behavior and confirmed outcomes ("Stage 2 = champion identified, success criteria agreed, mutual evaluation plan accepted"). Activity-based stages are gamed by reps who move deals forward to look active. Buyer-behavior-based stages reflect actual deal progression and produce reliable forecasts.

The Complete Funnel — Every Stage Defined

Each stage below is defined with entry criteria (what must be true for a lead/opportunity to enter this stage) and exit criteria (what must happen for it to advance). These are starting templates — calibrate to your specific business, segment, and sales motion.

★
AWARENESS (Pre-Funnel)

The pre-funnel stage encompasses all contact with your brand before a lead enters the managed funnel. A website visitor who reads a blog post, a prospect who sees a LinkedIn ad, a conference attendee who walks past your booth, an SDR target identified from a prospect list — all are in the Awareness stage. This stage is not tracked in the CRM at the individual level (unless the prospect takes a named action like filling out a form) but is measured in aggregate via marketing analytics.

How leads enter the system: Web form fill (demo request, content download, contact us), inbound phone call, SDR outbound response (reply to email or LinkedIn), event check-in scan, chat/chatbot conversation, direct referral. All named leads entering the CRM start here before scoring determines if they become MQLs.

MQL
MQL — Marketing Qualified Lead
~5% probability

An MQL is a lead that has met a defined score threshold (typically 50-75 points) combining fit score (does this person match our ICP?) and engagement score (have they taken actions suggesting purchase intent?). The score threshold must be empirically calibrated to maximize SAL acceptance rate — an MQL should convert to SAL at 50-70% rate; if below 30%, the threshold is too low.

Entry Criteria
  • Lead score ≥ threshold (e.g., 60 points)
  • Fit criteria: job title matches buyer persona
  • Fit criteria: company size in ICP range
  • Behavioral: at least one high-intent action (demo request, pricing page, etc.)
  • Not a competitor, current customer, or disqualified domain
Exit Criteria
  • Sales accepts → becomes SAL (within SLA)
  • Sales rejects with reason code → recycled back to nurture
  • SLA breach: no action within 24-48 hours → auto-escalation to SDR manager
  • Disqualified outright → marked Disqualified with reason
MQL SLA

MQLs must be reviewed and accepted or rejected within 24 hours (business hours) for high-intent MQLs (demo requests, pricing inquiries) and 48 hours for lower-intent MQLs (content downloads). SLA compliance should be tracked in the CRM and reported weekly. SLA violations are a leading indicator of pipeline leakage.

SAL
SAL — Sales Accepted Lead
~8% probability

An SAL is an MQL that a sales rep (typically an SDR or AE, depending on the motion) has reviewed and accepted as worth pursuing — meaning they believe the lead is a real person at a real company that could plausibly buy your product. Acceptance means the rep commits to attempting contact within the SLA window.

Entry Criteria
  • SDR or AE has reviewed the lead
  • Lead is a real person at a real company (verified)
  • Company size, industry, and geography within target parameters
  • Rep has accepted ownership and initiated contact attempt
Exit Criteria
  • Qualification call completed → becomes SQL
  • Unable to reach after X attempts → recycled to nurture sequence
  • Disqualified on first contact (wrong person, no budget, wrong stage) → closed disqualified
  • SLA: first contact attempt within 4 hours of acceptance; 5 attempts before recycle
SQL
SQL — Sales Qualified Lead
~12% probability

An SQL has passed initial qualification — typically via a brief 15-30 minute discovery/qualification call. The qualification framework (BANT, MEDDICC, SPICED — see ICP & Qualification page) has been applied to confirm that the basic requirements for a deal exist: there is a business problem your product addresses, there is probable budget, someone with authority has been identified, and the timeline is reasonable for your sales cycle.

Entry Criteria (BANT-Minimum)
  • Business Pain confirmed — a specific problem your product addresses
  • Budget probable — budget category exists or initiative has executive sponsorship
  • Authority located — decision-maker identified (though not necessarily engaged)
  • Timeline reasonable — not "in 3 years," active evaluation likely within 6 months
  • Qualification call documented in CRM with notes
Exit Criteria
  • Full discovery completed, pain confirmed → Stage 1 Opportunity created
  • ICP validated: company matches firmographic and technographic criteria
  • Economic Buyer identified (name, title on record)
  • Next steps agreed with prospect (not just "I'll reach out")
  • Disqualified if BANT fails → closed with reason code
1
Stage 1 — Discovery
15-20%

Discovery is the first formal opportunity stage. The rep has moved beyond qualification (confirming the basics exist) to deep discovery (understanding the full picture of the problem, the stakeholders involved, the decision process, and the business impact). This is the most critical stage — what you learn here determines your entire deal strategy for every subsequent stage.

Entry Criteria
  • Discovery call scheduled or completed
  • Opportunity record created in CRM
  • Initial pain documented
  • ICP validation confirmed by AE
Exit Criteria
  • Pain fully confirmed with specific business impact
  • ICP validated (not just probable)
  • Economic Buyer identified by name and title
  • Champion identified (internal advocate)
  • Decision process and criteria mapped
  • Compelling event / deadline identified
  • Next steps agreed in writing (MPA)

Key Questions for Stage 1: What does the problem cost you today? How is this being handled currently? Who else is involved in solving this? What would happen if you didn't fix this? Who has to sign off on the purchase? What other solutions are you evaluating?

2
Stage 2 — Evaluation / Solution Design
30-40%

Stage 2 is the evaluation phase — the prospect is actively evaluating your solution against alternatives (including competitors and the status quo). This stage typically includes a product demonstration, a proof of concept (POC), a sandbox trial, or a deep-dive working session. The champion is now confirmed and actively helping you navigate the internal process.

Entry Criteria
  • Mutual evaluation begun (demo, POC, or deep-dive scheduled)
  • Champion confirmed (internal advocate actively helping)
  • Evaluation scope agreed
Exit Criteria
  • Champion confirmed with access to Economic Buyer
  • Success criteria agreed in writing (how will they evaluate success?)
  • Decision criteria mapped (all factors in the evaluation)
  • Competitive landscape identified (who else is in the deal?)
  • Technical validation completed (security review, integration check)
  • Economic Buyer engaged at least once (email, meeting, or champion confirmed EB support)
The POC Trap

Extended POCs and trials are the #1 deal killer in B2B SaaS. Insist on a Mutual Success Plan before starting any POC: defined success criteria, defined timeframe (30 days maximum), defined decision process after POC completion, and EB engagement at POC kickoff. An open-ended POC without success criteria is just delay — and delay is the enemy of every deal.

3
Stage 3 — Proposal / Business Case
50-60%

Stage 3 begins when you have delivered a formal proposal or business case to the Economic Buyer. This is not the same as emailing a pricing deck to a champion — the EB must be directly engaged. The business case should quantify the ROI of the investment, summarize the evaluation results (POC success, reference checks), and make the commercial ask.

Entry Criteria
  • Proposal delivered to Economic Buyer directly
  • Business case built and presented (ROI documented)
  • Commercial terms provided
  • Success criteria from Stage 2 addressed in proposal
Exit Criteria
  • EB engagement confirmed (response, meeting, or champion confirms active consideration)
  • Evaluation criteria confirmed in our favor (champion provides honest assessment)
  • Legal and procurement process identified
  • Decision timeline confirmed
  • Verbal commitment or shortlisting confirmed
4
Stage 4 — Negotiation / Legal
75-85%

Stage 4 begins when a verbal commitment has been received and the deal is in commercial and legal review. The prospect has chosen your solution — the remaining work is finalizing contract terms, completing legal review (security questionnaires, MSA/DPA negotiation), and navigating procurement approval. This is not a done deal until contracts are signed — but the primary risk has shifted from "will they choose us" to "will internal process complete in time."

Entry Criteria
  • Verbal commitment received from EB or champion with EB confirmation
  • Contract/legal review initiated
  • Redline process or standard contract review underway
  • Procurement/finance stakeholder identified
Exit Criteria
  • Contract fully executed (signed by both parties) → Closed Won
  • Deal collapses during legal/negotiation → Closed Lost with reason
  • Deal pauses (budget freeze, re-org, no decision) → track separately
Stage 4 Risk Factors

Deals that die in Stage 4 almost always die due to risks that were present in earlier stages: legal requirements not surfaced, procurement process not understood, budget not truly confirmed, or the EB commitment was softer than believed. The best Stage 4 risk mitigation is thorough Stage 2 and Stage 3 qualification. In Stage 4, maintain weekly contact with champion, understand the legal timeline, and escalate executive-to-executive if review extends beyond agreed timeline.

✓
CLOSED WON
100%

Contract signed by all required parties. Revenue recognized per company accounting policy (ASC 606 in the US). CRM updated with contract value, start date, contract term, and renewal date. Deal is transitioned to Customer Success within SLA (typically 24-48 hours post-signature). All competitive data, deal notes, and lessons learned archived for analysis.

✗
CLOSED LOST
0%

The prospect chose a competitor, chose to delay (no decision), failed to secure budget approval, or went dark after sustained follow-up. Lost reason codes must be mandatory CRM fields — without consistent lost reason tracking, you cannot identify systematic weaknesses in positioning, qualification, or competitive response. Standard lost reasons: Lost to Competitor (specify which), Lost to No Decision / Status Quo, Lost — Budget not approved, Lost — Product gap, Lost — Timeline slip >6 months, Lost — Champion left, Lost — Price.

Lost deal analysis should be a monthly ritual. Which competitors appear most frequently? At which stage do most losses occur? What product gaps or pricing issues appear across multiple lost deals? This is your most important feedback loop for product roadmap, pricing strategy, and competitive enablement.

Stage Probability Defaults & Calibration

Probabilities are used in weighted pipeline forecasting. Default probabilities should reflect historical close rates at each stage, not aspirational targets. Calibrate annually based on prior year deal data.

Stage Default Probability Calibration Signal Common Miscalibration
MQL 5% % of MQLs that eventually close Often 0% in practice — remove from pipeline reports
SQL 10% % of SQLs that become opportunities that close Often set too high by optimistic reps
Stage 1 — Discovery 15-20% Historical Stage 1 close rate Commonly 8-12% in practice for many segments
Stage 2 — Evaluation 30-40% Historical Stage 2+ close rate Deals that stall in Stage 2 for 60+ days need discount
Stage 3 — Proposal 50-60% Historical Stage 3+ close rate If EB not engaged, 50% is likely overestimated
Stage 4 — Negotiation 75-85% Historical Stage 4+ close rate Legal/procurement delays can push deals to next quarter
Closed Won 100% N/A — actuals N/A

The Funnel Audit — Identifying and Fixing Leakage

A funnel audit examines conversion rates at every stage to find where pipeline is being lost at disproportionate rates. The audit framework: Volume × Conversion = Output. For every stage, ask: is the volume entering this stage sufficient? Is the conversion rate at or above benchmark? If either fails, identify root cause and intervene.

Funnel Audit Framework
Required Input Volume at Stage N = Required Output / Conversion Rate at Stage N Gap Analysis: Actual Output vs. Required Output at each stage Root Cause by Stage: Low MQL volume → Demand gen investment or ICP targeting problem Low MQL→SAL rate → Lead scoring too loose (quality problem from marketing) Low SAL→SQL rate → SDR call quality, wrong buyer, bad qualification framework Low Stage 1→2 rate → Discovery quality, product-market fit signal, champion identification Low Stage 2→3 rate → POC quality, competitive positioning, EB engagement Low Stage 3→4 rate → Business case, pricing, procurement navigation Low Stage 4→Close rate → Legal process, deal stall, last-mile commitment issues

Lead Lifecycle Model — Age-Out Rules & Re-Engagement

Leads that sit at a stage beyond a defined maximum age should either be disqualified or recycled into nurture programs. Without age-out rules, pipeline reports become cluttered with zombie opportunities that distort forecasting and waste rep time.

Stage Maximum Age (SMB/MM) Maximum Age (Enterprise) Age-Out Action
MQL (unworked) 48 hours 48 hours Auto-escalation to SDR manager if not accepted/rejected
SAL (uncontacted) 5 business days 5 business days Disqualify or auto-reassign to available SDR
Stage 1 — Discovery 30 days 60 days Manager review required; recycle to nurture if no progress
Stage 2 — Evaluation 45 days 90 days Manager pipeline review; require written next steps or downgrade
Stage 3 — Proposal 30 days 60 days Executive escalation if EB not responsive; consider no-decision
Stage 4 — Negotiation 30 days 45 days Legal escalation; executive-to-executive outreach; close or lose
Re-Engagement Programs for Aged Leads

Leads recycled from the funnel — particularly SQLs that went cold and MQLs that were disqualified — are not dead. They represent a warm, partially-educated audience. Recycle them into a structured nurture program: 6-8 email touches over 60-90 days covering use cases, customer stories, product updates, and value content. Automate triggers based on re-engagement signals (email open, website visit, content download). When a recycled lead re-engages meaningfully, route back to an SDR for re-qualification — not back to the original rep if the deal went cold due to relationship issues.

Funnel Velocity — Measuring and Improving Deal Speed

Pipeline velocity measures how quickly revenue moves through the funnel. Slower velocity means longer sales cycles, higher rep costs per deal, and reduced forecasting accuracy. The Pipeline Velocity formula combines four key levers into a single metric.

Pipeline Velocity Formula
Pipeline Velocity = (# Opportunities × Win Rate × Average ACV) / Average Sales Cycle Length (days) Example: 50 opps × 20% win rate × $50K ACV / 90 days = $5,556/day in pipeline velocity Improving velocity: ↑ # Opportunities: demand gen, SDR productivity ↑ Win Rate: better qualification, champion development, competitive response ↑ ACV: upsell motion, multi-year deals, better ICP targeting ↓ Sales Cycle: better discovery, mutual success plans, executive alignment
Days-in-Stage Benchmarks
Stage 1 — Discovery
SMB: 7-14 days | MM: 14-30 days | Enterprise: 30-60 days
If exceeding: discovery call quality issue or unclear next steps. Require mutual action plan at Stage 1 exit.
Stage 2 — Evaluation
SMB: 14-21 days | MM: 21-45 days | Enterprise: 45-90 days
POC/trial durations drive this. Time-box all evaluations. Open-ended POCs are sales cycle killers.
Stage 3 — Proposal
SMB: 7-14 days | MM: 14-30 days | Enterprise: 30-45 days
Slow EB response is the primary cause of Stage 3 stall. Champion must be your internal advocate pushing for EB engagement.
Stage 4 — Negotiation
SMB: 3-7 days | MM: 14-30 days | Enterprise: 30-60 days
Legal review is the primary driver. Know procurement timelines early. Surface legal requirements in Stage 2, not Stage 4.

The Bowtie Model — Revenue Beyond the Close

The Bowtie Replaces the Linear Funnel in SaaS

The traditional linear funnel ends at Closed Won — but in SaaS, the close is the beginning of the revenue relationship, not the end. The Bowtie Model (popularized by David Boyle and widely adopted in CS-led SaaS companies) extends the funnel through the entire customer lifecycle. The left side of the bowtie is the traditional acquisition funnel (awareness → MQL → SQL → opportunity → close). The right side is the expansion funnel: onboarding → adoption → value realization → renewal → expansion → advocacy.

In SaaS companies with NRR above 120%, the right side of the bowtie generates more revenue than the left side. This means that a CRO's job isn't just to close new business — it's to ensure the entire revenue system, including post-sale, is generating compounding growth. Tracking the right side of the bowtie requires the same rigor as the left: defined stages with entry/exit criteria, conversion rates, and velocity metrics.

Bowtie Right-Side Stages: Onboarding (time-to-value tracked), Adoption (DAU/MAU, feature breadth), Value Realization (first ROI moment documented), At-Risk / Health Score (see Health Scoring page), Renewal (renewal pipeline managed as a separate funnel), Expansion (upsell/cross-sell opportunity pipeline), Advocacy (reference program, G2 reviews, referrals). Each stage has a responsible owner (typically CS or a CSM/AE hybrid) and defined metrics.

Funnel Governance — Ownership, Audits & Cadences

A funnel definition document that lives in a Google Doc and is never enforced is worse than useless — it creates false confidence. Funnel governance means active, operational ownership of the funnel definitions, CRM enforcement of stage criteria, and regular audits of funnel health.

Owner
RevOps

Maintains the funnel definition document, enforces CRM field requirements at stage transitions, builds and maintains funnel analytics dashboards, runs monthly funnel audit, identifies conversion rate trends.

Owner
Marketing

Owns MQL definition and quality. Responsible for MQL-to-SAL rate above threshold. Reports on lead source quality by MQL-to-SQL conversion. Updates lead scoring model quarterly.

Owner
SDR Manager

Owns SAL-to-SQL conversion rate and MQL SLA compliance. Responsible for qualification standards and documentation. Reviews all recycled/disqualified leads for trend patterns.

Owner
Sales Management

Owns Stage 1-4 conversion rates and deal velocity. Reviews pipeline health weekly. Enforces stage criteria in pipeline reviews — no "hoped for" stage advancement without evidence-based exit criteria met.

Funnel Review Cadence
  • Weekly: Pipeline review with all AEs — every Stage 2+ opportunity reviewed for next steps, blockers, and forecast category. Flag any opportunity exceeding age-out thresholds.
  • Monthly: Full funnel health audit — conversion rates vs. prior month vs. benchmark, volume at each stage vs. plan, CPL by channel, MQL-to-SAL trends. Identify root causes for any metric below threshold.
  • Quarterly: Funnel definition review — are stage criteria still accurate for the current motion? Update conversion benchmarks based on trailing quarter actuals. Recalibrate stage probabilities. Review lost reason analysis and update competitive battle cards.
  • Annually: Full funnel architecture review — as the company scales, the funnel motion may need to evolve (new segments, new geographies, new sales motions like PLG or channel). Revisit all definitions against current go-to-market strategy.
