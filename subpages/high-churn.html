PHASE 6 — TROUBLESHOOTING
Diagnosing & Fixing High Churn & Low NRR
Churn is almost always caused upstream — by selling to the wrong customers, overpromising during the sale, or under-investing in CS. The fix starts before the customer signs.

Understanding the Churn Taxonomy
Before diagnosing churn, you must classify it correctly. Different types of churn have different root causes and require completely different fixes. Treating all churn as a customer success problem when 60% is an ICP mismatch problem will waste resources and produce no improvement.
Logo Churn vs. Revenue Churn

Logo Churn (Customer Churn Rate): the percentage of customers who cancel in a period. Formula: customers churned ÷ customers at start of period. Benchmark: world-class <5% annually for SMB, <3% for mid-market, <2% for enterprise. Logo churn is the headline number but not the most important one for SaaS economics.

Revenue Churn / NRR: Net Revenue Retention measures what happens to revenue from your existing customer base. NRR above 100% means your existing customers are growing with you through expansion — logo churn is offset by upsell. NRR below 100% means your business is shrinking even without considering new customer acquisition costs. This is the most important single number in SaaS retention economics.

Net Revenue Retention (NRR)
NRR = (Starting MRR + Expansion MRR − Churn MRR − Contraction MRR) ÷ Starting MRR × 100
World-class: 130%+ | Great: 120–130% | Good: 110–120% | Acceptable: 100–110% | Problem: below 100%
Voluntary vs. Involuntary Churn

Voluntary churn: the customer actively decided to cancel. This requires diagnosing why they left. Involuntary churn: the customer wanted to stay but couldn't — failed payments, credit card expiration, procurement contract lapse. For SMB SaaS, involuntary churn can account for 20–40% of total logo churn and is fixed with dunning automation (Churnkey, Stunning, Chargebee), not CS programs.

⚠ Churn Is a Lagging Indicator — By 6–18 Months

The churn you see today was caused by decisions made 6–18 months ago: who you sold to, what you promised during the sale, how onboarding was designed, and how CS was staffed and incentivized. This means churn improvement programs take time to show results. If your CRO or board expects churn to drop in 90 days, recalibrate expectations. Structural churn improvements typically take 2–3 quarters to appear in the data.

Root Cause 1: ICP Mismatch — Sold to Customers Who Were Never Going to Succeed
The most common and most damaging cause of high churn is an ICP problem: the sales team sold to customers who were not actually a good fit for the product. These customers may have matched the demographic ICP (right industry, right size) but failed on the psychographic or use-case fit — their workflow, maturity level, or internal resources were incompatible with achieving value from the product.
How to Detect ICP Mismatch in Churn Data

Run a cohort analysis comparing customers by ICP tier (Tier 1 = strong fit, Tier 2 = marginal fit, Tier 3 = out of ICP) and calculate 12-month NRR by tier. If Tier 1 customers have 120%+ NRR and Tier 3 customers have 70% NRR, the churn problem is concentrated in non-ICP customers. Further: compare the ICP score of recently churned customers vs. customers in their 3rd year of renewal. High-ICP customers renew; low-ICP customers churn.

Also look at win-to-churn velocity: if a customer acquired by a specific rep or through a specific channel churns within 12 months at 3x the rate of other customers, that rep is selling outside ICP or over-promising. Segment churn by sales rep, source channel, and deal size to isolate the pattern.

What to Do — Tightening Qualification Gates
  • Build a "churn risk score" into the sales qualification process: prospects who match the profile of churned customers require additional qualification before proceeding to Stage 3
  • Share churn cohort data with sales leadership quarterly — remove the abstraction between "we sold this" and "they churned"
  • Implement a CS pre-close review for deals above $50K ACV: CSM must review the deal and flag ICP/success risk before the contract is signed
  • Stop selling to segments or company types with structural churn above 25% annually even if it hurts short-term revenue — every out-of-ICP customer costs more in CS resources and contract value than they generate
Root Cause 2: Broken Onboarding and Low Adoption
Even the right customer will churn if they never achieve value. The period from contract signature to first value realization — Time to Value (TTV) — is the highest-risk window in the customer lifecycle. Customers who do not reach their "first aha moment" within the first 60–90 days are 3–5x more likely to churn than customers who do.

Onboarding Fix Playbook
  1. Define the "First Value Moment" with Precision Identify the single action or outcome that correlates most strongly with long-term retention for your product. This is your activation event. Every onboarding program must be designed to get the customer to that moment as fast as possible. Examples: Slack — send first message; Salesforce — first deal logged; Intercom — first message sent to customer.
  2. Rebuild Onboarding Around Outcomes, Not Features Feature-led onboarding ("here is how to use tab X") fails because customers don't care about features — they care about outcomes. Redesign onboarding around "here is how to achieve [specific business result]" with features introduced only as they contribute to that outcome.
  3. Assign a Dedicated Onboarding Owner for Strategic Accounts For accounts above $50K ACV, a dedicated implementation specialist — not the CSM — should own onboarding for the first 60 days. The CSM relationship begins after value has been delivered, not during implementation.
  4. Create an Escalation Trigger for Stalled Onboardings If a customer has not completed their first value milestone within 30 days of their target date, trigger an automatic escalation: CSM calls the sponsor, identifies the blocker, and creates a revised action plan. Stalled onboarding is the #1 leading indicator of 180-day churn.
Root Cause 3: Competitive Displacement
When customers leave because a competitor offered something materially better — functionality, pricing, integrations, support — the fix requires product roadmap alignment, not just CS program improvements. CS cannot retain customers against a genuinely superior product without executive and product team involvement.
How to Detect Competitive Displacement as the Primary Churn Driver

Implement a structured exit interview for every churned account above $20K ACV. The interview should be conducted by a CS leader or CRO — not the CSM who managed the account (who has a conflict of interest). Ask: "What was the primary reason for canceling?" and "Which product or solution are you moving to?" Track churn reasons in the CRM with a structured dropdown (product gap, pricing, competitive, support quality, company changes, ROI not realized) and analyze the distribution quarterly.

If "moved to competitor" accounts for more than 30% of churn reasons by revenue, you have a competitive retention problem. Build a competitive churn analysis: which competitor is winning your accounts, in which segments, and what specific capability gap are they exploiting?

Competitive Retention Plays
  • Executive escalation: For any account above $50K signaling competitive intent, the CRO or CEO calls the customer's executive within 48 hours — not to sell, but to understand and commit to resolution
  • Product roadmap alignment: If the competitor is winning on a specific feature, accelerate that feature on the roadmap and communicate the timeline to the at-risk account as a formal commitment
  • Retention pricing: Offer multi-year pricing with a price lock as a competitive retention tool — buyers who fear switching costs are more resistant to competitive displacement
  • Create switching costs: Deep integrations, data gravity, custom configurations, and trained internal users all increase the switching cost and reduce competitive vulnerability
The Churn Prevention Playbook: What Sales Can Do Before the Deal Closes
The highest-leverage churn prevention happens before the contract is signed, not after. Sales has the most information about the customer's expectations, motivations, and fit — and the opportunity to set realistic expectations that protect long-term retention.
Pre-Close Churn Prevention Actions
  • Conduct a "success criteria" conversation in discovery: Ask the champion "What does success look like for you in 90 days? What would you need to see to renew?" Document the answers and include them in the handoff to CS — this creates accountability and surfaces unrealistic expectations before they become churn drivers
  • Avoid feature promises not on the roadmap: The #2 cause of early churn is a promise made during the sale that the product cannot deliver. Implement a "sales promise tracker" — reps must log any capability commitments; CS reviews these at onboarding kickoff
  • Qualify internal adoption resources: Ask "Who on your team will own the day-to-day usage of this platform? Do they have bandwidth in the next 60 days for implementation?" If the answer is unclear, flag it as a churn risk
  • Do a CS handoff meeting before contract signing: For accounts above $30K ACV, the CSM joins the final sales meeting to begin the relationship and review what success looks like
The Health Score Early Warning System
A health score is a composite risk indicator calculated from product usage, support ticket volume, NPS/CSAT, engagement with CS, contract details, and financial signals. When built correctly, it predicts churn 60–120 days before it occurs — giving CS enough time to intervene.

The Save Play for Red Accounts
  1. Acknowledge the Problem Directly (Day 1) The CSM calls the primary contact and says: "Our data shows [specific usage metric] has declined significantly. I want to understand what's happening and make sure we're delivering the value you expected." Do not send a check-in email — call.
  2. Conduct a Success Audit (Days 2–5) Review the original success criteria from onboarding. What was the customer trying to achieve? Have they achieved it? If not, why not? Identify the specific gap — is it adoption, product, process, or support?
  3. Build a Mutual Success Plan (Week 2) Co-create a 60-day success plan with the customer: specific milestones, owner on both sides, success metrics. Get sign-off from a senior stakeholder, not just the end user. This is the retention MAP equivalent.
  4. Escalate if Stalled (Week 3) If the customer is non-responsive or the success plan reveals a product gap you cannot address, escalate to executive sponsorship — CRO or CEO call with the customer's executive. Executive-to-executive conversations save accounts that CSM-to-user conversations cannot.
  5. Multi-Year as Retention Tool (Week 4) For accounts where trust can be rebuilt, offer a multi-year renewal with a discounted rate and a committed roadmap item. Multi-year deals reduce annual churn risk by 60%+ because the switching decision is deferred while you have time to deliver additional value.
