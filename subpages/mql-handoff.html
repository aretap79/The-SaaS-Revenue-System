Why This Process Breaks More Often Than Any Other

The MQL handoff — the moment a marketing-generated lead transitions to sales follow-up — is the highest-friction point in the B2B revenue engine. Research by Forrester consistently shows that sales reps follow up on fewer than 25% of marketing-qualified leads within 24 hours, and marketing-generated leads convert to opportunities at rates 3-5x below their potential when the handoff process is broken.

The breakage occurs for predictable reasons: marketing and sales have different definitions of "qualified," the SLA is informal or nonexistent, speed to follow-up is not measured, and the feedback loop on lead quality is absent. The result is a political fight where marketing claims "sales ignores our leads" and sales claims "marketing sends us junk."

Definition — MQL (Marketing Qualified Lead)

A contact that has been identified by the marketing team — through a scoring model combining demographic and behavioral signals — as meeting a minimum threshold of ICP fit and engagement, and is therefore ready for initial sales contact. An MQL is not a buyer — it is a signal that warrants a human conversation. The qualification of whether they are actually qualified for a deal happens in that conversation.

MQL Definition Methodology

An MQL threshold is the minimum lead score required for a lead to be handed to sales. Setting this threshold is a balancing act: too low and sales drowns in poor-quality leads and stops trusting marketing; too high and you waste good leads by delaying follow-up until they've gone cold.

The threshold is typically set by calibrating for an MQL-to-SAL conversion rate of 50-70%. If fewer than 50% of MQLs are being accepted by sales as Sales Accepted Leads (SALs), your MQL definition is too loose. If acceptance is above 90%, you're leaving good leads on the table by setting the bar too high.

Lead Scoring Model Design

Lead scoring is the mechanism that determines whether a contact crosses the MQL threshold. The model combines two dimensions: (1) demographic fit (is this person at the right type of company in the right role?) and (2) behavioral engagement (have they shown intent through their actions?). Both must be present — a perfectly-fit persona who never engages is not an MQL; an engaged person at a tiny company that can't afford you is not an MQL either.

The SAL Concept

SAL — Sales Accepted Lead

A Sales Accepted Lead (SAL) is an MQL that the sales team has reviewed and accepted as meeting their definition of a qualified prospect worth pursuing. The SAL is the contractual moment where sales accepts responsibility for the lead's outcome.

Why SAL matters: without it, sales can ignore MQLs without accountability, or accept all MQLs without reviewing them (acceptance theater). The SAL stage forces a genuine review and creates a clear metric — MQL-to-SAL conversion rate — that holds both marketing (MQL quality) and sales (follow-up discipline) accountable.

SAL criteria should be explicit: "We accept this as a SAL if the contact is at a company with 50+ employees, in a qualifying industry, and the contact has a title that includes buying influence." The criteria should be documented in the SLA — not left to individual rep judgment.

The Bilateral SLA

Marketing Commits
What Marketing Delivers
  • MQLs that meet the agreed scoring threshold and demographic fit criteria
  • Complete contact records: name, email, company, title, phone if available
  • Full engagement history: which pages visited, what content downloaded, what events attended
  • Lead source and UTM data for attribution
  • Monthly MQL volume target by segment (SMB/MM/Enterprise)
  • MQL-to-SAL conversion rate ≥ 60% on a trailing 30-day basis
  • Notification of any campaign that generates a spike in MQL volume so SDR capacity can be planned
Sales Commits
What Sales Delivers
  • First attempt to contact within 5 minutes for demo requests; within 4 business hours for all other MQLs
  • SAL/reject decision made within 24 business hours of MQL assignment
  • Rejection requires a reason code (not qualified, wrong contact, competitor, wrong timing)
  • 3 attempts minimum over 7 business days before marking as "no response" and returning to marketing nurture
  • All call/email activity logged in CRM — visibility into follow-up effort is non-negotiable
  • Monthly feedback to marketing on MQL quality patterns — which lead sources produce the best SALs

The Speed-to-Lead Imperative

Harvard Business Review Research — Lead Response
9x
higher conversion rate when contacted within 5 minutes vs. after 1 hour (Harvard Business Review / InsideSales.com)
If you wait 30 minutes to respond to a demo request, conversion drops by over 100x compared to a 5-minute response. The buyer has already moved on — to a competitor, to a different research thread, or simply to the next item in their inbox. The moment of peak intent is also the moment of maximum responsiveness.

Speed-to-lead is the single highest-ROI improvement most sales organizations can make to their MQL process. It requires: (1) real-time CRM notifications when an MQL is assigned, (2) SDR schedules aligned with MQL inbound patterns (if 60% of demo requests come between 9-11 AM Tuesday-Thursday, SDR team must be fully staffed then), and (3) management accountability — first contact time is a measured and reviewed metric in every 1:1.

CRM Routing Configuration

Poor CRM routing is one of the most common causes of slow follow-up. Leads that sit in a queue, are mis-assigned to a rep in the wrong territory, or are not visible to the right SDR manager are wasted leads regardless of their quality. Best-practice routing includes:

  • Round-robin assignment within territory: MQLs in a defined geography or segment route to the available SDR in that segment on a round-robin basis, with automatic skip for SDRs at capacity
  • Auto-assignment speed: Lead should be assigned within 60 seconds of form submission — not batched hourly
  • Named account override: If the MQL's company is a named account already in an AE's territory, the lead goes directly to that AE (not SDR), flagged as an engaged contact in a strategic account
  • Out-of-hours handling: Define who owns MQLs that come in at 11 PM on a Friday — auto-responder? First available next business day? On-call SDR? This must be decided and encoded in the routing rules
  • Duplicate detection: If the contact or company already exists in the CRM as an open opportunity, merge the engagement data — don't create a duplicate MQL that confuses the rep

Rejected Lead Feedback Loop

Lead rejection is valuable data — if you capture it. Every rejection must include a reason code. The most common codes: Not ICP Fit, Wrong Contact, Already Customer, Too Early (not actively evaluating), No Budget, Competitor Deal, Bad Data. Reviewing rejection patterns monthly reveals systematic problems with lead quality that can be corrected before they waste more SDR time.

If "Not ICP Fit" is running at 40%+ of rejections, your MQL definition needs tightening — either the demographic score thresholds need adjustment or a specific campaign is attracting the wrong audience. If "Too Early" is high, you may be triggering MQLs on early-stage research behavior that needs more nurture before sales contact.

Failure Mode Taxonomy

Failure Mode 1
Marketing and Sales define "qualified" differently
Symptom
High MQL volume, low SAL acceptance rate. Sales says "these leads are garbage." Marketing says "sales doesn't work leads." MQL-to-SAL rate below 40%.
Fix
Run a joint "lead definition workshop" with marketing + SDR manager + top 2 AEs. Start from 20 won deals and 20 lost deals. Define the scoring model from actual data, not assumptions. Document and enforce in CRM.
Failure Mode 2
Slow speed-to-lead
Symptom
Average first contact >24 hours. Demo request-to-meeting conversion below 20%. Prospects tell reps "I already booked with your competitor."
Fix
Add real-time Slack notification to CRM assignment. Measure and display first-contact time on SDR leaderboard. Introduce Chili Piper or Calendly for instant booking directly on demo request form — eliminate the phone tag entirely.
Failure Mode 3
No feedback loop on lead quality
Symptom
Marketing continues spending budget on campaigns generating low-quality MQLs because no one tells them which lead sources have 40% rejection rates. SDRs stop working marketing leads because they've learned they're low quality — but marketing doesn't know.
Fix
Monthly marketing/SDR review meeting: 30 minutes reviewing rejection codes by campaign, lead source, and content asset. This is not a blame meeting — it is a calibration meeting. Make it happen consistently.
Failure Mode 4
MQL routing failures in CRM
Symptom
Leads assigned to wrong reps, to reps on PTO, or sitting unassigned in a queue for days. MQLs showing "no first contact" in the CRM despite the rep claiming they followed up.
Fix
Audit CRM routing rules quarterly. Build a RevOps dashboard showing unassigned MQLs and MQLs with first contact >24 hours. Create a Slack alert for MQLs uncontacted after 4 hours. Assign a backup owner (SDR manager) for all unassigned MQLs.
Failure Mode 5
SDR cherry-picks only the easiest MQLs
Symptom
SDRs work demo requests immediately but ignore content downloads and webinar MQLs that require more effort to convert. Follow-up attempt rate on non-demo-request MQLs below 50%.
Fix
Tie SDR quota attainment to total SAL creation (not just demo-request conversion). Require documented follow-up attempts as a mandatory field before an MQL can be moved to "no response." Review attempt rates in weekly 1:1s.
