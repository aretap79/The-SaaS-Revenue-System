What Revenue Capacity Modeling Is

Revenue capacity modeling is the process of projecting future revenue output based on the human and process inputs of your go-to-market engine — before that revenue is earned. It answers the question: "Given our current team size, ramp schedule, attrition rate, and average productivity, how much revenue can we realistically generate next quarter and next year?"

Most companies forecast revenue from the top down — start with a target and work backward to see if it's achievable. Capacity modeling works from the bottom up — start with the actual human assets in the GTM engine and model what they will produce. The bottoms-up model is more accurate and more operationally actionable because it connects revenue forecasts to specific hiring and enablement decisions.

The Bottoms-Up Capacity Formula

Expected Revenue = Σ across all reps: (Quota × Ramp Adjustment Factor × Attrition Adjustment Factor)

The 7 Model Inputs

Revenue Capacity Model — 7 Required Inputs
1
Fully-Ramp Rep Count
FTE × % Ramped (Month ≥ Ramp Threshold)
Count only reps who are past their ramp period. Ramping reps are tracked separately with their expected partial productivity. Reps who joined 2 months ago on a 5-month ramp are 40% productive — not 100%.
2
Average Rep Quota
ACV × Deals/Quarter Target
Full quota for the current period. If quotas vary by segment (SMB AEs have lower quota than Enterprise AEs), model each segment separately. Use attainable quota — not aspirational quota set 25% above market.
3
Expected Team Attainment Rate
Historical attainment × trend adjustment
What percentage of quota will the average rep achieve? Use trailing 4-quarter average — not best-quarter cherry-picking. If team historically attains 78%, model at 78%, not 100%. This is the most important "realism check" input.
4
Ramp Curve by Month
Productivity%(Month N) × Quota
For every rep hired in the period, apply the segment-specific ramp curve. A rep hired in Month 1 of Q1 will contribute ~20% of quota in Q1, ~60% in Q2, ~90% in Q3, and full quota in Q4. This creates the "ramp lag" that surprises most companies in their annual plans.
5
Attrition Rate
Annual Attrition % / 4 (per quarter)
Involuntary (PIP exits) + voluntary (resigned) attrition, annualized. Industry average is 25-35% for AEs. Attrition removes reps from your capacity calculation — and replacing them takes hiring time + ramp time before capacity is restored. Model 20-30% per year as a baseline.
6
Time-to-Hire
Days from job req open to Day 1
Average time to fill an open position. This determines how far in advance you must open a requisition to have a rep productive by a target date. Typical ranges: SDR 4-6 weeks; MM AE 6-10 weeks; Enterprise AE 8-14 weeks. Longer in competitive markets.
7
Planned Hires
Req Plan × Expected Fill Rate × Start Month
The specific hiring plan: how many reps you plan to add, in which segment, and in which month. Combined with ramp curves, this determines when those reps become productive contributors. Planned hires must be multiplied by an expected fill rate (typically 80-85% — not all reqs close on time).

The Hockey Stick Problem

Why Revenue Growth Curves Feel Like Hockey Sticks

The hockey stick — where revenue grows slowly for months then suddenly jumps — is not a natural market phenomenon. It is an artifact of hiring decisions made too late. Companies that wait until Q2 or Q3 to validate whether they need more headcount, then hire aggressively in Q3, will see the revenue from those hires only in Q1-Q2 of the next year (because of ramp lag).

Meanwhile, they relied on existing reps for Q2-Q3 results — leading to quarter-end crunches, over-discounting, and burnout. Then new reps arrive and pipeline starts building — creating the sudden growth jump in the following year that appears to be market validation but is actually just delayed headcount becoming productive.

The prevention: Run the capacity model 4-5 quarters forward at the beginning of every annual planning cycle. Identify when each planned hire cohort will be productive. Open reqs exactly (time-to-hire + ramp time) months before you need the revenue. This converts the hockey stick into a steady growth curve.

Capacity Model Summary Formula
Revenue Capacity = Σ(Rep Quota × Ramp% × Team Attainment%) − Attrition Impact + Expansion (from existing customers)
Run quarterly. Compare to board-committed revenue target. If gap exists, identify specific hiring, ramp, or expansion actions to close it — and model when those actions produce revenue given lead times.
Leading: Open reqs, time-to-fill, 30/60/90 onboarding completion rate, ramp efficiency at month 3 Lagging: Revenue per AE, team attainment %, annual attrition rate, capacity gap vs. target Capacity = Headcount × Quota × Attainment × Ramp Adj × (1 − Attrition)
