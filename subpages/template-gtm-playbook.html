What Is a GTM Playbook?

A GTM Playbook is the documented, iterated system that converts your founders' intuitive selling knowledge into a process that any trained rep can execute with consistent results.

The GTM Playbook is not a slide deck or a set of talking points. It is the comprehensive documentation of every step in your customer acquisition process — from how you define your ICP to how you transition a newly closed customer to CS. Every element of the playbook should be based on evidence: what has worked in actual customer conversations, validated by win/loss data, and refined through film review sessions where the team reviews real calls together.

The playbook exists to solve three problems simultaneously. First, it reduces rep ramp time by giving new hires a documented system rather than requiring them to learn everything through trial and error. Second, it makes your sales process inspectable — managers can see exactly which step of the process is breaking down when a rep underperforms. Third, it creates a foundation for iteration — when market conditions change, you update the playbook rather than hoping reps figure it out individually.

The GTM Playbook is never finished. It should have a version number and a governance process that specifies who can propose changes, how changes are validated, and how updated versions are distributed to the team. A playbook that isn't being updated is a playbook that is slowly becoming obsolete.

The Founder's Most Important Contribution

The most valuable version of the GTM Playbook is written by the founder before hiring the first sales leader. It captures the intuitive, pattern-matched knowledge the founder developed by closing the first 10–20 customers directly. This institutional knowledge is irreplaceable — it must be documented before it dissipates as the company scales and the founder moves further from day-to-day selling.

The 5-Component Structure

Every GTM Playbook should contain exactly these five components. They map to the five stages of a B2B sales motion: who you sell to, how you find them, how you qualify them, how you convince them, and how you ensure they succeed.

1
ICP Definition & Qualification
Define exactly who to target, what firmographic and behavioral signals qualify them, and what disqualifies them immediately
See also: template-icp.html
Firmographic Profile (Who They Are)
Industry / Vertical
List the 2–3 industries or verticals where you have the highest win rate and customer retention. Be specific — not "technology companies" but "Series A–C SaaS companies building B2B products."
Example: "Manufacturing companies with 50–500 employees that operate at least 2 production facilities in North America."
Company Size (Revenue or Headcount)
Define the range based on your top 10 customers. What size company is big enough to have the problem you solve but small enough that your price point is material to them?
Example: "200–2,000 employees. Below 200: problem isn't painful enough. Above 2,000: long sales cycles and enterprise procurement friction."
Buyer Title / Persona
Who signs the check? Who uses the product day-to-day? Who can block the deal? Define each persona by title, responsibilities, primary concern, and how to find them on LinkedIn.
Example: "Economic Buyer: VP Operations or COO. Champion: Director of Ops or Plant Manager. Blocker: IT Director (security/integration concerns)."
Technology Stack Signals
What tools in a company's stack indicate they are likely to buy? What tools indicate a competitor is in place?
Example: "Using Salesforce = likely to budget for adjacent tools. Using [Competitor] = displacement conversation needed. No CRM = may be too early-stage."
Behavioral / Situational Qualifiers (What They're Going Through)
Trigger Events — Buy Now Signals
What organizational events increase the likelihood they will buy in the next 90 days? List 4–6 specific trigger events. These become the basis of your prospecting sequence targeting logic.
Examples: "Just hired a new VP Operations (change agent, new budget)." / "Recently raised Series B or C funding." / "Job postings for ops or finance roles (scaling and feeling pain)." / "Recent mention of 'operational inefficiency' in press release or earnings call."
Problem Conditions — Must Be True
What business conditions must be present for your solution to be valuable? These are discovery questions, not assumptions — but they define what you're listening for in discovery.
Examples: "Manual processes that consume >3 hours/week of a skilled employee's time." / "More than one system of record for the same data type." / "Unable to answer 'how is the business performing?' without running multiple reports."
Disqualification Criteria (Who to Pass On)
Hard Disqualifiers — Exit the Conversation
What characteristics, conditions, or answers should cause you to stop pursuing a prospect immediately? These protect rep time and prevent the wrong customers from entering the funnel and consuming post-sale CS resources.
Examples: "Evaluating for a subsidiary only (no authority to expand across the org)." / "Budget cycle doesn't reset for >9 months." / "Currently in the middle of a platform migration — no bandwidth." / "Executive champion has just resigned."
2
Prospecting Methodology
How reps find ICP buyers, build lists, and execute outreach sequences that generate responses
Outbound + Inbound
List Building Process
Target Account Sources
Where do reps source target accounts? List each source, how to use it, and the minimum qualification filter to apply before adding an account to the outreach queue.
Sources: "LinkedIn Sales Navigator (filters: industry, headcount, seniority, title). Trigger event tools (Bombora, ZoomInfo intent signals). CRM import of company lookalikes from closed-won accounts. Inbound lead overflow from SDR qualification."
Account Research Checklist
Before any outreach, reps must complete this research. What are the 5–7 things a rep must know about a target account before reaching out?
1. Company size and recent funding. 2. Relevant trigger events in last 90 days. 3. Current tech stack (from LinkedIn jobs or G2 reviews). 4. Buyer persona titles confirmed. 5. Competitor presence (if any). 6. One specific business pain hypothesis based on research.
Persona Identification Method
How does the rep identify the correct buyer personas at a target account? Which title do they reach first — the champion or the economic buyer?
"Start with the Champion (Director-level). Use LinkedIn to confirm the title exists. Identify the economic buyer through the org chart. Identify the potential blocker in IT by checking for an IT Director with 'security' in their profile."
Outreach Sequence
Sequence Structure (Days & Touches)
Define the standard prospecting sequence for cold outreach. Include channel (email, phone, LinkedIn), timing, and the specific message intent at each touch. Document the exact email and call scripts for touches 1, 3, and 5 — the highest-leverage points.
Day 1: Personalized email referencing trigger event. Day 3: LinkedIn connection + note. Day 6: Follow-up email with one-sentence case study. Day 10: Phone + voicemail (if no response). Day 14: Break-up email with specific re-engagement offer. Day 21: Final LinkedIn message. Archive if no response.
Email Subject Line Principles
What makes your best-performing subject lines work? Document 5–8 tested subject lines with their open rate (if tracked), and extract the common principles.
Principle: Reference a specific, observable pain point rather than your product. "How [Similar Co] reduced report time by 60%" outperforms "Introduction to [Your Product]" by 2.5× open rate.
Call Talk Track (Opening + Bridge)
The first 30 seconds of a cold call determine whether you get a conversation or a hang-up. Document the exact talk track that your best reps use.
"Hi [Name], this is [Rep] from [Company]. I'll be direct — I'm calling because I noticed [specific trigger event]. We help [role] at companies your size [outcome without jargon]. Do you have 90 seconds so I can tell you why I called?"
3
Discovery Framework
The structured approach to uncovering pain, urgency, decision process, and budget before presenting anything
Most important component
Discovery Call Structure
Opening Frame (First 3 Minutes)
How does the rep open a discovery call? What is the agenda-setting script that establishes the rep as a peer, sets expectations for the call, and gets the buyer's permission to ask tough questions?
"Thanks for making time. I'd like to spend the first 20 minutes understanding your situation — your goals, what's working, and where things are frustrating. If it sounds like we can help, I'll share what that typically looks like for companies your size. If not, I'll tell you that directly. Does that work for you?"
Core Discovery Question Bank

Document the 15–20 discovery questions that have generated the most insight across your best calls. Organize by category. These become the foundation of rep training and film review.

Current State Questions (Where Are They Now?)
Questions that reveal the buyer's current processes, systems, and workarounds. What are they doing today to solve the problem you address?
"Walk me through how your team handles [process] today — from start to finish." / "How many people are involved in [process], and how long does it take?" / "What tools are you using to manage this?"
Pain Quantification Questions (How Bad Is It?)
Questions that convert vague frustration into specific, measurable business impact. Pain that can be quantified is pain that can be solved with a budget.
"If you had to estimate the hours per week your team spends on this, what would you guess?" / "What does that cost you in terms of [salary / opportunity cost / error rate]?" / "Has this caused any specific business problems you can point to?"
Future State Questions (What Would Success Look Like?)
Questions that let the buyer describe their desired outcome — which becomes the basis of your value proposition in the presentation.
"If we were having this conversation 12 months from now and things had gone really well, what would be different?" / "What would need to be true for this to be considered a successful investment?"
Decision Process Questions (How Do They Buy?)
Questions that map the buying process, identify stakeholders, surface budget realities, and clarify the evaluation timeline before you invest time building a proposal.
"If you decided to move forward, what would the approval process look like?" / "Who else would need to be involved in evaluating something like this?" / "Do you have budget allocated for a project in this area, or would this require a new approval?"
Discovery Recap (Bridge to Presentation)
The Discovery Recap Template
Before showing anything, the rep must recap what they heard. This earns the right to present, confirms understanding, and ties the presentation directly to the buyer's specific situation.
"Before I show you anything, let me recap what I heard to make sure I've got it right. You're currently [current state] and that's costing you approximately [quantified pain]. If you could solve this, the outcome you'd need is [future state]. And the decision would involve [stakeholders] with a timeline of [timeframe]. Does that capture it?"
4
Presentation & Demo Framework
A pain-tied, discovery-informed demo and proposal structure that connects product capabilities to buyer outcomes
Always pain-first
Demo Structure Principles
The Anti-Pattern: Feature-First Demos

The most common demo mistake is showing features without first tying them to pain. Every feature shown in a demo should be preceded by: "Earlier you mentioned [specific pain]. Here's how we solve that." Features shown without a pain anchor are quickly forgotten.

Demo Flow (Pain → Feature → Evidence → Next Step)
Structure every demo as a series of pain-feature-evidence sequences. One sequence per major pain point discovered. Never show more than 3 features in a first demo.
Pain anchor: "You mentioned your team spends 3 hours every Monday on manual reporting." → Feature: "This is the automated report builder — here's how it works for your use case." → Evidence: "A company similar to yours reduced that time to 20 minutes within the first week." → Question: "Does that solve the problem you described, or is there a nuance I'm missing?"
Objection Handlers

Document the 8–10 most common objections encountered in the presentation stage and the proven responses. Build this from actual call recordings, not from the sales team's assumptions about what buyers say.

Objection What's Behind It Proven Response Escalation Path
"It's too expensive." Budget constraint or perceived value gap — not always the same problem Acknowledge first, then quantify: "Let me make sure I understand — is it that the budget isn't available, or that you're not sure the value is there? Because those are different conversations." Then tie back to the quantified pain from discovery. If genuinely budget-constrained: phased implementation, smaller initial scope. If value gap: schedule a value engineering session with your champion.
"We're already doing this with [internal solution]." Status quo bias or sunk cost fallacy on existing investment "Totally understand. A lot of our customers had something in place before they came to us. Can I ask — what does it cost you to maintain that, and does it actually solve [specific pain] the way you need it to?" Surface the total cost of the internal solution including maintenance, opportunity cost, and error rate. If the internal solution is genuinely good enough for them, disqualify gracefully. Don't win customers who shouldn't be customers.
"We need to involve IT." Security, integration, or procurement concern — almost always legitimate "Absolutely — that's the right call. Let me make this easy for you. Can we schedule a 30-minute call with your IT counterpart? I have a one-pager that covers our security architecture, data practices, and typical integration scope that usually addresses the main concerns up front." Reduce friction, don't avoid it. Prepare IT-specific materials (SOC 2, data residency, API documentation) before the meeting. Have your technical lead available if needed.
"Now isn't the right time." Competing priorities or lack of urgency Acknowledge and anchor to their stated pain: "I hear you. Can I ask — the problem you described earlier with [specific pain], is that still causing the impact you mentioned, or has something changed?" If pain is still real, reconnect to urgency. If circumstances changed, ask when to reconnect with a specific date. If genuinely not ready: set a follow-up with a specific date trigger ("call me after Q3 closes"). Put in nurture sequence. Do not hold in active pipeline with a theoretical close date.
5
CS Onboarding & LIR Achievement
The handoff process and first-90-day onboarding plan designed to drive every new customer to LIR achievement within the defined time window
LIR is the north star
Sales-to-CS Handoff Protocol
Handoff Document — Required Fields
Every closed deal must include a structured handoff document completed by the AE before the first CS call. This document is the foundation of the CS onboarding plan.
Required fields: (1) Why they bought — the specific pain and future state from discovery. (2) Champion and economic buyer names + roles. (3) Success criteria stated by the customer. (4) Any commitments made during the sales process. (5) Integration or implementation requirements. (6) Known risks or concerns (budget reviewer who wasn't sold, IT who is skeptical). (7) Recommended LIR milestone timeline based on company size and complexity.
First-90-Day LIR Achievement Plan
Milestone Day Target Owner Success Criteria Risk Signal (If Not Met)
Kickoff Call Day 1–3 CSM + AE Customer can articulate their success criteria. LIR event explained. First 30-day milestone agreed. No-show or disengaged champion → escalate to economic buyer within 5 days
Technical Setup Complete Day 7–14 CSM + Implementation Product connected to customer's environment. First data imported or first workflow configured. IT blocking → loop in AE immediately. Do not wait past Day 14 without escalation.
First Value Event Day 14–21 CSM Customer completes first meaningful action in product tied to their stated pain. CSM documents outcome. No product login or minimal usage → CSM calls the champion proactively. Not an email.
LIR Event Check-In Day 30 CSM Customer is on track to complete LIR event by T (defined time window). Rate ≥ 50% of the way to the LIR threshold. If behind: schedule emergency onboarding session. Identify and remove blockers. Escalate if needed.
LIR Achievement Day T (e.g., Day 45) CSM Customer has completed the LIR event as defined. CSM documents and records in CRM. LIR not achieved → this customer is at elevated churn risk. Activate intervention protocol immediately.
First QBR Day 90 CSM QBR connects product metrics to customer's stated business outcomes. Expansion opportunities identified. Customer declines QBR → relationship risk signal. Schedule alternative touchpoint at the executive level.
Film Review Program
Weekly Film Review — Format and Rules
The Film Review is the primary mechanism for improving the playbook. Document the format, the rules, and the process for incorporating learnings back into the playbook.
Format: 60-minute weekly session. Rotating rep submits a call recording (30 minutes, any stage). First 3 minutes: rep self-evaluates — what went well, what they'd change. Remaining 27 minutes: structured team review against playbook criteria. Rule: no one reviews their own manager's calls. Rule: all feedback is behavioral and specific, never personal. Learnings: anything that generates consensus improvement is proposed as a playbook edit within 48 hours and reviewed at the next week's session.

Playbook Governance Process

The playbook is a living document. These governance rules ensure it stays current, accurate, and team-owned rather than becoming an artifact that nobody reads.

Governance Element Owner Cadence Description
Version Control Sales Leader Every change Every published version gets a version number (e.g., v2.3) and a changelog entry. Reps must always use the current version. Previous versions archived but accessible.
Change Proposal Process Any team member Any time Any rep or CSM can propose a change using the standard change proposal template. Proposals must include: specific section to change, evidence for the change (call data, win/loss pattern), and proposed new text.
Change Review Sales Leader + 2 reps Weekly (at Film Review) Pending proposals reviewed at weekly Film Review. Accepted changes published within 48 hours. Rejected changes documented with rationale to prevent duplicate proposals.
Quarterly Audit Sales Leader Quarterly Full playbook review against current win/loss data. Any section not validated by recent wins is flagged for evidence-gathering or removal. ICP definition reviewed against last quarter's cohort LIR data.
New Rep Certification Sales Leader + Peer End of ramp Every new rep must demonstrate mastery of all 5 playbook components before carrying a full quota. Certification includes: ICP quiz, role-play of discovery call, review of one recorded presentation against rubric.
Annual Reset Sales Leader + Founder Annual Once per year, the full playbook is rebuilt from scratch based on current data — not incrementally patched from the previous version. This prevents "playbook debt" where outdated sections are never cleaned up.
