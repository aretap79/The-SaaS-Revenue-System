Why Deal Review Is the Manager's Highest-Leverage Activity

A sales manager who spends their time in meetings, on Slack, and in administrative work is squandering their most valuable resource: proximity to active revenue opportunities. The single highest-ROI activity for a sales manager is structured, rigorous deal coaching — the practice of sitting with a rep and helping them see their deals more clearly.

The Iannarino Principle: Deal Coaching Multiplies Your Team

Anthony Iannarino writes in Eat Their Lunch and throughout his body of work that the most effective sales managers function as force multipliers — they do not sell deals themselves, they develop the capability of their reps to win deals independently. Deal coaching is the mechanism. Every hour a manager spends coaching a rep's deal is an investment that compounds: the rep wins the current deal and carries better judgment into every future deal. Managers who skip deal coaching in favor of reporting and Slack messages produce transactional reps who depend on the manager to close their deals forever.

The Leverage Math

A manager with 8 AEs, each carrying $500K in quarterly pipeline, oversees $4M in active opportunities. If structured deal coaching improves the team's win rate from 22% to 27% — a 5-point improvement that is achievable with rigorous coaching — that is an additional $200K in closed revenue per quarter from the same pipeline, with no additional headcount. The manager's coaching is not a cost center. It is a revenue lever.

Deal Coaching ROI Model
Coaching Uplift = (New Win Rate − Current Win Rate) × Total Pipeline Under Management
Example: 8 AEs × $500K pipeline = $4M managed. Win rate improvement from 22% → 27% = $200K incremental revenue per quarter. Over four quarters: $800K additional ARR from coaching investment alone.

The Two Types of Deal Review: Pipeline vs. Deal Coaching

Most sales organizations conflate two fundamentally different activities under the label "pipeline review." Pipeline review and deal coaching have different purposes, different formats, different participants, and different outcomes. Mixing them produces a single meeting that does neither well.

Dimension Pipeline Review Deal Coaching Session
Purpose Assess quantity, coverage, and stage distribution of pipeline Improve quality and strategy on specific active opportunities
Question Being Answered "Do we have enough pipeline to hit the number?" "What is the best strategy to close this specific deal?"
Frequency Weekly (all deals, all reps) Bi-weekly or monthly (top 3–5 deals per rep)
Duration 30–45 minutes (full team) 30–60 minutes (1:1 per deal)
Manager Role Reviewer / interrogator Coach / thinking partner
Primary Output Pipeline gap identification, coverage ratio, forecast update Revised deal strategy, next 3 actions, risk mitigation plan
What Gets Measured Pipeline value, stage accuracy, velocity, coverage ratio MEDDICC completeness, champion strength, deal risk score
The Meeting Collapse Failure Mode

When pipeline review and deal coaching are collapsed into a single meeting, the result is a meeting that superficially covers many deals without deeply coaching any. Reps learn to give clean summary updates that satisfy the review without actually thinking through deal strategy. The manager checks a box ("I reviewed the pipeline") without moving any deal forward. This is management theater — the appearance of oversight without the substance of coaching.

The MEDDICC Deal Review Framework

MEDDICC (Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Identify Pain, Champion, Competition) is the most rigorous deal qualification and review framework in enterprise SaaS. Developed at PTC in the 1990s and evangelized by Mark Meddius, John McMahon, and others, MEDDICC functions both as a qualification framework and as a deal review template. Walking through each letter for each deal surfaces exactly what the rep knows, what they don't know, and where the deal is at risk.

Letter Element Definition Deal Review Questions Red Flag if Missing
M Metrics Quantified business impact of the problem and solution "What does this problem cost them today, in dollars or hours?" / "What's the measurable ROI of solving it?" Rep is selling features, not outcomes. Buyer has no reason to prioritize or fund.
E Economic Buyer The person with budget authority and final sign-off power "Who owns the budget for this purchase?" / "Have you met with the Economic Buyer directly?" / "What is their awareness of this initiative?" Deal will die in committee or at legal/procurement. Rep is selling to people without authority.
D Decision Criteria The formal and informal requirements the buyer will use to evaluate and select a vendor "What does success look like to them?" / "What are their 3 must-haves?" / "How do they compare vendors?" You are being evaluated on criteria you don't control and may not even know about.
D Decision Process The steps, timeline, and people involved in making the final purchase decision "Walk me through how a decision like this gets made at [Company]." / "What happens after the business case is approved?" / "Is there a procurement or legal step?" Unexpected steps or stakeholders will delay or kill the deal after verbal commit.
I Identify Pain The compelling business problem that drives urgency to solve "What is the cost of inaction?" / "What triggered this evaluation now vs. 6 months ago?" / "What happens if they don't solve this by end of year?" No urgency. Deal will slow-walk forever without a compelling event.
C Champion An internal advocate who has power, wants you to win, and will fight for you internally "Who is actively working to make this deal happen on the inside?" / "Has your champion introduced you to the Economic Buyer?" / "Would your champion take a reference call for us?" Without a true champion, the deal has no internal engine. It depends entirely on external rep effort.
C Competition The current or alternative solutions being evaluated, including "do nothing" "What else are they evaluating?" / "What is the incumbent solution?" / "What would happen if they decided not to change anything?" Rep is surprised by a competitor late in the process. "Do nothing" is always in the evaluation.
MEDDICC as a Deal Health Signal, Not a Checkbox

The purpose of walking through MEDDICC is not to confirm that each field is filled in the CRM. It is to identify the gaps in the rep's understanding of the deal — the questions they haven't asked, the stakeholders they haven't met, the criteria they don't know. A deal where the rep can answer every MEDDICC question with specificity is a deal that is well-understood. A deal where the rep says "I'm not sure" or "I think so" on multiple letters is a deal in serious risk.

The Deal Review Cadence: Weekly, Bi-Weekly, Monthly

The cadence of deal review determines whether the manager stays ahead of deal risk or reacts to missed quarters after the fact. The structure below is designed to create layered visibility — light-touch weekly checks on all deals, deep coaching sessions on the highest-value opportunities, and quarterly business reviews that assess system-level patterns.

Meeting Frequency Scope Duration Output
Weekly Pipeline Review Weekly All active deals across all reps 30–45 min (team) Pipeline coverage ratio, stage accuracy, forecast update, alerts on stalled/at-risk deals
Deal Deep-Dive (1:1) Bi-weekly Top 3–5 deals per rep, MEDDICC review 45–60 min per rep Revised deal strategy, next 3 actions with owner and deadline, champion status, deal risk score
Deal Strategy Session As-needed (for deals >3× ACV or high strategic value) One deal; multiple stakeholders 60–90 min Full account map, competitive analysis, executive engagement plan, mutual action plan
Monthly QBR (internal) Monthly All won/lost/slipped deals from prior month; pipeline for next 90 days 90 min (full team + leadership) Win/loss patterns, rep-level performance, pipeline generation assessment, forecast accuracy review

The Deal Review Meeting Structure: Running a 60-Minute Session

A poorly structured deal review devolves into a status update where the rep describes what happened and the manager nods along. A well-structured deal review is a thinking workout — the manager asks questions, the rep is forced to articulate their understanding, and gaps surface naturally. The structure is everything.

  1. Rep Prep (Before the Meeting) — 15 minutes of rep's time The rep completes a pre-meeting template: for each deal under review, they document their current MEDDICC assessment, the last 3 activities, the next 3 planned actions, the primary risk, and the probability of close this quarter. This forces the rep to think before the meeting — not during it. Managers who skip the pre-meeting prep waste the first 20 minutes of the session on setup.
  2. Context Setting (5 minutes) Manager reviews the pre-meeting notes. Rep gives a 2-minute verbal summary: deal amount, close date, current stage, last buyer interaction, and the one thing that is keeping them up about this deal. The manager does not speak during this summary — they listen for what is said and what is conspicuously absent.
  3. MEDDICC Walk-Through (20–30 minutes) Manager asks one MEDDICC question at a time. Rep answers. Manager probes. The manager's goal is not to fill in a template — it is to identify the gaps: the questions the rep hasn't asked, the stakeholders they haven't mapped, the criteria they don't understand. For each gap: "What would you need to do to get that answer before the close date?"
  4. Deal Strategy Discussion (10–15 minutes) Manager and rep discuss the 3 highest-risk elements of the deal and what the rep will do about each. "You said you don't have a champion yet. What's your plan to build one?" The manager offers options, not answers. "Have you considered…?" and "What would happen if you…?" are coaching frames. "Here's what you should do" is telling, not coaching.
  5. Action Capture (5 minutes) Manager and rep agree on the next 3 specific actions, each with an owner and a date. These are logged in CRM. The next deal review begins by reviewing whether these actions were completed. Accountability for deal actions is what makes deal review sessions compound over time.

Deal Qualification Scoring: The MEDDICC Score

Qualitative deal review is necessary but insufficient for managing a pipeline at scale. A quantitative MEDDICC scoring system enables managers to compare deals across reps, identify portfolio-level risk, and trigger escalation for deals below a threshold.

MEDDICC Deal Score (0–100)
Score = Σ (Element Score × Weight) for all 7 MEDDICC elements
Each element scored 0 (unknown/missing), 5 (partially understood), or 10 (fully documented and validated). Weighted: Champion (×2), Economic Buyer (×1.5), Identify Pain (×1.5), all others (×1). Total possible = 100. Deals scoring below 50 should not be in commit forecast. Deals scoring below 30 should be flagged for active coaching or disqualification.
MEDDICC Score Deal Health Forecast Category Manager Action
80–100 Well-qualified; low information risk Commit Validate close plan; ensure no late-stage surprises
60–79 Moderate gaps; manageable risk Best Case Deep-dive on missing elements; assign specific gap-closing actions
40–59 Significant gaps; high forecast risk Pipeline Intensive coaching; reassess close date; may need executive involvement
Below 40 Poorly qualified; unknown risk Omit from forecast Escalate for deal strategy session; consider disqualification or parking

The "4 Qs" Framework: Four Questions That Determine Deal Viability

For managers who need a rapid deal health assessment — during a pipeline review call or a pre-forecast conversation — the 4 Qs framework provides a 2-minute filter that surfaces the most critical deal risks without a full MEDDICC walk-through.

The 4 Qs for Rapid Deal Assessment

Q1: Do we have a compelling event? Is there a specific internal or external trigger that creates urgency for the buyer to make a decision by the close date? (Board mandate, budget reallocation, competitor action, fiscal year-end, leader change.) Without a compelling event, close dates are guesses.

Q2: Do we have a champion? Can the rep name one specific person inside the buyer organization who has power, wants them to win, and will fight internally for the deal? (Not a friendly contact — a champion who has skin in the game.)

Q3: Do we understand the decision process? Can the rep describe the specific steps — in sequence — that will occur between now and a signed contract? (Legal, procurement, security review, board approval.) Are there any steps the rep has not yet discovered?

Q4: Do we know the competition? Can the rep name every vendor being evaluated, including the "do nothing" option, and articulate the buyer's perception of each option's strengths and weaknesses?

Scoring the 4 Qs

Each Q is binary: Yes or No. A "maybe" or "I think so" is a No. Deals that score 4/4 are well-controlled. Deals that score 2/4 or below are high-risk regardless of dollar value and should not be in the commit forecast. The 4 Qs are not a replacement for MEDDICC — they are a triage tool to identify which deals need the full MEDDICC coaching session.

Feeding Forecast Integrity from Deal Review Quality

Forecast accuracy is a downstream function of deal review quality. Organizations that run rigorous, MEDDICC-grounded deal reviews produce forecasts that are accurate to within ±10%. Organizations that skip deal review and ask reps to self-report their own close probabilities produce forecasts that swing 40–60% from commit to actual. The mechanics are direct: better deal understanding → more accurate stage assignment → more reliable forecast.

Forecast Accuracy ← Deal Review Quality Chain
Rigorous Deal Review → Accurate MEDDICC Scores → Correct Stage Assignment → Accurate Forecast Category → Reliable Commit
Each link in the chain is only as strong as the honesty and rigor of the deal review session. Reps who sandbag their MEDDICC scores, managers who accept incomplete answers, and stage criteria that aren't enforced all break the chain at different points.
The Commit-to-Close Ratio

The most useful measure of forecast integrity is the commit-to-close ratio: the percentage of deals in the Commit forecast category that actually close in the committed period. High-performing teams maintain a commit-to-close ratio of 85–95%. Teams with poor deal review discipline typically run at 55–70% — meaning nearly half of what goes in Commit doesn't close. The gap between these numbers represents the quality gap in deal review.

Deal Strategy: Executive Introduction, Multi-Threading, and Stall Recovery

Deal review surfaces deal risk. Deal strategy is what you do about it. The three most common strategic challenges revealed by deal review are: no executive engagement, single-threaded relationships, and stalled deals with no urgency. Each requires a distinct tactical response.

When to Introduce Executives

Executive introductions should be part of the deal strategy for any deal above 2× ACV or with an Economic Buyer who has not yet been engaged by the rep. The introduction should be choreographed: the champion should facilitate the introduction, the AE should prepare the executive with context ("I'd like our CEO to connect with your VP — here's the value for both parties"), and the executive's participation should be planned around a specific purpose (strategic alignment, peer-to-peer credibility, removing a procurement obstacle). Spontaneous executive involvement without a specific purpose wastes the executive's capital and signals desperation.

Multi-Threading Strategy

A single-threaded deal — one where the rep has a relationship with only one contact at the buyer — is the most vulnerable deal in the pipeline. If that contact leaves, gets sick, loses internal support, or changes priorities, the deal is over. Multi-threading means intentionally building relationships with 3–5 stakeholders across the buyer organization: the champion, the Economic Buyer, at least one technical evaluator, and ideally one executive sponsor. The SDR handoff briefing should identify all known stakeholders; the AE's discovery process should systematically expand that map. Deal review should always ask: "Who else have you talked to at this account?"

Stall Symptom Root Cause Recovery Strategy
"We're still evaluating" No compelling event; buyer not prioritizing Introduce or surface a compelling event; quantify cost of delay
Champion goes quiet Champion has lost internal support or has deprioritized Reach out directly; ask "What's changed internally?" Simultaneously multi-thread to another stakeholder
Meetings keep rescheduling Low priority; champion is not protecting the process Test champion commitment: "If we reschedule again, I want to make sure this is still a priority. Should we pause the evaluation?" A true champion will fight for the meeting.
Procurement delay Process step not identified in decision process review Get champion to walk through procurement requirements immediately; use mutual action plan to parallel-process legal and procurement with business approval
"Budget not approved yet" Business case not made to Economic Buyer; or urgency insufficient Help champion build the business case; request direct meeting with Economic Buyer to present ROI model

Coaching Through Deal Review: Asking vs. Telling

The most common mistake managers make in deal review sessions is telling the rep what to do. Telling produces compliance. Asking produces judgment. The manager's goal is not to win any individual deal — it is to develop the rep's capacity to win deals independently over their entire career. Coaching through questions is the mechanism.

The Coaching Question Distinction

Telling (low development): "You need to get to the Economic Buyer. Ask your champion to set up an intro this week."

Asking (high development): "You mentioned you haven't met the Economic Buyer yet. What's your theory about why that is? What would it take to change that? What options do you have?"

The rep who is told what to do executes the tactic but learns nothing. The rep who is asked questions reasons through the situation, arrives at the answer themselves, and builds the mental model that makes them better in the next deal — and every deal after that.

The Manager's Coaching Question Bank
  • "What is the one thing about this deal that worries you most?"
  • "What does the buyer stand to lose personally if they don't solve this problem?"
  • "If the champion left tomorrow, what would happen to this deal?"
  • "What do you know about the decision process that you didn't know 30 days ago?"
  • "Have you tested whether [contact] is truly a champion? What tests have you run?"
  • "If we lose this deal, what's the most likely reason?"
  • "What would a competitor do right now that we haven't done?"
  • "What does the buyer need to believe to say yes by [close date]? Do they believe it?"
  • "What would you do differently if this deal were 3× bigger?"
  • "What has the buyer done recently that made you more or less confident in the close date?"

Red Flags in Deal Review: What Looks Good but Isn't

Experienced revenue leaders develop pattern recognition for deals that appear healthy but are actually at high risk. The red flags below are the most common disguises that stalled, lost, or fake deals wear in pipeline reviews. Surfacing these early saves the quarter.

Red Flag What the Rep Says What Is Actually Happening The Manager Asks
The Champion Without Power "My champion is super engaged — we talk weekly" The contact is friendly and available but has no budget authority or organizational influence "Has your champion introduced you to the Economic Buyer? Has your champion spent any political capital for you internally?"
"We're Evaluating" vs. "We're Buying" "They're doing a full evaluation and we're one of three vendors" The buyer is in "explore" mode, not "buy" mode — there may be no real decision intent or budget behind the evaluation "What's driving the evaluation right now? Is there an approved budget for this? What happens if the evaluation concludes and they don't select anyone?"
The Vanishing Close Date "The close date slipped to next quarter — they had some internal things come up" No compelling event exists; buyer is not motivated to decide by any particular date "What specifically happened internally? What would need to change for the timing to firm up? Is there a trigger that creates urgency for them?"
Big ACV, No Economic Buyer Contact "The deal is $180K — we're in legal" Rep has never spoken to the Economic Buyer; champion is carrying the deal but may not have authority to approve this spend level "Has the Economic Buyer formally approved this spend? What does your champion say about the EB's awareness of this deal?"
The Reference Delay "They want to speak to two references before we move forward" Buyer is not convinced internally; the reference request is a delay tactic while they resolve internal objections the rep doesn't know about "What specifically are they hoping to learn from the references? Who asked for references — your champion or someone else? What would a positive reference call change for them?"

Deal Review Templates: Questions, Fields, and Outputs

The deal review template creates consistency across the manager-rep interaction. It ensures that the same quality of interrogation is applied to every deal, and that the outputs of the session are captured in a format that feeds forecast integrity and rep development tracking.

The One Non-Negotiable of Deal Review

Every deal review session must end with 3 specific actions, each with an owner and a date. Not "follow up with the champion" — that is a category, not an action. "Email [Name] by Thursday with the ROI model we discussed, and ask for a 30-minute call with the Economic Buyer in the next two weeks" is an action. The specificity of the action reflects the specificity of the coaching. Vague actions produce vague results. Specific actions produce movement.
