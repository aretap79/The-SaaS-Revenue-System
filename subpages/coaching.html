The Coaching vs. Managing Distinction
The most fundamental shift a new sales leader must make — and the one most resist — is moving from doing to developing. Understanding the distinction in precise terms is the prerequisite to making the shift.
Iannarino's "Coaching Over Inspection" Principle

Anthony Iannarino argues that most sales managers operate primarily as inspectors: they check CRM entries, review pipeline coverage, chase forecast submissions, and monitor activity dashboards. These activities measure what has already happened. Coaching — by contrast — changes what will happen next. The inspector asks: "Did you do it?" The coach asks: "How can I help you do it better?" The inspector's intervention ceiling is compliance. The coach's intervention ceiling is transformation.

Managing (Inspection)Coaching
Reviews what happened Shapes what will happen next
Focuses on outputs and metrics Focuses on the behaviors and capabilities that produce outputs
"Your pipeline coverage is 1.8x. Fix it." "Your pipeline coverage is 1.8x. When you look at your prospecting over the last 30 days, what do you think caused the gap? What would you do differently?"
Creates compliance Creates capability
Replaces rep judgment with manager judgment Develops rep judgment through guided reflection
Scales with manager bandwidth Compounds — reps become more capable over time and require less management
Required but insufficient The primary value-add of sales leadership

A critical nuance: inspection is not bad. CRM data hygiene, pipeline coverage, activity standards — these require inspection to maintain. The point is that inspection is the floor, not the ceiling. A manager who only inspects is doing the minimum. A manager who primarily coaches — while also maintaining inspection as a hygiene function — is doing the job.

The 3 Types of Sales Coaching
Sales coaching is not a single activity. It spans three distinct domains that address fundamentally different development needs. Each type requires a different approach and different frequency.
Type 1: Skill Coaching

Skill coaching addresses the core selling competencies: discovery, prospecting, storytelling, objection handling, negotiation, executive presence, demo technique, closing. Skill gaps are the most common cause of underperformance in early-tenure reps (under 18 months) and the most frequently ignored cause of underperformance in mid-tenure reps (who are assumed to have the skills but may have developed bad habits).

When to use it: When you observe a specific skill deficit in a call recording, role-play, or deal review. "I listened to your discovery call with Acme and noticed you moved to demo before uncovering the business impact. That is a skill gap I want to work on with you."

Frequency: Every rep, every week — at least one skill element addressed in the 1:1 or via call review.

Type 2: Deal Coaching

Deal coaching addresses a specific live opportunity in the rep's pipeline. The goal is to help the rep increase their probability of closing the deal and, simultaneously, to help them develop the judgment to handle similar deals better in the future. Deal coaching is not the manager taking over the deal — it is the manager asking questions that develop the rep's strategic thinking about the deal.

When to use it: For any deal above a materiality threshold (e.g., any deal representing >10% of quota). During weekly 1:1s for all active deals in late stage.

Frequency: Weekly for all deals in Stage 3+. Monthly for all deals in pipeline.

Type 3: Career Coaching

Career coaching addresses the rep's long-term professional development. What does the rep want to become? What skills and experiences do they need to get there? How does their current role serve their long-term goals? Career coaching is the most powerful retention tool available to a sales manager — it demonstrates genuine investment in the rep as a person, not just as a revenue producer.

When to use it: Monthly (minimum). Quarterly formal review. At major career transitions (promotion eligibility, year-end review, personal life changes that affect career priorities).

Frequency: Monthly check-in; formal quarterly career review.

How to Run a Skill Coaching Session
The structure of an effective skill coaching session follows a four-stage process that must be sequential. Skipping stages produces advice-giving, not coaching.
  1. Observe Review a specific call recording, attend a deal review, shadow a live call, or observe a rep's demo. You must have specific, concrete, observed examples to coach from. Coaching based on abstract impressions ("you seem to struggle with objections") is not specific enough to produce behavior change. "At minute 14 of your call with Acme, the prospect objected on price and you immediately offered a 15% discount without probing for the underlying concern" is specific enough.
  2. Ask First — Elicit Self-Assessment Before offering your own feedback, ask the rep what they noticed. "How do you think that call went? What would you do differently?" Reps who identify their own gaps develop self-awareness and ownership that they don't develop when their gaps are identified for them. Reps often know what went wrong — they just need permission and structure to say it. When the rep's self-assessment aligns with yours, you confirm rather than tell. When it diverges, you have more information about their blind spots.
  3. Provide Specific, Behavioral Feedback Share one or two specific behavioral observations — not character assessments. "When the prospect asked about pricing, you gave a number before understanding the context of their budget and decision process. The result was that you entered negotiation from a position of weakness." Specific + behavioral + consequence. Not "you gave in too quickly on price" (too vague) and not "you are bad at negotiating" (character attack that triggers defensiveness).
  4. Practice and Apply Every coaching session should end with the rep practicing the corrected behavior — immediately, in the session. For call technique, this means a brief role-play. "Let's run the price objection scenario again. You start from where the prospect raises it." Practice converts intellectual understanding into muscle memory. Without practice, feedback produces insight but not behavior change. End with the rep committing to applying the technique in a specific upcoming call and reporting back.
The GROW Coaching Model
The GROW model (Goal, Reality, Options, Will/Way Forward) is the most widely used coaching framework in professional development contexts. It is equally effective for sales coaching and is particularly powerful for career and development coaching where the rep needs to do the thinking, not the manager.
StageQuestionsPurpose
Goal "What do you want to achieve in this conversation?"
"What would a successful outcome look like for you?"
"What is the specific result you are working toward?"		 Establishes the rep's own definition of success. Creates ownership of the coaching session.

"What data do you have?"		 Grounds the conversation in current facts. Prevents solutions being designed for a misdiagnosed problem.

"On a scale of 1–10, how committed are you to this plan? What would make it a 10?"

Deal Coaching with MEDDICC
MEDDICC (Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Identify Pain, Champion, Competition) is both a qualification framework and a deal coaching framework. Each element generates specific coaching questions that expose deal risk and develop rep judgment.
MEDDICC ElementCoaching QuestionRisk Signal
Metrics "What is the quantified business impact of solving this problem? Have they validated these numbers?" Rep can't articulate specific financial impact. Deal will stall at legal/procurement when they can't justify the investment.
Economic Buyer "Have you spoken directly with the person who controls the budget and has final sign-off? What did they say about this initiative?" Rep is selling to champion only. Deal will be blocked at the final decision stage by someone they have never met.
Decision Criteria "Do you know the formal criteria they are evaluating vendors against? Have you seen the evaluation scorecard?" Rep is assuming decision criteria based on technical features. Competitor may be winning on business criteria the rep is not addressing.
Decision Process "Walk me through every step from where you are today to signed contract. Who touches the paper at each stage?" Rep is unaware of legal, security review, procurement, or board approval requirements that will extend the timeline.
Identify Pain "What is the cost of the current situation? What happens to the business if they do nothing?" Rep is selling features, not solving pain. No urgency, no compelling event, deal will die at end of pipeline.
Champion "Who is your champion? Have they sold internally for you without you in the room? What evidence do you have?" Rep has a coach, not a champion. A coach gives information; a champion takes action on your behalf. Different probability of close.
Competition "Who are you competing against? What does the prospect like about the competitor? What does your champion say about their position?" Rep is unaware of competitive activity or dismissing it. Late-stage competitive surprises are almost always predictable with earlier investigation.
Using Call Recordings for Coaching
Gong, Chorus (now Clari Copilot), and similar conversation intelligence platforms have transformed coaching by giving managers direct, searchable, analyzable access to rep behavior in front of customers. The question is not whether to use them — it is how to use them without creating a surveillance culture.
The Right Way to Use Call Recording in Coaching
  • Ask reps to nominate calls for review: "Before our 1:1, share one call you felt good about and one you struggled with." This creates ownership and self-reflection, and it directs your attention to calls the rep themselves has identified as learning opportunities.
  • Use AI analysis as a starting point, not a verdict: Gong's talk ratio, filler word count, and question analysis are signals, not conclusions. A rep with a 70% talk ratio might have been doing a brilliant product walkthrough for a technically complex deal — or they might be pitching when they should be listening. The number tells you where to look, not what to conclude.
  • Celebrate excellent calls publicly: Share clips of outstanding calls with the team in Slack or Gong. "Alex nailed this objection at minute 8 — this is the model for how we handle pricing concerns." Positive modeling is as important as corrective coaching.
  • Establish a no-shame norm: Call recordings only work for coaching if reps are not afraid of them. Make explicit: "I use call recordings to help everyone get better — including me. They are never used as evidence against you in a performance conversation unless there is a conduct issue. They are a development tool."
Gong Coaching Benchmarks (B2B SaaS)
Talk ratio: Rep 40–60% | Customer 40–60% (optimal discovery call)
Questions asked: 15–25 per hour-long call (elite discovery)
Monologue length: <2 min for rep; <2.5 min for customer (conversational)
Next step rate: >80% of calls end with explicit next step scheduled
Topic switch rate: Manager monitors to detect rep failing to follow customer's lead
Source: Gong Labs research across 100M+ sales calls. These are baselines — your specific product, market, and sales motion may have different optimal ranges.
The Coaching Cadence
Coaching without a cadence is ad-hoc and insufficient. A structured cadence ensures every rep receives consistent development investment regardless of their current performance or the manager's immediate attention.
TouchpointDurationFrequencyContent
Weekly 1:1 30–45 min Weekly (non-negotiable) Pipeline review (10 min) + deal coaching (10 min) + skill coaching from call review (10 min) + career/development check-in (5 min). Rep drives the agenda — they come prepared with their top 2–3 questions and priorities.
Call Review Session 30 min Weekly (pre-1:1) Review 1–2 recorded calls before the 1:1 so coaching is grounded in observed behavior. Rep reviews their own calls first and comes with self-assessment.
Field Coaching / Live Deal 1–4 hours Monthly (minimum) Manager joins a live customer call, demo, or on-site visit. Either shadow (silent observer) or active participation. Debrief immediately after with the observe → feedback → practice structure.
Quarterly Career Review 60–90 min Quarterly Review of 90-day development plan progress. Goals for next 90 days. Career conversation using GROW model. What does the rep need to develop to reach their stated next-level goal?
Annual Review 90–120 min Annual Full year performance review. Career trajectory discussion. Comp plan review and preview. 12-month development plan. Manager assessment of rep's trajectory and investment plan for next year.
Coaching Underperformers, Average Performers, and Top Performers Differently
The biggest mistake in sales coaching is applying the same approach to every rep. The coaching strategy must be tailored to where the rep is on the performance spectrum.
Underperformers (Bottom Quartile, <70% Quota)

The primary diagnostic question is: will or skill? Underperformance caused by skill gaps is addressed through intensive coaching — specific, frequent, structured. Underperformance caused by will (low motivation, poor fit, wrong role) cannot be coached away. Applying intensive coaching to a will problem wastes the manager's most scarce resource (time) and delays the inevitable.

  • Increase coaching frequency to twice weekly during active improvement plan.
  • Focus exclusively on 1–2 specific skill gaps rather than trying to address everything at once.
  • Set 30-day behavioral commitments and review compliance weekly.
  • If no improvement in 60 days of structured coaching, escalate to formal performance management.
Average Performers (50th–75th Percentile, 80–110% Quota)

Average performers are the highest-leverage coaching investment in the entire sales organization. Moving an average performer from 90% to 120% quota attainment generates far more revenue impact per coaching hour than moving an underperformer from 50% to 90%. Average performers typically have identifiable skill gaps that are limiting their ceiling — they are capable of more with specific development.

  • Identify the 1–2 skill gaps that are limiting their ceiling (typically: executive engagement, complex negotiation, or strategic account management).
  • Assign stretch goals: larger deals, more complex deals, different verticals.
  • Create development opportunities: let them shadow top performers, present in team meetings, take on mentorship of new hires.
Top Performers (Top Quartile, >120% Quota)

The most common coaching mistake with top performers is ignoring them because they "don't need it." This is wrong on two dimensions. First, every elite performer has a ceiling they can still push through — the role of coaching is to identify what that ceiling is and build toward it. Second, ignoring top performers signals that the manager's investment is contingent on producing poor results — which breeds resentment and increases turnover.

  • Shift coaching focus from skill gaps to ceiling expansion: What would it take to go from $1.2M to $2M? Larger ACV? More strategic accounts? Better executive relationships?
  • Focus on career coaching: Where do they want to go? Are they building toward management? A more strategic IC role? What experiences do they need?
  • Leverage them as team assets: pair them with developing reps (creates teaching depth), have them present on strategy to the team, involve them in hiring panels.

Manager-as-Coach vs. Manager-as-Player

The most common trap for first-time sales managers — especially those promoted from top performer roles — is reverting to being a player when things get difficult. "Let me just take this call with you" becomes "let me just take this call." The manager who runs deals instead of coaching reps is temporarily boosting their team's near-term close rate while permanently limiting the team's long-term capability. Every time the manager plays instead of coaches, they are borrowing from the future. The goal is to build a team that produces excellent outcomes without the manager in every deal — that is the only definition of managerial leverage.
